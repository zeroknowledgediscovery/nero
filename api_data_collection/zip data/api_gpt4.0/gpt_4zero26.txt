However, Spain and Portugal weren't the only players in this era of exploration. England, France, and the Netherlands, too, started asserting their presence. The English explorer John Cabot landed on the North American continent in 1497 under the patronage of King Henry VII. France launched Jacques Cartier, who explored the St. Lawrence River, unveiling the vast lands of North America. Simultaneously, Dutch explorers experimented with different routes, navigating through uncharted waters to establish trading posts and colonies in far-off lands.

This Age of Exploration was not just about land conquest and treasure hunt; it revolutionized world-view. It heralded the concept of a global world, interconnected through trade, religion, and culture. This large-scale interchange profoundly impacted societal living and ecological patterns, influencing global demography.

Yet, this age also bore witness to the brutal side of human ambition. The discovery of the 'New World' led to the colonization of the indigenous people's lands, their exploitation, and the horrific transatlantic slave trade. The ambitious quest for wealth came at the cost of cultural devastation and human suffering.

The Age of Exploration ended in the early 17th century, but its profound influences imprinted history. It marked the beginning of globalization, led to the rise of Western Europe as global powers, initiated direct contact amongst all continents - a phenomenon that shaped the course of human history, for better or worse. This age of valor and ambition, discovery and devastation, continues to offer important lessons in global relations, human ambition, and the ethical consequences of human actions.
