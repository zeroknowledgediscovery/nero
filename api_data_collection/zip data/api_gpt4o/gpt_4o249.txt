Enrique Sanchez, a man of modest origins and the proud child of Spanish immigrants, lived on King Edward Avenue, a road famed for its winding, sunlit alleys and the persistent scent of salt wafting from the nearby sea. Each day he arose with the dawn, his heart as steadfast as the rhythm of his footsteps upon the lacquered floorboards of the small terraced home he occupied. A plumber by trade, Enrique’s hands were calloused and stained, years of toil evident in the crescents of grime permanently etched beneath his fingernails.

Despite the unrelenting routine dictated by dripping taps and leaky pipes, Enrique found solace in his art—an unexpected talent that had lain dormant until nights of solitude prompted the swirling of paint across canvas. His small living room was a gallery of dreams, an assortment of landscapes, portraits, and abstract mysteries penned in the margin of his life.

She appeared one spring morning, a breath of the season itself woven into the fabric of her being, a bouquet of daffodils clutched hastily in hand as she moved into the house next door. A bump of crates and tumbling laughter marked her arrival. Her name was Alice Green.

As days turned to weeks, Enrique became accustomed to the subtle hum of her presence, a melody that drummed alongside his heartbeat. She was there at the edge of his consciousness, her smile a half-crescent of moonlight, casting faint shadows on his thoughts.

Their acquaintance was inevitable, born of adjacent gardens and shared fence-post conversations. She was a maritime navigator, her days spent charting courses across the endless blue, her absence noted by the quiet that swallowed the street each time she left for the sea. When together, they spoke of stars and oceans, plumbing and paintings, creating a tapestry of camaraderie threaded through with glimmers of something more.

“Why do you paint?” she asked one afternoon, her voice a soft contralto that blended with the birdsong. Enrique paused, wrench in hand, gaze fixed on the patch of azaleas that framed her feet.

“To remember,” he replied, setting the tool aside, “and sometimes, to forget.”

“Is it easier to remember or forget?” Alice leaned against the fence, eyes alight with curiosity.

“A little of both, I think,” Enrique confessed, brushing a speck of dirt from his trousers. “Paint captures what words sometimes cannot. It’s a gamble, one that often pays in beauty or despair.”

She smiled then, that radiant crescent, revealing the hint of venturesome journeys etched within her. “Show me sometime?”

Enrique nodded, heart swelling with the promise of her interest, and so it began—a ritual of exchanges and lingering gazes, their friendship weaving tighter threads into the loom of existence.

The summer promised a luminous golden sheen upon Blackpool. King Edward Avenue flourished under the sun’s watchful eye, gardens blooming extravagantly as if in competition with one another. It was during this bucolic charm that Alice received news of her mother’s ill health, a casting shadow that tugged her heart back to the tumultuous embrace of the sea.

“I have to go,” she told Enrique. Their evening tea lost its warmth, going cold beneath the cloud of her words.

“How long?” Enrique questioned, unwilling to part with the image of her etched against the twilight.

“A month, maybe more. I need to sort things back home and go back to sea.” Her uncertainty made the air between them dense, thick with unsaid sentiments.

Enrique opened his mouth to respond, but the words scattered like doves startled from rest. Instead, he nodded, accepting the inevitable with a grace he did not feel. The loss of her presence chafed against his consciousness, leaving the days stretched thin between past laughter and future echoes.
