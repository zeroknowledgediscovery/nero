The sun crept lazily through the slats of the blinds, speckling the worn-out carpet of the tiny orphanage room in Sacramento. Dark strands of hair fanned across her pillow like shadows splayed across an empty canvas. She lay there, gripping the quilted edges of her blanket, as if expecting the end of a winding dream that never seemed to part with the night.

Each morning began like this, a restless dance between sleep and awakening, with ears tuned for the distant, shrill annunciations of the neighborhood roosters. Clara had lived in the orphanage for as long as memory served—its walls were her cradle, and its sounds were her lullaby. But until recently, it was the chaos of life rather than cockcrows that defined her mornings. Pancakes indeed offered a temporary distraction from the spiral of anxiety that tightened in her chest whenever she imagined confronting the rooster cries. Her pancakes sat largely untouched, syrup pooling around their edges like the muddy waters of untold adventures.
