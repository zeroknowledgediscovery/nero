The morning sun cast long shadows across the ornate chambers of the royal palace as King Edmund Rothschild III adjusted his crown one final time before the mirror. Perth's monarchy had endured for centuries, weathering political storms and social upheavals, yet on this particular Tuesday, the thirty-two-year-old sovereign felt an unusual restlessness stirring within his chest.

"Your Majesty," called his personal aide, Marcus, from the doorway. "The council meeting begins in twenty minutes."

Edmund nodded absently, his gaze drifting toward the large windows overlooking the city below. Perth stretched out before him like a living tapestry, its citizens going about their daily routines, unaware of the peculiar longing that had been gnawing at their king for weeks now.

It had started innocuously enough. While walking through the palace's east wing, Edmund had heard the most extraordinary sound emanating from one of the servant quarters. The melody was haunting, yet uplifting, carried by the gentle strumming of what he later learned was a guitar. The music had stopped him in his tracks, and for several minutes, he had stood transfixed in the hallway, listening to the unknown musician practice scales and simple chord progressions.

"Marcus," Edmund said suddenly, turning from the window. "Cancel the morning appointments."

His aide's eyebrows shot up in surprise. "Sir? The agricultural committee has been waiting three weeks for this audience."

"Reschedule them. I have something more pressing to attend to."

Before Marcus could protest further, Edmund strode past him into the corridor, his royal robes flowing behind him like purple waves. He had made a decision that would change not only his own life but the very foundation of how the people of Perth viewed their monarchy.

The music shop on Wellington Street was a modest establishment squeezed between a bakery and a used bookstore. Edmund had sent a palace scout to locate it the previous evening, instructing them to find the finest musical instrument retailer in the city. When he pushed through the glass door, a small bell chimed overhead, and the elderly shopkeeper looked up from tuning what appeared to be a violin.

"Good morning, sir. How might I help you today?" The man's eyes hadn't yet registered the identity of his customer.

"I'm looking to purchase a guitar," Edmund replied, deliberately keeping his voice casual. "Something suitable for a beginner."

The shopkeeper, whose nameplate read 'Harold Fitzgerald,' set down his violin and approached with professional enthusiasm. "Ah, excellent choice. The guitar is a wonderful instrument for someone just starting their musical journey. Acoustic or electric?"

"I'm not certain. What would you recommend?"

Harold studied Edmund carefully for the first time, and his expression shifted slightly as recognition began to dawn. "Forgive me, but you look remarkably familiar. Have we met before?"

Edmund had anticipated this moment. While Perth wasn't an enormous city, he had managed to maintain a relatively low public profile, preferring to conduct state business from within the palace walls rather than making frequent public appearances. However, his face was certainly known to most citizens through official portraits and occasional television broadcasts.

"I don't believe so," Edmund said smoothly. "About that guitar?"

Harold blinked, clearly uncertain but too polite to press the matter further. "Right, yes. For a beginner, I'd suggest starting with an acoustic. It's more traditional, doesn't require amplification, and you can take it anywhere. Let me show you a few options."

The next hour passed like a dream for Edmund. Harold proved to be an excellent teacher, patiently explaining the basics of holding the instrument, positioning fingers on the fretboard, and producing clean notes. The king's hands, more accustomed to signing legislation and shaking hands with dignitaries, felt clumsy and awkward wrapped around the guitar's neck.

"It takes time," Harold assured him, demonstrating a simple C major chord. "Even the most accomplished musicians started exactly where you are now."

Edmund attempted to mirror the finger placement, resulting in a muffled, buzzing sound that bore little resemblance to music. Despite the frustration, something deep within him felt alive in a way it hadn't for years.

"I'll take this one," he announced, indicating a beautiful mahogany acoustic guitar with mother-of-pearl inlays around the sound hole.

"Excellent choice. That's a Martin D-28, one of our finest instruments. It's quite an investment for someone just beginning, though. Perhaps you'd like to consider something less expensive until you're certain about your commitment to learning?"

"No, this one feels right." Edmund pulled out his wallet, hoping Harold wouldn't notice the royal insignia embossed on the leather. "Do you offer lessons?"

"I do indeed. Tuesday and Thursday evenings, or by appointment. Shall I put you down for next Tuesday?"

"Actually," Edmund hesitated, "would it be possible to arrange private lessons? I work unusual hours and prefer not to learn in a group setting."

Harold's expression grew thoughtful. "Private lessons are certainly available, though they are more expensive. Might I ask what you do for work? You have an interesting manner of speaking."

The question hung in the air like smoke. Edmund realized that maintaining his anonymity would be increasingly difficult, but he wasn't ready to reveal his true identity. The thought of Harold treating him differently, with the formal deference everyone else showed him, was unbearable.

"I work in government," he said finally, which was technically accurate. "Schedule can be unpredictable."

"Ah, a civil servant. Well, that explains the unusual hours. Yes, I can certainly arrange private lessons. Shall we say Thursday evenings at seven? I close the shop at six, so we'd have complete privacy."

Edmund agreed enthusiastically, paid for the guitar and his first month of lessons, and left the shop with a spring in his step that hadn't been there in years. The instrument case felt substantial in his hand, like it contained possibilities rather than simply wood and metal strings.

The return journey to the palace proved more complicated than anticipated. Several citizens recognized him despite his attempt at casual dress, and word of the king carrying a guitar case spread quickly through the city's gossip networks. By the time Edmund reached the palace gates, a small crowd had gathered, their faces displaying mixtures of curiosity and confusion.

"Your Majesty," called out Mrs. Henderson, the wife of a prominent local businessman, "is that a musical instrument?"

Edmund paused at the gates, considering his response. He could deny it, claim it belonged to someone else, or simply ignore the question entirely. Instead, he found himself smiling genuinely for the first time in months.

"Yes, Mrs. Henderson. I've decided to learn guitar."

The murmur that rippled through the crowd was not entirely positive. Edmund heard whispered phrases like "unseemly" and "beneath his station," but he also caught fragments of more encouraging responses. A young girl near the front of the group clapped her hands together excitedly.

Prime Minister Harrison exchanged glances with the other council members, and Edmund recognized the look that passed between them.
