Carlos felt his throat constrict as he watched the gentle waves lap against the shore. What had once been his sanctuary, his escape from the violence and corruption that plagued Rio's streets, had become his greatest torment. The transformation had been gradual at first - a reluctance to swim during his morning routine, then an avoidance of the beach altogether, until finally even the sound of running water in his apartment made his hands shake.

"Tell them to proceed," he managed, stepping backward instinctively as a larger wave crashed closer to his feet. "I'll review the scene from the boardwalk."

Luciana's dark eyes studied him with the kind of penetrating gaze that made her such an effective interrogator. She had been his partner for five years before his promotion to chief, and she knew him well enough to recognize when something was deeply wrong. The other officers might whisper about his recent behavior, but Luciana understood that Carlos Eduardo Monteiro had never been a man given to weakness or irrationality.

As he climbed the stone steps leading away from the beach, Carlos caught fragments of conversation from a group of uniformed officers standing near the patrol cars. The words "afraid" and "water" drifted to his ears, followed by barely suppressed laughter. He quickened his pace, his jaw clenched with the familiar mixture of shame and anger that had become his constant companion.

The victim, when finally retrieved from the ocean, told a story Carlos knew all too well. Another young man, probably no more than twenty-five, with the telltale signs of gang involvement tattooed across his chest and arms. Three bullet wounds to the torso, execution-style, likely dumped from a boat in the early hours of the morning. It was the fourth such case this month, part of an escalating war between rival factions that controlled different segments of Rio's drug trade.

The irony struck him again - he could study images of bodies pulled from the water with professional detachment, but the thought of investigating a crime scene at the marina or along the lagoon made his palms sweat. His phobia had become so severe that he'd started declining invitations to social events if they involved restaurants overlooking the bay, and he'd moved his apartment from Ipanema to a high-rise in Tijuca where he couldn't see the ocean.
