Professor Edmund Hartwell had always considered himself a man of letters, not loops and purls. At fifty-three, his life revolved around medieval manuscripts and the dusty corridors of Lancaster University's English department. Arthritis, the doctor had declared with clinical detachment, prescribing exercises that Edmund found both tedious and ineffective.

"Perhaps you should try something more... engaging," his colleague Dr. Sarah Pemberton had suggested during their weekly faculty meeting. "Something that keeps the hands active but the mind occupied." She'd mentioned knitting almost as an afterthought, the way one might suggest taking up watercolors or learning Italian. Edmund had dismissed the notion immediately, filing it away with other well-meaning but impractical advice.

Yet the suggestion lingered, nagging at him like a half-remembered quotation he couldn't quite place. By Thursday, his hands had seized up so badly that he could barely hold his fountain pen.
