But beneath the ordinary surface of this prairie settlement thrives a hidden world of fairies, creatures no larger than a human thumb, living in elaborate communities carved into the sandstone cliffs and hidden among the native grasses.

Rosemary Thornwick was not like other fairies in her clan. While her kin spent their evenings dancing under moonbeams or weaving spider silk into gossamer garments, Rosemary could be found hunched over tiny cooking fires, experimenting with combinations of prairie rose petals and wild mint, or carefully grinding saskatoon berries into the finest powders. The fairy community of Lethbridge had always been peaceful, content to live alongside the human world without interference. They built their homes in abandoned gopher holes, cultivated gardens in spaces between fence posts, and held their greatest celebrations in the sheltered areas beneath the High Level Bridge, where the acoustics made even the tiniest fairy voice carry like a symphony.

Rosemary's passion for culinary arts had earned her both admiration and gentle mockery from her peers. She had developed techniques for smoking microscopic portions of fish from the river, created delicate soufflés from dandelion fluff, and even learned to brew wines from the essence of various wildflowers that grew throughout the short prairie summers.

Her kitchen, if it could be called that, was a marvel of fairy engineering. Tucked into a natural cave formation in the coulees overlooking the city, it featured cooking surfaces made from polished pebbles, storage containers crafted from hollowed acorns, and an intricate system of mirrors that redirected sunlight to provide both illumination and cooking heat. The walls were lined with carefully organized shelves holding her collection of spices, each one painstakingly harvested and prepared by her own hands.

On this particular evening in late summer, as the sun began to set over the vast prairie sky, painting the coulees in shades of gold and crimson, Rosemary was putting the finishing touches on what she considered her masterpiece: a seven-course feast designed to celebrate the harvest season. The menu included delicate appetizers of crystallized morning dew served on rose petal crackers, a soup made from the essence of prairie sage, tiny portions of honey-glazed grasshopper prepared with wild thyme, and a dessert that incorporated the first frost flowers of the season.

She had been planning this meal for months, carefully timing the preparation to coincide with the peak ripeness of various ingredients. The other fairies in her community had initially shown polite interest in her invitation, but as word spread about the elaborate nature of the feast, more and more had expressed genuine excitement.
