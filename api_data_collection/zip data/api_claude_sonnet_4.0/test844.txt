The autumn wind carved through Colorado Springs with surgical precision, rattling the windows of the old Victorian house where Eve Blackwood had lived for as long as she could remember. She pressed her pale fingers against the frosted glass, watching students hurry across the campus quad below, their breath forming small clouds in the crisp October air. At nineteen, Eve possessed an ethereal beauty that seemed almost translucent in certain light—porcelain skin stretched over delicate bones, eyes the color of winter storms, and hair so black it absorbed light rather than reflecting it.

Her condition, as the doctors diplomatically called it, had been apparent since childhood. Eve felt emotions differently than others, experienced relationships as chess games rather than genuine connections. She understood people the way an entomologist understands insects—clinically, methodically, with fascination but without true empathy. Her parents, wealthy philanthropists who collected rare books and donated generously to local charities, had tried everything: therapy sessions that Eve manipulated for her own amusement, medications that she pretended to take, even a brief stint at a private facility in Aspen that she'd talked her way out of within three weeks.

The doorbell's chime echoed through the house's cavernous rooms, interrupting her observations. Eve glided down the mahogany staircase, her bare feet silent against the polished wood. Through the beveled glass of the front door, she could see a tall figure shifting nervously from foot to foot, clutching a worn leather satchel.

"You must be the tutor," she said, opening the door before he could knock again. The young man standing on her doorstep was not what she had expected. Instead of the elderly professor she'd imagined, she found herself face-to-face with someone perhaps twenty-five, with sun-weathered skin and calloused hands that spoke of physical labor rather than academic pursuits. His dark hair was slightly too long, curling at the edges, and his green eyes held the restless quality of someone who'd seen distant horizons.

"James Morrison," he said, extending his hand. "I'm here about the literature tutoring position."

Eve studied his face with the intensity of a predator evaluating prey, noting the slight nervous twitch at the corner of his left eye, the way he unconsciously shifted his weight to favor his right leg, the small scar above his eyebrow that suggested a childhood accident or fight. "Please, come in," she said, stepping aside to allow him entry. The house's interior was a study in controlled elegance: antique furniture arranged with museum-like precision, oil paintings in heavy gilt frames, and shelves upon shelves of first-edition books that her father collected but rarely read. James's eyes widened slightly as he took in the opulent surroundings, and Eve found herself analyzing his reaction with scientific interest.

"Your family has quite a collection," he said, gravitating toward the nearest bookshelf where leather-bound volumes of Dickens and Austen stood in perfect rows.

"My father's obsession," Eve replied dismissively. "He believes that surrounding himself with great literature will somehow elevate his soul. Eve felt something shift inside her chest—a flutter of genuine interest rather than mere predatory fascination. Most people recoiled from her calculated observations or tried desperately to please her. James seemed neither intimidated nor particularly impressed by her wealth or beauty. James turned to face her fully, and for a moment, she saw something flash in his eyes—pain, perhaps, or disappointment. "Sometimes we take the opportunities that present themselves," he said.
