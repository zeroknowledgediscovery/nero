Magnus Thornweaver pressed his back against the cold brick wall of the narrow Sunderland alley, his breath forming small clouds in the frigid morning air. The ancient cobblestones beneath his worn leather boots glistened with frost, and somewhere in the distance, he could hear the dreaded sound that made his powerful warlock blood turn to ice water in his veins. The unmistakable crow of a cockerel echoed through the maze of terraced houses that surrounded him.

His trembling fingers clutched at the obsidian pendant hanging around his neck, its surface smooth from years of nervous handling during moments exactly like this one. Magnus had faced demons from the seventh circle of hell without breaking a sweat, had stared down ancient dragons whose breath could melt steel, and had even negotiated peace treaties with the vengeful spirits of long-dead kings. But the sound of a simple farm bird reduced him to a quivering mass of anxiety and shame.

The irony wasn't lost on him that a man who could summon lightning from clear skies and bend reality to his will with nothing more than a whispered incantation was absolutely terrified of creatures that most people considered nothing more than alarm clocks with feathers. Magnus had tried everything over the years to overcome this peculiar phobia. He'd consulted with the most learned scholars at the Mystical Academy of Hexham, undergone hypnotherapy with a specialist in Newcastle, and even attempted to face his fears head-on by visiting a petting farm in Durham. That last experiment had ended with three fire engines, two ambulances, and a very confused farmer whose prize rooster had somehow been transformed into a pile of rainbow-colored confetti.

Another crow pierced the morning silence, closer this time, and Magnus felt his knees buckle slightly. He needed to get home to his flat on Roker Avenue before any of the neighborhood chickens decided to greet the dawn with their infernal noise. Moving as quietly as his shaking legs would allow, he began to navigate the familiar route through the back streets and shortcuts he'd memorized specifically to avoid the areas where local residents kept poultry.

The terraced houses on either side of him were identical in their Edwardian architecture, their small front gardens separated by low walls and iron railings that had seen better decades. Most of the windows were still dark at this early hour, their inhabitants blissfully unaware that one of the most powerful warlocks in northern England was creeping past their doorsteps like a common burglar. Magnus pulled his dark wool coat tighter around his shoulders and quickened his pace.

He'd been out all night dealing with a particularly stubborn poltergeist that had taken up residence in the basement of Henderson's Fish and Chips on High Street West. The spirit had been causing quite a disturbance, turning the oil in the fryers into what appeared to be luminescent green soup and rearranging the letters on the menu board to spell out increasingly creative profanities. It had taken Magnus the better part of six hours and three different banishment rituals to convince the entity to move on to whatever afterlife awaited it.

Such was the life of a modern warlock trying to make an honest living in a city that most people associated more with football and shipbuilding than supernatural phenomena. Magnus had inherited his magical abilities from his grandmother, Millicent Thornweaver, who had been something of a legend in the local mystical community before her death fifteen years earlier. She'd left him her cottage, her extensive library of spell books, and unfortunately, her rather unusual client base.

Most days, Magnus dealt with the sort of mundane magical problems that plagued ordinary citizens who happened to live in an area with a higher than average concentration of mystical energy. Haunted washing machines, gardens that grew backwards into the soil, mirrors that showed reflections from three days in the future, and the occasional curse that needed lifting from someone who'd been rude to the wrong person at the local market. It wasn't glamorous work, but it paid the bills and allowed him to live quietly without drawing too much attention to himself.

The morning air carried with it the familiar scents of the city awakening coal smoke from chimneys, the salt tang from the nearby coast, and the ever-present aroma of bacon cooking in countless kitchens. Magnus turned the corner onto Thornhill Terrace and immediately froze. There, perched on the low wall directly in his path, sat the largest, most magnificent cockerel he had ever seen in his life.

The bird was easily twice the size of any rooster that had any right to exist, with iridescent black feathers that seemed to shimmer with an otherworldly energy. Its comb was a brilliant shade of crimson that reminded Magnus uncomfortably of fresh blood, and its eyes held an intelligence that seemed far too keen for a simple farm animal. The creature regarded him with what could only be described as amusement, tilting its head to one side as if studying an interesting specimen.

Magnus felt his breathing become shallow and rapid. Sweat began to bead on his forehead despite the cold morning air, and his hands started to shake so violently that he had to clench them into fists to stop the trembling. The cockerel continued to stare at him, and Magnus became increasingly convinced that this was no ordinary bird. There was something distinctly magical about the creature, something that made the pendant around his neck grow warm against his chest.

He tried to take a step backward, but his legs seemed to have forgotten how to function properly. The rational part of his mind knew that he could easily deal with this situation with magic. A simple teleportation spell would have him home in seconds, or he could transform the bird into something less threatening, like a butterfly or a small potted plant. But the phobia that had plagued him since childhood wasn't interested in rational solutions. It wanted him to panic, to run, to do anything except confront the source of his terror.

The cockerel opened its beak, and Magnus braced himself for the sound that would send him into a complete psychological meltdown. Instead of the expected crow, however, the bird spoke in a voice that was distinctly human, distinctly male, and distinctly amused.

The bird continued to regard him with those unnaturally intelligent eyes, its head cocked at an angle that suggested it was thoroughly enjoying Magnus's obvious discomfort.

"I can see from your expression that you weren't expecting conversation," the cockerel continued, its voice carrying a slight accent that Magnus couldn't quite place but reminded him of someone who had spent considerable time in academic circles. The combination of his phobia and the sheer impossibility of the situation had left him in a state of psychological paralysis that no amount of magical training had prepared him for. His grandmother Millicent had taught him to handle demons, spirits, and all manner of supernatural creatures, but her lessons had never covered the proper etiquette for conversing with articulate poultry, particularly when said poultry belonged to a species that sent him into fits of terror.

The morning air was growing warmer as the sun climbed higher, and Magnus could hear the sounds of the neighborhood beginning to wake up around them.
