The morning mist clung to the hills surrounding Mercy General Hospital like a shroud, thick and oppressive in ways that made even the most seasoned staff members quicken their pace through the parking lot. Valya Gronski pressed her massive frame against the rusted chain-link fence that bordered the employee entrance, her yellowed fingernails scraping against the metal links as she watched the early shift arrive. At seven feet tall and nearly four hundred pounds, she cut an imposing figure that seemed to absorb the pale dawn light rather than reflect it.

Her obsession had begun three months ago with a simple rejection. Nurse Patricia Kellerman had been working the night shift when Valya stumbled into the emergency room, clutching her bleeding hand and demanding immediate attention. The injury was self-inflicted, though Valya would never admit it – a deliberate slice across her palm made with a kitchen knife in a moment of desperate loneliness. She had hoped for sympathy, for gentle hands to tend her wound, for someone to care about her pain.

Instead, Patricia had treated her with professional detachment, her latex gloves creating a barrier between them as she cleaned and bandaged the cut. When Valya tried to grab the nurse's wrist, asking for her phone number in a voice that rumbled like distant thunder, Patricia had firmly pulled away and called for security. The humiliation burned through Valya's chest like acid, transforming her desperate need for connection into something far more sinister.

Now she knew Patricia's schedule by heart. Seven AM to seven PM, four days a week. She knew about the small birthmark on Patricia's left thumb, visible when she removed her gloves. She knew that Patricia drove a blue Honda Civic with a dent in the rear bumper. Most importantly, she knew that Patricia was beautiful in ways that made Valya's heart ache with violent longing.

The fence rattled as Valya shifted her weight, pulling out the ancient flip phone she kept in her coat pocket. The device was cracked and held together with electrical tape, but the camera still functioned well enough for her purposes. Through the viewfinder, she watched Patricia emerge from her car, coffee in one hand, purse slung over her shoulder. The morning light caught the honey-colored strands in Patricia's brown hair, and Valya felt that familiar surge of rage mixed with desire that had come to define her waking hours.

But Patricia wasn't alone today. Walking beside her was another nurse, a petite woman with short black hair and quick, nervous movements. Valya had seen her before – Sarah Chen, according to the employee directory she had memorized during her late-night internet searches. Sarah worked in pediatrics, which meant she had gentle hands, soft touches, the kind of maternal instincts that Valya craved with an intensity that frightened even her.

As the two women approached the employee entrance, they paused near the fence where Valya crouched in the shadows. Their conversation drifted through the morning air, punctuated by Sarah's musical laughter and Patricia's lower, more serious tones.

"I'm telling you, Pat, something's not right about that security situation," Sarah was saying, adjusting her scrubs with delicate fingers. "Three different nurses have reported feeling watched lately. Jenny from the ICU swears she saw someone taking pictures through the cafeteria windows last week."

Patricia shook her head, her movement sending waves through her hair that made Valya grip the fence tighter. "Hospital administration doesn't want to hear about it unless we have concrete evidence. You know how they are – liability and lawsuits are all they care about."

"Maybe we should start varying our schedules," Sarah suggested, pulling out her ID badge. "Or parking in different spots. Just until they figure out what's going on."

The words hit Valya like physical blows. They were talking about her, she realized, discussing ways to avoid her surveillance. The rejection was happening all over again, multiplied and magnified by their casual dismissal of her presence in their lives. Her breathing grew ragged, producing small puffs of vapor in the cool morning air.

As Patricia and Sarah swiped their badges and entered the building, Valya raised her phone and captured a series of quick photographs. The images were blurry and taken from an awkward angle, but she could make out their hands clearly enough. Patricia's slender fingers wrapped around her coffee cup, the familiar birthmark visible on her thumb. Sarah's small hands adjusting her stethoscope, wedding ring glinting in the fluorescent light from the doorway.

The photos would join the dozens of others in Valya's collection – images stolen from parking lots and through windows, cropped and enhanced to focus on hands, on thumbs, on the intimate details that spoke to her of vulnerability and softness. She posted them to her Facebook account under fake names, adding captions that grew progressively more disturbing with each upload.

Twenty minutes later, Valya was trudging back to her apartment through the industrial section of town where she lived in isolation. The building was a converted warehouse with thin walls and unreliable heating, populated by the kinds of people who asked no questions and expected no friendship. Her unit was on the fourth floor, accessible only by a freight elevator that groaned and shuddered with each trip.

The apartment itself was a testament to obsession. Every available wall space was covered with printed photographs, newspaper clippings, and hand-drawn sketches of the hospital staff. At the center of it all was a shrine dedicated to Patricia – a collage of images surrounding a stolen name tag that Valya had retrieved from a dumpster behind the hospital. Candles burned constantly around the display, their wax dripping onto the scarred wooden floor.

Valya settled into her computer chair, which creaked ominously under her weight, and began uploading the morning's photographs to her various social media accounts. She had seventeen different Facebook profiles, each with carefully crafted backstories and stolen profile pictures. The deception allowed her to friend hospital employees without raising suspicion, giving her access to their personal information and daily updates.

As she worked, her massive fingers surprisingly nimble on the keyboard, Valya composed captions for the new images. "Beautiful hands make beautiful mistakes," she typed beneath Sarah's photo. For Patricia's image, she wrote something more direct: "Some people don't know what they're missing until it's too late."

The posting process took nearly an hour, during which Valya consumed an entire bag of stale donuts and two liters of cola. Sugar was her primary fuel now, the only thing that seemed to quiet the constant hunger that gnawed at her from within. It wasn't hunger for food exactly, though she ate compulsively. It was hunger for acceptance, for love, for the kind of human connection that seemed to come so easily to everyone else.

Her phone buzzed with a Facebook notification. Someone had liked one of her posts – not one of the recent ones, but an older image of a different nurse entirely. Valya clicked through to investigate and found herself looking at the profile of Detective Humphrey Castellanos, a man she didn't recognize with kind eyes and an athletic build that spoke of physical discipline.

According to his profile, Humphrey was a detective with the local police department and an avid rower who had moved to the area from Bakersfield several years ago. His photos showed him on various waterways, his rowing shell cutting through calm morning waters with precision and grace. There was something peaceful about the images that made Valya pause in her scrolling.

But peace was a luxury she couldn't afford. The detective's interest in her posts meant trouble, meant questions she wasn't prepared to answer. Valya quickly blocked the profile and deleted the liked post, her heart racing as she considered the implications. How had he found her? How many of her accounts had he discovered?

The paranoia that had been building for weeks suddenly crystallized into sharp focus. They were watching her now – not just the nurses she followed, but law enforcement. The net was tightening around her activities, threatening to end the only source of purpose and connection she had left in her miserable existence.

Valya stood and began pacing the length of her apartment, her heavy footsteps shaking the floor and probably disturbing the neighbors below. She needed a plan, needed to accelerate her timeline before the authorities could interfere. The casual observation and photography that had sustained her for months was no longer enough.
