The morning sun cast long shadows across the Sunshine Coast as Captain Miranda Still adjusted her aviator sunglasses and gazed out at the pristine runway. Twenty-three years of flying commercial aircraft had taught her to read the sky like an old friend, but today something felt different. The radio crackled with static, and beneath the usual chatter of air traffic control, she heard something that made her blood run cold.

A high-pitched cackle echoed through the transmission, followed by what could only be described as the most obnoxious raspberry she'd ever heard. The sound was deliberate, malicious, and utterly inhuman.

"Control tower, this is Captain Still. Did you copy that unusual transmission?" she spoke into her headset, her voice steady despite the unease creeping up her spine.

"Negative, Captain Still. All frequencies appear normal from our end," came the reply from ground control.

Miranda frowned. In all her years of flying, she'd developed an intuition about things that didn't belong in the sky. This wasn't her imagination – something was out there, something that shouldn't exist.

The first incident had occurred three weeks ago in downtown Brisbane. Young Marcus Thompson, a university student, had been walking home from his part-time job when he encountered what witnesses could only describe as a "small, green creature with pointed ears and yellow eyes." The police report, which Miranda had somehow gotten her hands on through her brother-in-law who worked in law enforcement, detailed how the creature had blown raspberries at Marcus while making what the officer delicately termed "racially motivated statements."

But the raspberry wasn't the worst part. Within minutes, Marcus found himself completely wrapped in toilet paper from head to toe, mummified so thoroughly that passersby initially thought it was some kind of street performance. By the time the fire department freed him, the creature had vanished, leaving behind only a trail of torn toilet paper and the lingering smell of something ancient and musty.

The second incident happened in Surfers Paradise. Tommy Chen, a nineteen-year-old lifeguard, reported a similar encounter. The racist taunts had been worse this time, more targeted, more vicious. Tommy described feeling paralyzed by some invisible force as toilet paper materialized from thin air, spinning around him like a tornado until he was completely immobilized.

What disturbed Miranda most about these reports wasn't just the bizarre nature of the attacks, but the pattern she was beginning to recognize. The creature, which witnesses had started calling Benjamin after it apparently introduced itself with that name while hurling slurs, seemed to be targeting young men specifically. The racist component suggested something deeper, more sinister than random mischief.

As Miranda performed her pre-flight checks on the Boeing 737, her mind wandered to her grandfather's stories. Old Pete Still had been a pilot too, flying reconnaissance missions over the Pacific during World War II. He used to tell her tales that the family dismissed as the ramblings of an old man, stories about creatures he'd seen in the clouds, things that military reports would never officially acknowledge.

"There's more in this world than what people want to believe, Mandy," he'd told her when she was twelve years old, using the nickname only he was allowed to call her. "The sky holds secrets that would make most folks lose their minds if they knew the truth."

"Just static," Miranda replied, making a split-second decision not to involve James in what might be a very dangerous situation. If this creature was real, if it was as malicious as the reports suggested, she couldn't risk putting her crew or passengers in harm's way.

She walked to the gate and quietly informed the ground crew that the flight would be delayed due to a minor mechanical issue that needed inspection.
