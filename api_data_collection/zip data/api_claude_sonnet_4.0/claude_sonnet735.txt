The salt-tinged wind whipped across Southend Pier as Zara Matthews clutched her textbooks against her chest, her dark hair dancing wildly in the October breeze. She'd always found solace in the longest pleasure pier in the world, especially when the weight of her final year at Westcliff Academy threatened to crush her spirit. The sound of seagulls crying overhead mixed with the distant laughter of tourists, creating a symphony that had become the soundtrack to her contemplative walks.

At seventeen, Zara possessed an intensity that made her teachers uncomfortable and her peers keep their distance. Her emerald eyes held depths that seemed far too knowing for someone her age, and she had a habit of staring off into space as if she could see something others couldn't. Which, as it happened, she sometimes could. But those were thoughts she kept buried deep, dismissed as daydreams or tricks of an overactive imagination.

"You're late again, Matthews." The voice cut through her reverie like a blade through silk. Zara didn't need to turn around to know who had spoken. That particular combination of disdain and authority belonged to only one person at Westcliff Academy.

Adrian Blackwood stood behind her, his perfectly pressed uniform somehow managing to look effortless despite its precision. His dark hair caught the afternoon light, revealing auburn highlights that Zara had noticed far too many times for her own comfort. At eighteen, he carried himself with the confidence of someone who had never doubted his place in the world, never questioned whether he belonged.

"Late for what exactly?" Zara turned slowly, raising an eyebrow with practiced indifference. "Last I checked, taking a walk wasn't subject to your approval, Blackwood."

His grey eyes flashed with something that might have been amusement if it had come from anyone else. "Student Council meeting. You know, that thing you volunteered for at the beginning of term? Before you decided that showing up was optional?"

Zara's stomach dropped. She had completely forgotten about the meeting, too lost in her own thoughts about the strange dreams that had been plaguing her for weeks. Dreams where she stood in this exact spot, but everything looked different – older, somehow, as if she were seeing Southend through the lens of a different time entirely.

"Right," she said, hoping her voice sounded steadier than she felt. "The meeting where we discuss the thrilling topic of autumn formal decorations. How could I forget?"

Adrian stepped closer, close enough that she could smell his cologne – something expensive and understated that probably cost more than her family spent on groceries in a month. "You know, Matthews, your complete disregard for responsibility would be impressive if it wasn't so predictable."

There it was, the familiar spark of anger that ignited whenever Adrian Blackwood opened his mouth. They'd been circling each other like this since primary school, locked in a dance of mutual antagonism that had only intensified over the years. He was everything she wasn't – wealthy, privileged, effortlessly successful. The golden boy of Westcliff Academy, destined for Oxford or Cambridge, while she scraped by on scholarships and sheer determination.

"And your complete inability to function without controlling everyone around you would be pathetic if it wasn't so transparent," Zara shot back, taking a step forward herself. She refused to be intimidated, even though he towered over her five-foot-four frame.

For a moment, they stood frozen in their familiar tableau of hostility. But something was different today. The usual cold calculation in Adrian's eyes had been replaced by something warmer, something that made Zara's pulse quicken in a way that had nothing to do with anger.

"You have paint in your hair," he said quietly, his voice losing its edge.

Zara's hand flew instinctively to her temple, where her fingers found a streak of blue acrylic. She'd been working on a canvas that morning, losing herself in swirls of color that seemed to move and shift when she wasn't looking directly at them. Another one of those strange moments that she tried not to think about too deeply.

"Art class," she mumbled, suddenly self-conscious.

"I know." Adrian's response was so soft she almost missed it. "I've seen your work in the exhibitions. It's..." He paused, seeming to struggle with the words. "It's extraordinary."

The compliment hit Zara like a physical blow. In all their years of verbal sparring, Adrian had never said anything remotely positive about her. She searched his face for signs of mockery or manipulation, but found only an honesty that made her chest tight.

"Don't," she said, stepping back. "Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Pretend to be nice. Whatever game you're playing, I'm not interested."

Adrian's expression hardened again, the moment of vulnerability disappearing behind his usual mask of superiority. "Trust me, Matthews. The last thing I want to do is be nice to you."

"Good," Zara said, though the word felt like sandpaper in her throat. "Because the last thing I need is your fake sympathy."

"I'll be there when I'm there," she called over her shoulder, not trusting herself to look back.

For just a moment, the careful walls he'd built around himself had crumbled, revealing something vulnerable and real beneath the surface.
