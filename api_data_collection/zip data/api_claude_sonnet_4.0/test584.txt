She had been pounding these pavements for three years now, ever since the divorce, when everything familiar had crumbled and she'd needed something—anything—to keep her moving forward. The rhythmic slap of her feet against wet concrete had become her meditation, her therapy, her salvation.

But she ran with the determination of someone who had discovered that motion could mask the stillness of despair. Her neighbours in the cramped flat above Mrs. Patterson's bakery had grown accustomed to the sound of her alarm at five-thirty each morning, followed by the careful creak of floorboards as she dressed in the dark.

This particular Tuesday felt different, charged with possibility. The Great British Amateur Baking Championship had received over ten thousand applications this year, and she'd submitted hers on a whim after Mrs. Patterson had praised her experimental lavender and honey scones.

"You've got something special, love," the elderly baker had said, flour dusting her apron as she'd watched Sarah knead dough with the same focused intensity she brought to her running. "Something that can't be taught."

Sarah had laughed it off then, but the seed had been planted. Baking had become her other refuge, a creative counterpoint to the physical release of running. Where running emptied her mind, baking filled it with precise measurements and careful timing. Both required patience, discipline, and faith in the process.

The email arrived at 6:47 AM, just as Sarah was stretching against the garden wall behind the bakery. Her phone buzzed, and she almost ignored it—her ritual was sacred, and interruptions felt like tiny betrayals. But something made her glance at the screen.

"Congratulations, Sarah McKenzie. You have been selected as one of twelve finalists for the Great British Amateur Baking Championship."

Her hands trembled as she read the words again, then a third time. The email went on to detail the filming schedule, location requirements, and—her heart sank—travel arrangements. Contestants were responsible for their own transportation to the studio in London, though accommodation and meals would be provided once there.

The train fare to London would cost more than she earned in a week, and she had exactly £37.50 in her current account. At seventy-three, Mrs. Patterson still arrived at the bakery before dawn, maintaining routines established over forty years of business.
