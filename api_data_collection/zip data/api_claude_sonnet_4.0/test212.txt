She pressed her face against the cold window of her cramped office above the bakery, watching commuters hurry past with their heads down and their secrets tucked away. At thirty-four, she'd built a modest legal practice handling divorces, petty theft cases, and the occasional drunk driving charge. Nothing that would make headlines or repair the damage she'd done to her family's reputation.

Her phone buzzed against the mahogany desk her grandfather had left her—one of the few family heirlooms she hadn't been forced to sell. The caller ID showed Detective Morrison's number, and Lindy's stomach tightened. Morrison only called when something had gone terribly wrong.

"Harrington speaking."

"Lindy, we need you down at Naperville General. There's been another incident." Morrison's voice carried that particular strain that came from staring at crime scenes too early in the morning.

"Another what kind of incident?" But even as she asked, Lindy felt the familiar chill of premonition crawling up her spine.

"Another doctor. Third one this month. Same... signature."

Lindy closed her eyes and leaned back in her chair, which creaked in protest. The signature Morrison referred to wasn't something you'd find in any criminology textbook. Three doctors in four weeks, all found in their offices after hours, all killed with what appeared to be crude but effective weapons, and all discovered completely bald. "I'll be there in twenty minutes."

The drive to the hospital gave her time to think, which wasn't necessarily a blessing. Two years ago, she'd been Lady Lindy Harrington-Blackwood, married to pharmaceutical heir Jonathan Blackwood, living in a mansion that required its own zip code, and practicing law as a hobby rather than a necessity. The Harrington family had a reputation stretching back four generations. Her great-grandfather had been a circuit judge, her grandfather a district attorney, her father a respected civil rights lawyer. They'd built their legacy on justice and integrity, and Lindy had managed to tarnish both in a single afternoon. Now she was just another lawyer in a strip mall office, trying to prove she deserved the family name.

Naperville General's parking lot buzzed with police cars and news vans. Lindy parked her aging Honda between two squad cars and straightened her blazer—navy blue, professional, hiding the fact that she'd bought it at a discount store. Image mattered, especially when you were trying to rebuild one.

The crime scene hit her like a physical blow. Dr. Chen had been a small woman, probably in her early forties, and she'd died hard. "That's what they think. No signs of struggle related to that part. Whoever did this took their time with it."

Morrison flipped through his notebook. "Dr. Rodriguez was an emergency room physician, Dr. Patel was in oncology, and Dr. Chen was cardiology. Different departments, different schedules, different social circles as far as we can tell. Only thing they had in common was working in hospitals."

"Maybe nothing. Maybe everything." Lindy looked up at the windows of the hospital, wondering how many other doctors were working late tonight, wondering if they were being watched. "Morrison, I want to see the files on the other two victims. Everything you have."

Morrison studied her face for a long moment. They both knew why she was really here, why she needed this case. Her family's reputation, her own sense of worth, everything depended on proving she could still do the work she'd been born to do.

"Meet me at the station tomorrow morning. Early."

As she drove home through the empty streets of Naperville, Lindy couldn't shake the feeling that something was watching her from the shadows between streetlights. She checked her rearview mirror obsessively, but saw nothing except her own haunted reflection.

Her apartment was a far cry from the mansion she'd once called home. Two bedrooms, one bathroom, and furniture from three different garage sales. But it was hers, bought with money she'd earned, and there was something to be said for that. She poured herself a glass of wine—cheap but drinkable—and spread her notes across the kitchen table.

Three doctors. Three hospitals. Three bald corpses. By three in the morning, she had filled several pages with notes, questions, and theories.
