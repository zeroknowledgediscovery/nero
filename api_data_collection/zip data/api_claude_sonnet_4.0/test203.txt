The fog rolled thick through Edinburgh's cobbled streets that October morning, carrying with it whispers of something unnatural stirring in the highlands beyond. Mr. Thomas Cook adjusted his white apron for the third time that hour, his weathered hands trembling slightly as he arranged the breakfast service at The Crown & Anchor. At sixty-two, he'd served thousands of meals, heard countless stories from travelers, but nothing had prepared him for what he'd witnessed three nights prior.

The memory haunted him still—that impossibly tall figure silhouetted against the moonlit moor, her movements graceful despite her enormous stature. She'd been crouched over something, or rather someone, and the flash of what appeared to be an old camera had illuminated the scene for just a moment. Long enough for Cook to see the young man's face, frozen in terror, and her delicate fingers positioning something silver near his mouth.

"Cook! Table seven needs their porridge!" barked Mrs. Henderson, the inn's proprietress, her voice cutting through his reverie.

"Aye, right away," he muttered, ladling the steaming oats into a bowl. His mind wandered again to the newspaper clipping he'd hidden in his coat pocket—the third young man found dead in the highlands this month, each discovered with peculiar marks around their lips, as if someone had pressed something against them repeatedly.

The bell above the tavern door chimed, and Cook glanced up to see a constable entering, his uniform damp with morning mist. Constable Murray was a regular, usually cheerful, but today his face bore the grayish pallor of a man who'd seen too much.

"The usual, Constable?" Cook asked, though he already knew the answer would be different today.

"Just tea, Cook. Strong as you can make it." Murray slumped onto a stool at the bar, removing his cap to reveal thinning brown hair. "Found another one this morning."

Cook's blood ran cold. "Another...?"

"Fourth young man in six weeks. Same as the others—bonnie lad, probably nineteen or twenty. But this time..." Murray's voice dropped to barely above a whisper. "This time we found something else."

The constable reached into his coat and produced a daguerreotype, its edges cracked and yellowed with age. Cook squinted at the image—it showed a pair of lips, young and full, captured in remarkable detail despite the photograph's obvious antiquity.

"Strangest thing," Murray continued, accepting the steaming cup of tea with grateful hands. "The photograph was placed right on the lad's chest, like some sort of calling card. And those marks around his mouth—they're getting more pronounced with each victim."

Cook felt his knees weaken. He'd seen those same careful, deliberate movements three nights ago, watched that enormous figure work with the patience of an artist perfecting their craft. The pieces were beginning to form a picture he didn't want to complete.

"Constable," he began hesitantly, "have you considered that perhaps... perhaps this isn't the work of an ordinary person?"
