Her calloused hands, stained with years of wire grease and copper residue, trembled slightly as she set down her multimeter. At forty-three, she had spent the better part of two decades proving herself in a profession dominated by men who still occasionally snickered when she introduced herself as "Doctor" rather than simply Maya the electrician.

The title had cost her everything - her marriage, her relationship with her twin daughters, and nearly her sanity. Seven years of night school while working double shifts, clawing her way through advanced electrical engineering coursework that most of her classmates tackled with the luxury of full-time study and family support. Her ex-husband Marcus had grown tired of microwaved dinners and a wife who fell asleep over textbooks instead of paying attention to his needs. The girls, Samantha and Rebecca, now twenty-one and living with their father in Manchester, barely returned her phone calls.

Maya wiped her forehead with the back of her sleeve, leaving a streak of grime across her weathered brow. The workshop smelled of ozone and old coffee, punctuated by the persistent drip of water through a roof that had needed repair since before she'd inherited the building from her mentor, old Eddie Grimshaw. Eddie had been the only person who'd ever believed she could make something of herself in this business, and his death three years ago had left her more isolated than ever.

The radio crackled to life in the corner, interrupting her brooding with the evening news. "Police are investigating reports of another missing woman in the Greater Manchester area. Sarah Chen, aged twenty-seven, disappeared while walking home from her job at the textile factory last Tuesday evening. This marks the fourth disappearance in as many weeks, prompting authorities to warn women to avoid traveling alone after dark."

Maya's stomach clenched. She knew Sarah Chen - had rewired the woman's flat just six months ago when the previous electrician had botched the job so badly it was a miracle the building hadn't burned down. Sarah had been kind, offering tea and biscuits while Maya worked, chatting about her plans to save enough money to bring her elderly mother over from Hong Kong. She was the sort of person who deserved better than whatever fate had befallen her.

The workshop door chimed as someone pushed through, bringing with them a gust of cold air and the sound of footsteps on wet pavement. Maya looked up to see Detective Inspector Carol Hutchinson, her rain-soaked coat dripping onto the already stained concrete floor. Carol was one of the few people in Bolton who still spoke to Maya with genuine warmth, a friendship forged years ago when Maya had helped the police with a particularly complex case involving electrical sabotage at the town hall.

"Evening, Maya," Carol said, her voice carrying the exhaustion of someone who'd been working far too many consecutive shifts. "I know it's late, but I need to pick your brain about something unusual we found at the latest crime scene."

Maya gestured toward the pair of mismatched chairs she kept for the occasional client consultation. "Sarah Chen?"

Wires snaked across the ceiling in patterns that seemed almost organic, pulsing with an eerie green glow that matched the paint covering the victims. Multiple generators hummed in the background, their outputs carefully regulated through a series of custom-built control panels that would have required significant expertise to construct.
