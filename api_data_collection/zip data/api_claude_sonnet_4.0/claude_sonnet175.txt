He pressed his palm against the cold glass, watching the city below stir to life while something ancient and terrible stirred within his dreams. The coffee in his other hand had grown cold hours ago, but he couldn't bring himself to move from this vigil he'd been keeping since before dawn.

Three nights running, the same nightmare had visited him. A figure in tattered robes standing over a glass bowl, golden shapes floating lifeless on the surface of dark water. The figure would turn, and Alejandro would glimpse eyes that held the hunger of centuries, a face that seemed familiar yet impossibly distant. Then he would wake, gasping, his sheets soaked with perspiration that smelled faintly of sulfur.

"Mijo, you look like death warmed over," his grandmother Elena called from the kitchen, her voice carrying the authority of someone who had raised seven children and buried two husbands. She appeared in the doorway wearing her faded blue robe, her silver hair braided down her back in the same style she'd worn since she was fifteen. "Come eat something before you waste away to nothing."

Alejandro turned from the window, managing a weak smile. "I'm fine, Abuela. Just couldn't sleep again."

Elena's dark eyes narrowed as she studied his face. She had always possessed an uncanny ability to see through his pretenses, a gift that had served her well during his troubled adolescence when strange things began happening around him. Glasses would crack when he was angry, plants would wither or bloom based on his moods, and once, memorably, he had accidentally turned his least favorite teacher's hair bright purple for a week.

"The dreams again?" she asked, moving closer with the measured steps of someone whose joints protested morning movement.

He nodded, not trusting his voice. Elena reached up and placed her weathered hand against his cheek, and he felt some of the tension leave his shoulders. Her touch had always been a balm, grounding him when the world seemed to shift beneath his feet.

"Thirty-two years old and still running to your grandmother when the monsters come calling," she murmured, but her tone held only love. "Though I suspect these monsters are different from the ones that used to hide under your bed."

She was right, of course. The nightmares of his childhood had been simple things – faceless bogeymen and imaginary creatures that fled when she turned on the lights and sang old lullabies. These new visions carried weight, a sense of inevitability that settled in his bones like winter cold.

Elena guided him to the small kitchen table where she had already set out two cups of fresh coffee and a plate of arepas still warm from the griddle. The familiar ritual of sharing breakfast with her helped push back the lingering dread from his dreams, at least temporarily.

"Tell me about them," she said, settling into the chair across from him.

Alejandro hesitated, then began describing the recurring nightmare. As he spoke, Elena's expression grew increasingly troubled. She reached across the table and took his hand when he described the golden shapes floating in the dark water.

"Goldfish," she said quietly. "In the dream, they were goldfish."

"Yes, but how did you—"

"Because I've been having the same dream, mijo. And I suspect we're not the only ones."

The arepa turned to ash in his mouth. "What do you mean?"

Elena rose and walked to an old wooden cabinet that had belonged to her own grandmother. From behind a collection of religious candles and faded photographs, she withdrew a leather journal so old its binding had gone soft as cloth. She returned to the table and opened it carefully, revealing pages covered in spidery handwriting in multiple hands and languages.

"Our family has carried certain... responsibilities for generations," she began. "I had hoped they would pass you by, that you could live a normal life. But the signs have been growing stronger."

Alejandro stared at the journal, recognizing some of the writing as Elena's younger hand, other sections written in Spanish so archaic he could barely understand it. Still other passages appeared to be in languages he didn't recognize at all, though strange symbols seemed to shift and writhe at the corners of his vision as he looked at them.

"What kind of responsibilities?"

"We are guardians, protectors of the balance between light and shadow. For centuries, our bloodline has produced those capable of wielding forces most people can't even imagine." Elena turned several pages, stopping at an illustration that made Alejandro's blood freeze. Below the image, text in multiple languages surrounded a single phrase written in Spanish: "El Devorador de Luz" – The Light Eater.

Alejandro pushed back from the table, his chair scraping against the worn linoleum. "This is insane, Abuela. You're talking about magic and prophecies like they're real things."

"Are they not?" Elena asked mildly. "What would you call what happened to Señora Martinez's roses last month when you walked past her garden thinking about María leaving you?
