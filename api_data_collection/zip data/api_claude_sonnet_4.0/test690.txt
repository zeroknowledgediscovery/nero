Marcus Holloway had always been precise with numbers, but when it came to human interaction, the mathematics never quite added up. After seventeen years of balancing books for various Tampa businesses, watching the endless parade of receipts and invoices blur together like rain on a windshield, he made a decision that surprised even himself. He would become a hermit.

The announcement didn't go over well at Henderson & Associates, the mid-sized accounting firm where Marcus had spent the last eight years methodically climbing from junior associate to senior accountant. His supervisor, Janet Kowalski, adjusted her reading glasses and stared at him across her cluttered desk as if he had just declared his intention to join a traveling circus.

"A hermit?" Janet's voice carried the particular strain of disbelief reserved for employees who request extended leave during tax season. "Marcus, you can't be serious. We're three weeks out from the April deadline, and the Riverside Shopping Center account is a complete disaster. Johnson left all his files in complete chaos before he transferred to the Miami office."

Marcus shifted uncomfortably in the chair that had become as familiar as his own living room furniture. The fluorescent lights hummed overhead with their characteristic electrical persistence, casting everything in the same unflattering pallor that had illuminated his working life for nearly two decades. He had rehearsed this conversation multiple times during his commute, but now the words felt foreign and unwieldy in his mouth.

"I've already finished the preliminary work on Riverside," he said, his voice steadier than he felt. "The discrepancies in their quarterly reports stem from a systematic error in how they've been categorizing maintenance expenses versus capital improvements. I've prepared detailed notes for whoever takes over the account."

Janet leaned back in her chair, which responded with a protesting squeak that somehow managed to sound both tired and annoyed. Outside her office window, Tampa's afternoon traffic crawled along Kennedy Boulevard in its typical sluggish dance of brake lights and exhaust fumes. Marcus had driven that same route every day for eight years, following the same sequence of traffic lights and construction zones with the methodical precision he brought to everything else in his life.

"Marcus, I need to ask you something, and I want you to be completely honest with me." "Is this about what happened with the Brennan audit? Because if you're having some kind of reaction to that situation, we can talk about it. The Brennan audit. Marcus felt something tighten in his chest at the mention of it. Three months earlier, he had uncovered what appeared to be systematic embezzlement within Brennan Construction's payroll system. The discovery should have been routine, the kind of forensic accounting that Marcus actually enjoyed for its puzzle-like quality. Instead, it had turned into a nightmare of depositions, hostile confrontations, and ultimately a lawsuit that painted Marcus as either incompetent or malicious, depending on which lawyer was speaking.

"It's not about Brennan," Marcus said, though they both knew that wasn't entirely true. "I've just been thinking about my life, about what I want from it. And I've realized that I don't want to spend it in offices anymore. I don't want to interact with people who see me as either a problem to be solved or a tool to be used."

Janet replaced her glasses and studied him with the same methodical attention she brought to reviewing financial statements. "Marcus, you're talking about throwing away a career that you've spent nearly two decades building. You have seniority here, respect, a pension plan that's actually worth something. And for what? To sit alone in your house and do what exactly?"

The question hung in the air between them like smoke from a cigarette that no one wanted to acknowledge. Marcus had been asking himself the same thing for weeks, lying awake in his modest two-bedroom house in Seminole Heights, listening to the sounds of his neighbors living their lives through thin walls. What would he do with his time? How would he support himself? What did a modern hermit actually look like?

"I have savings," he said finally. "Enough to last several years if I'm careful. And I'm always careful."

"Savings don't last forever, Marcus. And then what? You come back here in three years, five years, and try to restart your career? The accounting world doesn't stand still for people who decide to drop out."

Marcus stood up from the chair, feeling suddenly claustrophobic in Janet's small office with its motivational posters and family photographs and the persistent smell of microwaved coffee. Through the window, he could see the parking lot where his ten-year-old Honda Civic sat in its assigned space, the same space he had parked in every day for eight years. Soon, someone else would park there, someone who wouldn't mind the fluorescent lights or the endless parade of other people's financial problems.

"I understand your concerns," he said, "and I appreciate everything you've done for me here. But I've made my decision. I'll work through the end of the month to transition my accounts, but after that, I'm done."

Janet sighed and turned back to her computer screen, where a spreadsheet full of billable hours and project deadlines awaited her attention. "I'll need your letter of resignation by tomorrow morning. HR will want to schedule an exit interview, and you'll need to return your key card and any company materials you have at home."

"Marcus?" Janet looked up at him again, and for a moment her professional demeanor softened into something that might have been genuine concern. "Are you sure you're okay? I mean, really okay?"
