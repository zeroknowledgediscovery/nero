The morning fog clung to Plymouth's harbor like a shroud, thick and gray and unforgiving. Detective Inspector Sarah Groeneveld stood at her office window, watching the fishing boats disappear into the murk one by one. She'd been staring out at nothing for twenty minutes now, her coffee growing cold in her hands, her mind wrestling with something that didn't make sense.

Three teenagers. Three different locations across the city. All found with their ears dyed an unnatural shade of lilac.

The first had been discovered in Mayflower Park, sprawled beneath the war memorial like some grotesque offering. Sixteen-year-old Marcus Webb, his school uniform still pressed and clean except for the massive trauma to his chest. The forensics team had worked the scene for hours, but it was the ears that haunted everyone who saw the body. Someone had taken great care to dye them that precise color, working the purple tint into every fold and crevice.

Sarah turned away from the window and picked up the case file again. The second victim, Emma Harrison, age seventeen, had been found two days later near the Barbican. Same cause of death, same methodical attention to the ears. The killer had used what appeared to be professional-grade hair dye, applied post-mortem with surgical precision. It was deliberate, ritualistic, and completely baffling.

The third body had appeared yesterday morning in Central Park. Thomas Chen, fifteen years old, discovered by his own mother when she'd come looking for him after he failed to return home. The woman's screams had brought half the neighborhood running, and Sarah had arrived to find the crime scene contaminated by well-meaning civilians and traumatized family members.

She dropped into her chair and rubbed her temples. Twenty-three years on the force, and she'd never encountered anything quite like this. The killer wasn't leaving traditional calling cards or obvious clues. No messages, no theatrical staging beyond the ear dyeing itself. Just efficient, brutal murders followed by this bizarre cosmetic ritual.

"Inspector?" Detective Constable Mills knocked on her door frame. "The pathologist's report just came in on the Chen boy."

Sarah waved him in. Mills was young, maybe twenty-five, still enthusiastic about the job in a way that made her feel ancient. He handed over the manila folder with the eagerness of someone who still believed every case could be solved with enough hard work and determination.

"Same pattern?" she asked, though she already knew the answer.

"Identical. Single blow to the chest, massive internal trauma. Time of death estimated between ten and midnight. And the ears..." Mills paused, his usual confidence faltering slightly. "Dr. Peterson thinks the dye job took at least an hour. Whoever did this wasn't in any hurry."

Sarah nodded grimly. An hour spent with a dead teenager, carefully applying hair dye like some demented beautician. It spoke to a level of control and detachment that made her skin crawl. This wasn't a crime of passion or opportunity. It was methodical, planned, executed with the kind of precision that suggested experience.

Her phone buzzed against the desk. Sarah glanced at the screen and felt her stomach drop. Detective Chief Inspector Hartwell, probably calling to ask why she hadn't caught their killer yet. She let it ring twice before answering.

"Groeneveld."

"Sarah, I need you in my office. Now." Hartwell's voice was tight with tension. "We've got a problem."

The walk down the corridor felt longer than usual. Other detectives looked up from their desks as she passed, their expressions ranging from curious to sympathetic. Word traveled fast in a station this size, and everyone knew she was heading up the investigation into what the local press had started calling the "Lilac Ear Murders." It was a sensationalized name that made her cringe, but it had stuck nonetheless.

Hartwell's office smelled like stale cigarettes and instant coffee. The Chief Inspector was standing behind his desk, his usually immaculate uniform wrinkled and his gray hair disheveled. In the chair across from him sat a woman Sarah didn't recognize, middle-aged with the kind of severe expression that suggested authority.

"Detective Inspector Groeneveld, this is Agent Patricia Walsh from the National Crime Agency," Hartwell said without preamble. "Agent Walsh, DI Groeneveld is leading our investigation into the recent murders."

Walsh stood and extended her hand. Her grip was firm, professional, and cold. "Inspector. I'm afraid I have some disturbing news about your case."

Sarah remained standing, sensing that whatever was coming would be easier to absorb on her feet. "What kind of news?"

"Similar murders have been reported in Manchester, Birmingham, and Leeds over the past week. Same methodology, same signature with the ear dyeing. We're looking at a coordinated series of killings across multiple jurisdictions."

The words hit Sarah like a physical blow. She'd been treating this as a local problem, something contained within Plymouth's borders. The idea that it was part of something larger, more organized, changed everything.

"How many victims total?" she asked.

"Eleven confirmed so far. All teenagers, all killed in the same manner. But there's more." Walsh opened her briefcase and pulled out a thick folder. "We have reason to believe these aren't all the work of one individual. The geographic spread and timing suggest multiple killers working independently but following the same pattern."

"Copycats?" Hartwell asked.

"That's our working theory. Someone commits the first few murders, the details leak to the media, and suddenly you have people across the country imitating the crimes. It's not unprecedented, but the speed of replication in this case is unusual."

Sarah sank into the remaining chair, her mind racing. If Walsh was right, they weren't dealing with a single serial killer but with some kind of viral phenomenon. Multiple killers, multiple crime scenes, multiple jurisdictions all working against them.

"What do we know about the original?" she asked. "The first killer?"

"Based on timeline analysis, we think your Plymouth murders may be among the earliest. Possibly the source." Walsh spread out a map covered in red pins. "The pattern suggests the behavior originated somewhere in the Southwest and spread outward."

Sarah studied the map, noting how the pins clustered around major population centers. London had three markers, Manchester had two, and there were single pins scattered across smaller cities throughout England and Wales.

"Any witnesses? Security footage? Physical evidence linking the scenes?"

"Nothing concrete. Whoever started this is careful, methodical. They leave minimal forensic evidence beyond the dyed ears themselves. But we did find something interesting at the Birmingham scene." Walsh pulled out a photograph showing what appeared to be a boot print in mud. "Size fourteen, possibly larger. Industrial work boot with a distinctive wear pattern."

Sarah leaned forward to examine the image. The print was massive, suggesting someone well above average height and weight. Combined with the physical evidence from her own scenes – the force required to inflict the chest trauma, the ability to move the bodies with apparent ease – it painted a picture of someone unusually large and strong.

"Any other physical descriptors?"

"A witness in Leeds reported seeing a tall figure near the discovery site around the estimated time of death. Very tall, possibly six and a half feet or more. But the sighting was at night, and the witness admits to being intoxicated, so it's not entirely reliable."

Hartwell cleared his throat. "Agent Walsh will be coordinating the multi-jurisdictional response. Inspector Groeneveld, I want you to work closely with the NCA team. Share everything – witness statements, forensic reports, your investigative insights. This has gone beyond a local matter."

Sarah nodded, though part of her resented having her case absorbed into a larger investigation. These were her victims, her crime scenes, her responsibility to their families. But she also recognized the reality of the situation. If there really were multiple killers operating across the country, Plymouth CID didn't have the resources to handle it alone."I'll need full access to your case files and crime scenes," Walsh continued, pulling out a tablet and stylus. Sarah watched the agent's efficient movements, the way she organized documents with practiced precision. Everything about Walsh suggested someone accustomed to taking control, to imposing order on chaos. It was probably exactly what this investigation needed, but Sarah couldn't shake the feeling that something important would be lost in the transition from local to national case. The victims would become statistics, their individual stories subsumed into a larger pattern of violence.

"There's something else you should know," Sarah said, reaching for her own files. "The dye job isn't random. I had our forensics team analyze the color composition – it's not just any purple. The specific shade matches a discontinued hair dye called 'Twilight Lilac' that was pulled from the market about eighteen months ago due to chemical safety concerns. Someone either has old stock or knows how to recreate the exact formula."

Walsh looked up from her tablet with renewed interest. "That's significant. How difficult would it be to obtain this particular dye?"

"Nearly impossible through normal retail channels. But there are ways – online auction sites, professional salon suppliers, possibly even manufacturing it independently if someone had the chemical knowledge. Our forensics expert thinks the consistency across all three Plymouth victims suggests the killer has access to a substantial quantity of the product."

"Or they're very skilled at replication," Walsh mused, making notes on her tablet. "This could be our first real lead. If we can track down sources of Twilight Lilac across all the affected areas, we might be able to identify connection points between the killers."

Hartwell stood and moved to the window, his hands clasped behind his back in a gesture Sarah recognized as his thinking pose. "How do we know the copycats are using the same specific dye? The media reports just mention purple or lilac coloring."

"We don't know yet," Walsh admitted. "That's why getting forensic samples from all jurisdictions is crucial. If the copycats are using different products, it might help us distinguish between the original killer and the imitators. It could also tell us whether the specific dye choice has significance beyond just the visual impact."

Sarah felt a familiar tightness in her chest, the sensation that always accompanied breakthrough moments in an investigation. The dye wasn't just a signature – it was a clue. Someone with access to discontinued professional hair products, someone with enough supply to treat multiple victims, someone methodical enough to spend an hour on post-mortem cosmetic work. It suggested a particular type of killer, possibly someone with connections to the beauty industry or chemical manufacturing.

"I want to revisit the crime scenes," she said suddenly. "All three of them, with fresh eyes. If this killer is the original, there might be additional evidence we missed while focusing on the obvious signature elements."

Walsh nodded approvingly. "Good thinking. Sometimes the most important clues are the ones that don't fit the established pattern. I'll have our forensics team coordinate with yours – they might spot something your people overlooked, or vice versa."

"There's one more thing that's been bothering me," Sarah continued, leafing through the pathologist's reports. "The victims don't seem to have any obvious connections. Different schools, different social circles, different neighborhoods. Marcus Webb was from a working-class family near the docks. Emma Harrison lived in one of the posh estates near the university. Thomas Chen's parents run a restaurant in Chinatown. If our killer is selecting these kids specifically rather than opportunistically, what's the selection criteria?"

"Age range?" Hartwell suggested.

"Fifteen to seventeen, but that's not particularly narrow. And the geographic spread within Plymouth suggests the killer is mobile, comfortable operating across different areas of the city. That implies local knowledge, possibly someone who's lived here for a significant time."

Walsh was taking detailed notes, her stylus moving rapidly across the tablet screen. "What about timing? Any patterns in when the bodies were discovered versus estimated time of death?"

Sarah consulted her files, spreading the timeline reports across Hartwell's desk. "Marcus Webb was killed on a Tuesday night, body discovered Wednesday morning by a jogger. Emma Harrison died Thursday night, found Friday afternoon by tourists visiting the Barbican. Thomas Chen was murdered Saturday night and discovered Sunday morning by his mother."

"Every other night," Walsh observed. "That suggests either cooling-off periods between killings or a schedule driven by external factors. Work commitments, family obligations, something that dictated when the killer had time and opportunity."

"Or psychological compulsion," Sarah added. "Some serial killers follow internal rhythms, cycles that make sense only to them. The two-day interval could be significant to the killer's mental state or personal mythology."

The three of them stared at the timeline in silence, each lost in their own thoughts. Sarah found herself visualizing the killer's week – Tuesday night hunting and killing Marcus Webb, Wednesday and Thursday cooling down or planning, Thursday night taking Emma Harrison, another rest period, then Saturday night claiming Thomas Chen. It suggested someone with remarkable self-control, able to manage their compulsions according to a predetermined schedule.

"If the pattern holds," Hartwell said quietly, "and if they're still in Plymouth, the next murder would be Tuesday night."

The words hung in the air like a physical presence. Sarah checked her watch – it was Thursday afternoon. They had five days to identify and apprehend the killer before another teenager became a victim. Five days to process evidence, coordinate with multiple agencies, track down leads, and somehow make sense of a crime pattern that defied easy explanation.

Walsh closed her tablet and stood. "I'll need office space and access to your computer systems. My team will arrive tomorrow morning – two forensics specialists, a behavioral analyst, and a data coordinator. We'll try to minimize disruption to your ongoing operations."

"Appreciated," Hartwell replied, though his tone suggested he had little choice in the matter. "Inspector Groeneveld will be your primary liaison. She knows the local terrain and has established relationships with the victims' families."

Sarah nodded, already mentally reorganizing her schedule to accommodate the federal presence.
