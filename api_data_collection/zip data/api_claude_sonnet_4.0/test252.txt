The rain hammered Los Angeles like bullets against concrete, each drop exploding into a thousand smaller fragments that caught the neon light bleeding from storefront windows. Detective Sarah Weems pressed her palm against the cool glass of her apartment window, watching the city blur into watercolor streaks of red and blue. The coffee in her other hand had gone cold hours ago, but she kept sipping it anyway, tasting nothing but bitter grounds and her own exhaustion.

Three nurses. Three goddamn nurses, and nobody seemed to care except her.

The first had been Maria Santos, forty-two, mother of three, found in the parking garage beneath St. Mary's Medical Center with her throat opened like a second mouth. The second was Jennifer Polk, twenty-six, fresh out of nursing school, discovered in her apartment bathroom, posed like she was taking a bath except the water was pink and her eyes stared at nothing. The third was yesterday's nightmare: Carol Hutchinson, fifty-five, veteran ICU nurse, found in the hospital's supply closet with her uniform folded neatly beside her cooling body.

But it wasn't just the murders that kept Sarah awake. It was the fingers.

Each victim was missing the ring finger from their left hand. Clean cuts, surgical precision, like whoever did this knew their way around medical instruments. The crime scene techs found traces of vinegar at each location, just drops really, but enough to make Sarah's nose wrinkle with recognition. Her grandmother used to preserve vegetables in white vinegar, filling mason jars with cucumbers and onions that would keep through the winter months.

Sarah took another sip of cold coffee and tried not to think about what else someone might preserve in vinegar.

The phone rang, cutting through the rain's steady percussion. Sarah didn't need to check the caller ID to know it was Lieutenant Morrison. He only called this late when another body had turned up or when he wanted to pull her off a case. Tonight, she suspected it might be both.

"Weems," she answered, her voice rough from too many cigarettes and not enough sleep.

"We got another one," Morrison said without preamble. "Mercy General. Nurse named Patricia Gomez, thirty-eight, found about an hour ago in the chapel."

Sarah closed her eyes. The chapel. Even killers weren't supposed to violate sanctuaries, but apparently this one didn't give a shit about holy ground. "Same M.O.?"

"Ring finger's gone. Smell of vinegar. But Sarah..." Morrison paused, and she could hear him lighting one of his cheap cigars. "This one's different. Whoever did this, they left something behind."

"What kind of something?"

"You need to see it for yourself. And Sarah? Bring your A-game. The brass is breathing down my neck on this one. Four dead nurses in two weeks, and the media's starting to call our perp the Finger Collector. We need this wrapped up before the mayor starts making phone calls."

Sarah was already reaching for her jacket before Morrison hung up. The leather was still damp from her last crime scene visit, but she pulled it on anyway, checking that her service weapon was secure in its holster. Four nurses. The bastard was accelerating, which meant he was either getting sloppy or getting confident. Both possibilities worried her.

The drive to Mercy General took twenty minutes through empty streets slick with rain. Sarah had worked homicide for eight years, long enough to develop a sixth sense about crime scenes. Some felt angry, violence crackling in the air like electricity before a storm. Others felt sad, heavy with loss and missed opportunities. But as she walked through the hospital's automatic doors, breathing in the antiseptic smell that never quite masked the underlying odor of human suffering, she felt something else entirely.

She felt watched.

Morrison was waiting for her near the information desk, his bulk filling out a wrinkled suit that had seen better decades. His face was gray with fatigue, and when he looked at her, Sarah saw something she'd never seen before in her lieutenant's eyes: fear.

"It's bad, Sarah. Real bad. The body's been moved, but the scene..." He shook his head. "Thirty-two years on the force, and I've never seen anything like this."

They walked through corridors that should have been bustling with late-night medical activity but instead felt eerily quiet. A few nurses glanced at them as they passed, their faces tight with worry. Sarah couldn't blame them. Someone was hunting their profession, turning healers into victims, and nobody knew who might be next.

The chapel was small, maybe twenty pews facing a simple wooden cross. Sarah had expected to find it cordoned off with yellow tape and crawling with crime scene techs, but instead it was nearly empty. Just Morrison, herself, and Detective Rick Valdez from the crime scene unit, who looked like he'd rather be anywhere else in the world.

"Where's the body?" Sarah asked.

"Morgue," Valdez replied. "But you need to see where we found her first."

He led them to the front pew, where someone had arranged hospital flowers in the shape of a heart. The petals were wilted and brown at the edges, and they gave off a sweet smell that made Sarah's stomach turn. But it wasn't the flowers that made her breath catch in her throat.

It was the message scrawled across the wooden seat in what looked like dried blood.

"FOR MY LOVE DETECTIVE WEEMS. JANE KNOWS YOU'RE LONELY TOO."Sarah stared at the message until the words burned themselves into her retinas. The dried blood had flaked away in some places, leaving gaps in the letters that made them look like broken teeth. Her hands began to shake, and she shoved them deep into her jacket pockets where Morrison and Valdez couldn't see them trembling. The name Jane hit her like a physical blow, dredging up memories she'd spent three years trying to bury beneath case files and sleepless nights.

Jane Winters had been her partner, her best friend, and eventually something more complicated than either of them had been ready to handle. They'd worked homicide together for four years, solving cases that other detectives couldn't crack, moving through crime scenes with an almost telepathic understanding of each other's thoughts. Sarah had loved her in a way that went beyond professional respect, beyond friendship, beyond anything she'd ever felt before or since. Then Jane had taken her service weapon to the roof of their precinct building on a Tuesday morning in March and painted the sky with her brains.

Morrison was saying something, but his voice sounded like it was coming from underwater. Sarah forced herself to focus on his words, to push Jane's ghost back into the dark corner of her mind where she belonged. The killer knew about Jane somehow, which meant he'd been watching Sarah, studying her, learning her vulnerabilities. The thought made her skin crawl with the sensation of invisible eyes tracking her every movement.

"Sarah, you with us?" Morrison's voice was sharp with concern. "I know this is personal now, but I need you to stay professional. Can you do that, or do I need to pull you off this case?"

The question snapped her back to the present. She couldn't let Morrison sideline her, not when she was the killer's obvious target. He'd left this message for her specifically, which meant she was either his next victim or his key to being caught. Either way, she needed to stay involved. She'd failed Jane by not seeing the signs of her depression, by not recognizing how close her partner had been to breaking. She wouldn't fail these murdered nurses by walking away when they needed her most.

"I'm fine," she lied, pulling her hands out of her pockets and hoping they'd stopped shaking. "Tell me about the scene. How was the body positioned?"

Valdez consulted his notebook, though Sarah suspected he had the details memorized by now. "Patricia Gomez was found kneeling in front of the cross, hands clasped like she was praying. Her uniform was intact except for some blood spatter, and her left ring finger was removed with the same precision we've seen in the other cases. Time of death was approximately eleven thirty PM, based on body temperature and rigor mortis."

Sarah walked closer to the altar, studying the way the light from the stained glass windows cast colored shadows across the floor. Even in this sacred space, she could smell the faint trace of vinegar that had become the killer's signature. The scent made her think of preservation, of keeping things that should have been allowed to rot and decay. She wondered what the killer was doing with the severed fingers, whether he was keeping them as trophies or if they served some deeper purpose in his twisted psychology.

The chapel's silence was oppressive, broken only by the distant sounds of hospital life filtering through the walls. Sarah had never been particularly religious, but standing in this space where someone had committed such a violation felt like witnessing blasphemy. The killer had chosen this location deliberately, knowing that murdering someone in a place of worship would add another layer of horror to his crime. He was playing psychological games, trying to get inside their heads, and Sarah had to admit it was working.

"Any witnesses?" she asked, though she already knew the answer. The killer was too careful, too methodical to leave witnesses behind.

Morrison shook his head. "Chapel's usually empty after ten PM. Security cameras show Patricia entering at eleven fifteen, but they don't cover the interior. No one saw her killer come or go, and the staff was too busy with a trauma case in the emergency room to notice anything unusual."

Sarah studied the bloodstained pew again, focusing on the handwriting rather than the message itself. The letters were carefully formed, each one deliberate and precise. This wasn't the frantic scrawling of someone in the grip of madness, but the methodical work of someone who wanted to make sure his message was clearly understood. The killer had taken time to write it, which meant he'd felt safe in this space, confident that he wouldn't be interrupted.

The implications were disturbing. Either the killer was familiar enough with the hospital's routines to know when the chapel would be empty, or he had some other reason to feel secure while committing his crimes. Sarah made a mental note to check whether all four victims had worked at different hospitals or if there were connections between the medical facilities. Someone with access to multiple hospitals would have to be medical personnel themselves, or perhaps a vendor or maintenance worker who traveled between sites.

"I want to see the body," Sarah said, turning away from the altar and its bloody message. "And I want a complete background check on all four victims. Not just their employment history, but their personal lives, their relationships, any connection they might have had outside of work."

Morrison nodded, but his expression remained troubled. "Sarah, about that message. The Jane reference. Is there anything you need to tell me? Any reason why this killer might know about your personal history?"

The question hung in the air between them like smoke from one of Morrison's cigars. Sarah knew she should tell him about Jane, about their relationship and its tragic end, but the words stuck in her throat like broken glass. Jane's suicide had been ruled a personal crisis unrelated to police work, and Sarah had never corrected that official conclusion. The truth was more complicated, involving a case they'd worked together, a suspect who'd gotten too close to both of them, and secrets that had ultimately destroyed the woman Sarah had loved.

She realized that both Morrison and Valdez were waiting for her answer, their faces reflecting the fluorescent hospital lighting in harsh angles and deep shadows. The killer's message had changed everything, transforming what had been a hunt for a serial murderer into something personal and infinitely more dangerous. Someone out there knew her history, her pain, her failures, and was using that knowledge as a weapon against her."Jane was my partner," Sarah said finally, the words scraping her throat raw. "She died three years ago. Suicide. I don't know how this bastard knows about her, but he's done his homework." The admission felt like pulling shrapnel from an old wound, each word tearing at carefully constructed scar tissue. Morrison's expression softened with understanding, while Valdez had the decency to look away, suddenly finding his crime scene notes fascinating enough to require intense scrutiny.

The weight of their scrutiny, even Morrison's sympathetic version of it, made Sarah's chest tighten with familiar anxiety. She'd spent three years perfecting the art of deflection, of steering conversations away from Jane's memory whenever well-meaning colleagues asked about her former partner. The official story was clean and simple: Detective Jane Winters had suffered from undiagnosed depression and taken her own life during a particularly difficult period. The truth was messier, involving a serial rapist named Marcus Webb who'd developed an unhealthy obsession with both detectives during their investigation of his crimes.

Webb had been brilliant in the way that only truly dangerous predators could be, a psychology professor who understood exactly how to manipulate and terrorize his victims. He'd targeted women in the medical field, following them home from their shifts, learning their routines, breaking into their apartments to leave small signs of his presence. A moved photograph here, a rearranged medicine cabinet there, subtle violations that made his victims question their own sanity before he escalated to physical assault. Sarah and Jane had spent months tracking him, diving deep into his psychological profile, trying to anticipate his next move while he played an increasingly personal game of cat and mouse with his pursuers.

The case had consumed them both, bleeding into their off-duty hours until they found themselves spending entire weekends together, comparing notes over Chinese takeout and too much wine. Somewhere in those late-night strategy sessions, their professional partnership had evolved into something more intimate, something that scared Sarah with its intensity even as she found herself falling deeper into Jane's orbit. Jane had been brilliant and funny and damaged in ways that made Sarah want to protect her from the world's cruelties, including the specific cruelty of hunting monsters who wore human faces.

But Webb had been watching them even as they hunted him, documenting their growing closeness, photographing them through restaurant windows and outside Sarah's apartment building. When they'd finally arrested him, his computer had contained hundreds of images of both detectives, along with detailed psychological profiles he'd constructed by observing their behavior. He'd known about their relationship before they'd fully acknowledged it themselves, and during his interrogation, he'd used that knowledge like a scalpel, cutting into their insecurities with surgical precision.

Sarah pushed the memories away, focusing instead on the immediate problem of a killer who seemed to have access to information that should have been buried with Jane. The connection couldn't be coincidental, which meant either their current suspect had some link to the Webb case, or he'd conducted extensive research into Sarah's background for reasons she didn't want to contemplate. The personal nature of his message suggested the latter, implying a level of obsession that transformed her from hunter to hunted in ways that made her service weapon feel inadequate against the weight of unseen eyes.

"We need to expand the investigation," she said, her voice steadier than she felt. "This isn't just about the nurses anymore. Whoever's doing this has been watching me, studying my history, which means he's probably been planning these murders for longer than we originally thought." The implications of that realization sent ice water through her veins, because it suggested a level of premeditation that went far beyond opportunistic killing. Someone had invested considerable time and energy into learning her vulnerabilities, which meant the murders themselves might be secondary to some larger purpose she couldn't yet comprehend.

Morrison was already reaching for his phone, probably to call in additional resources and request protective surveillance for Sarah. But she knew that conventional security measures would be useless against someone smart enough to research her personal history and disciplined enough to kill four people without leaving usable forensic evidence. The killer had demonstrated both patience and precision, qualities that suggested military or medical training, possibly both. He was playing a long game while they struggled to understand the rules, always staying several moves ahead of their investigation.

The morgue was located in the hospital's basement, reached through a maze of corridors that smelled of industrial disinfectant and something else Sarah couldn't identify, something organic and unpleasant that seemed to cling to the walls like invisible mold. "This one's consistent with your previous victims, but there are some interesting developments I think you need to see." The body looked smaller in death, diminished in a way that always struck Sarah as profoundly wrong, as if the absence of life had physically contracted the flesh and bone that remained. Patricia Gomez had been somebody's daughter, possibly somebody's mother, a healer who'd dedicated her career to easing suffering, and now she was reduced to evidence in a criminal investigation.

Dr. Lim pulled back the sheet with practiced efficiency, revealing Patricia's pale form marked by the precise violence that had ended her life. "Whoever did this used proper surgical techniques, possibly with medical instruments. There's no tearing or hesitation marks, no signs that the finger was removed through brute force.
