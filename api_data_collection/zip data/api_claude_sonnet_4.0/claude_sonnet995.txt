Marcus Whitmore had always been different, though no one at the University of Surrey could quite put their finger on how. Perhaps it was the way he stared too long during lectures, his pale green eyes tracking movements with predatory precision. Or maybe it was his habit of sitting alone in the cafeteria, methodically dissecting his meals while other students chatted and laughed around him. Whatever it was, Marcus existed on the periphery of campus life, neither fully accepted nor entirely rejected.

The transformation began on a particularly grey Tuesday in October. Marcus sat in his cramped dormitory room, surrounded by textbooks on human anatomy and psychology. His flatmate had moved out weeks ago, citing "weird vibes" and "unnatural behavior," leaving Marcus with the entire space to himself. The walls were covered with detailed diagrams of the human muscular system, nervous pathways, and digestive processes. To any casual observer, it might have seemed like dedicated academic study. The truth was far more sinister.

"You're losing weight again," observed Jamie Chen, Marcus's only real friend, as they walked across the campus quad. Jamie was everything Marcus wasn't – popular, outgoing, with an easy smile that made people gravitate toward him. Their friendship had begun in their first year over a shared love of obscure horror films and had somehow endured despite Marcus's increasingly odd behavior.

Marcus shrugged, adjusting his oversized jacket. "Haven't been hungry lately. Not for the usual things, anyway." There was something in his tone that made Jamie glance at him sideways, but he didn't press the issue. Marcus had always been prone to cryptic statements and dark humor.

The idea had been gestating for months, growing stronger with each passing day. It started innocuously enough – a fascination with true crime documentaries, particularly those involving cannibalism. Marcus found himself studying the cases of Jeffrey Dahmer, Albert Fish, and Issei Sagawa with academic intensity. But what began as morbid curiosity gradually evolved into something more personal, more urgent.

Dr. Helena Morrison noticed Marcus's peculiar research interests during their weekly tutorial sessions. As his psychology supervisor, she was accustomed to students exploring darker aspects of human behavior, but Marcus's focus seemed unhealthily specific.

"Your essay on anthropophagic behaviors throughout history was... comprehensive," she said carefully, adjusting her wire-rimmed glasses. "Perhaps too comprehensive for an undergraduate paper. The level of detail regarding preparation methods was quite unnecessary."

Marcus met her gaze without flinching. "I believe in thorough research, Dr. Morrison. Understanding the complete picture requires examining every aspect, regardless of how uncomfortable it might make others feel."

"Intellectual curiosity is admirable, Marcus, but obsession can be dangerous. I'm concerned about your recent work. The tone has become increasingly... personal."

"Personal how?" Marcus's voice remained steady, but his fingers drummed against the armrest of his chair.

"You write about these historical figures as if you understand their motivations intimately. There's an empathy there that's... unsettling."

Marcus smiled then, a thin expression that didn't reach his eyes. "Perhaps I simply have a better grasp of human nature than most people are comfortable acknowledging."

After leaving Dr. Morrison's office, Marcus walked aimlessly through the town center. Surrey's suburban landscape stretched endlessly in all directions – rows of identical houses, pristine gardens, people living their mundane, predictable lives. He felt disconnected from it all, as if watching the world through thick glass. The hunger was growing stronger, more specific in its demands.

That evening, Marcus found himself in the university library's basement archives, poring over historical accounts and medical journals. The fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting harsh shadows across the aged volumes. Most students avoided this section, preferring the digital resources upstairs, which suited Marcus perfectly. Here, surrounded by forgotten knowledge, he could research without interruption.

A particular passage in a 19th-century explorer's journal caught his attention. The author described witnessing ritualistic cannibalism among a remote tribe, detailing not just the act itself but the profound sense of connection and power the participants seemed to experience. Marcus read the passage multiple times, each reading intensifying the gnawing hunger that had taken residence in his chest.

His phone buzzed with a text from Jamie: "Haven't seen you in days. Everything alright? Want to grab dinner?"

Marcus stared at the message for several minutes before responding: "Dinner sounds perfect. I know exactly what I want."

The next morning brought news that would change everything. A jogger had discovered human remains in Stoke Park, just a few miles from campus. The local news reported that the body appeared to have been "partially consumed by animals," though the police were investigating other possibilities. Marcus watched the coverage from his dormitory room, feeling a strange mixture of excitement and disappointment. Someone else had acted on impulses he'd only fantasized about.

Jamie knocked on his door around noon, carrying takeaway containers from their favorite Indian restaurant. "Thought you might be hungry," he said, settling into the room's only chair. "You've been acting weird lately, even by your standards."

"Define weird," Marcus replied, accepting the container but making no move to open it.

"Weirder than usual weird. Disappearing for days, not answering texts, and when you do show up, you look like you haven't slept or eaten. Plus, there's this thing you do now where you stare at people like you're... I don't know, measuring them."

Marcus finally opened his container, stirring the curry without enthusiasm. "Maybe I'm just finally seeing people clearly. Do you ever think about what we really are, Jamie? Beneath all the social conditioning and moral posturing?"

"Jesus, Marcus. It's Tuesday afternoon. Can we maybe save the existential crisis for after we eat?"

But Marcus wasn't listening anymore. His attention had shifted to Jamie's hands as they gestured – the delicate bone structure, the way tendons moved beneath the skin.
