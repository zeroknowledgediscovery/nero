To her neighbors, she appeared to be just another middle-aged woman living quietly in suburbia. They had no idea that behind her unremarkable exterior lived someone who had taken seven lives over the past decade. Vera had perfected the art of invisibility, blending seamlessly into the fabric of small-town life while harboring secrets that would horrify those around her.

Today was different, though. Today, Vera had made a decision that surprised even herself. She was going to adopt a pet – specifically, a guppy. The idea had struck her three days ago while watching a nature documentary about tropical fish. Something about their delicate movements and vibrant colors had stirred an unfamiliar emotion within her. Perhaps it was loneliness, or maybe a twisted desire to nurture something instead of destroy it for once.

The pet store downtown was bustling with families and children excitedly pointing at various animals. Vera felt out of place among the cheerful chaos, but she pressed on toward the aquarium section. The employee, a young man with multiple piercings and bright blue hair, greeted her enthusiastically.

"Looking for anything specific today?" he asked, his genuine smile making Vera uncomfortable. She wasn't used to warmth directed her way.

"A guppy," she replied curtly. "Just one."

"Oh, that's unusual. Most people get at least a pair since they're social creatures. Are you sure you only want one?"

Vera's jaw tightened. She didn't appreciate being questioned about her choices, even regarding something as trivial as fish. "I said one."

The employee shrugged and helped her select a small male guppy with an iridescent blue tail. As Vera watched the tiny creature swimming alone in its plastic bag, she felt an unexpected kinship. Both of them were solitary beings, navigating their worlds without companions.

Back home, Vera carefully set up the small aquarium she'd purchased. She found herself oddly meticulous about the process, ensuring the water temperature was perfect and the decorative plants were arranged just so. When she finally released the guppy into its new home, she stood watching it for nearly an hour. She decided to name him Neptune.

The next few days brought an unwelcome parade of opinions from her neighbors. Mrs. Henderson from next door was the first to comment when she spotted the aquarium through Vera's window during what was supposedly a casual visit but was clearly reconnaissance.

"Oh my," Mrs. Henderson said, peering at Neptune swimming circles around his castle decoration. "Just the one fish? Seems a bit sad, doesn't it? Fish are meant to have company."

"He's fine," Vera replied, fighting the urge to imagine what Mrs. Henderson would look like at the bottom of a lake.

"Well, if you say so, dear. Though I can't help but think you might be happier with a nice dog or cat. Something with more... personality."

After Mrs. Henderson left, Vera found herself defending her choice to herself. What did these people know about companionship anyway? She'd lived alone by choice for years, finding solace in solitude that others seemed incapable of understanding. Neptune was perfect precisely because he asked for nothing more than food and clean water. No judgment, no expectations, no disappointment.

"I heard you got yourself a pet fish! That's so sweet. My cousin has this huge tank with like fifty fish. Maybe you could get some more? I bet your little guy gets lonely."

Vera signed her receipt with unnecessary force. "He's not lonely."

Even her mailman, Bob Crawley, weighed in during their brief interaction at her mailbox. "Funny thing about fish," he said conversationally. "They're smarter than people think. I read somewhere they recognize faces. Yours probably knows you pretty well by now."

"I'm sure he does," Vera replied, though she wondered if that was necessarily a good thing. If Neptune could truly see her, what did he make of the darkness behind her eyes?

Despite the unsolicited advice from seemingly everyone in town, Vera found herself growing attached to her aquatic companion. She established routines around his care, feeding him at precisely the same times each day and talking to him while she cleaned his tank. These one-sided conversations revealed more about herself than she'd shared with another living being in years.

"You know, Neptune," she said one evening while watching him glide through the water, "people think we're both incomplete. They can't fathom that some of us prefer our own company."

The guppy seemed to pause near the glass, his tiny black eyes focusing on her face. Vera chose to interpret this as understanding rather than mere curiosity about potential food.
