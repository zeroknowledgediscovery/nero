The cobblestones of Oldham gleamed wet under the dim streetlights as Jonty McAllister made his way through the narrow streets toward the old schoolhouse. His military boots echoed against the stone, a rhythmic percussion that had become second nature after three years of service. The weight of his rifle across his shoulder felt lighter tonight, perhaps because his mind was elsewhere, drifting toward the warm classroom where Miss Eleanor Hartwell would be waiting with her stack of well-worn books and that gentle smile that could make even the most hardened soldier forget about the war raging beyond their small industrial town.

Jonty had never imagined himself sitting in a classroom again after leaving school at fourteen to work in the cotton mills. But the army had different plans, and when they discovered that half their recruits couldn't read the simplest orders or write a letter home to their families, they established evening classes in towns across northern England. That's how a twenty-two-year-old soldier from the roughest part of Oldham found himself learning to read proper literature from a woman who seemed to have stepped out of one of those romantic novels his sister Mary was always hiding under her pillow.

Eleanor Hartwell was nothing like the stern-faced teachers from his childhood. Where they had wielded rulers like weapons and spoken in harsh, clipping tones, she moved through the classroom with an almost musical grace, her voice rising and falling as she read passages from Dickens or Wordsworth. Her dark hair was always pinned back neatly, though rebellious strands would escape throughout the evening to frame her face in a way that made Jonty lose track of whatever lesson she was teaching.

The other soldiers in the class – there were eight of them in total – had noticed his distraction weeks ago. They would nudge him with their elbows when Miss Hartwell asked a question and he was staring off into space, or they would tease him afterward about how he always found reasons to stay behind and help clean the blackboard or arrange the chairs. But Jonty didn't care about their ribbing. For the first time since he'd put on the uniform, he had something to look forward to besides the next meal or the next leave.

Tonight felt different, though. There had been rumors circulating through the barracks all week about new deployments, about teachers and nurses being called up for service closer to the front lines. Jonty tried to push these thoughts away as he climbed the stone steps to the school entrance, but they clung to him like the persistent drizzle that never seemed to stop in this part of England.

The classroom was already warm when he entered, heated by a small coal stove in the corner that Eleanor always arrived early to light. She looked up from her desk as he walked in, and that smile – the one that had been haunting his dreams – spread across her face.

"Good evening, Private McAllister. You're early tonight."

"Evening, Miss Hartwell." He removed his cap and ran a hand through his sandy hair, suddenly feeling awkward in his heavy boots and uniform. "Thought I might help you set up, if you don't mind."

She gestured toward the stack of books on her desk. "That would be lovely. Would you mind distributing these? We're starting 'Great Expectations' tonight."

As Jonty moved around the small classroom, placing a copy of the novel on each desk, he found himself stealing glances at Eleanor. She was wearing a deep blue dress tonight, one he hadn't seen before, and there was something different about her expression – a tension around her eyes that hadn't been there the previous week.

The other soldiers began filtering in as the church bells chimed seven o'clock. There was Tommy Briggs, a cheerful lad from Manchester who could barely write his own name but had a gift for memorizing poetry; old Herbert Shaw, who'd been a clerk before the war and read faster than anyone else in the class; the Morrison twins, James and John, who always sat in the back and whispered to each other; and three others whose attendance was less regular but whose eager faces showed how much these evening lessons meant to them.

Eleanor called the class to attention, but instead of opening her copy of Dickens, she remained standing behind her desk, her hands clasped in front of her. The room fell silent except for the soft hiss of the coal stove and the distant sound of rain against the windows.

"Gentlemen," she began, her voice carrying a slight tremor that made Jonty's chest tighten with apprehension. "Before we begin tonight's lesson, I have something important to tell you all."

The soldiers exchanged glances. Tommy Briggs shifted uncomfortably in his seat, while Herbert Shaw leaned forward with a frown creasing his weathered face.

Eleanor took a deep breath. "I've received notice that I'll be leaving Oldham at the end of this week. The Women's Army Auxiliary Corps has accepted my application, and I'm to report for duty in France."

The words hit Jonty like a physical blow. He felt the blood drain from his face as he stared at her, trying to process what she'd just said. Around him, he heard the other soldiers murmuring among themselves, expressing disappointment and asking questions about who would replace her, but their voices seemed to come from very far away.

"I know this comes as a surprise, and I want you all to know how much these past months have meant to me. Jonty watched as she moved from behind the desk, her usual graceful composure intact despite the gravity of her announcement. "Miss Hartwell," Tommy Briggs spoke up from his seat near the front, his Manchester accent thick with emotion, "begging your pardon, but why would you want to go to France? Eleanor smiled at Tommy with genuine warmth, though Jonty could see the sadness behind it. "That's very kind of you to worry, Private Briggs, but I feel I need to do my part where the need is greatest. The idea of continuing these classes without Eleanor seemed impossible to Jonty. Herbert Shaw cleared his throat and adjusted his wire-rimmed spectacles. "Miss Hartwell, if I may ask, when exactly will you be departing? "I leave on Friday morning," Eleanor replied, her fingers tightening slightly on the book in her hands. "I've also written individual recommendations for each of you, should you wish to continue your education after the war. The Morrison twins exchanged one of their wordless glances before James – or perhaps it was John, Jonty had never been entirely certain which was which – raised his hand tentatively. Eleanor's expression softened even further. "I would be delighted to correspond with any of you who wish it. In fact, I rather hope you'll write to me about your continued reading and studies. When she reached Jonty's desk, she lingered for just a moment longer than she had with the others. The remainder of the class passed in a strange, melancholy atmosphere. Eleanor gamely attempted to begin their study of Dickens' novel, reading aloud the famous opening passage about Pip's encounter with the convict in the graveyard, but it was clear that none of them could concentrate properly. The weight of her impending departure hung over everything, making even Dickens' masterful prose seem flat and distant. When nine o'clock finally arrived and she dismissed the class, the usual cheerful chatter was replaced by subdued farewells and promises to write. Jonty remained seated at his desk, watching her movements and trying to calm the nervous energy that had been building in his chest since her announcement. "Thank you for staying," Eleanor said as she finished gathering the books. She returned to her desk and sat down, gesturing for him to move to the chair beside it rather than remaining at the student desk. "I wanted to speak with you privately because your progress has been quite exceptional, and I feel there are opportunities you should know about." She opened a folder and withdrew several sheets of paper covered in her neat handwriting. "Your essays have shown not just technical improvement, but real insight and original thinking. The piece you wrote last week about your experiences in the mills particularly impressed me."

Jonty felt heat rise in his cheeks as he remembered the assignment she was referring to. She had asked them to write about a significant experience from before their military service, and while the other soldiers had chosen topics like their first jobs or memorable holidays, he had found himself writing about the morning he'd watched a young girl lose three fingers to one of the spinning machines. He had tried to capture not just the horror of the accident, but the way the other workers had simply stepped over the blood and continued with their tasks, how the foreman had been more concerned about the damaged machine than the injured child. He'd written about the sound the girl had made – not quite a scream, more like the cry of a wounded animal – and how it had haunted him for months afterward, appearing in his dreams and making him flinch every time he heard similar noises in the mill.

"I wasn't sure I should have written about that," he admitted, shifting uncomfortably in the chair. "Seemed perhaps too grim for a classroom assignment." The memory of putting those words on paper was still vivid – he had written and rewritten the essay three times, each version becoming more honest and raw than the last. He had almost torn it up entirely before finally handing it in, certain that Eleanor would find it too disturbing or inappropriate.

Eleanor shook her head firmly, her dark eyes meeting his with an intensity that made his breath catch. "On the contrary, it was exactly the kind of writing the world needs to hear. You didn't just tell me what happened – you made me feel the moral weight of it, the way that girl's suffering was diminished and ignored by a system that values productivity over human dignity. That's not just good writing, Private McAllister, it's important writing." She leaned forward slightly, and he caught a faint scent of lavender soap mixed with chalk dust and coal smoke. "You have a gift for seeing the human story within larger social issues, and more importantly, you have the courage to write about difficult truths that others might prefer to ignore."

The praise made him feel simultaneously proud and uncomfortable, as if he were wearing clothes that didn't quite fit properly. He had never thought of himself as having any particular gifts beyond a strong back and steady hands, certainly nothing as elevated as writing ability. "There's something else," Eleanor continued, her voice taking on a note of urgency that made him look up sharply. Jonty stared at the letter, the words seeming to blur and shift before his eyes. Publication. His writing, printed in an actual newspaper, read by strangers across northern England and beyond. The concept was so foreign to everything he had known about his life and his place in the world that he couldn't immediately process it. He thought about his mother, who cleaned houses for wealthy families and had never learned to read beyond the most basic level, about his father who had died in a mill accident when Jonty was twelve, about his sister Mary who was clever enough for university but would spend her life working in shops or factories because that was simply how the world worked for people like them.

"I don't understand," he said finally, his voice coming out rougher than he'd intended. "Why would anyone want to read what I have to say? I'm nobody special, just another soldier from the mills. Eleanor's expression grew fierce, and for a moment he glimpsed the steel beneath her gentle exterior. "That's exactly why people need to read what you have to say. Because you're not nobody – you're somebody whose story has been ignored and dismissed for far too long. You represent thousands of young men whose experiences matter, whose insights and observations are just as valid and important as those of university graduates and professional writers." She stood up and began pacing behind her desk, her hands gesturing emphatically as she spoke. "The world is changing, Private McAllister. This war is forcing us to confront questions about class and privilege and who gets to tell the stories that shape how we understand our society. Your voice – authentic, honest, uncompromising – is exactly what's needed."

Her passion was infectious, and Jonty found himself beginning to believe that maybe, just maybe, she was right. Maybe the stories he carried – not just his own, but those of every person he'd known in the mills, every family struggling to make ends meet in the narrow streets of industrial Oldham – maybe those stories did deserve to be heard.
