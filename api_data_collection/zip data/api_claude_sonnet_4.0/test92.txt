The morning sun cast long shadows across the glass towers of Neo-Geneva, where the Corporate Matriarchy had ruled for three decades. Marcus Thornfield pressed his face against the cold window of his dormitory room, watching the streams of businesswomen in their sharp suits flowing like rivers through the streets below. At seventeen, he had never seen so many powerful women in one place, and the sight both fascinated and terrified him.

"Another day in paradise," muttered his roommate Jake, rolling over in his narrow bed. The State Education Facility housed boys until their eighteenth birthday, when they would be assigned roles in the new world order. Some would become assistants, others would work in manufacturing, but few would ever hold real power.

Marcus had heard the stories from the older boys—whispered tales of how women had systematically taken over every major corporation, every government position, every seat of influence. They called it the Great Rebalancing, claiming it was compensation for centuries of male dominance. But Marcus's great aunt Beatrice had told him darker truths during her secret visits.

The breakfast bell chimed, and hundreds of boys shuffled toward the cafeteria. Marcus found himself thinking about Aunt Beatrice's last visit three weeks ago. She had pulled him aside, her weathered hands gripping his shoulders with surprising strength.

"They're not what they seem, Marcus," she had whispered, her eyes darting nervously toward the surveillance cameras. "The women running everything—there's something wrong with them. Something inhuman. I've been watching, researching, documenting everything for years."

At the time, Marcus had thought his great aunt was losing her mind. She was ninety-three years old, after all, and had lived through the transition period when men still held some power. Perhaps she was simply bitter about the changes, unable to adapt to the new reality.

But then came the dreams. Night after night, Marcus found himself haunted by visions of corporate boardrooms filled with women whose eyes glowed with an otherworldly light. In these dreams, he could see through their human facades to something reptilian beneath, something that viewed humanity as nothing more than livestock to be managed and controlled.

"Thornfield, Marcus," called the dormitory supervisor, Ms. Rodriguez. She stood at the entrance to the cafeteria, her tablet glowing as she checked off names. "Report to Administrator Chen's office after breakfast."

Marcus felt his stomach drop. Boys were only summoned to the Administrator's office for disciplinary issues or job assignments. Since he had maintained perfect behavior scores and wasn't scheduled for placement until next year, this could only mean trouble.

The cafeteria buzzed with the usual morning conversations—discussions about work assignments, complaints about the food, speculation about life after graduation. Marcus picked at his synthetic protein scramble, unable to focus on anything except the upcoming meeting. Jake tried to engage him in conversation about the upcoming recreational activities, but Marcus could barely hear him over the roar of anxiety in his head.

After breakfast, Marcus made his way through the sterile corridors toward the administrative wing. The walls were lined with portraits of successful corporate leaders—all women, all smiling with the same unsettling intensity that reminded him of his dreams. Their eyes seemed to follow him as he walked, and he found himself quickening his pace.

Administrator Chen's office occupied a corner of the building, with floor-to-ceiling windows that offered a panoramic view of the corporate district. When Marcus knocked, a crisp voice called for him to enter.

"Sit down, Marcus," Administrator Chen said without looking up from her computer screen. She was a woman in her fifties, with silver-streaked black hair pulled back in a severe bun. Like all the female administrators, she wore a perfectly tailored business suit that seemed to give her an aura of unquestionable authority.

Marcus settled into the chair across from her desk, trying to keep his hands from trembling. The office was decorated with awards and certificates, testament to Administrator Chen's success in managing the facility. Behind her desk hung a large photograph of her shaking hands with some of the most powerful women in the Corporate Matriarchy.

"Your academic scores are exemplary," Administrator Chen said, finally looking at him with eyes that seemed to pierce straight through to his thoughts. "Your psychological evaluations show remarkable stability and adaptability. You would make an excellent addition to any corporate team."

She paused, studying him with an intensity that made Marcus uncomfortable. "However, there have been some concerning reports about your recent behavior. You've been withdrawn, distracted during lessons, and your dormitory supervisor noticed you talking to yourself during sleep."

Marcus felt a chill run down his spine. They were monitoring even his dreams now. "I haven't been feeling well lately," he said, trying to keep his voice steady. "Perhaps it's stress about the upcoming transitions."

Administrator Chen's smile was cold and calculating. "Stress is natural, Marcus, but we need to ensure that all our young men are properly prepared for their roles in society. I'm scheduling you for enhanced psychological counseling with Dr. Vance. She specializes in helping boys overcome adjustment difficulties."

The mention of Dr. Vance sent another wave of fear through Marcus. He had heard whispers about her sessions—boys who went in for counseling often came out different, more compliant, with glazed expressions and no memory of what they had discussed. Some never returned to the dormitory at all.

"That's not necessary," Marcus said quickly. "I'm sure I can manage these issues on my own."

"I'm afraid it's not optional," Administrator Chen replied, her smile never wavering. "Dr. Vance has an opening this afternoon. Report to her office at fourteen hundred hours."

As Marcus left the Administrator's office, his mind raced with possibilities. None of them were good. If Dr. Vance was like the other women in authority positions, if she possessed the same inhuman qualities his great aunt had warned him about, then this afternoon's session could be his last as himself.

He needed to find a way to contact Aunt Beatrice, but communication with the outside world was strictly controlled. Boys were allowed to send messages to approved family members once per month, and all correspondence was monitored and censored. There had to be another way.

During the lunch period, Marcus slipped away from his group and made his way to the facility's library. The older building had been constructed before the current surveillance systems were installed, and he remembered Aunt Beatrice mentioning that there might be blind spots in the monitoring coverage.

The library was nearly empty, with only a few boys studying at scattered tables. Marcus made his way to the back section, where older physical books were still stored. These volumes had been deemed harmless by the censors—mostly technical manuals and historical texts that supported the official narrative of the Corporate Matriarchy's rise to power.

Hidden behind a stack of engineering textbooks, Marcus found what he was looking for—an old communication terminal that had been forgotten during the facility's last technology upgrade. It wasn't connected to the main network, but it still had access to the local city communication grid.

With trembling fingers, Marcus activated the terminal and began composing a message to his great aunt. He had memorized her private communication code years ago, though he had never expected to use it in an emergency situation.

"Aunt B," he typed quickly, glancing over his shoulder to ensure he was still alone. "In serious trouble. They want to send me to Dr. Vance today. I think you were right about everything. The dreams are getting stronger. I can see what they really are. Please help. - M"

He transmitted the message and quickly shut down the terminal, his heart pounding as he heard footsteps approaching. A younger boy rounded the corner, looking for a study table, and Marcus pretended to be browsing the technical manuals.

The afternoon dragged by with excruciating slowness. Marcus attended his scheduled classes—Corporate Ethics, Administrative Protocols, and Proper Workplace Behavior—but couldn't concentrate on any of the lessons. His instructors, all women with the same unsettling smiles and penetrating gazes, seemed to sense his distraction.

"Mr. Thornfield," called Ms. Patterson during Administrative Protocols. "Perhaps you could share with the class the proper procedure for handling confidential documents?"

Marcus stammered through an answer, drawing concerned looks from his classmates. He was normally one of the top students, but today his mind was elsewhere, desperately searching for a way out of his predicament.

As the clock approached fourteen hundred hours, Marcus found himself walking slowly toward the medical wing where Dr. Vance maintained her office. Each step felt like a march toward his execution, and he found himself wondering if he would emerge from this session as the same person who entered.

The medical wing had a different atmosphere than the rest of the facility. The lighting was softer, more clinical, and the air carried the sterile scent of disinfectants and something else—something organic and unsettling that Marcus couldn't identify. The walls were painted in calming pastels, but somehow the colors seemed artificial, as if they were designed to lull patients into a false sense of security.

Dr. Vance's office was located at the end of a long corridor lined with treatment rooms. As Marcus approached, he could hear muffled sounds from behind some of the doors—voices speaking in low, rhythmic tones, and occasionally what sounded like whimpering or crying.

He knocked on Dr. Vance's door, and immediately heard her voice inviting him to enter. The office was larger than he had expected, with comfortable seating arrangements and walls covered with certificates and degrees. Dr. Vance herself was a woman in her forties, with auburn hair and kind eyes that should have been reassuring but somehow made Marcus feel even more uneasy.

"Please, sit wherever you feel comfortable, Marcus," Dr. Vance said, gesturing toward several chairs and a couch. "I want you to think of this as a safe space where you can speak freely about anything that's troubling you."

Marcus chose a chair near the door, positioning himself so he could make a quick exit if necessary. "I'm not sure why I'm here," he said carefully. "I've been feeling fine lately."

Dr. Vance smiled and settled into her own chair, pulling out a tablet to take notes. "Administrator Chen mentioned that you've been having some sleep disturbances. Dreams can often be windows into our subconscious concerns. Would you mind telling me about what you've been experiencing?"

The question hit Marcus like a physical blow. If he admitted to the dreams, to seeing through the facades of the women in power, they would know that somehow he had developed the ability to perceive their true nature. But if he lied, Dr. Vance might have ways of extracting the truth.

"Just normal dreams," he said, trying to sound casual. "Nothing worth discussing."

Dr. Vance made a note on her tablet, and Marcus noticed that her fingernails were longer and sharper than they had appeared from a distance. "Marcus, I want you to know that many young men experience anxiety and confusion during their transition period. It's perfectly natural to have concerns about your place in our society."

She leaned forward slightly, and Marcus caught a glimpse of something behind her eyes—a flicker of the same otherworldly glow he had seen in his dreams. His breath caught in his throat, and he had to force himself not to react visibly.

"Sometimes," Dr. Vance continued, "our minds create elaborate fantasies to help us cope with feelings of powerlessness or inadequacy. These fantasies might involve imagining that authority figures are somehow different from what they appear to be, or that there are hidden conspiracies at work in our daily lives."

She was describing his dreams and his great aunt's warnings with uncanny accuracy. Either she was an exceptionally skilled psychologist, or she knew exactly what he had been experiencing because she was part of whatever conspiracy he had uncovered.

"I haven't had any fantasies like that," Marcus said, but his voice cracked slightly, betraying his nervousness.

Dr. Vance stood up and moved to a cabinet on the far side of the room. "I'd like to try a relaxation exercise with you, Marcus. Sometimes when we're carrying tension and anxiety, it helps to use guided meditation to access our deeper thoughts and concerns."

She returned with a small device that looked like a combination of headphones and some kind of scanning equipment. The sight of it made every instinct in Marcus's body scream warnings, and he found himself standing up abruptly.

"I don't think I need any kind of treatment," he said, backing toward the door. "I should probably return to my scheduled activities."

"Marcus," Dr. Vance said, and her voice had taken on a different quality—deeper, more commanding, with harmonics that seemed to vibrate in his bones. "Sit down. We're not finished yet."

He found his body responding to her command against his will, his legs beginning to move back toward the chair. The compulsion was strong, almost overwhelming, but something deep inside him resisted. Perhaps it was the same ability that allowed him to see through their disguises, but Marcus managed to break free from her mental influence and bolt toward the door.

"Security," Dr. Vance called into her communication device, her facade of kindness evaporating completely. "Patient attempting to leave without authorization. Initiate containment protocols."

Marcus burst through the door and ran down the corridor as alarms began blaring throughout the medical wing. Behind him, he could hear Dr. Vance shouting orders, and the sound of heavy footsteps as security personnel mobilized to intercept him.The facility's emergency lighting bathed the corridors in harsh red light as Marcus sprinted through the maze-like passages of the medical wing. His heart hammered against his ribcage, and the sound of his own ragged breathing seemed deafeningly loud in the sterile hallways. Behind him, the cacophony of pursuit grew closer—boots pounding against polished floors, voices barking commands, and the electronic chirping of communication devices coordinating his capture. He had no clear destination in mind, only the desperate need to escape the nightmarish reality that Dr. Vance had almost forced him to confront.

The main corridors would be crawling with security by now, so Marcus veered toward the service passages that maintenance workers used to move between floors. These narrow spaces were dimly lit and filled with the mechanical hums and clicks of the facility's infrastructure, but they offered his only chance of avoiding the systematic search that was undoubtedly already underway. As he squeezed through a particularly tight junction between air circulation ducts, he could hear voices echoing from the main hallway just meters away, discussing search patterns and containment strategies with the cold efficiency of a military operation.

His hands trembled as he navigated the cramped passages, and Marcus realized that everything his great aunt had told him was not only true but far worse than he had imagined. The women who controlled their society weren't just corrupt politicians or power-hungry executives—they were something else entirely, beings capable of mental manipulation and coordinated action that suggested a hive-mind intelligence. The implications were staggering and terrifying, because if they could control minds and maintain such perfect coordination, how many humans were even aware of their true situation?

After what felt like hours but was probably only minutes, Marcus emerged from the service passages into a storage area in the facility's basement. The room was filled with obsolete furniture and outdated equipment, creating a labyrinth of shadows and hiding places that might buy him precious time to think. His communication device had been confiscated during his flight from Dr. Vance's office, so he had no way of knowing whether his great aunt had received his earlier message or if she was even in a position to help him. The weight of his isolation pressed down on him like a physical force, and for a moment he considered simply surrendering and accepting whatever fate awaited him.

But then he remembered the glazed expressions on the faces of boys who had returned from extended sessions with Dr. Vance, and he knew that surrender would mean the death of everything that made him who he was. If these creatures could rewrite human consciousness as easily as editing a document, then resistance was not just about personal survival but about preserving some fragment of authentic humanity in a world that had been systematically conquered and controlled. The thought gave him strength, and he began searching through the stored equipment for anything that might help him escape the facility entirely.

Among the discarded items, Marcus found an old maintenance uniform that was roughly his size, along with a work tablet that still had some charge remaining in its battery. The uniform would provide basic camouflage if he could reach areas of the facility where maintenance workers were expected, and the tablet might give him access to building schematics or communication systems that weren't monitored as closely as the main networks. As he changed clothes in the darkness between two filing cabinets, Marcus tried to formulate a plan that went beyond simply avoiding capture for the next few hours.

The tablet's interface was outdated but functional, and Marcus was relieved to discover that it still had access to the facility's internal networks through a maintenance portal that required only basic authentication. Within minutes, he had pulled up detailed floor plans of the entire complex, including service tunnels, emergency exits, and areas that were typically off-limits to students. More importantly, he found references to external communication arrays that were used for facility operations but weren't part of the heavily monitored networks used for student correspondence.

His exploration of the network revealed disturbing information that confirmed his worst fears about the scope of the conspiracy he had uncovered. Internal documents referenced "biological compatibility assessments" and "neural integration protocols" being conducted on students who had been selected for "advanced placement opportunities." The clinical language couldn't disguise the horrifying reality—some of the boys who had disappeared from the facility hadn't been given job assignments at all, but had been subjected to medical procedures that sounded more like alien experimentation than career counseling.

Marcus felt sick as he read through reports describing the systematic evaluation of young men for various purposes that were described in coded language but clearly involved their exploitation as resources rather than their development as citizens. Some were being assessed for "cognitive integration," others for "biological harvesting," and still others for "reproductive optimization"—terms that painted a picture of humanity being managed like livestock by their supposed protectors and leaders. The documents made repeated references to "the Collective" and "integration timelines," suggesting that whatever had taken over human civilization was working toward some larger goal that required the systematic processing of the male population.

The sound of footsteps in the corridor above reminded Marcus that he couldn't remain hidden in the basement indefinitely, and he began planning his route to one of the external communication arrays. According to the building schematics, there was a maintenance access point on the roof that connected to the city's emergency communication network—a system that might not be monitored as closely as the standard channels. Getting there would require traversing several floors of the facility while avoiding the security teams that were undoubtedly still searching for him, but it represented his best chance of contacting his great aunt and potentially alerting others to the danger they all faced.

As he prepared to leave the storage room, Marcus reflected on how completely his understanding of the world had changed in the span of a few hours. This morning, he had been a student preparing for his assigned role in what he believed was a female-dominated but fundamentally human society. Now he understood that he was a prisoner in a processing facility run by creatures that viewed humans as resources to be managed and consumed, and that his entire generation had been raised in ignorance of their true situation. The weight of this knowledge was almost unbearable, but it also filled him with a sense of purpose that transcended his own survival—if he could escape and spread word of what was really happening, perhaps others could be saved from the fate that awaited them.

The maintenance uniform felt strange against his skin as Marcus crept through the facility's service areas, following routes that he hoped would keep him away from the main search patterns. Every shadow could conceal a security officer, every sound might herald his discovery and capture, but he forced himself to move with careful deliberation rather than panicked haste.
