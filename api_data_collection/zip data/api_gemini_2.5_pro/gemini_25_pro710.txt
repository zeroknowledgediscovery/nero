The *Busaw* was not just attacking Tala; it was trying to drown her in the ghosts of its previous meals.

Her free hand went to the *mutya* in her pocket, the river-stone cool and solid and real. She drew on its history, its centuries of being smoothed by the patient, unceasing flow of the Pasig River. She drew on her own history, the memory of her grandmother teaching her to read not just words, but the energies of a place, the stories held in a person’s eyes. She was not a tower of glass and steel; she was a mangrove, her roots sunk deep in the muddy, life-giving soil of her culture, flexible enough to bend with the tide, strong enough to hold the land together. The wave of failure washed over her and broke against the simple, unshakeable fact of her own purpose.

“Your hunger is a weakness,” Tala said, her voice a calm counterpoint to the psychic storm. “It has no memory. It only knows how to take. It doesn't know how to build.” She opened her worn leather satchel. The cloying perfume of the *Busaw*’s rot was instantly challenged by a different, older collection of scents: the sharp, clean smell of dried bay leaves, the deep, earthy aroma of vetiver root, the faint sweetness of crushed star anise. Your pathetic little rituals are nothing.” The voice was a discordant chorus now, Isabelle’s smooth alto layered with the dry, whispering hunger of the thing itself. The lights in the office flickered violently, and a hairline crack, thin as a spider's thread, appeared on the vast window overlooking the city.

Tala ignored the theatrics. She untied the abaca twine and unrolled the scroll. It was not covered in arcane symbols or ancient script. It was a simple genealogical chart, meticulously hand-drawn, tracing a family line back seven generations. Not a line of kings or conquerors, but of farmers. Each name was accompanied by a single, simple phrase: *Nagtanim ng palay*. Planted rice. *Nag-alaga ng kalabaw*. Tended the water buffalo. *Nagpatayo ng bahay*. Built a house. It was a litany of creation, a history not of triumphs but of acts of cultivation. It was a story of beginnings, over and over again. Tala held it up, not as a shield, but as a mirror. “This is a story you cannot eat,” she said. “It has no end. It has no victory. It is only the work. The planting. The tending. It is the story of everything you are not.”

The effect was instantaneous and violent. If the stolen despair was a flood, the scroll was a source of pure, fresh water, and to the *Busaw*, it was poison. The creature recoiled with a hiss that was pure static, a sound of profound revulsion. The energy of patient, cyclical creation was anathema to its nature of linear, rapacious consumption. It could not feed on it; it could not understand it. The story of a seed patiently tended until it became rice was a narrative with no profit, no climax of conquest it could devour. The air in the room, which had been cold and pressing, now felt charged and electric. The *Busaw*’s concentration shattered, and for a moment, the psychic ghosts of its victims were not weapons, but simply sad, lost echoes in the room.

"You bring the filth of the fields, the stink of the soil, into this place," the creature snarled, its form shimmering violently. The beautiful dress and sharp features blurred, revealing for a terrifying second the thing beneath: a shifting, hungry darkness, a hole in the shape of a person. "I will erase you. Tala did not flinch. She dropped the scroll and reached into her satchel one last time. Her fingers closed around the lumpy bundle of black cloth tied with red string—the remedy she had prepared for Mang Leo. She tore it open and threw its contents into the air. A shower of coarse sea salt, crushed guava leaves, and charcoal dust filled the space between them. To a human, it would be nothing. To the *Busaw*, it was a storm of indigestible truths. The salt was the memory of the ocean, patient and eternal. The charcoal was the memory of fire, of transformation and cleansing. And the guava leaves, in the language of healers, were for cleansing wounds, for closing what should not be open. The particles of this simple, earthly cure filled the sterile air, each grain a tiny anchor to the real world, a world the *Busaw* could not consume.

The creature shrieked, a sound of pure agony that cracked the great window from top to bottom. The salt and charcoal dust clung to its wavering form like embers, not burning it, but revealing its insubstantial nature. It was a parasite, and its sterile host environment had been contaminated by the messy, undeniable truth of the earth. The pride, the luck, the ambition it had stolen and hoarded within itself turned sour, poisoned by this infusion of simple, generative power. Its stolen energy, its very substance, was being neutralized. With a final, desperate burst of will, the dark form imploded, collapsing in on itself before exploding outward in a silent, psychic shockwave. Silence returned. A true, empty silence this time, not a predatory one. The oppressive cold was gone, leaving behind only the sterile chill of the air-conditioning. The great office, a moment before a battleground of spirits, was now just a large, empty room with a broken window. The immense desk of volcanic rock stood bare. She reached into her pocket, took out the single, perfect *tibig* seed, and placed it in the very center of the vast, black desk. A promise of a new beginning. A seed planted in a wasteland of ambition. Then, without a backward glance, she turned and walked out, leaving the door ajar, letting the sounds and smells of the real world begin their slow, patient work of reclamation.Her exit was as quiet as her entrance. As Tala walked back down the long, silent corridor, the receptionist at the marble desk looked up. To them, she had become invisible once more, her purpose in this sterile world fulfilled. She pushed through the revolving doors, and the artificially cooled air was instantly replaced by the thick, warm breath of the city.

The street felt different. Lighter. The psychic frequency of Makati, that high-tension wire of ambition and anxiety, had slackened. The monolithic pressure that had been bearing down on this whole district, originating from the 48th floor, was gone. In its place was a subtle confusion, a scattering of dissonant notes. It was the psychic equivalent of the power flickering out for a single, jarring second. The predator was no longer presiding over its kingdom.

She took the MRT back, the train car a rattling cocoon of tired bodies. As the metal snake slithered over the city, she closed her eyes and cast her senses out one more time, not to hunt, but to listen. She aimed her focus towards Quiapo, past the general hum, searching for a specific note. She found it: a faint, stubborn spark of indignation. Mang Leo, his voice raspy but firm, arguing with a supplier about the quality of his charcoal. It was a good sound. The fire in his gut had been rekindled, and its first expression was not joy, but the simple, powerful act of refusing to be cheated. His energy was a tentative thread, but it was his own. He was sitting on the floor of a room that smelled of new paint, a forgotten box of colorful plastic bricks open before him. He was not building anything yet, but he was remembering the joy of it, a feeling so alien to his corporate self that it felt like a memory from another man's life. The seeds she had planted were taking root.

To step off the train at the Carriedo station was to be submerged back into life. The sensory overload—the shouts of vendors, the saccharine smell of banana-cue, the roar of a hundred jeepneys, the crush of the crowd—was no longer an assault. It was a baptism. It washed the sterile cold of Makati from her skin, the memory of the silent, predatory office fading against the overwhelming, messy, glorious reality of the streets. This was her world, not of polished surfaces and invisible transactions, but of open palms, loud arguments, and shared humanity. The symphony was chaotic, but every note, even the sour ones, was authentic. It felt like coming home.

Her apartment was a single room above a bustling pawnshop, accessible by a narrow, steep staircase in a shared alley. The sounds of the street below were a constant presence, the music of her life. The room was not a home so much as a workshop, a sanctuary of purpose. Jars of dried herbs, labeled in a neat, spidery script, lined one wall. Bundles of roots and strange-smelling woods hung from the ceiling rafters. A low table held a collection of *mutya*, each stone resting in its own small, hand-carved wooden bowl. Books with cracked spines and faded covers were piled in every corner, their subjects ranging from herbal remedies to pre-colonial history to treatises on urban sociology. This was the armory and the infirmary for a janitor of the soul.

She was drained, the psychic effort having left a deep, cellular weariness in her bones. Before she could rest, she had to cleanse. She lit a tightly bound bundle of sage and sweetgrass, the thick, aromatic smoke coiling around her like a protective spirit. Walking slowly through the small room, she let the smoke permeate every corner, pushing out the last lingering residue of the *Busaw*'s cold. Later, sitting by her open window, she looked out over the tangled rooftops of Quiapo, the sky above bruised purple and orange by the setting sun. The city was settling into its evening rhythm. She knew the fight wasn't truly over. The *Busaw* was not destroyed, only broken. A single, great predator had been shattered into a thousand hungry whispers, scattered on the wind. They would find new hosts, weaker ones. They would not be able to consume a man’s entire life’s work in one sitting, but they would be a plague of minor curses. Her days took on a new rhythm. The focused hunt was replaced by a constant, low-level vigilance, a state of being she practiced while doing the most mundane things. She was doing just that, navigating the morning crush at the Quiapo market, the worn leather straps of her bayong digging into her shoulder. The air was a thick soup of smells: the bloody, metallic tang from the meat stalls, the sharp funk of bagoong fermenting in large clay jars, the sweet perfume of mangoes and the clean, green scent of freshly washed vegetables. She was here for bitter melon and ginger, but as always, she was also listening. She stopped at the stall of Aling Zeny, a woman whose face was as creased and detailed as a city map and who served as the market’s unofficial broadcast system.

It had found a home in a small, old resentment, a memory from five years ago when Elena’s *puto bumbong* had won a neighborhood fiesta prize. The condominium in Libis was one of a dozen identical towers, monuments of glass and concrete that promised a lifestyle of clean, orderly success. She had built her entire identity on a lie, and now that the lie was gone, she felt she had no identity left.

He let go of the chaos he was absorbing and focused on the simple, grounding feeling of the whisk hitting the bowl, the promise of nourishment, of a simple, tangible act. He projected not calm, but normalcy. The quiet, boring, beautiful feeling of a day starting over.

Rina watched, mesmerized, as Tala cooked the eggs. It was an act so mundane, so profoundly disconnected from the grand spiritual drama she had been living, that it broke through her paralysis. Marco, too, began to practice his own form of intervention, a far more delicate and remote craft. It was during one of his deep, city-wide scans that Marco found her.
