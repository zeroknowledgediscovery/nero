The floorboards of the old gymnasium complained under the singular pressure of the wheel. A low, drawn-out groan that was, to Lars, a kind of music. The house lights went down, plunging the entire theatre into an absolute, breathless black. Then, a single, sharp-edged circle of white light snapped on, pinning him and his bike to the stage floor. He walked the bike to the spot, his heart hammering against his ribs like a trapped bird. The applause for Elin faded into a tense, expectant silence. He took a breath, the cold air filling his lungs. He was not a boy in a gym. He was not a little circus act. He was a story, waiting to be told. The low, resonant hum of his music began, and he swung his leg over the frame.The music was a living thing in the darkness, a low, pulsing drone that seemed to emanate from the floor itself. His body, once tight with a desperate, ragged energy in the gym, now moved with a cold, deliberate precision. He executed the opening sequence, a series of slow, controlled wheelies and frame stalls, with a liquidity that surprised even himself. The too-perfect floor was no longer an enemy; it was a canvas. Following Sofia’s direction, he found the small, glowing red light of the active camera and let his gaze rest there, not with a forced smile, but with the quiet intensity of his focus. He was not performing for them; he was allowing them to witness his private ritual. The story unfolded, not of a lonely boy, but of a master in his element, a figure carving out order from the encroaching chaos. The stabs of synth in the music punctuated his movements, a tailwhip executed with surgical grace, a bar spin so fast it was a blur of chrome.

Then came the centerpiece. He pedaled backward, initiating the spin. The Pirouette of Despair. The world dissolved into a dizzying vortex of light and shadow. Instead of the risky jump, he transitioned out of the spin into the handlebar stall Sofia had choreographed. The bus ride back to the hotel was a world away from the silent, solitary journey he’d taken to Oslo. The shuttle was buzzing, a rolling celebration. He wasn't that boy anymore, the one who rode in circles in an empty gym, trying to cheat the gravity of his own life. The producers, wary of him injuring himself on the slick studio floors, had reluctantly agreed to let him practice there. It was perfect. The raw, unforgiving surfaces reminded him of the old gymnasium, a place where a fall was an honest, painful transaction with gravity, not a slip on a polished lie. He spent the first day not attempting tricks, but simply riding in huge, sweeping circles, letting the scale of the space recalibrate his senses. He needed to unlearn the jaunty banjo music, to wash the memory of its false cheer from his muscles.

He began by deconstructing his own vocabulary. He took every trick he knew and pushed it past its logical endpoint. A tailwhip became a double tailwhip, a move that required a violent, almost self-destructive burst of energy and left him sprawled on the concrete more often than not. A bar spin became a dizzying, continuous rotation until his arms screamed and the bike was wrenched from his grasp. He was searching for the breaking point, the ragged edge where control met chaos. He was collecting data in the form of bruises. His left shin was a tapestry of mottled purple, and the heel of his right hand was raw from a clumsy fall. This was the necessary cost of research and development. The idea, when it came, was born from a mistake. During a particularly fast spin, his feet slipped from the pedals. For a single, heart-stopping moment, he was airborne and completely separate from the bike, which continued its rotation beneath him. He crashed, of course, a tangled, ignominious heap of limbs and metal. But as he lay on the cold ground, the sharp pain in his hip radiating outwards, the image remained burned into his mind: that brief, terrifying moment of detachment. It was like seeing a ghost.

That was it. The Ghost. A trick where he would intentionally launch himself from the pedals mid-spin, becoming a satellite to his own machine, only to reconnect with it as it came back around. It was illogical, a violation of the fundamental union between rider and bike. To master it would not just be difficult; it would be an act of faith, a belief that the pedals would be exactly where they needed to be, at the exact fraction of a second his feet came back down. He spent the next two days in a private, self-inflicted hell. He wrapped his shins in spare rags and gaffer tape. The loading bay became a cacophony of his failures: the sickening scrape of the pedal crank against the concrete, the loud clatter of the frame hitting the ground, and his own sharp, involuntary gasps of pain. He wasn’t just trying to land a trick; he was trying to solve an equation of momentum, timing, and nerve, and every wrong answer was paid for in flesh.

Elin found him late one afternoon, sitting on a stack of wooden pallets, staring at a fresh, bloody scrape on his forearm. She didn’t say anything at first, just sat down beside him and handed him a bottle of water and a slightly squashed banana. "Having fun finding your joy?" she asked, her tone dry. He grunted, taking a long pull from the water bottle. "It’s a ghost," he said, more to himself than to her. "It doesn't want to be caught." Elin peeled the banana and broke it in half, offering him a piece. "No good ghosts do," she said, chewing thoughtfully. "Mine are being difficult, too." She gestured to her practice bag. Protruding from it were her clubs, but now, tied to the end of each one was a single, long, black ribbon. "They wanted streamers. They never specified the colour." He could picture it immediately: the dark ribbons turning her high-risk juggling act into a kind of sombre, beautiful vortex, a cascade of mourning instead of a party trick. It was a brilliant, subversive piece of artistic jujitsu. "That's good," he said, a genuine smile touching his lips for the first time that day. "That's really good."

His own progress was less elegant. Henrik and Sofia stopped by the next day to check on his 'Creation of the Impossible.' They found him limping, his t-shirt stained with sweat, the air thick with his frustration. He tried to explain the concept of The Ghost, but his words felt clumsy. He attempted it for them, a demonstration that ended, like all the others, with a chaotic fall. Sofia winced, her choreographer’s mind seeing only the danger, the lack of grace. "Lars, your ankle," she said, her voice tight with concern. "You can't go into the live show like this. You're going to break yourself." Henrik was quieter, his expression unreadable. He walked over to the bike, which lay on its side, and nudged it with his foot. "I sold the network on 'triumph', Lars," he said, his voice low and dangerous. "Not 'a kid repeatedly falling over in the dark'. I need to see something by tomorrow, or we go back to the banjo."

The ultimatum hung in the air long after they had gone. It was no longer just about landing the trick for himself. His autonomy, his voice, the fragile trust he had built with them—it was all on the line. He worked long after the studio had emptied, under the cold, humming service lights of the loading bay. He was exhausted, his body a single, unified ache. The fury and frustration had burned away, leaving behind a strange, empty calm. He was beyond the point of forcing it. He picked up his bike, his movements slow and deliberate. He would try one more time, and then he would go back to his hotel room and accept defeat. He pushed off, building speed in a wide, silent circle. He went into the spin, and as the bike came around, he jumped. He closed his eyes. For one, silent, perfect second, there was nothing. No bike, no ground, no gravity. Just air. And then his feet found the pedals. They weren't there because he’d aimed for them. They were there because he had known, on a level deeper than thought, where they would be. He landed, the bike absorbing the shock, rolling forward in a perfectly straight, silent line. He didn't wobble. He didn't fall. He came to a slow stop in the center of the vast, empty space. He didn't shout. He didn't even smile. He just stood there, straddling the frame, his chest rising and falling, his hands trembling on the grips. He could feel the echo of the landing in the soles of his feet, a low, resonant thrum. He had done it. The ghost was real. And he had just learned how to hold it.The next morning, Lars summoned Henrik and Sofia to the loading bay. The air was cold, and his breath plumed in front of him. He didn’t offer a preamble or an explanation. He simply nodded to them, picked up his bike, and began to ride. His movements were different from the day before, stripped of the ragged anger and frustration. He rode with a heavy, deliberate weariness, his body a testament to the failures that had brought him here. The routine he’d pieced together in his mind was a story in two acts: the brutal, repetitive fight, and the single, quiet moment of release. He built speed, the whir of his wheels echoing in the concrete cavern, and then launched into a series of intentionally flawed, aggressive moves. A spin that was almost too fast, a hop that landed with a jarring thud. He was showing them the cost, the ugly, necessary work.

Then, he set himself for the final act. He rode into the spin, and he jumped. For a fraction of a second, he hung suspended in the air, a separate entity from the machine rotating beneath him. It was a moment of terrible beauty, a man letting go of his only tool in a moment of supreme risk. Henrik took an involuntary step forward. Sofia’s hand flew to her mouth. Then, with a solid, definitive thud that echoed in the quiet bay, his feet found the pedals. He rolled out of it, not into a triumphant wheelie, but into a slow, straight, perfect line, coming to a dead stop twenty feet away. He stood there, his back to them, his shoulders rising and falling with his breath. He had not only caught the ghost; he had tamed it.

Sofia was the first to speak, her voice a hushed, reverent whisper. "My God." Henrik was silent for a long moment, his producer’s mind racing, processing not the trick but its impact, its narrative weight. He walked slowly towards Lars, his footsteps loud in the silence. He stopped beside him and looked not at Lars, but at the bike, as if it were a holy relic. "That's a million-dollar second, Lars," he said, his voice low and charged with excitement. "That single moment of silence when you're in the air. That's our finale. That's triumph." He turned to face Lars, a fierce, brilliant grin on his face. "The Creation of the Impossible. The banjo is officially dead."

The next few days were a flurry of focused, creative energy. The collaboration was no longer a negotiation; it was a conspiracy. Henrik scrapped the proposed music and commissioned a new piece, a tense, minimalist electronic score that was more of a soundscape. It started with a low, anxious pulse and built with a relentless, percussive beat that felt less like music and more like a countdown, layered with the recorded sounds of metal scraping on concrete—the actual audio of Lars’s failures. Sofia, fully converted, abandoned all thoughts of "performance." Her direction was now about subtraction. "Don't look at the camera this time," she instructed. "Look at the bike. Look at the floor. The stage would begin in near-total darkness, pierced only by fragmented shards of light that sliced across the stage at random, like flashes of bad memory. Only in the final moment, as he rolled out from The Ghost, would a single, warm, steady spotlight find him, illuminating not a performer, but a survivor.

On the eve of the live show, Lars found Elin on the rooftop, the black ribbons on her clubs fluttering in the wind like captive birds. The air was sharp with the promise of autumn. They stood in comfortable silence for a while, looking out at the sprawling city, two strange planets in their own orbits who had somehow found a similar trajectory. "So," she said, without taking her eyes off the horizon. "Ready to show them your ghost?" He nodded, the single word feeling insufficient. We found a way to tell our own story, even when they tried to write it for us. That's the real win, you know." He met her gaze, and in her clear, honest eyes, he saw a reflection of his own hard-won certainty. She was right. The votes, the judges, the headlines—they were all secondary. The real victory had already happened, in the quiet, bruised moments of creation.

Backstage on the night of the show, the atmosphere was a thick soup of hairspray and panic. Lars felt strangely insulated from it all, a bubble of calm in the centre of the storm. His mother had sent a text an hour earlier: "Anders is here with me! In the audience! Break a leg, honey!" The thought of his step-brother watching from the darkness, his face a mask of cool judgment, should have terrified him. Instead, he felt a remote, clinical curiosity. He wondered what Anders would see. A circus act? A marketing ploy? Or would he, for just a second, see the ghost? Elin performed just before him. Her act, with the swirling black ribbons, was mesmerizing. It had transformed from a trick into a spell, a dark and beautiful piece of theatre that ended with her catching the final club and holding it aloft in a gesture of solemn defiance. The audience was quieter than last week, more captivated, and their applause was deeper, more thoughtful. As she came off stage, she passed him in the wings, her face flushed. She didn't shout or hug him. She just met his eyes and gave him a slow, single nod. *Your turn.*

He walked the bike out into the darkness. The stage was black. The only sound was the low, anxious pulse of his music starting. He took a breath, and pushed off into the slicing, chaotic lights. He rode his failures. Every near-miss was a memory of a fall, every jarring landing a ghost of a past pain. He was not performing a routine; he was offering a testimony. The percussive beat intensified, a frantic, hammering countdown. He could feel the eyes on him, two million of them, and for the first time, he felt the gaze of his step-brother, a specific, targeted pressure in the vast anonymity of the audience. He pushed the thought away. This wasn't for Anders. This wasn't for his mother. This was for the boy in the gym. This was for him. He built his speed, spun, and leaped. The music cut out. The lights went dead. Absolute silence. Absolute black. He was airborne. He was weightless. He was free. He was a ghost. Then, the thud. The solid, world-affirming sound of his feet reconnecting with his machine. A single, warm spotlight bloomed, catching him as he rolled forward in a straight, unwavering line of pure control. He came to a stop, his body still, his breathing the only sound in the universe. He had survived.For a long moment, the world was nothing but the warmth of the spotlight on his skin and the echo of his own heartbeat in his ears. The silence that followed his landing was not empty. It was dense, thick with the held breath of thousands of people, a solid object in the air. He didn't move. He didn't offer a triumphant gesture. He simply stood, astride the bike, letting the final, clean vibration of the landing fade from the bones in his feet. He had caught the ghost, and now he was holding it still for everyone to see. The silence finally broke, not with a crack, but with a deep, swelling roar that seemed to rise from the very foundations of the theatre. It was a different sound from the week before, less a cheer of surprise and more a guttural acclamation of respect, a tidal wave of sound that washed over him not as an assault, but as an embrace.

The house lights came up, and the host appeared at his side, looking less like a slick television personality and more like a genuinely stunned spectator. He wisely kept his distance, holding the microphone as if not wanting to disturb the sanctity of the moment. The camera cut to the judges, who were not on their feet this time. They were sitting, leaning forward, their faces a study in rapt concentration. It was the folk singer who spoke first, his voice gravelly, his usual warmth replaced by a kind of solemn reverence. "There are songs about work," he said, his eyes fixed on Lars. "About building and breaking and the cost of making something new. You just performed one of those songs without a single note. "I was wrong. That was prose. That was brutal, honest, perfect prose. We didn't see the trick, Lars. We saw the work. We saw every fall. We saw the cost. And that is a much, much greater thing to witness." The mogul, Henrik's counterpart in the world of commerce, had his hands steepled in front of his face. He looked at Lars with the stunned respect of a man who recognizes a force of nature. "Forget what I said about star power," he said, his voice quiet but carrying across the hushed theatre. "That was integrity. On a stage that's all about illusion, you just gave us the most real thing I've seen in a decade."

Being led offstage was like walking out of a dream. The roaring applause softened into a distant thunder as the heavy stage door closed behind him. The backstage area, usually a hive of frantic activity, was oddly quiet. The crew members and other contestants who lined the corridor didn't rush him or slap his back. They simply nodded as he passed, their expressions a mixture of awe and something that felt like kinship. This was a language they all understood: the lonely, obsessive pursuit of a difficult thing. He saw Henrik and Sofia standing at the end of the hallway, by the entrance to the green room. Sofia had tears streaming down her face, but she was smiling, a wide, luminous grin. Henrik's face was unreadable, but as Lars approached, the producer simply reached out and gripped his shoulder, his hand firm and steady. "You kept your deal," he said, his voice low and hoarse. "You gave me better." It was the highest praise he could possibly offer.

He was making his way towards the green room, the adrenaline beginning to ebb and leaving a deep, bone-weary exhaustion in its place, when a voice cut through the respectful quiet. "Lars!" It was his mother. She was standing in the doorway of a sterile, brightly lit service corridor, a place visitors were definitely not supposed to be. She was fluttering with a nervous, vicarious excitement. And beside her, standing with his arms crossed, was Anders. He was wearing a dark, impeccably tailored suit, a stark contrast to Lars's sweat-soaked t-shirt and grime-streaked jeans. His blond hair was perfect. He looked as though he had been carved from marble and disapproval. "Oh, Lars, that was just… I mean, your father and I were just holding our breath! Weren't we, Anders?" his mother said, turning to her other son for confirmation.

Anders didn’t look at her. His gaze was fixed on Lars, a cool, analytical stare that tried to peel back the performance and find the flawed boy underneath. "It was impressive," Anders said, the word coming out clipped and precise, as if it were a business evaluation. "The dramatics were effective. Henrik is a very good producer. He knows how to sell a sad story." It was the same weapon, the same poison, but this time when it struck, it hit a surface it could not penetrate. Lars looked at his step-brother, at the expensive suit and the carefully constructed facade of success, and he felt a profound and final sense of release. He was no longer a clumsy, unevolved life-form to be pitied. He was an artist who had just bled for his work on national television. Anders was just a man in a suit who sold empty boxes of glass and wood.

Lars simply nodded, a slow acknowledgment of the words, but not their meaning. He didn't have the energy for anger, and he no longer had the need for justification. "I'm glad you got to see it," he said, his voice quiet and level, free of any bitterness. The simple, neutral statement seemed to unbalance Anders more than any argument could have. His step-brother had come expecting a fight, or at least a wounded retreat. He was prepared for a confrontation with the moody, inadequate boy he had always known. He was not prepared for this calm, exhausted man who looked at him not with anger or fear, but with a kind of benign indifference. Just then, Elin appeared at the end of the corridor, a bottle of water in each hand. She stopped when she saw the family tableau, her bright eyes taking in the scene with a quick, perceptive glance. She gave Lars a small, questioning look.

He turned his back on his mother's anxious fidgeting and Anders's cold, frustrated stare. He took a step, and then another, walking away from the sterile service corridor and back towards the warmth and noise of his new reality. He walked towards Elin, who waited for him with a small, knowing smile, ready to hand him a bottle of water. He was leaving the ghosts behind, letting them fade into the fluorescent hum of the empty hallway. He had a story to finish, but it wasn't theirs anymore.The distance from his family to Elin was only twenty paces, but it felt like crossing a national border. The fluorescent hum of the service corridor faded behind him, replaced by the muffled, thrumming energy of the backstage world. Elin didn't say a word as he approached, just held out the bottle of water. He took it, their fingers brushing, and the simple, human contact was an anchor. He saw in her eyes that she had understood everything: the brittle pride in his mother’s voice, the suit of armor his step-brother wore, the silent, final turning away. Her expression wasn't one of pity, but of quiet, fierce solidarity. She tipped her own water bottle towards him in a silent toast, a salute to a battle he hadn't realized he was fighting until it was over. He drank, the cool water a balm on his raw throat, and in that shared, unspoken moment, he felt the last frayed threads connecting him to the house on the hill finally snap.

The green room was a jarring cacophony of relief and anxiety. Those who had already been declared safe for the week were buzzing with nervous energy, while those still in jeopardy were clustered in tense, quiet knots. Lars found a corner, the exhaustion hitting him so hard his knees felt weak. He sank onto a soft chair, the water bottle cold against his thigh. He watched the drama unfold on the large monitor with a sense of profound detachment. When the host finally announced the name of the finalist going home—a young, technically brilliant tap dancer whose smile had always seemed a little too bright—a wave of genuine sadness washed over the room. The girl handled it with a heartbreaking professionalism, her smile finally faltering as she hugged the other contestants goodbye. Watching her, Lars felt a pang of something he hadn’t expected: a sliver of guilt. His success, his story, had taken up so much space in the show’s narrative tonight. He had not only caught his own ghost; he had inadvertently cast a shadow over someone else’s dream.

The shift in his status became uncomfortably clear later that night at the hotel. There was a soft knock on his door. He opened it to find Anton, the young opera singer, standing in the hallway. Anton’s stage presence was that of a seasoned virtuoso, but now, in the quiet corridor, he just looked like a nervous teenager, his hands fidgeting with the hem of his t-shirt. “You’ve officially ruined it for the rest of us, you know.” Elin’s voice drifted on the wind. They were on the rooftop again, their default sanctuary from the escalating madness. The lights of the city felt colder tonight, less a promise and more a vast, indifferent witness. “My producers had a meeting with me today about ‘deepening my emotional narrative’,” she continued, making air quotes with her fingers. “Apparently my ‘baking a cake’ routine wasn’t cutting it. Lars’s first and only instinct was to look at Elin. She was already looking back at him, a wry, knowing smile playing on her lips. It was the obvious, natural choice. They were the outliers, the weirdos, the ones who spoke the same language of bruises and obsession. A juggler and a BMX rider. It was absurd, which made it perfect. For a frantic minute, the space was a blur of hurried whispers and anxious, darting glances as the finalists scrambled to secure their alliances. Their initial brainstorming session took place in a large, empty dance studio, a space that felt too bright and clean for the messy work of creation. An optimistic energy filled the air. The producer’s threat echoed in the silent studio, stripping away Lars’s anger and leaving only a cold, stark clarity. He had been so fiercely protective of his solitude, he had ended up completely alone, on the verge of losing everything. Then he thought about Elin’s. He saw her in his mind's eye, not as a distraction, but as she truly was: a whirlwind of grace and calculation, keeping five objects in a perfect, flowing orbit. She wasn't just throwing them in the air and hoping for the best. She was letting them go, trusting that her hands would be there when they came down. Her art wasn't about control.
