The sweat on Flin’s brow was a currency of fear, not effort. It beaded and traced rivulets through the grime on his temples. Each rep he pushed, the hydraulic press sighing in protest, was a testament to a terror that had nothing to do with muscle failure. He was terrified of me. They all were, at first.

“Breathe, Flin,” I said, my voice deliberately soft. A counterpoint to the rhythmic groan of the machine. “Don't hold the air in your chest. Let it flow down, into your core. Find your center.”

He grunted, his face a purple mask of strain. “Easy for you to say, Trainer Kael.” The title was formal, deferential. The way all men spoke to all women, but with an extra coat of caution when they spoke to me. I was a licensed Physical Condoner, a shaper of male flesh, a profession that put me in a position of intimate authority that made most of them tremble.

“Nothing is easy,” I corrected, placing two fingers on his sternum. “It’s just simple. Your body knows what to do. Your mind is what gets in the way. You’re fighting yourself, not the weight.”

He stared up at me, his eyes wide in the sterile, white light of my studio. The high-rise gym was my sanctuary, a glass-and-chrome cage suspended a thousand feet above the sprawling metropolis of Coruscant Prime. Below, the city’s arteries pumped with the ceaseless traffic of grav-sleds, their lights a smear of neon red and electric blue against the permanent, engineered twilight. We lived in a world built by Matrons, ruled by Matrons, a world where the sun was an inconvenience that had been edited out of the sky.

His eyes flickered. For a moment, the fear was replaced by confusion, then a dawning glimmer of understanding. He took a stuttering breath, then another, deeper one. “You listened,” I said, offering him a water pouch. “For the first time, you actually listened.”

He took the pouch, his fingers brushing mine. He flinched back as if burned. The old conditioning. The ingrained subservience. Men did not initiate contact. Men received. They obeyed. They served. It was the foundational text of our society, the Catechism of the Matron. My mother’s catechism.

After Flin had showered, paid his credits, and shuffled out of my studio with a newfound straightness in his spine, I was left alone with the silence. It was a heavy, weighted thing, broken only by the distant hum of the city. I ran a sanitizing light over the equipment, the violet beam erasing the ghost of Flin’s sweat and fear. It was a simple, repetitive task. A meditation. But today, it offered no peace.

A private comm panel, dark and inert on my desk, chimed. Not the pleasant, melodic tone for a client booking, but a discordant, three-note signal I hadn’t heard in years. A signal that meant only one person was trying to reach me. Silas.

My hand hovered over the console, my heart a frantic drum against my ribs. I should ignore it. I should wipe the console, smash it, and pretend the chime had been a phantom of my overactive imagination. To answer it was to invite a past I had meticulously buried under years of discipline and denial. But Silas would not have broken his silence for anything trivial.

With a curse that was little more than a whisper of air, I tapped the screen. It flickered, resolving into the holographic image of a man’s face. He was old, far older than the Rejuvenation Edicts allowed. His face was a roadmap of hardship, his white hair thin, his eyes, though, were as sharp and clear as I remembered. They were kind eyes, but now they were shadowed with an urgency that chilled me to the bone.

“Kaelen,” he rasped, his voice a gravelly echo from a life I’d left behind. He was broadcasting from a place of deep shadow, the background a blur of what looked like corroded metal and dripping water. A sewer, maybe. Or the bilge of a forgotten freighter. “I’m sorry. I know my promise. But the time for promises is over.”

“Silas,” I breathed, my own voice tight. “You shouldn’t have called. It’s not safe.”

“Nowhere is safe anymore, little one,” he said, a sad smile touching his lips. “Your mother has seen to that. The Tether is… tightening. She is building something. A new focus for her power. The air in my studio suddenly felt thin, stolen. The Pulse. It was his name for it. The thing I made Flin feel. The energy, the current, the life field that I had always been able to sense, to touch. My mother felt it too. But she didn’t call it the Pulse. She called it the Tether. And she did not connect to it; she bound it, twisted it, and used it to dominate.

“Why are you telling me this?” I asked, my voice dangerously level. “What do you want me to do? Lead a protest? Write a strongly worded letter to the Supreme Matron?”

Silas’s image flickered. “Don’t,” he warned gently. “Don’t let the cynicism be your shield. You were never meant for a shield. You were meant for a sword.” He leaned closer, his holographic eyes boring into me. “Her design has a flaw. An access conduit, a thermal exhaust port, if you will, that leads directly to the primary reactor. It’s shielded, fortified. No weapon can get through. No pilot is good enough. The room began to spin. The glass walls of my studio seemed to melt away, showing me the dizzying drop to the city below. “No.”

Supreme Matron Elara. The architect of the modern galaxy, the woman who brought order to the chaos, the unifier. The tyrant. The monster.

My mind flashed back, an unwanted memory breaking through my walls. I was a child, no older than five, sitting on her lap in the gardens of the First Spire, before it became a fortress. The air was real, the sunlight was real. Her hair, the same black as mine, smelled of night-blooming jasmine. She wasn’t the Supreme Matron then, just… mother. The memory shattered. The woman who said that was gone, consumed by the very power she sought to control.

“She is not the woman who raised you,” Silas said, as if reading my thoughts. “The Tether has consumed her. What is left is just a vessel for its hunger. A dark heart pumping poison through the veins of the galaxy.”

“You’re an old man living in a sewer, telling me to commit matricide based on whispers and prophecies,” I snarled, lashing out. “This is insane.”

“Is it? Look around you, Kaelen. At the world she has made. A world of timid men and iron-fisted women. A world of absolute control, where love is a liability and obedience is the only virtue. The transmission cut out, leaving the comm panel dark and silent once more.

My steps were leaden as I walked to the corner of the room, to a simple meditation mat. I knelt, my knees protesting. And beneath it, for the first time, I could truly feel what Silas meant. A deeper, darker vibration. A thrum of wrongness. The Tether. It felt like cold, grasping fingers, reaching out from the core of the galaxy, from the place my mother sat on her throne. It felt like her.

A cold certainty settled in my gut. Silas wasn't lying.

With a resolve I didn’t know I possessed, I stood. I walked to the far wall, a blank expanse of brushed metal. I pressed my palm against a specific spot, a panel so perfectly fitted it was invisible. I took them in my hands. The familiar weight was comforting. I closed my eyes, took a deep breath, and did what Silas had taught me all those years ago. I didn’t force it. I didn’t command. The cynicism I had worn like armor crumbled. The fear was still there, a cold serpent in my belly. But now, it had a companion: a furious, burning purpose. I was a personal trainer. I found a worn satchel, packed the batons, a water re-sequencer, and all the credits I had. I took one last look at my pristine studio, my cage of glass and steel, and walked out the door, not looking back. To Rykar, a nod of grim trust. To Vorl, a look of fierce, shared pride. To Kaelen, a long, searching gaze that acknowledged the terrible weight of what she was about to do. She clasped Kaelen’s shoulder, her grip surprisingly strong. “Make it count,” she said. It was an order, a plea, and a benediction all in one.

Kaelen took the systems officer seat behind and to the right of him, a space filled with tactical displays and energy management consoles. Rykar squeezed his broad frame into a tiny, unlit compartment behind them, a grim gargoyle armed to the teeth, his only view a small repeater screen showing the pilot’s feed.
