The rain didn't so much fall as it congealed, a greasy slick of city-filtered water that clung to the high-rise windows of the Thorne Ascendancy Clinic. Inside, Dr. Aris Thorne felt a universe away from the meteorological misery of the streets below. He was a man insulated by success, by triple-paned glass, and by a profound, unshakeable belief in his own importance. He adjusted the knot of his silk tie, the material a whisper of expense against his starched collar, and admired the way the recessed lighting caught the silver in his temples. He was a healer. A sculptor of flesh and fortune. He fixed the broken, the unsightly, the merely inconvenient, and was paid handsomely for the privilege.

The door to his private consultation room wasn't locked. It never was. His clients were vetted, his staff were loyal, and the building's security was a technological fortress. The soft, electronic chime that announced a visitor was as much a part of the room's ambience as the faint scent of antiseptic and expensive cologne. So when the heavy oak door was shouldered inward with a crack of splintering jamb, the sound was an impossible intrusion, a vulgar shattering of his perfect world.

The creature that stood there was an affront to everything Aris Thorne believed in. It was a troll, but not the kind from storybooks, not a lumbering, dim-witted giant. This one was compact, maybe a little over six feet, a creature of granite and gristle packed into a surprisingly humanoid frame. Its skin was the colour of wet cement, pocked and uneven. Two yellowed tusks, chipped and scarred, jutted from its lower jaw, forcing its lips into a permanent, snarling grin. It wore a patchwork of scavenged leather and filthy canvas, and in its thick, three-fingered hands, it cradled a grotesque contraption of copper piping, a pressurized canister, and a wide, bell-shaped nozzle.

"Doctor Thorne," the troll rasped, her voice a low rumble, like stones grinding together. It was a female, then. The realization did nothing to calm the frantic, hummingbird beat of his heart. "The man who makes everyone pretty."

Aris fumbled behind him, his hand scrabbling for the panic button beneath his mahogany desk. He found it, his thumb pressing down with sweaty desperation. Nothing happened. A small, cruel smile widened the troll's mouth, revealing rows of blunt, powerful teeth.

"Oh, I cut the wires, love. Can't have the big men in blue interrupting our little chat, can we?" She took a step into the room, her heavy boots scuffing the pristine Italian marble. The smell that rolled off her was of damp earth, stagnant water, and something else, something acrid and deeply unpleasant.

"What do you want?" Aris managed, his voice a reedy thing he didn't recognize. "Money? I have money."

The troll laughed, a gravelly, wheezing cackle that seemed to shake the very air. "Money? You think this is about money?" She hefted the strange weapon, her knuckles white. "This is about justice. You think you can just… fix things? Play God with your little scalpels and your chemical peels? You make people forget what's real. You carve them up and tell them they're better for it." Her eyes, small and dark and burning with a ferocious intelligence, narrowed. "I'm here to give you a dose of reality, Doctor. Something that can't be tucked, or lifted, or lasered away."

Before Aris could scream, she raised the splurge-gun. There was a wet cough, a hiss of compressed air, and a thick, viscous stream of chartreuse goop erupted from the nozzle. It hit him square in the chest, the impact surprisingly forceful, knocking him back into his expensive leather chair. The substance was warm and impossibly sticky, and the smell was overwhelming—a nauseating blend of spoiled milk, ozone, and something metallic, like old blood. It clung to him, a second skin of pure vileness, already beginning to soak through his shirt, an irritating itch spreading across his skin. He gagged, his gorge rising.

The troll, Jenifer, watched him, her head cocked. She didn't move to strike again. She just watched him squirm, watched the horror and revulsion dawn on his face. And then she laughed again, that same horrifying, grinding sound. It wasn't the laugh of a simple brute; it was the knowing, satisfied laugh of an artist admiring her work. With one last, contemptuous look, she turned and lumbered out of the ruined office, the sound of her heavy boots receding down the hallway, leaving Dr. Aris Thorne alone with his new, permanent disfigurement and the echoing sound of her monstrous mirth.

By the time the first responders arrived, the clinic was in chaos. The Bile-Slurry, as the media had dubbed the substance, was notoriously difficult to remove and its effects, while not life-threatening, were devastating. It caused a severe, pustulating rash and a semi-permanent discolouration of the skin, a sickly greenish-yellow stain that no amount of scrubbing could fully erase. The psychological toll was even worse. Dr. Thorne was hysterical, a gibbering wreck who could only point at his own chest and weep.

The initial wave of city police did their best, taping off the area, taking preliminary statements from the terrified staff. They were out of their depth, and they knew it. This was the fifth such attack in as many months, all targeting high-profile medical professionals. The perpetrator was smart, elusive, and, according to the one blurry security image they had, non-human. This was a matter for the Department of Para-Natural Affairs, and more specifically, for the woman who now stepped out of a nondescript black sedan that had sliced silently through the throng of emergency vehicles.

Lady McCarry did not get wet. The congealed rain seemed to shy away from the severe lines of her Tuckahoe-grey trench coat. She was a tall woman, lean and corded with the kind of muscle that came not from a gym, but from relentless, punishing training. Her face was a collection of sharp angles, framed by dark hair pulled back into a ruthlessly tight bun. Her eyes, the colour of a winter sky, took in the entire chaotic scene in a single, sweeping glance, missing nothing, cataloguing everything. The title, 'Lady', was not one of nobility, but a regimental honorific from the Tuckahoe Garrison, a place that forged soldiers with the unyielding discipline of tempered steel. McCarry was its finest example.

She ignored the babble of a junior officer trying to brief her and walked straight into the building, her own boots making crisp, authoritative sounds on the wet pavement. Inside, the air was thick with the Bile-Slinger's signature stench. While other officers grimaced and covered their mouths, McCarry seemed not to notice, her expression a mask of detached professionalism. She entered Dr. Thorne's ruined office, her gaze sweeping over the splintered doorframe, the goop-splattered chair, the weeping man being tended to by paramedics.

"Report," she said, her voice calm and low, yet it cut through the noise like a razor.

A harried detective, a man named Henderson with tired eyes and a stained tie, turned to her. "Lady McCarry. It's the same as the others. Almost identical M.O. Gains access, bypasses localized security, incapacitates the target with the slurry, and vanishes. No witnesses got a clear look at her face, just a big, grey... thing."

McCarry knelt, careful not to let her coat touch the contaminated floor. She studied the point of impact on the chair, the spray pattern on the wall behind it. "The delivery system is consistent," she mused, more to herself than to Henderson. "Pressurized, single stream. Not a splash effect. It's aimed. Precise." She ran a gloved finger along the marble floor, well away from the main spill. She felt a faint, gritty residue. "She's leaving more than just slime behind."

She stood and walked over to the shattered doorframe. The wood was split clean through. "This isn't a lockpick job. Brute force. But look at the angle." She pointed to the deepest part of the impact. "Low. She hit it with her shoulder, not a ram. She's strong, but she's not a giant. The initial reports are exaggerating her size."

Her gaze drifted to the window, to the city lights bleeding through the rain-streaked glass. Five doctors. A plastic surgeon, a renowned cardiologist, a neurologist, a dermatologist, and now Thorne, a cosmetic specialist. The pattern wasn't just 'doctors'. It was doctors who were famous, who were lauded as miracle workers. The ones who appeared on magazine covers and morning talk shows. The ones who represented the pinnacle of medical celebrity.

"The jealousy is the key," she murmured.

"Ma'am?" Henderson asked, leaning closer.

"This isn't random," McCarry stated, turning her piercing gaze on him. "This is personal. Our troll doesn't just hate doctors. She hates what they represent. Success. Adulation. The power to 'fix' people." She looked back at the sobbing Dr. Thorne. "She's not just disfiguring them. She's mocking them. Turning their own ideals of perfection into a grotesque joke."

A young forensics technician approached them hesitantly, holding a sample bag with a pair of tweezers. "Ma'am, we found this embedded in the doorframe. It must have broken off on impact."

Inside the bag was a small, curved shard of a yellowish-white material. It was hard, like bone or ivory. McCarry took the bag, holding it up to the light. It was a piece of a tusk. Jagged, broken. A flaw in the monster's formidable exterior.

A slow, cold smile touched Lady McCarry's lips. It was a predator's smile, devoid of warmth but full of purpose. "She's getting arrogant," she said. "Getting sloppy. She left a piece of herself behind." She handed the bag back to the technician. "Run a full analysis. I want to know everything. Mineral composition, protein markers, geographic trace elements. I want to know where she eats, where she sleeps, where she sharpens her teeth."

McCarry turned and walked towards the door, her coat swirling around her like a cape. The hunt had been a frustrating exercise in reacting to the troll's whims. Now, for the first time, she had a scent. A piece of the quarry. The game had changed. Jenifer the troll thought she was the one delivering lessons, punishing the proud and the vain. But Lady McCarry of Tuckahoe was about to teach her a lesson of her own: that for every monster who laughs in the darkness, there is another monster, equally patient and far more disciplined, hunting them in the light. And the justice they face will not be a laughing matter. The city's damp, subterranean veins, the forgotten aqueducts and storm drains, suddenly seemed like the most logical place in the world to McCarry. It was where the unwanted things went. It was where a creature fuelled by resentment and bile would feel most at home. And it was where she would go to drag her into the unforgiving light of the law.Detective Henderson watched her go, feeling a familiar mix of awe and unease. Lady McCarry moved through the world as if she were its sole occupant, the rest of them merely temporary fixtures in her landscape. He’d worked with the DPA enough to know that their methods were as unconventional as their quarry. They operated in the grey spaces the law was not equipped to handle, and McCarry was the greyest of them all. He turned his attention back to the snivelling wreck of a man who, an hour ago, had been a king in his own private kingdom. The troll’s justice was cruel, but looking at Thorne’s blubbering, Henderson felt a disturbing lack of sympathy.

The headquarters of the Department of Para-Natural Affairs was a brutalist concrete monolith squatting in a forgotten industrial district, a building that made no apologies for its ugliness. It was a fortress of function over form. Inside, the corridors were stark, lit by humming fluorescent strips, and smelled faintly of ozone and oiled steel. McCarry strode through them, her trench coat shedding a fine mist that evaporated before it could touch the polished concrete floor. She didn't go to her office but straight to the subterranean levels, to the forensics and materials analysis labs. There, under lighting bright enough to feel sterile, a gnome in a lab coat, his spectacles perched precariously on the end of his nose, was already running the tusk fragment through a mass spectrometer.

"Anything, Alistair?" McCarry asked, her voice echoing slightly in the sterile chamber.

The gnome, whose name was Dr. Alistair Finch, squinted at a glowing monitor. "Patience, Lady, patience. It's not magic, it's science. And this is a fascinating bit of biology. Standard calcium phosphate matrix, yes, but with unusual trace mineral inclusions. High concentrations of iron, copper… and Polychlorinated Biphenyls. PCBs. Nasty stuff. Banned for decades, but it lingers. It doesn't just pass through a system; it bioaccumulates. This troll isn't just drinking contaminated water; she's been marinating in it for years." He tapped a few keys, and a map of the city appeared on a large screen, a complex web of blue and green lines representing the water and sewer systems. A section on the old east side, near the defunct Blackwood Tannery, began to flash red. "The concentration is highest here. The Tannery runoff pipe was supposed to have been sealed in '98, but the city cut corners. It's been leaking a toxic cocktail into the Acheron tributary conduit ever since."

Miles away, in the damp, echoing dark beneath the city, Jenifer the troll tended to the splintered stump of her tusk. The nerve was exposed, sending a dull, throbbing ache through her jaw, a constant reminder of her brief moment of carelessness. Her home was a nexus of several large storm drains, a cavernous space she had furnished with the detritus of the world above. A cracked but comfortable-looking leather sofa, likely discarded from a lobby, sat on a platform of wooden pallets. Shelves made of scavenged planks were lined not with books, but with jam jars filled with bolts, wires, and odd bits of machinery. Taped to the curved concrete wall was her gallery: glossy magazine pages and newspaper clippings featuring the smiling, confident faces of her victims. Dr. Thorne was the newest addition. Beside them was an older, faded photograph of a young troll girl, her tusks small and clean, her smile wide and genuine. It was a picture of herself, from a time before the Sickness. Before the growths had started, before local doctors had dismissed her as a lost cause, a para-natural anomaly not worth their precious time. They had looked at her with disgust, not compassion. They saw a monster, not a person in need of help. So, she had become the monster they saw.

The pain in her jaw was a familiar fire, a companion to the rage that always simmered just beneath her skin. She picked up a small, hand-cranked grinder, her calloused fingers surprisingly deft as she began to carefully file down the jagged edges of the broken tusk. The sound of grinding bone was loud in the subterranean silence, a counterpoint to the steady drip-drip-drip of water seeping through a crack in the ceiling. She had been sloppy. Arrogant. The doctor’s perfect, terrified face had been too satisfying, and she had put too much of herself into the blow that broke his door, and a piece of her own body. A new pain was a small price to pay for the art she had created on his pristine suit, but it was a weakness nonetheless. She would have to be more careful. Her work was not yet finished. There were still so many pretty people in the world who needed to be taught what it meant to be ugly.

Back in the DPA labs, McCarry stared at the flashing red sector on the map. The Acheron conduit. A maze of forgotten tunnels and toxic sludge, a place no sane person would ever venture. It was perfect. She turned from the screen, her course of action set. "Thank you, Alistair. Keep this line open. I want real-time environmental monitoring when I'm down there. Air quality, motion sensors, thermal." She was already walking away, her mind shifting from analysis to action. She wasn't taking a tactical team. A full squad would be too loud, too clumsy for the tight confines of the conduits. This was a hunt, and a hunter worked best either alone or with a single, trusted spotter. She knew exactly who to call. Reaching her spartan office, she bypassed the desk and went straight to a tall, steel locker. Inside was not a change of clothes, but an array of sleek, matte-black equipment. Body armour, a rebreather, and a long, slender rifle that looked more like a piece of high-tech surveying equipment than a weapon. It was a custom-built pneumatic tranquillizer rifle, loaded with a paralytic agent potent enough to drop a rhino. McCarry was not an executioner; she was an officer of the law, and Jenifer the troll would be brought in alive to face a justice far more sterile and confining than a gout of bile.The phone on her desk was a concession to the modern world she barely tolerated. She picked up the receiver and punched in a three-digit extension, a direct line that bypassed all switchboards. It was answered on the first ring, not with a greeting, but with the sharp crackle of a soldering iron being set in its stand and a grumbled, "What's broke now?"

"Glitch," McCarry said, her voice flat. "I need a spotter. Acheron tributary conduit, entry point under the Blackwood bridge. Target is a Class Three troll, aggressor classification. Bile-Slinger."

There was a pause, then a wheezing laugh from the other end. "The artist! Been reading about her. Puts the 'puke' in 'pustule'. Nasty work. I'm in. Give me twenty to grab my good boots. The ones that are only seventy percent hole." The line went dead. McCarry hung up the receiver, her expression unchanged, and holstered the tranquilizer rifle across her back. Twenty minutes was all the time she needed.

The Blackwood bridge was a forgotten relic of iron and rust, spanning a grimy, weed-choked canal. Beneath it, hidden by shadows and a decade's worth of graffiti, was a circular maintenance hatch. When McCarry arrived, it was already slightly ajar. A wisp of foul-smelling air escaped, a greeting from the world below. A small, wiry figure emerged, pulling a set of oversized night-vision goggles up onto his forehead. This was Glitch. A goblin of indeterminate age, with skin the colour of old parchment and large, intelligent eyes that seemed to take in everything at once. He was covered in a fine sheen of oil, and his coveralls had more pockets than fabric.

"Evening, Lady," he chirped, wiping a greasy hand on an even greasier rag. "Smells like she's been brewing up a fresh batch." He peered past McCarry at the distant, glittering lights of the city skyline. "Fancy part of the under-city. High ceilings, vintage toxins. The real estate down here is to die for." He grinned, revealing a row of needle-sharp teeth, and gestured to the open hatch. "After you. Rank has its privileges, and breathing the fresher sewer gas is one of 'em."

The descent was into a world of absolute blackness and oppressive damp. The air was a thick cocktail of sewage, decay, and the Bile-Slinger's unique, metallic tang. Glitch, moving with a quiet, scuttling grace, took the lead, his multi-spectrum goggles cutting through the gloom. McCarry followed, her boots making measured, silent steps on the slimy catwalk. The only sounds were the distant rush of water and the quiet voice of Alistair in her earpiece, a stream of sterile data in a world of filth. "Airborne PCB concentration is now one hundred and fifty parts per million, Lady. I recommend you check the seal on your rebreather. Methane levels are stable but present." McCarry's hand went to the small device covering her mouth and nose, an automatic, disciplined gesture. She felt no revulsion, no discomfort. This was merely a different kind of battlefield, and she was as at home here as she was in her spartan office.

Jenifer froze, the hand-cranked grinder falling silent in her grasp. The deep hum of the conduit was her constant companion, a sound she knew as intimately as her own breathing. And it had changed. It was a subtle thing, a shift in the echo, a new, faint vibration carried through the concrete and water. It was the sound of foreign bodies, of intruders. For months, she had been the ghost, the one who moved unseen through the world above. Now, something was moving through hers. She set the grinder down without a sound and picked up a heavy, iron pipe, her knuckles white around its rusted circumference. Her lips pulled back from her tusks, the ache in her jaw forgotten, replaced by the hot, familiar thrill of imminent conflict. They had come for the monster. She would not disappoint them.

"We've got infrastructure," Glitch whispered, his voice a crackle in McCarry's ear. He pointed his long, thin fingers towards a section of the tunnel wall where scavenged electrical cables were neatly fastened to the concrete, running deeper into the darkness. "She's got power. Not just a squatter. She's building something." He scurried ahead, his small frame allowing him to peer into a side-passage. "Found her trash heap. Chemical drums, empty canisters, medical-grade tubing. Looks like she's been raiding hospital dumpsters."

McCarry knelt, running a gloved finger over a fresh set of tracks in the sludge. They were large, three-toed, and deep. "She's not running. She's fortifying." As they pressed on, the stench of the Bile-Slurry became almost overpowering. They found the first of her defenses a hundred yards later. It was a simple tripwire, strung low across the walkway, connected to a net filled with shattered glass and rusty cans suspended from the ceiling. Glitch spotted it instantly, his goggled eyes picking up the glint of the wire.

"Crude," he sniffed, carefully stepping over it.

"A doorbell," McCarry corrected him, her voice low. "She knows we're here now." She raised her hand, signalling a halt. The tunnel ahead opened into a much larger chamber, a junction of several massive storm drains. A faint, unsteady light flickered from within, casting long, dancing shadows. "Alistair, thermal," she commanded under her breath.

"One signature, Lady," came the immediate reply. "Large, endothermic. Troll physiology. Stationary, center of the chamber. Vitals are elevated. She's waiting for you."

McCarry unslung the pneumatic rifle, the motion fluid and economical. She motioned for Glitch to stay put, to cover their rear. He nodded, pulling a wicked-looking pistol that spat electrified darts from his voluminous coveralls and melting back into the shadows. McCarry crept forward to the edge of the opening, pressing her back against the cold, curved wall. She risked a glance around the corner. The chamber was vast, and in its center, illuminated by a single, bare bulb powered by a car battery, sat Jenifer the troll. She wasn't hiding. She was sitting on her scavenged sofa, the iron pipe resting across her lap, staring directly at the entrance where McCarry was concealed. She was waiting, a statue of granite and gristle, a queen upon her squalid throne. And on her face was that same, terrible, knowing smile.The silence in the vast, damp chamber stretched, thick and heavy with unspoken threats. The single bulb sizzled, casting a jaundiced light that made Jenifer’s wet-cement skin gleam. It caught the dull metal of the pipe in her lap and the ferocious intelligence burning in her small, dark eyes. McCarry remained perfectly still at the edge of the tunnel, the rifle a seamless extension of her body. She was a statue of grey efficiency, analyzing trajectories, windage from the water flow, the subtle rise and fall of the troll’s barrel chest. She was calculating the precise dose and delivery needed to fell the creature without killing it, a problem of physics and biology. But Jenifer was not just a problem to be solved; she was the architect of this entire, sordid stage.

"Another one," the troll’s voice rumbled, not a shout, but a conversational growl that carried easily across the echoing space. "They send the pretty ones to fix the broken. Now they send the hard ones to fix the ugly." Her gaze was fixed on the shadows where McCarry stood, seeing more than any normal eyes should in the gloom. "Tell me, Lady of the Tuckahoe Garrison. Is my crime ugliness? Or is it that I'm making everyone else see it, too? Is it that I hold up a mirror, and you don't like the reflection?"

McCarry stepped out from the tunnel mouth, moving into the sickly light with a deliberate, unhurried pace. She did not raise the rifle to her shoulder. "Jenifer. My name is Lady McCarry, Department of Para-Natural Affairs. You are under arrest for five counts of aggravated assault and battery. The time for mirrors is over. You have the right to remain silent. I suggest you exercise it." Her voice was a monotone, a recitation of protocol that was its own form of weapon, designed to strip the moment of its drama and reduce it to a simple matter of procedure.

Jenifer’s cackle was a rockslide. It was a sound of pure, undiluted contempt that seemed to shake the very foundations of the law McCarry represented. "Rights," she spat, the word a curse. She gestured with one thick-fingered hand to the faded photograph taped to the concrete wall. "I had a right not to be poisoned by this city. I had a right to a doctor who saw a sick girl, not a monster to be shooed away. Your world of rights and laws is for the ones who live in the sun, not the things that crawl in the filth it creates." With a sudden, explosive movement, she kicked out with one heavy boot, not at McCarry, but at the car battery beside her. The connection post snapped, the wires sparked violently, and the single bulb went out, plunging the entire chamber into a profound and absolute darkness.

The blackness was instantaneous and disorienting, but for McCarry it was just another variable. The world dissolved into a symphony of sound and data. The rush of water, the drip from the ceiling, the scrape of Jenifer’s boots on concrete, and Alistair’s calm voice in her ear. "Signature is mobile, Lady. Moving left." McCarry pivoted, her body already reacting, raising the rifle. The soft *pfft* of the pneumatic system was absorbed by the cavernous dark. A guttural grunt followed, the sound of a heavy impact, but it was followed by the clang of the iron pipe swinging wildly through the air, seeking purchase on flesh and bone. The first dart had hit, but it hadn't stopped her.

A roar of pure fury echoed through the chamber, a sound of pain and outrage. Jenifer was a hurricane of motion in the dark. McCarry heard a heavy chain being pulled, followed by a groan of stressed metal. A section of the catwalk above them gave way, and a cascade of foul-smelling sludge and debris rained down, a messy, effective attempt to break her footing and foul her weapon. McCarry sidestepped the worst of it, her boots maintaining their grip on the slick floor, her discipline an anchor in the chaos. Jenifer wasn't just fighting; she was deconstructing the battlefield, turning her home into a weapon.

It was Glitch who saw the real threat. Through his multi-spectrum goggles, the darkness was an open book. Jenifer wasn't just swinging a pipe; she was retreating toward a corner of the chamber where a tangle of wires led to a familiar-looking contraption: the charging station for her splurge-gun. The tranquilizer had only enraged her, and she was falling back to her most effective weapon. "Lady, she's going for the goo-gun!" his voice crackled urgently in her ear. Without waiting for an order, the goblin raised his own weapon. There was no loud report, just a faint *thwip* and a brief, brilliant flash of blue-white light as his electrified dart struck the jury-rigged charging unit. The apparatus exploded in a shower of brilliant, hot sparks, momentarily illuminating the entire scene in a hellish, strobing tableau.

In that fraction of a second, McCarry saw everything. The troll, snarling, her face a mask of incandescent rage, momentarily blinded by the flash. The now-useless splurge-gun, its power source obliterated. The first tranquilizer dart protruding from the thick muscle of her shoulder. McCarry dropped the rifle, letting it hang from its sling, and drew a compact, weighted baton from her belt. Closing the twenty feet between them in four long, silent strides, she moved not like a soldier, but like a predator. The fight was no longer about range; it had become intimate.

She met Jenifer's wild, recovering swing of the pipe not with a direct block, but with a fluid deflection, using the troll's own momentum against her. The iron bar scraped harmlessly against the concrete wall. McCarry's return strike was not to the head or chest, but a precise, brutal blow to the side of Jenifer's knee. There was a wet crunch of cartilage and a bellow of agony that was starkly different from her earlier roars of rage. This was real, debilitating pain. As the troll staggered, her leg buckling, McCarry delivered a second, powerful strike with the butt of the baton to the nerve cluster at the base of her jaw, just below the jutting stump of her broken tusk.

Jenifer did not collapse. Even with one leg compromised and a potent paralytic agent beginning to sluggishly crawl through her veins, she was a creature of incredible resilience. She dropped the pipe and lunged, not to attack, but to grab. Her huge, three-fingered hands closed around McCarry’s left arm in a crushing grip, the sheer force threatening to snap the bone. Her face, contorted in pain and desperation, was inches from McCarry’s. For a moment, they were locked together, two different kinds of monster in a grim embrace. McCarry could smell the damp earth and stagnant water on her breath, and she could see, past the rage in the troll's eyes, a flicker of the terrified girl from the photograph. Then, Jenifer’s eyes rolled back in her head, the fury extinguished by the creeping drug. Her grip loosened, and she slumped to the ground, a mountain of defeated flesh and resentment, the sound of her heavy fall echoing in the sudden, ringing silence.

McCarry stood over the unconscious form, her own breath coming in short, controlled bursts. Her left arm throbbed with a fiery pain where Jenifer had grabbed her, and she knew there would be a bruise the shape of a troll’s hand blooming there by morning. Glitch appeared at her side, his pistol smoking faintly. "Well," he said, nudging Jenifer's boot with his toe, "she's not pretty when she's sleeping, either." McCarry ignored him. Her gaze fell upon the faded photograph, still taped to the wall, now spattered with a fine mist of sewer water. The smiling troll girl with the clean tusks. She looked from the picture to the defeated creature on the floor, the broken tusk, the pustuled skin, the body warped by toxins and rage. Her professional mask was unshaken, but a cold, hard thought formed in her mind. Jenifer’s crime hadn’t been making people ugly. It was that she had been made ugly, and no one had cared. McCarry keyed the comms unit on her collar. "McCarry to Central. Target is neutralized. Requesting a containment and extraction team at the Acheron conduit. And medical," she added, after a brief pause, looking at the dart in the troll's shoulder. "For the prisoner."The extraction team moved with the silent, sterile efficiency of a colony of ants dissecting a carcass. They were not police or paramedics; they were DPA Sanitization and Containment Technicians, clad head-to-toe in white hazmat suits that reflected the harsh beams of their shoulder-mounted lamps, making them look like ghostly apparitions in the subterranean gloom. They did not speak, communicating through encrypted, short-range comms and a series of precise hand signals. They documented the scene with multi-spectrum scanners, bagged every piece of Jenifer’s pathetic life—the scavenged wires, the clippings, the faded photograph—and then loaded the troll herself onto a levitating containment stretcher. A magnetic field shimmered into existence around her unconscious form, a cage of pure energy that hummed with quiet power.

McCarry stood back, watching the process with a detached, supervisory gaze, her right hand instinctively massaging the deep, throbbing ache in her left arm. The pain was a grounding presence, a physical receipt for a job completed. Glitch, perched on a dry section of catwalk and methodically cleaning his electro-dart pistol with a rag, watched the procession with a cynical eye. "All that fuss for a one-troll revolution," he muttered, loud enough for only McCarry to hear. "They'll probably stick her in a white room and study her shit for a decade. Call it 'rehabilitation'. In the end, they just trade one kind of sewer for another." McCarry didn't respond. Glitch’s cynicism was a known quantity, a predictable part of the landscape. Her own thoughts were more orderly, compartmentalized. The case was closed. The threat was neutralized. The paperwork was waiting.

Back at the brutalist monolith of the DPA headquarters, the building’s sterile air felt like a scouring agent, cleansing the lingering stench of the conduit from her coat and hair. Her first stop was the infirmary, a place that smelled of the same antiseptic as Dr. Thorne’s clinic but lacked any of its opulent comforts. The room was all brushed steel, white tile, and unforgiving fluorescent light. A medical officer, a man with the perpetually tired eyes of someone who spends his days patching up DPA agents, ran a diagnostic scanner over her arm. The screen displayed a flaring, angry image of deep tissue bruising and micro-fractures in the ulna.

"She gave you a proper souvenir, Lady," the officer said, preparing a hypospray of analgesic and bone-knitting agents. "Class Three Troll grip is no joke. Another ten percent pressure and she'd have snapped it clean." He administered the shot, and a wave of cool numbness washed away the throbbing ache. "You’ll be in a brace for a week. Standard procedure." McCarry simply nodded, her gaze distant. She was not thinking about her arm, but about the sheer, desperate force that had been required to inflict the injury. It was not the calculated application of strength; it was the raw, untempered power of a cornered animal, fighting with the last of its will. The officer’s work was the antithesis of Jenifer's art; it was quiet, efficient, and designed to erase, not inflict, permanent marks.

Later, in her spartan office, she completed the final report. The document was a testament to her methodology: concise, factual, devoid of emotion. *Subject: Jenifer [No Last Name Recorded]. Species: Troll, Urban Subtype. Motive: Psychological trauma stemming from chronic exposure to industrial toxins (see attached analysis from Dr. Finch) and subsequent perceived medical neglect, resulting in a series of retaliatory assaults against high-profile medical practitioners.* It was all there, neat and tidy. But the words felt like a hollow container for the truth. They didn't capture the roaring fury in the dark, the defiant grin on a face society had deemed monstrous, or the pathetic, faded image of a smiling girl taped to a concrete wall. McCarry picked up the small evidence bag containing the shard of broken tusk. A flaw. A piece of the monster that had led to its downfall. She stared at it for a long moment before dropping it into the case file's evidence pouch. It was just a piece of calcium phosphate and accumulated poison. It was just another case closed.

Jenifer awoke to the sound of a quiet, rhythmic beeping and the taste of recycled air. The first thing she registered was the pain, a dull, comprehensive map of her defeat. Her knee was a globe of fire, immobilized in some kind of restrictive brace. Her jaw throbbed with a deeper, cleaner ache than before, and when her tongue cautiously explored the broken tusk, it found the jagged edges gone, replaced by a smooth, polished surface. Someone had fixed it. The thought was so absurd, so hideously ironic, that a dry, rasping sound that might have been a laugh or a sob escaped her lips.

Her eyes slowly focused. She was in a cube. Everything was white. The walls, the ceiling, the floor, the thin blanket covering her. There were no shadows, no cracks, no comforting dampness. The air was dry and cool and carried no scent. A single, soft light emanated from the ceiling, a diffuse glow with no discernible source. It was the most horrifying place she had ever been. It was a sensory deprivation chamber designed to look like a hospital room. She was on a bed, not a scavenged sofa, and a thin tube was taped to the back of her hand, feeding a clear liquid into her veins. Nutrition. Hydration. Medication. The ultimate violation. They were keeping her alive, making her comfortable. They were fixing her.

She tried to sit up, but her muscles felt like waterlogged sandbags. The paralytic lingered, a ghost of its former power, leaving her weak and clumsy. The beeping beside her bed increased in tempo, an electronic tattletale. A section of the seamless white wall slid open with a soft hiss, and a figure entered. It was not a doctor, not a guard, but something in between. A man in a simple grey uniform, his face impassive. He held a small data slate. He did not look at her as a person, but as a checklist item.

"Jenifer. My name is Proctor." His voice was as flat and featureless as the room. "Your acute injuries have been stabilized. The compound fractures in your left patella have been set. Your broken tusk has been capped to prevent infection. A full biological and psychological evaluation will begin at 0800 tomorrow." He looked up from his slate then, his eyes meeting hers for the first time. They were the colour of dishwater, holding neither malice nor compassion. They held nothing. "Welcome to the Department of Para-Natural Affairs Containment and Rehabilitation Facility. You will be treated with professional courtesy as long as you remain compliant." He turned and left, the white wall sliding shut behind him, sealing her back into the silent, sterile void. Jenifer stared at the spot where he had been, the rhythmic beeping of the monitor the only sound. The rage was gone, scoured away and replaced by a cold, heavy dread. They hadn't just caged her. They had taken her ugliness, her art, her pain, and were trying to file it all down, to smooth it over, just like they had her tusk. They were going to make her pretty. And in the perfect, shadowless, silent white room, Jenifer the troll finally began to scream.Jenifer’s scream was a raw, primal thing, the sound of a soul being flayed. It beat against the seamless white walls of her cell, but the sound-dampening materials absorbed it hungrily, stealing its power, rendering it impotent. It was the first and last scream they would get from her. When the sound finally died in her throat, leaving it raw and bleeding, a profound, chilling silence took its place. In the crushing absence of sound and shadow, she found a new kind of armour. Her rage had been a fire, hot and loud and purifying. This was different. This was ice. Cold, hard, and utterly unmoving. She lay on the bed, feeling the slow, rhythmic push of chemicals into her veins, and began to wait. The world had tried to erase her with poison and neglect; now it would try with kindness and therapy. It was all the same assault.

Days in the white room bled into one another, marked only by the silent hiss of the wall-panel door and the arrival of the Proctor with his trays of nutrient paste and his vapid, procedural updates. Jenifer did not speak. She did not eat the paste, though the IV in her arm kept her from starving. She simply watched, her small, dark eyes missing nothing, cataloguing the precise timing of the door, the slight wear on the Proctor’s grey boots, the way the diffuse light never, ever changed. They had taken her home, her art, her freedom. She would not give them the satisfaction of her participation. Her compliance would be purely physiological; her mind was a fortress they would never breach.

The monotony was broken on the fourth cycle. The door hissed open to admit the Proctor, but he was followed by a woman. She was human, dressed in a crisp, light-blue tunic and matching trousers, her blonde hair cut in a severe, sensible bob. She carried a data slate, and her smile was a well-practiced instrument of disarming professionalism. It did not reach her eyes, which were the pale, analytical grey of a microscope lens. Jenifer felt an instant, visceral loathing for her that dwarfed anything she had felt for Dr. Thorne. He had been an arrogant fool; this woman was a scalpel.

"Jenifer. My name is Dr. Annelise Hale," the woman said, her voice smooth and modulated, a stark contrast to Jenifer's own gravelly rasp. "I'm the head of Behavioural Deconstruction and Realignment here. I'm not here to punish you. I'm here to help you understand the path that led you here." Dr. Hale pulled up a simple white stool, its legs making no sound on the floor, and sat a careful, prescribed distance away. "We can begin whenever you're ready. Tell me about your work. The… slurry. The chemical composition was quite complex. Was the chartreuse colour a deliberate aesthetic choice?" Jenifer stared at the opposite wall, her expression a granite mask. She focused on the memory of the satisfying *splat* the slurry had made against Thorne's expensive shirt, the look of perfect, uncomprehending horror on his face. She held that image in her mind like a precious jewel. Dr. Hale's smile tightened by a barely perceptible degree. "Silence is a valid response. It tells me you feel you have no voice. We're going to work on giving you one back."

Miles away, in the world of rain and ambition, Lady McCarry was a creature of routine. She signed off on the final inventory of seized materials from Jenifer's lair, her signature a sharp, angular slash of ink. She reviewed after-action reports from two other minor DPA cases—a poltergeist infestation in a municipal records office and a pixie-smuggling ring operating out of a flower market. Her arm, encased in a sleek black support brace, was a minor inconvenience, a temporary flaw in her otherwise efficient machine of a body. The case of the Bile-Slinger was, by every metric she valued, closed. And yet, it refused to remain so. The media had latched onto the story with rabid glee. "Monster Troll Behind Bars!" screamed the tabloids. "City Breathes Sigh of Relief as DPA Nabs Hideous Fiend," reported the more respectable news outlets. The narrative was simple, satisfying, and utterly false. They painted Jenifer as a mindless beast, a random eruption of subterranean horror. They never mentioned the Blackwood Tannery, the poisoned water, or the doctors who had turned her away. The truth was messy and inconvenient, so it was trimmed away, leaving a clean, marketable lie. McCarry found the inaccuracy irritating, like a persistent, low-grade hum she couldn't tune out.

Her desk comm chimed, a polite, electronic intrusion. "Lady McCarry," said the disembodied voice of her aide, "a call for you. It's the office of Dr. Aris Thorne. They're… insistent." McCarry felt a flicker of annoyance. She had no time for the bleating of victims. Her job was capture, not aftercare. "Put him through," she ordered, her voice clipped. The line clicked. "Lady McCarry? This is Thorne. Aris Thorne." The voice was not the reedy, terrified thing she had heard on the paramedic's recording. The terror was gone, replaced by an ugly, simmering rage that crackled over the line. "I'm told you have the beast. I want to know what's being done. I want to know when it will be put down. An animal that vicious can't be allowed to live. It's a threat to public safety. My safety."

McCarry's gaze drifted to the window, to the city lights that seemed to promise so much and hide even more. "The DPA does not have a death penalty, Doctor. The subject is in a secure facility undergoing evaluation." There was a sputtering sound on the other end of the line. "Evaluation? Evaluation? Are you insane? It melted my suit! It stained my skin! Do you have any idea what this has done to my practice? To my reputation? I am a laughingstock! I demand justice!" McCarry's knuckles, where her hand rested on the desk, were white. "You have your justice, Doctor. The attacks have stopped. You are safe." "Safe?" Thorne shrieked, his voice climbing into a hysterical register. "I'm not safe until that… thing… is a footnote in a biology textbook! I have influence, Lady McCarry. I have friends. I will not have this creature coddled and studied while I am permanently… soiled! I will see it destroyed. You will help me, or I will make your department's life a living hell." The line went dead.

McCarry stared at the receiver for a long moment before placing it carefully back in its cradle. The threat was empty; DPA funding and authority came from sources far beyond the reach of a disgraced cosmetic surgeon. But the call had dislodged something. It had brought the ugliness of the case bubbling back to the surface. She pulled open the drawer where the physical case file was kept. She bypassed her own neat report, the lab results, the inventory lists. Her fingers found the small, plastic evidence bag containing the faded photograph of the smiling troll girl. She looked at the innocent, happy face, at the small, clean tusks. Then she thought of Dr. Thorne, his voice dripping with venom and wounded vanity, demanding the death of a creature his world had created. He hadn't seen a sick girl any more than the first doctors had. He only saw a monster that had ruined his reflection. McCarry slid the photograph back into the file, but as she closed the drawer, a new, unwelcome thought took root in her orderly mind. Jenifer had been caught, but McCarry suddenly wasn't sure that justice had been served at all.The sterile quiet of the DPA was a lie, and for the first time in her career, Lady McCarry could hear the discordant noise beneath it. Dr. Thorne’s venomous, self-pitying tirade was a symptom, not the disease. The disease was the rot of a city that created monsters in its dark, forgotten corners and then recoiled in performative horror when they crawled into the light. The system she served so faultlessly was designed to contain these eruptions, not to prevent them. It was a city of janitors, not engineers, and she was the most efficient janitor of them all. The thought was a burr under the saddle of her discipline, an irritating friction against the smooth functioning of her professional life. The case was closed, but the justice was incomplete. It was a flawed equation, and McCarry did not tolerate flawed equations.

With a resolve that was as sudden as it was absolute, she rose from her desk. The brace on her left arm was a stiff, unwelcome reminder of the raw, untamed force she had subdued, a force born of desperation. Her path did not lead her to the archives or the firing range, but back down into the building’s sterile guts, towards the Containment and Rehabilitation wing. It was a section of the DPA she rarely visited. Her work ended at the point of capture; what came after was the domain of a different kind of specialist, the doctors and proctors who disassembled minds instead of tactical situations. Her presence there was an anomaly, a deviation from protocol that caused the Proctor on duty to straighten his spine.

As she approached the designated cell, the seamless white panel hissed open and Dr. Annelise Hale stepped out. She held her data slate like a shield, and her professional smile was a thin, brittle veneer over a core of possessive authority. She registered McCarry’s presence, and then the support brace on her arm, and her smile tightened. "Lady McCarry. An unscheduled visit. The subject remains in a state of deep tactical withdrawal. My sessions are delicate work. I can't have them disrupted by… external stimuli." The implication was clear: McCarry was a hammer, and this was a place that required scalpels.

"I won't be long, Doctor," McCarry replied, her voice leaving no room for argument. She met the psychologist’s gaze with her own winter-sky stare. "I am not here to conduct a session. I am here to view the asset." The term was cold, procedural, a deliberate choice to reassert her own jurisdiction. Dr. Hale’s jaw clenched almost imperceptibly before she stepped aside, her expression a mixture of pique and sharp, analytical curiosity. She would be watching from the observation suite, McCarry knew. This, too, was a factor in her calculations.

The cell was an assault of pure, featureless white. The lack of sensory input was a weapon in itself, designed to turn a mind inward until it consumed itself. Jenifer was on the bed, as still as she had been on her scavenged sofa in the conduit, but the coiled power was gone, replaced by a dense, unassailable stillness. Her skin, cleaned of the conduit’s grime, was a patchwork of the sickly greenish-yellow stain and its natural grey, a living testament to her own art. The capped tusk was a small, obscene pearl of civilization on a landscape of raw nature. Her eyes were open, staring at a point on the ceiling, but McCarry knew she was not seeing it. She was in a place no white room could ever reach.

McCarry did not speak at first. She simply stood in the center of the room, a column of Tuckahoe-grey against the pervasive white, letting her presence register. She was not a doctor with a placating smile or a proctor with a tray of nutrient paste. She was the woman who had hunted her in the dark and met her fury with disciplined, overwhelming force. After a long moment, Jenifer’s head turned with an audible crack of vertebrae. Her dark eyes, no longer burning with rage but frozen with a cold, hard contempt, fixed on McCarry. It was the first voluntary movement anyone had seen her make in four days.

"Why are you here?" Jenifer’s voice was a dry rasp, the sound of stone grinding on stone after a long drought. "Did you forget a piece of evidence? Want to scrape something else off me for your files?"

McCarry ignored the taunt. She held up her own data slate, the screen glowing with a single, high-resolution image: the faded photograph of the smiling troll girl. "This was on your wall. Taped next to the men you attacked. It doesn't fit the pattern."

Jenifer’s gaze flickered to the screen, and for a heartbeat, a fissure of some raw, powerful emotion cracked her icy facade before it was sealed over again. A low, grinding sound rumbled in her chest, the ghost of her terrible laugh. "You think it's a pattern you can understand. A puzzle you can solve. You see a picture of what was, and you file it under 'motive'. You are very good at your job, Lady." Her eyes lifted from the slate and locked onto McCarry's. "Tell me. Why did you bring the picture? The others, the ones who want to fix me, they're afraid of it. They don't want to be reminded that I was ever anything but this." She gestured weakly at her own body with a cuffed hand.

The question was a parry, turning McCarry's own inquiry back on her. It was a demand for an honesty that had no place in a DPA file. McCarry considered a procedural answer—*It’s an anomalous data point*—but dismissed it as a cowardice she could not afford. "I don't know," she said, the admission costing her more than the physical pain in her arm. "It feels… important. More important than the rest."

Jenifer stared at her for a long, silent moment, the rhythmic beeping of her vital signs monitor the only sound in the room. There was a strange, new light in her eyes, not warmth, but a flicker of grim understanding. The shared recognition of two creatures who understood the world in terms of hard, unpalatable truths. "Thorne," Jenifer rasped, the name a foul taste in her mouth. "The pretty doctor. Has he been crying to you? Demanding my head? He seems the type."

"He has expressed his dissatisfaction with your continued existence," McCarry confirmed, her voice flat.

Another grating chuckle. "Of course he has. His perfect world has a stain on it now, and he wants you to scrub it out. For him, justice is a clean mirror. What is it for you, Lady McCarry?" Before McCarry could formulate a response, Jenifer’s eyes slid away, her focus retreating back into the impenetrable fortress of her mind. "Get out," she rasped, the words holding a sudden, immense weariness. "You can't fix this. You're just another part of the cage."

McCarry did not press. She had what she came for, though she wasn't entirely sure what it was. As the white panel hissed shut behind her, sealing Jenifer back into her silent tomb, she saw Dr. Hale waiting down the corridor, her arms crossed, her expression unreadable. "An interesting technique, Lady," the doctor said, her voice laced with a fine, sharp edge of professional condescension. "Antagonizing the subject to elicit a baseline emotional response. Crude, but it yielded data." McCarry walked past her without a word. She had not been gathering data. She had been taking a bearing. The hunt for Jenifer the troll was over. But the hunt for what came next, for a justice that was more than just a clean mirror or a locked cage, had just begun. And it would lead her into territory far more dangerous and complex than any toxic sewer.The walk back from the containment wing was a journey through hostile territory of a different sort. Every sterile corridor, every humming light fixture, every silent, hurrying DPA functionary seemed a part of the vast, intricate machine of containment, a system that valued order over justice. Dr. Hale’s condescending words echoed in McCarry’s mind, not as an insult, but as a diagnosis of the entire establishment. They were all so busy gathering data, so focused on deconstructing the anomaly, that they never thought to deconstruct the circumstances that created it. The DPA was a reactive force, a finely-honed tool for cleaning up messes, and McCarry was its sharpest edge. But the filth from the Blackwood Tannery wasn't a mess to be cleaned; it was a foundation that had been laid, deliberately and with callous indifference.

She bypassed her own office and went straight to the DPA’s digital archives, a cavernous, climate-controlled chamber where the city’s secret history was stored on silent, black servers. The room was Alistair Finch's true home. The gnome was there, his face illuminated by the green glow of a holographic data-stream, his fingers flying across a virtual console. He was so engrossed in tracing the arcane energy patterns of a recently apprehended djinn that he didn't notice her until she stood directly beside him. He jumped, nearly dropping a coil of fibre-optic cable.

"Lady! By the great gear-grinder, you move like a vacuum. Don't do that." He adjusted his spectacles, his curiosity immediately overpowering his surprise. "Don't tell me our Bile-Slinger has a spectral accomplice. The paperwork would be a nightmare." McCarry’s expression remained unchanged. "The case is closed. A new one is open. I want the name of the contractor paid to seal the Acheron tributary conduit, and I want to know where every last dollar of that payment went."

Alistair blinked, his large eyes wide with interest. This was a significant departure from their usual fare of tracking cryptids and dispelling hexes. The summons came less than an hour later. Director Vance wanted to see her. His office was on the top floor, a stark contrast to the rest of the building. It had a real window, a vast pane of armoured glass that looked out over the glittering, rain-slicked expanse of the city. Vance was a man who had been ground smooth by decades of political friction. He stood with his back to her, watching the traffic crawl far below. "I received a report from Dr. Hale," he said without turning. "She's concerned your interaction with the troll subject has 'destabilized the therapeutic environment'."

"My interaction confirmed the subject's motive and state of mind," McCarry stated, her voice even. "It was a productive debriefing."

"It was a deviation, McCarry," Vance said, finally turning. His face was a roadmap of weary compromises. "And it has come at an inconvenient time. Dr. Thorne is making a nuisance of himself. That evening, she saw what Vance meant. In the DPA's communal mess hall, a news broadcast was playing on a large wall-screen. There was Dr. Aris Thorne, his face a carefully constructed mask of tragic victimhood. The sickly yellow stain from the Bile-Slurry was still visible on his neck, a prop he used to great effect. "This… creature… is not a misunderstood soul," he said, his voice trembling with manufactured emotion. McCarry looked away in disgust, the tasteless nutrient broth she was consuming suddenly feeling as synthetic and false as Thorne's performance.

Her comm-link vibrated with a text-only message from a highly encrypted channel. It was from Glitch. The message was short and to the point. *Found your sewer rats. The contractor was a company called 'Terra-Seal Solutions'. Got paid 3.2 million. Dissolved six weeks later. McCarry stood, leaving her half-eaten meal on the table. She walked to the window of the mess hall, her gaze sweeping over the same skyline that graced Director Vance's office. The city was a monument of light and steel, a testament to wealth and power. But now, she could see the rot in its bones. She could feel the poison seeping from its foundations. Silas Croft. The injustice now had a name. The monster on the news was a distraction, a sideshow. The real monster wore a suit, lived in a penthouse, and had built his kingdom on a bed of toxic sludge and lies. Her hand rested on the cool metal of the brace on her arm, the dull ache a constant, physical reminder of the price of that corruption. The hunt had changed direction. She was no longer hunting a troll in the dark. She was hunting a king in his castle of glass.Silas Croft’s kingdom was a spear of smoked glass and burnished steel that stabbed at the perpetually bruised sky. Croft Tower was the tallest building in the city, an architectural declaration of dominance that made the Thorne Ascendancy Clinic look like a provincial doctor’s office. From his penthouse, Silas Croft could survey his dominion, a glittering tapestry of lights woven from the very fabric of the city’s commerce and politics. He was not a doctor who fixed faces; he was a developer who built them, carving his will onto the skyline with the unassailable authority of capital.

McCarry stood in the rain-soaked shadows of a lesser building across the street, the black tactical brace on her arm a rigid counterpoint to the fluid lines of her dark, non-reflective gear. Her trench coat was gone, a casualty of a mission that no longer required its regimental authority. This was not DPA business. This was personal, and it required a different uniform. The city looked different to her now. The patterns of traffic were not just urban life; they were the arteries pumping wealth into hearts like Croft’s. The gleaming skyscrapers were not just architecture; they were tombstones for the inconvenient truths buried beneath their foundations. The law, she was beginning to understand, was simply the set of rules the winners wrote after the game was over.

"He's a creature of habit," Glitch’s voice crackled in her earpiece, a tinny whisper of static and grease. An hour later, McCarry was back in her element, the oppressive dark of the city’s underbelly. The air was thick with the familiar smell of damp concrete and decay, a scent she now associated not with monsters, but with origins. The maintenance valve was exactly where Glitch said it would be, a grim metal circle set into the concrete. While she worked on the magnetic lock with a specialized decryption key, Glitch, who had scuttled down to join her, was busy disabling the tower's wastewater sensors. The ascent through the tower’s service corridors was a journey from one world to another. The grimy, dripping concrete of the sub-levels gave way to clean, painted cinder block, which in turn became sterile, white-tiled passages. The air grew progressively cleaner, cooler, and drier, the sounds of the city fading until the only noise was the faint, ever-present hum of the building's life support. They moved like ghosts, Glitch’s small, scuttling form a shadow at McCarry’s heels. He was in his glory, disabling sensor panels and slicing into security camera feeds with the joyful focus of a master artist at his canvas. The penthouse office was as cold and perfect as a mausoleum. Floor-to-ceiling windows displayed the city like a conquered territory. The furniture was minimalist and severe, the art on the walls abstract and expensive. There was no clutter, no sign of life, save for a single, framed photograph on the massive obsidian desk. It was not of a wife or children. It was Silas Croft, younger, shaking hands with the city mayor from that era, both of them beaming over a scale model of the redeveloped Blackwood district. "The architect and his masterpiece," McCarry murmured, her voice flat. Glitch had already found the hidden panel behind a piece of abstract sculpture and was physically jacking into the hardened server. His console lit up with cascading lines of code as he battled the system’s defenses. "Now this is a security suite," he muttered, his brow furrowed in concentration. "He didn't cheap out here.
