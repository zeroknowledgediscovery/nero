The email had been sitting open on her laptop for three hours, the cheerful, bold-faced ‘Congratulations!’ looking more like a threat with every passing minute. Kira O’Connell, sparky from Sutherland Shire, finalist on Britain’s Got Talent. It was a sentence so ridiculous, so utterly bonkers, that every time she read it a laugh bubbled up in her chest, a hysterical little thing that sounded more like a sob. She’d close the laptop, walk to the window of the tiny South London flat they’d put her in, and stare at the drizzle. Then, compelled by a masochistic need for confirmation, she’d walk back, flip the screen up, and there it would be. Still there. Still real.

London drizzle wasn’t like Sydney rain. Sydney rain was an event; it hammered down, washed the world clean, and then cleared out for the sun to reclaim its territory. This… this was different. It was a persistent, clammy mist that seeped into your bones, a fine grey spray that promised nothing but more grey. It coated the windowpane, blurring the council estate opposite into a watercolour of damp brick and sorrowful-looking satellite dishes. A proper gloomy day, the kind that made you want to crawl under a doona and not come out 'til spring. Back home, a day like this would still have a certain brightness to it, the memory of sun just behind the clouds. Here, the light felt old, tired. Recycled.

She ran a hand through her hair, still damp from a shower that had run lukewarm halfway through. The boiler in this place was a relic, probably installed around the time of the Blitz. She’d had a look at it, of course. Couldn’t help herself. The wiring was a dog’s breakfast, a tangle of ancient fabric-coated cables and newer, plastic-sheathed ones that made the professional in her itch. Don’t touch it, the production assistant, a perpetually frazzled young woman named Becca, had warned her. You’re not a contractor, you’re talent. Talent. Kira snorted. She was a woman who knew her way around a three-phase supply and could terminate a CAT6 cable in her sleep. ‘Talent’ was for the singers with their perfect teeth and the dance troupes with their synchronised backflips. She was just a sparky with a weird hobby.

The hobby. That was the source of all this madness. It had started in her dad’s garage, years ago. He’d been a tinkerer, a man who saw potential in junk. He’d brought home a small Tesla coil from a university surplus sale, a beautiful thing of copper and brass, and together they’d figured out how to make it sing. Not just buzz and crackle, but play actual music, the oscillating sparks creating audible notes in the air. The ‘singing lightning,’ he’d called it. After he was gone, working on the coils was the only thing that felt right. She’d built bigger ones, more complex ones, learned to modulate the frequencies with a precision that was part science, part art. It was her secret language, a way of talking to him across the void.

It was her mates, Damo and Shaz, who’d signed her up. A drunken night after a scorching day on a new-build site in Cronulla. They’d been watching clips of BGT on YouTube, laughing at the eccentrics and the sob stories. "You should do it, Kez," Damo had slurred, poking her in the ribs. "Show those Poms what a real sparky can do." She’d laughed it off, but Shaz, ever the instigator, had already been tapping away on her phone. A few weeks later, an email arrived inviting her to a preliminary audition in Sydney for the international production team. She’d gone along for a laugh, she told herself. Just to say she’d done it. She’d set up her smallest coil, played a crackling, ozone-scented rendition of 'Waltzing Matilda', and the producers, jaws on the floor, had fast-tracked her straight to the London auditions.

Even then, it hadn't felt real. The flight, the hotel, walking out onto that enormous stage at the Palladium. The lights were blinding. She could just make out the silhouettes of the judges at their long desk, looking like gods on a distant Olympus. She’d hidden her shaking hands by gripping the laptop she used to control the coils. She’d explained, her voice croaking, what she was about to do. She saw the skepticism on their faces, especially Simon Cowell’s. He had the bored, seen-it-all expression of a man who’d witnessed a thousand mediocrities. Then she’d flicked the switch. The air had crackled. A single, violet streamer of lightning had erupted from the top of the coil, and the first, haunting notes of ‘Hallelujah’ filled the cavernous theatre. It wasn't a perfect sound, more like a ghostly organ made of pure energy, but it was hers. It was her dad’s. When the last spark faded and the smell of ozone hung in the air, the silence was absolute. And then the room had exploded. A standing ovation. Four yeses. Simon’s face had cracked into a genuine, astonished smile. "In all my years," he’d said, his voice echoing through the monitors, "I have never, ever seen anything like that. Genius. Utter genius."

And now, this. The final. Her, a bunch of child singers, a dog act, a magician who seemed to be genuinely bending the laws of physics, and a gaggle of impossibly handsome male tenors. She felt like a platypus at a peacock convention.

Her phone buzzed on the formica countertop, rattling against a mug of now-cold tea. ‘MUM’ flashed on the screen. Kira took a deep breath, pasted on a smile she didn’t feel, and answered.

“Hi, Mum.”

“Kira, love! We saw the email! Your aunty Carol forwarded it, it’s all over the community Facebook page! ‘Sutherland Shire Sparky Shocks the UK!’ That’s what they’re saying! Are you alright? Are you excited? You must be over the moon!” Her mum’s voice was a torrent of frantic, loving energy, a familiar sound that instantly transported Kira back to their little house overlooking Gymea Bay.

“Yeah, Mum, it’s… it’s pretty wild,” Kira managed, twisting the phone cord around her finger. The flat had a landline, another bafflingly archaic feature.

“Wild? It’s incredible! Your father… oh, he would have been so, so proud, love. He’d be telling everyone down at the bowlo. He’d have a t-shirt printed.”

Kira’s throat tightened. That was the crux of it, wasn't it? The reason she couldn't just pack it in and go home. “Yeah. Yeah, he would.”

“Are you eating properly? You sound a bit peaky. It’s not all just baked beans and toast, is it? I can send a care package. Damo’s mum is flying to visit her cousin next week, she could bring some Tim Tams. And some proper tea bags.”

“I’m fine, Mum. The food’s… fine. And Becca, the production lady, she makes sure we’re all fed.” The food was, in fact, mostly beige and served in catering trays, but her mum didn’t need to know that.

“And what about the act? For the final? Have you got something special planned? It has to be bigger, love. The final is always bigger.”

Kira looked over at the shipping crate in the corner of the small living room. It had arrived two days ago, a huge wooden box containing ‘Zeus’, her largest coil. It was a monster, nearly two metres tall, with a secondary coil of painstakingly wound copper wire that gleamed even in the dim light. Beside it were the schematics for the finale performance, pages and pages of complex diagrams and code. It was ambitious. Probably too ambitious. She was integrating three smaller coils, programming a polyphonic piece, a medley of Queen songs, that would require a level of power and control she’d never attempted before. It was dangerous. One miscalculation, one stray frequency, and she could blow every light in the studio. Or worse.

“I’m working on it,” she said, the understatement of the century. “It’ll be bigger.”

“That’s my girl. Just… be careful, won’t you? That thing… it still makes me nervous. All that electricity.”

“It’s my job, Mum. I know what I’m doing.” The lie tasted sour. She mostly knew what she was doing. This new routine, though, was pushing the very edge of her knowledge.

They talked for a few more minutes, the conversation turning to neighbourhood gossip and the health of her mum’s prize-winning roses. It was so normal, so wonderfully mundane, that it made her current reality feel even more like a fever dream. When she hung up, the silence of the flat descended again, heavier than before.

She needed air. Not this damp, recycled London air, but just… out. She pulled on her Blundstones, grabbed a jacket, and headed for the door. As her hand touched the knob, there was a sharp rap from the other side. She froze. It was probably Becca, coming to drag her to another pointless photo shoot or a media training session where they’d try to sand down her Aussie accent into something more palatable for the British public.

She swung the door open, a resigned sigh on her lips. But it wasn’t Becca. It was another contestant, a young guy, maybe early twenties, with a floppy, artfully dishevelled haircut and a jawline that could cut glass. He was wearing a leather jacket that was probably worth more than her car back home. Leo Vance. The crooner. The bookies' favourite. He’d sung a Michael Bublé song in the semi-finals and half the female population of the United Kingdom had spontaneously ovulated.

He flashed a smile so white it was practically a high-beam. “Hey. You’re Kira, right? The lightning lady?”

“Yeah, that’s me,” she said, instinctively crossing her arms. “The sparky.”

“Right, right. Cool. Listen, bit of a random one, but my Wi-Fi’s gone down and I’ve got a Skype with my vocal coach in, like, ten minutes. I don’t suppose I could piggyback off yours? I’d be eternally grateful.” He gestured down the hall with his thumb. “I’m just in 3B.”

Kira hesitated. The last thing she wanted was this preening pop star wannabe in her space. But it was a simple enough request, and saying no seemed needlessly rude. “Uh, yeah, sure. Come in. The password’s on a sticky note on the router. Over there.” She pointed towards the small desk.

Leo sauntered in, bringing with him a gust of expensive cologne that smelled of sandalwood and ambition. “Ah, you’re a lifesaver. This place is amazing, isn’t it? But the infrastructure is a bit… vintage.” He spotted the massive crate in the corner. “Whoa. Is that the doomsday device?”

“Something like that,” Kira mumbled, busying herself by tidying a pile of papers that didn’t need tidying.

“Mad. Absolutely mad. I don’t know how you do it. I’d be terrified of frying myself.” He got his laptop connected and gave her another blinding smile. “Right, you’re a star. I’ll be out of your hair in a tick.”

She just nodded, retreating to the small galley kitchen to pretend to look for something in the fridge. She could hear him through the open door, his voice changing as his call connected, becoming smoother, more professional.

“Maestro! How are you?… Yeah, feeling great, feeling really confident. We’re just going to run through the crescendo on the final chorus, I feel like I can give it a little more… a bit more vulnerability, you know?”

Kira rolled her eyes, pulling out a carton of milk and sniffing it. Vulnerability. This guy wouldn’t know vulnerability if it bit him on his perfectly sculpted arse. Her act was vulnerability. It was a chunk of her heart, a tribute to her dad, made of wires and lightning, and she was about to splash it all over national television for people like him to call a ‘doomsday device’.

She leaned against the counter, the cold of the linoleum seeping through her jeans. The chasm between her world and theirs had never felt wider. Leo was talking about brand synergy and post-show album potential. The other day, she’d overheard the magician discussing his Vegas residency offer with his agent. And her? Her biggest plan for after the final was to fly home, hug her mum, and get back to work on Mrs. Henderson’s kitchen rewiring.

Leo finished his call and popped his head into the kitchen. “All done. Thanks again, you’re a proper legend.” He paused, looking at her properly for the first time. His smile faltered for a second. “You okay? You look a bit… rattled.”

“Just a lot on my mind,” she said, turning to put the milk back in the fridge. “Big week.”

“Tell me about it,” he said, his media-friendly persona slipping back into place. “The pressure is immense. But you’ve just got to embrace it, you know? This is the dream. We’re living the dream.”

Kira looked from his face, so full of easy confidence, to the grey, drizzling world outside her window. The dream. It felt more like a very strange, very stressful anxiety attack. “Yeah,” she said, forcing a smile that felt like cracking plaster. “The dream.”He lingered for a moment, leaning against the doorframe, a pose that was probably second nature to him from a thousand photoshoots. The blinding smile was gone, replaced by a flicker of genuine curiosity. “That smile didn’t quite reach your eyes, did it? You’re a bit like a kangaroo in the headlights.” He let out a short, self-deprecating laugh. “God, that’s a terrible analogy, isn’t it? I’m rubbish at this. Look, I just mean you seem properly spooked. My first big audition, I threw up in a bin just off Carnaby Street. Full on, violent, exorcist-level stuff. The nerves are a killer.”

Kira was momentarily thrown. The confession was so unexpected, so… human. She’d had him pegged as a robot assembled from magazine clippings and boy-band DNA. She found her voice, a dry rasp. “This is a bit more than nerves. This whole thing…” she gestured vaguely at the room, the window, the unseen world of television production, “...it’s not my world. I fix things that are broken. I don’t… perform.” The word felt foreign in her mouth, like a badly translated phrase.

“But you do, though,” Leo insisted, pushing off the doorframe and taking a half-step closer. His cologne was less overpowering now, more of a warm, woody scent. “What you do up there, it’s not just fixing something. It’s magic. Honestly. The rest of us, we sing, we dance, we tell a few gags. It’s stuff people have seen before, just maybe a different version of it. But you… nobody has ever seen anything like you. You’re not in the competition. You *are* the competition.” He paused, then gave a wry smile. “Don’t tell the other tenors I said that, they’ll string me up by my vocal cords.”

He gave her a final, encouraging nod. “Just don’t let the big scary machine get the better of you. Tame the beast, yeah? See you at rehearsals.” And with that, he was gone, his footsteps echoing lightly down the hallway. The door clicked shut, and the silence rushed back in, but it was different now. His words, meant to be a compliment, had somehow drawn the battle lines even more clearly. *Tame the beast.* He thought Zeus was the beast. He didn’t understand. The beast wasn’t the machine in the corner. It was the expectation, the stage, the millions of people watching. Zeus wasn't the beast; it was her shield.

A sudden, fierce wave of possessiveness washed over her. He didn’t get it. None of them did. This wasn’t a gimmick. It wasn’t a ‘doomsday device’ or a ‘big scary machine’. It was a conversation. It was memory made tangible. It was the only goddamn thing in this whole grey, bewildering city that made any sense. Galvanised by a surge of defiant energy, she strode over to her tool bag, pulled out a small pry bar, and jammed it into the seam of the giant wooden crate. The screech of nails pulling from wood was a satisfyingly violent sound in the quiet room. She worked her way around the lid, the muscles in her back and shoulders bunching with the effort, a familiar and welcome ache.

With a final groan of protest, the lid came free. She heaved it off and set it against the wall. And there it was. Zeus. It was beautiful. Even disassembled for shipping, its power was obvious. The base, a solid block of varnished wood housing the primary capacitors and circuitry, was the anchor. But the star was the secondary coil, a tall, slender cylinder of polished acrylic, wound with nearly a mile of copper wire so fine it looked like spun gold. It lay nestled in a bed of custom-cut foam, gleaming under the flat’s single, dim lightbulb. The air suddenly smelled different, tinged with the clean, sharp scent of copper and solder and ozone, the perfume of her father’s garage.

She reached in, her fingers tracing the perfect, concentric lines of the winding. Each loop had been done by hand. Months of work. Her work. This was real. This was solid. Not like the fleeting promises of fame or the saccharine encouragement of pop singers. This was physics. Cause and effect. You put power in, you modulate the frequency, and if you get it exactly right, lightning comes out. No opinions, no judges, just laws. The laws of the universe. She felt the knot of anxiety in her stomach loosen its grip, replaced by the familiar, humming tension of a complex job to be done.

Kira reached back into the crate and pulled out the rolled-up schematics. She spread them across the threadbare carpet, the pages crackling as she unrolled them. They were a chaotic masterpiece of her own making: Kirchhoff’s laws jostling for space with hand-scrawled snippets of Queen lyrics; complex equations for resonant frequencies next to timing cues for the light show she’d have to program. ‘A Kind of Magic’ transitioned into the thumping bassline of ‘Another One Bites the Dust,’ which then needed to seamlessly flow into the soaring, operatic climax of ‘Bohemian Rhapsody.’ It was a symphony of sparks she was proposing, a high-wire act of electrical engineering and musical theatre. It was ludicrous. It was dangerous. It was the only thing she wanted to do.

She knelt on the floor, the whole bewildering world of Britain’s Got Talent shrinking until it was nothing but these pages. The drizzle on the window, the memory of Leo’s cologne, the ‘Congratulations!’ on her laptop—it all faded into a distant hum. Here, on the floor of a dingy London flat, surrounded by the guts of her creation, she wasn’t Kira O’Connell, the terrified finalist. She was Kez the sparky. And she had work to do. She picked up a socket wrench, its cool, heavy steel a familiar comfort in her palm. The first bolt needed tightening.The click of the socket wrench was a deeply satisfying sound, a precise, mechanical punctuation mark in the damp quiet. Bolt by bolt, Kira anchored the heavy base of Zeus to its frame. The work was methodical, absorbing, a physical meditation that pushed the swirling anxieties into a far corner of her mind. Here, there was only the plan, the tools, and the ordered sequence of tasks. She fitted the main capacitor bank into place, the block of high-voltage components sliding home with a solid thud. She worked with an unhurried, ingrained efficiency, her hands moving with the certainty of long practice. Every component had its place, every wire a destination. It was a language she understood intimately, a world away from media interviews and the terrifying subjectivity of public opinion.

Lifting the secondary coil was the most delicate part of the operation. She handled the tall, gleaming cylinder as if it were a priceless artefact, which, to her, it was. The nearly-invisible copper wire was the most vulnerable part of the entire assembly; a single deep scratch could create a point of failure, causing a catastrophic arc-over. With her muscles straining, she carefully maneuvered it into position, her breath held tight in her chest until it was seated securely on its insulating stand. She ran a reverent hand over its smooth, cool surface, a silent greeting to the heart of the machine. This was where the magic happened. This column of acrylic and copper was the voice box, the vocal cords of the lightning she was here to conduct.

With the main structure assembled, she turned to the wiring. This was her sanctuary. She unspooled cables, her fingers expertly stripping insulation, crimping terminals, and tightening connections onto the heavy copper busbars. Unlike the chaotic tangle behind the flat’s walls, her work was a model of clarity. Every path was logical, every connection secure, every cable labelled and tied down. She attached the heavy-gauge grounding strap, running it to the one part of the flat she trusted: the cold water pipe under the kitchen sink, which she’d scraped clean to ensure a perfect metal-to-metal bond. Safety first, always. Her dad’s voice, clear as if he were standing beside her. Don’t get cocky, Kez. The electricity doesn’t care how clever you are. It just wants a path to ground. You give it the one you want, or it’ll find one you don’t.

The machine now stood before her, a two-metre tall idol of wood, copper, and plastic, dominating the small room. It looked utterly out of place against the faded floral wallpaper and worn-out furniture, a piece of a high-tech laboratory dropped into a forgotten corner of the past. It was time for a low-power test, just to check the primary circuits and the controller interface. She connected her laptop, the modern silver rectangle a stark contrast to the raw, analogue power of the coil. After a final visual inspection, she took a deep breath and clicked an icon on her screen. A low hum filled the room, the sound of capacitors charging, and a bank of LEDs on the base flickered to life. The air grew thick and heavy, and the faint, clean scent of ozone began to prickle at her nostrils. On the top of the coil, the metal toroid, which looked like a giant silver doughnut, began to fizz almost imperceptibly. A single, hair-thin purple spark, no more than a centimetre long, jumped from the metal to the air, then vanished. It worked. The beast was awake. A real, bone-deep smile finally touched Kira’s lips.

The smile was immediately erased by a series of sharp, frantic raps on the door. It wasn’t the polite tap of a neighbour or the casual knock of Leo. This was the sound of purpose, of a schedule being violated. It had to be Becca. With a sigh, Kira navigated the obstacle course of her own making and opened the door. The production assistant stood in the hallway, a clipboard clutched to her chest like a shield, her face a mask of harried stress. Her eyes flicked past Kira, widened, and then widened again until they looked like they might pop out of her head.

“Oh my God,” she breathed, her voice a horrified whisper. She took a step into the room, her gaze fixed on the towering form of Zeus. “What is that? What are you doing? You can’t… you can’t build *that* in here! Health and safety! The fire marshal! The building codes! Kira, the wiring in this block is pre-war!”

“It’s fine, Becca,” Kira said, her voice calm and even. The confidence the work had given her was a shield of her own. “I’m just doing a preliminary assembly and some low-power diagnostics. It’s not even fully plugged into the mains, I’m running it off an isolated power pack for the test.”

“Diagnostics? It’s a lightning machine!” Becca’s voice was rising in pitch, teetering on the edge of hysteria. “We have a tech rehearsal scheduled for tomorrow morning at the studio! That’s where you’re supposed to do this! With a fire crew on standby and a team of paramedics!” She waved her clipboard, which seemed to contain the sum of all her anxieties. “You’re meant to be at a costume fitting right now. They need to see how the fabric moves with your… act.”

Kira almost laughed at the image. Her ‘costume’ was a pair of sturdy black jeans, a plain shirt, and her steel-capped Blundstones. There wasn’t much to fit. “I can’t do a tech rehearsal on a machine that hasn’t been properly calibrated,” she explained, as if talking to a client who didn’t understand why their new extension needed a new fuse box. “This isn’t a guitar you just take out of a case and play. It needs to be tuned. The resonant frequency of this coil will be different in the open space of the studio than it is in here. I need a baseline. I need to test the control software. Doing it cold tomorrow would be a waste of everyone’s time. And it would be dangerous.”

Becca looked from Kira’s implacable face to the monstrous coil and back again. She was clearly out of her depth, her media-studies degree having failed to prepare her for a contestant who treated their flat like a high-voltage engineering lab. “The producers are not going to like this. You’re our wildcard act. Our big surprise. You’re not supposed to be a liability.”

The word stung. Liability. She was the one who understood the risks, the only one in the entire production who had any clue how to manage them. She was the expert, and they were treating her like a reckless child. “The only liability,” Kira said, her voice dropping, losing its patient edge and gaining a steely firmness, “would be me walking on that stage with an untested piece of equipment capable of drawing thirty kilowatts. I know what I’m doing, Becca. This is my job. Now, I’ll be at the studio tomorrow at eight a.m. sharp, and I’ll be ready for your tech rehearsal. But today, I need to work.” She looked past the frazzled assistant to the half-assembled machine. The beast wasn’t the coil. It was this whole circus. And for the first time, she felt ready to tame it.Becca’s mouth opened and closed a few times, like a fish gasping for air. The practiced authority she usually wore had been stripped away, leaving only raw panic. She looked from the coil to her clipboard and back again, as if the answer to handling a two-metre Tesla coil in a residential flat might be found in the neatly typed schedule for costume fittings. Her programming had failed. Kira’s calm, unshakeable confidence was a variable she couldn’t compute. Finally, she took a shaky step back into the hallway, pulling the door with her as if to contain the madness.

“Fine,” Becca said, her voice tight and thin. “Fine. But this is going in my report. All of it. The producers will hear about it, Kira. I’m telling you now, they won’t be happy. Eight a.m. Don’t be late.” The door clicked shut, and her footsteps retreated down the hall at a pace just shy of a full-blown run.

Kira let out a long, slow breath she hadn’t realised she was holding. The confrontation had left a tremor in her hands, a buzz of adrenaline that had nothing to do with electricity. She leaned her forehead against the cool wood of the door for a moment, then turned back to the room. The sight of Zeus, standing proud and powerful in the dim light, settled her instantly. Becca and her reports and her producers belonged to the world of grey drizzle and lukewarm showers. This, this was her world. She sat on the floor, crossed her legs, and pulled her laptop into her lap. The hardware was assembled; now came the soul. Her fingers began to move across the keyboard, lines of code appearing on the screen. Each command was a musical note, each subroutine a verse. This was the intricate choreography of the lightning, the digital brain that would tell the raw power where to go and what to sing. She was no longer just a sparky; she was a composer, a conductor, and the hours melted away into the rhythmic clicking of keys and the quiet, expectant hum of the machine.

The next morning arrived with a clamour of heavy footsteps and the rumble of a trolley on the pavement. The transport crew, two burly men who communicated mostly in grunts, surveyed the situation in the flat with weary professionalism. Getting Zeus out was a feat of engineering in itself, a slow, careful dance of tilting and turning through the narrow doorway and down the cramped stairwell. A few neighbours peeked out, their faces a mixture of curiosity and alarm at the strange, futuristic totem being wrestled from flat 3A. Kira supervised every move, her voice sharp and clear with instructions, ignoring the bewildered stares. Strapped into the back of the truck, sitting on an upturned crate beside her silent, monolithic companion, she felt a strange sense of peace. The city rolled past the open rear doors, a smear of red buses and grey buildings, but her focus was entirely on the precious cargo beside her, a secret weapon on its way to the front line.

Entering the Palladium through the rear stage door was like stepping into another dimension. The cramped, musty corridors of her apartment block gave way to a cavernous, subterranean world, a sprawling labyrinth of black-painted corridors, thick bundles of cables thick as pythons, and stacks of flight cases stencilled with the names of rock bands and West End shows. It smelled of dust, old timber, and stale coffee. The air buzzed with a low-level, purposeful energy as crew members in black t-shirts moved about their tasks. Kira saw the magician, looking surprisingly normal in jeans and a hoodie, quietly practising a series of coin manipulations at a folding table. Further on, the famous finalist dog, a scruffy Border Terrier named Patch, was joyfully chasing a squeaky toy thrown by his owner, oblivious to the high stakes of his own future. It was a strange tableau of the mundane underpinning the magnificent.

Her crew chief was a man named Alistair, a grizzled Scot with a formidable grey beard and eyes that had seen everything from malfunctioning pyrotechnics to diva tantrums. He watched with a critical, unimpressed air as Zeus was wheeled onto the vast, empty stage. He asked a few more pointed questions about frequency shielding and potential RF interference with the wireless microphone system. Kira answered each one concisely, professionally. A silent, mutual respect, as solid and unspoken as a good weld, began to form between the old stagehand and the young sparky. This was a language he understood. This wasn't talent; this was a trade.

Setting up on stage felt like building an altar in a cathedral. The empty crimson-velvet seats of the auditorium gaped at her like a thousand open mouths. High above, the crystal chandeliers were dark, and the judges’ desk sat forlornly under a white dust sheet. With the help of Alistair’s team, she positioned Zeus and the three smaller coils, running the thick, armoured power cables to the dedicated distribution box they’d provided. From the corner of her eye, she saw Becca appear in the wings, flanked by a man in a fire officer’s uniform holding a clipboard and an industrial-sized CO2 extinguisher. Becca’s arms were crossed so tightly she looked like she was trying to physically hold herself together. Kira ignored them all, focusing on the final connections, her movements precise and economical. She was in her element. The stage was just another work site.

“Alright, lightning lady,” Alistair’s voice boomed through the theatre’s PA system. “Stage is yours. Let’s see what this thing can do. Low power first.” Kira took her place at her laptop, set up on a small lectern. She could see the silhouettes of other contestants gathering in the wings, drawn by curiosity. Leo Vance was there, leaning against a stack of speakers, his usual swagger replaced by a look of intense focus. She took a breath, rolled her shoulders, and initiated the sequence. The three smaller coils hummed to life, and the first ethereal, crackling notes of ‘A Kind of Magic’ filled the theatre. The sound was purer here, the vast space allowing the notes to form and decay without echoing off nearby walls. It was working. The harmonies were crisp, the melody clear. A small smile touched her lips.

Now for the beast. As the song transitioned, she brought Zeus into the sequence. The low hum deepened into a thrum that vibrated through the floorboards, a tangible presence in the air.
