The first sign of trouble, as is so often the case, was almost comically small. It manifested in Mrs. Gable’s award-winning rockery, a tiered masterpiece of alpine succulents and carefully selected granite that was the undisputed jewel of Wragley Crescent. She had been out for her morning inspection, secateurs in one hand, a mug of lukewarm tea in the other, when she noticed the anomaly. A tiny white speck on the path, then another, and another, arranged in a bizarrely straight line leading from the bird bath to her prize Sempervivum ‘Plum Parfait’.

At first, she thought it was some new and aggressive form of fungal bloom, or perhaps the profligate droppings of a particularly unwell starling. Bending closer, her knees cracking like twigs in a frost, she saw what they really were. Mummies. Tiny, insectoid mummies. Each individual black ant, one of the many that marched their tireless trade routes across her patio, had been painstakingly encased in a tight wrapping of what looked like single-ply toilet paper. They were laid out like miniature sarcophagi in a pharaoh's procession, a silent, papery death march. Mrs. Gable stared, her mind struggling to categorise the sight. It wasn’t just mess. It wasn’t vandalism in the conventional sense. It was… meticulous. It was insane. And as she looked closer at the lead ant, she saw a minuscule dewdrop of what she could only assume was spittle, clinging to the tip of its papery shroud. A final, contemptuous indignity. She put her mug down on the flagstones, went inside, and dialled the police.

Detective Chief Inspector Alan Ley looked less like a senior investigating officer and more like a man who had come to read the gas meter. His suit, a shade of charcoal that had seen too many long nights, hung on his frame as if on a wire coat hanger he’d forgotten to remove. His hands, resting on the steering wheel of the unmarked Mondeo, were broad and calloused, the nails permanently underlined with a faint darkness that no amount of scrubbing could fully erase. They were an electrician’s hands, because for fifteen years before he’d joined the force, that’s precisely what Alan Ley had been.

His partner, Detective Sergeant Miles Kendrick, a man whose ambition was as sharp and well-pressed as his suit, fidgeted in the passenger seat. “Still don’t see why we’re responding to this one personally, sir. Public nuisance call. Ants. Uniforms could handle it.”

Ley didn’t take his eyes off the suburban street. It was a street of quiet, respectable anxieties: lawn envy, bin-day politics, the subtle twitch of a net curtain. “Uniforms logged it, Miles. They logged it as ‘unusual’. And when a PC with ten years on the beat logs something as unusual, I tend to think there’s a current flowing somewhere it shouldn’t be.” He switched off the ignition. “Besides, I had a call about a similar thing over by Markeaton Park yesterday. A line of them, right up the leg of a park bench. At the time I thought it was just kids.”

“And now?”

“Now,” Ley said, opening his door, “it’s a circuit. Let’s go see the fault.”

The scene in Mrs. Gable’s garden was exactly as described, only more unsettling in the flesh. The precision was what struck Ley first. This wasn’t the chaotic cruelty of a child with a magnifying glass. Each ant was wrapped with an unnerving neatness, the paper twisted at each end. It was the work of someone with time on their hands and a deep, simmering well of… something. Ley crouched down, his knees protesting. He ignored the forensics officer who was attempting to bag one of the papoosed insects without it disintegrating. He saw past the absurdity that had Kendrick sighing with thinly veiled impatience.

He saw the intent. The hatred. It was such a big word for such a small crime, but Ley knew it was the right one. This was a scream of impotent rage made manifest in the most pathetic way imaginable. You don’t do this to an ant, not with this level of detail, unless you want to do it to the world but don’t have the nerve. He could almost feel the energy behind it, a low, malicious hum.

“The paper is a cheap brand,” Ley murmured, more to himself than to Kendrick. “The kind you get in public toilets or office blocks. Thin. Breaks easily. Which makes the neatness of the wrapping even more… impressive.”

“Impressive, sir?” Kendrick’s disbelief was palpable. “It’s toilet paper. On ants.”

“It’s control, Miles,” Ley said, standing up and brushing dust from his trousers. “Absolute, total control over something that can’t fight back. It’s a power trip for someone who has no power. This person, our Kyle, as the press will inevitably call him, is testing the wiring. He’s sending a little surge through the system to see what trips.”

“Kyle?”

“It’s always a Kyle,” Ley said distractedly, his gaze sweeping the rooftops of the neighbouring houses. “Or a Kevin. Or a Barry. Someone with a grievance against existence itself.”

He was right about the press. By the end of the week, after three more incidents—one in a children’s playground, one along the disabled access ramp to a local library, and one, most brazenly, in a neat line on the pavement outside the Derby Council House—the name had stuck. “The Derby Dastard,” the local paper tried first, but it didn’t have the right ring of pathetic menace. It was a radio phone-in that birthed the moniker that caught the public imagination: “The Mummyfesto Killer.” It was hyperbolic, of course. The only victims were formicidae. But it spoke to the unnerving ritualism of it all.

For DCI Ley, it became an obsession. While his colleagues saw a city-wide prank, a minor nuisance to be swept away, Ley saw the blueprint for something worse. Hatred, in his experience, was like a faulty current. It started small, a flicker in the system, a blown fuse in a forgotten junction box. But if you didn’t find the source, if you didn’t ground it, it would build. It would look for a path of least resistance, and eventually, it would find one. And then the whole house would burn down.

He spent his evenings not at the pub, but in his small, tidy garage, a place that still smelled of solder and PVC insulation. On a vast whiteboard, where other detectives might have pinned suspects and timelines, Ley had drawn a schematic of Derby. The locations of the ant attacks were marked with red X’s. He drew lines between them, trying to find a pattern, a logical sequence, but there was none. It was random. Spasmodic. The twitching of a dying nerve.

He thought about the perpetrator. The kind of person who would not only conceive of such an act but would carry it out with such focus. They would need nimble fingers. Good eyesight. And a profound, bottomless reserve of spite. They’d be the sort of person who felt perpetually overlooked, a ghost in their own life. The raspberry-blowing, a detail reported by a single, near-deaf witness who’d thought it was a passing car backfiring softly, was the signature. It wasn’t just killing; it was mocking. The ultimate act of a troll. The sound was the punctuation mark on a sentence of pure contempt.

“What are you doing out here, Alan?” His wife, Sarah, stood at the door, a silhouette against the warm light of the kitchen.

“Thinking,” he said, pointing a dry-erase marker at the board. “He’s escalating.”

“By wrapping more ants?” She said it gently, without judgment. She’d been married to him long enough to know that his mind worked on a different voltage to most people’s.

“The locations,” Ley explained, tapping the X outside the Council House. “It’s getting bolder. More public. He’s moving from the quiet residential circuits to the main fuse board. He’s enjoying the attention. The articles, the radio shows. He’s feeding off it. But the energy has to go somewhere. This isn’t enough. It won’t be enough for long.”

He looked at the board, at the chaotic map of miniature cruelties. He felt a strange, unwilling connection to the man he was hunting. Ley spent his life fixing things, connecting wires, making sure the current went where it was supposed to. This man, this troll, spent his life doing the opposite. He was introducing chaos into the system. He was cutting wires, causing short circuits, just to watch the sparks fly. And Alan Ley, the electrician from Derby, knew with an unshakeable certainty that it was his job, and his alone, to rewire this city before the lights went out for good. He could feel the countdown. The low hum of malice was getting louder.The next morning, the incident room was a hive of grudging activity. The "Mummyfesto" case, as it was now officially designated on the whiteboard (to Kendrick's eternal chagrin), had been granted a small corner of the office, a sort of quarantine zone for what most of the squad considered a particularly elaborate joke. Ley ignored the snickers and the rolled eyes. He was hunched over a series of magnified photographs of the mummified ants, his electrician's eye for detail seeking out patterns the way it once sought out frayed wiring or a poorly earthed socket. Each wrap, each twist of paper, was a signature.

"Forensics report is in, sir," Kendrick announced, dropping a thin folder onto Ley's desk with a soft thud of disapproval. "The paper is, as you predicted, a generic single-ply. They've traced the pulp composition. It's consistent with the dispensers used in council buildings, public libraries, and several large-chain office supply warehouses. Basically, it could have come from anywhere in a fifty-mile radius." He paused, letting the futility of the information hang in the air. "The spittle on the lead ant from the Gable scene contained no usable DNA. And the 'raspberry' sound from the witness near the library? Audio analysis suggests it was most likely, and I quote, 'a small, damp, labial fricative'."

"A raspberry," Ley translated, not looking up from the photos. "He's mocking us. He's mocking everything." He finally pushed the photos aside and looked at his sergeant, his gaze steady. "I want a list, Miles. A list of all industrial cleaning contracts for council buildings and major office blocks in the city centre. I want to know who stocks their toilets. And I want you to start cross-referencing anyone who has filed a recent workplace grievance, been fired, or made redundant from those companies or the buildings they service. Start with janitorial staff, maintenance, low-level admin. People who are invisible."

Kendrick’s jaw tightened almost imperceptibly. It was a colossal, needle-in-a-haystack task, a wild goose chase predicated on his governor's eccentric theory about an angry toilet paper user. "Sir, with respect, the manpower..."

"The manpower is two of us, Miles, because nobody else will take this seriously. Which is exactly the point," Ley cut in, his voice dropping to a low, intense register. "This is what he is. He's the person you don't see. The person you walk past every day. The one who cleans up your mess, who stocks the loo roll, who fixes the flickering light in the corridor that’s been annoying you for weeks. And he knows you don't see him. Now he’s making damn sure we do. Get the list."

For two days, they waded through data. Ley took on the CCTV, sitting for hours in a darkened viewing suite, his eyes scanning jerky, monochrome footage from the various locations. He wasn't looking for a face. He was looking for a presence. He fast-forwarded through the streams of shoppers, commuters, and school children, his brain filtering out the normal rhythms of the city. He was searching for the anomaly, the piece of static on the screen. He found him, or a ghost of him, three times. A recurring figure in a drab, grey anorak, always on the periphery. At the library, a man loitering by the bike racks for twenty minutes, never once looking at the cameras. Outside the Council House, the same grey shape, seemingly waiting for a bus that never came. At the park, a figure sitting on a different bench, head down, utterly still. In every clip, the person was unremarkable to the point of being aggressively bland. They were human background noise.

The call came in on the third day, a Thursday. It wasn't a member of the public this time; it was a PCSO doing a routine patrol on the Silk Mill footbridge, the modern steel-and-glass structure that spanned the River Derwent. The officer sounded shaken, his voice tight with a mixture of revulsion and disbelief. Ley and Kendrick were there in fifteen minutes. The morning mist was still clinging to the water, and the bridge was cordoned off, eerily silent. In the very centre of the walkway, placed with geometric precision, was a dead starling. Its wings were folded tight to its body, its head tucked down. And it was encased, from the tip of its beak to its tail feathers, in the same cheap, single-ply toilet paper, wound with a terrible, patient neatness. At its feet, arranged in a perfect circle, lay a dozen mummified woodlice. It was a shrine. A grotesque offering.

Kendrick swore under his breath, his professional composure finally cracking. This was no longer just ants. The unspoken line had been crossed, from bizarre eccentricity into the realm of tangible cruelty. The press, who had been treating the case as a bit of light entertainment, would now descend with renewed, more serious fervour. This was different. This had teeth.

Ley crouched beside the pathetic, papery corpse. The wrapping was tighter here, more assured. The artist was honing his craft. He noted the way the paper was tucked into the crevices of the bird's folded wings, the care taken to cover every last millimeter of feather. This took time. This took dedication. And then he saw it, placed just in front of the starling's shrouded head, so small it was almost invisible against the grey of the bridge floor. A single, dry teabag. A cheap, no-frills brand, the paper tag still attached, fluttering in the slight breeze.

“He was here for a while,” Ley murmured, his breath fogging in the cool air. “He sat here, had a cup of tea, and did this.” He pointed to the teabag. “He’s leaving us props now. He knows we’re looking. He’s enjoying the dialogue.”

Back in the garage that night, the whiteboard looked different. It was no longer a map of pranks; it was the diary of a collapsing mind. Ley added the new location, drawing a thick red line from the Council House to the footbridge. From a public symbol of power to a place of transit, a place between places. It was a symbolic journey. He pinned a photo of the mummified starling to the board, then a close-up of the teabag. He remembered the lukewarm mug of tea in Mrs. Gable’s hand on that first morning. He remembered the spittle. It was all about a bitter, cold contempt. The teabag wasn’t just a prop; it was a parody of civility, a mockery of the quiet, domestic rituals of the people he despised.

Sarah found him there hours later, staring at the board, a cold mug of his own tea forgotten in his hand. “Did you find him?” she asked softly.

“No,” Ley said, his voice heavy. “But I can hear him. He’s tapping on the pipes. He’s telling us the pressure is building. The bird… the bird was him testing a new kind of fuse. A bigger one. He’s checking to see how much the system can take before something blows. And he’s not going to stop until it does.” Alan Ley, the man who built his life on order and safe currents, knew he was in a race against a man who was building a bomb out of toilet paper and rage. And the ticking was getting faster.The vindication, when it came, tasted like ash. The atmosphere in the morning briefing had undergone a stark phase transition. The smirks were gone, replaced by a tense, focused silence. The "Mummyfesto" whiteboard, now wheeled to the centre of the room, was no longer a monument to a governor’s eccentricity but the focal point of a major incident. Ley found himself with a small, dedicated team and the baleful eye of his Superintendent, who promised resources with one breath and demanded a swift resolution with the next. The mummified starling had done what Ley’s instincts could not: it had made the threat real.

Kendrick was a man transformed. The sharp suit was still present, but the ambition behind it was now channelled, honed to a fine point and aimed squarely at the task Ley had set him. He worked the phones with a relentless, quiet fury, his voice a low monotone of authority as he sliced through layers of corporate and municipal bureaucracy. By lunchtime, he approached Ley’s desk, a sheaf of printouts in his hand. “Sir, the teabag. It’s a brand called ‘CaterServe Essentials’. The city’s sole supplier is a company called Albion Office & Janitorial. They have the exclusive contract for all council-run properties, the university, the public libraries, and they supply the temp agencies who staff those locations. It’s the same procurement channel as the toilet paper.”

Ley nodded slowly, a grim satisfaction settling in his gut. It was exactly as he’d thought. A closed system. “They’re the tools of his trade, Miles. The very things he works with, the symbols of his invisibility. He’s taking the mundane artifacts of his overlooked life and turning them into weapons of psychological terror.” He looked back at the city map on the whiteboard. The X’s were a rash, an allergic reaction of a city to a poison in its veins. The perpetrator wasn't just picking locations at random; he was choosing places he knew, places where he was a ghost in the machine. He was haunting his own life.

The next call came not over the police radio, but directly to Ley's desk phone. The switchboard operator patched it through, her voice tight with alarm. It was Mrs. Gable. Her voice was unrecognisable from the crisp, indignant tones of their first encounter. Now it was a thin, ragged thread of panic, stretched to the point of breaking. "He's been here again," she whispered, the words catching in her throat. "He came to my house. To Marmaduke."

Ley was already on his feet, grabbing his keys. "Stay on the line with her," he snapped to Kendrick. "Get a patrol car there for support, lights off, no siren. Tell her not to touch anything. We're on our way." The drive back to Wragley Crescent was a blur of quiet streets, but for Ley, the city now felt charged with a humming, predatory energy. He had been so focused on the public circuit board, the Council House, the library... he'd missed the obvious. An electrician always checks the wiring back to the source. The fault had returned to its origin point.

Mrs. Gable was huddled in her doorway, wrapped in a thick cardigan despite the mildness of the day, her face pale and slack with shock. She pointed a trembling finger towards the patio. There, on the same flagstones where Ley had first examined the mummified ants, sat a large, ginger Persian cat. The cat, Marmaduke, seemed entirely unperturbed. He was washing a paw with languid indifference. But around his neck, he wore a collar. It was a necklace of the damned, a string of tiny, perfectly mummified mice, each one encased in the familiar white paper, their little papery tails sticking out at odd angles. They were strung together with a horrifying delicacy. To complete the tableau, a single, sodden teabag was perched on the cat’s head, clinging to the fur between his ears like a pathetic, brown fascinator.

Kendrick made a noise somewhere between a gasp and a groan, turning away to speak quietly and urgently into his radio. Ley, however, walked slowly forward and crouched down, his gaze forensic. The work was finer this time. The knots holding the mice together were more complex, the wrapping on each tiny body was tighter, more uniform. This was not a man losing control; this was a man gaining it. He was practising. Rehearsing. He was perfecting his technique on a canvas that was escalating in size and significance.

"He's never like this with strangers," Mrs. Gable stammered from the doorway. "Marmaduke. He runs from the postman. He hides from everyone. But he just... he just came to the back door, wearing... wearing *that*." She dissolved into quiet, helpless sobs.

Ley stood up, his eyes sweeping not the garden, but the houses that surrounded it, the windows that overlooked the scene. The perpetrator had been close. He had handled the cat, had taken the time to fit the grotesque collar around its neck. The cat's calmness wasn't a mystery; it was a message. The cat knew him. He was a local. Or he was someone who had a reason to be here, someone who blended in, someone who belonged. The ghost was starting to take on a physical shape. The attacks were no longer a broadcast to the city at large. This was targeted. This was personal. He was stepping out of the public shadows and onto a private doorstep. The faulty current wasn't just causing flickers anymore; it was actively trying to make a connection. And Alan Ley knew, with a certainty that chilled him to the bone, that the next time the connection was made, it would not be with an animal.The investigation pivoted on the purr of a cat. While the forensics team meticulously dismantled the macabre necklace and bagged the mummified mice, Ley knelt on the grass, stroking Marmaduke, who had sauntered over as if seeking praise for his ordeal. The cat wasn't traumatised; it was placid. It had allowed someone to spend a considerable amount of time in close proximity, handling it, fitting the grotesque collar. The message was chillingly clear: the perpetrator wasn't a phantom who had slipped in and out under the cover of darkness. He was a known quantity, at least to the cat. He belonged in this quiet suburban ecosystem.

"I want a full canvass of the Crescent, Miles," Ley said, his voice low and tight as he stood up. "Not the usual 'did you see anything?' rubbish. I want to know who lives here, who works here, who visits here. Gardeners, cleaners, carers, delivery drivers, sulky teenagers visiting their grandparents. I want the entire human flowchart for this street. Cross-reference it with the Albion list. Our man isn't just haunting the city; he has a home base. The attacks weren't random at all. They were an orbit, and this," he gestured to Mrs. Gable's pristine house, "is the centre of gravity."

Back at the station, the "Mummyfesto" corner of the office expanded, swallowing the adjacent desks. The atmosphere of mockery had fully evaporated, replaced by a grim, anxious energy. Wragley Crescent became their world. A large-scale map of the street was pinned to the board, and every house was annotated with the details gleaned from the canvassing teams and Kendrick’s data-mining. It became a tapestry of quiet lives: the retired colonel in number 12 who polished his car every Sunday; the young family in number 7 with the perpetually screaming toddler; the reclusive woman in number 21 who only ever received pharmaceutical deliveries.

Ley found himself drawn to the profile of number 15. An elderly woman, Elspeth Penwright, lived there alone. Frail, housebound. She had a son, Arthur, who visited three times a week to bring her shopping and help around the house. The patrol officer's notes described him as quiet, mid-forties, and "unmemorable." He kept his eyes down. He never made conversation. He blended into the floral wallpaper of the street's polite society.

Kendrick appeared at Ley’s elbow, holding a single sheet of paper as if it were a bomb component. His face was pale, his eyes bright with the thrill of the chase. "Sir. I've got him. Arthur Penwright. He was a temp cleaner, contracted out by an agency to Albion Janitorial. His final placement before termination five weeks ago was a six-month contract at the Derby Council House. He was let go for 'non-compliance with operational procedures' and 'a consistently negative disposition'. Before the Council House, he worked the cleaning shifts at the Markeaton Park public facilities and the central library. And his mother, Elspeth Penwright, lives at 15 Wragley Crescent. He's been on our map the whole time."

The pieces didn't just fall into place; they slammed together with the force of a completed circuit, sending a jolt of pure, cold clarity through Ley. It was perfect. The pathetic, impotent rage of a man fired from a job that already made him invisible. The choice of weapons: the cheap toilet paper and teabags he was forced to handle every day. The locations: the scenes of his own professional humiliation. And the origin point: Mrs. Gable’s rockery, the award-winning symbol of neighbourhood pride, a world away from the grubby reality of his own life, right on the street where he had to perform the dutiful role of a son. The cat, Marmaduke, would know him from his weekly visits. He would have watched Mrs. Gable preening over her succulents, the personification of a world that had no place for him.

"His grievance isn't against existence, Miles," Ley murmured, staring at the name on the paper. "It's specific. It's against a world that he believes has looked down on him, stepped over him, and finally, discarded him. The ants, the bird... he wasn't just testing the wiring. He was leaving his notice. This is his resignation from the human race."

He looked at the map, at the neat houses of Wragley Crescent, and felt a surge of urgency that was almost painful. The escalation was not a matter of if, but when. The progression from insect to bird to small mammal was a clear, horrifying trajectory. Arthur Penwright was done mocking symbols. He had targeted a living creature in a private home. The next step on that ladder was terrifyingly obvious. He was done sending surges through the system from a distance. Now he was preparing to make direct, physical contact with the source of his perceived misery.

"Get a surveillance team on his flat, discreetly," Ley commanded, his voice devoid of all its earlier weariness, now sharp and energised. "I want to know every move he makes. And get a protection detail assigned to Mrs. Gable, unmarked car, plain clothes. Tell them to be invisible. Our man hates being noticed, but he's an expert at noticing others. We are not going to let this house burn down." Alan Ley grabbed his jacket. The electrician was going to the fuse box. He just had to pray he wasn’t too late to stop the fire.The surveillance began with the grey misery of a Tuesday dawn. Arthur Penwright’s flat was on the third floor of a ten-year-old block named ‘The Willows’, a building with no willows and no character, just stacked beige boxes overlooking a supermarket car park. It was the architectural equivalent of a sigh. From an unmarked van smelling faintly of stale coffee and anxiety, Ley and Kendrick watched the blank window. For hours, nothing happened. Penwright was a black hole, his presence defined only by the absence of light and movement. The waiting was a specific kind of torture, a low-voltage current of suspense that frayed the nerves.

"He's just sitting in there," Kendrick muttered, lowering his binoculars. "What's he doing? Planning his next papier-mâché masterpiece?"

"He's charging," Ley replied, his gaze fixed on the building. "Or maybe he's admiring his work. The media coverage is all over it this morning. 'The Mummyfesto Manhunt'. They're calling him a 'pulp-fiction phantom'. He'll be reading every word. For the first time in his life, people are talking about him. The world is finally noticing the ghost." Ley couldn’t just watch the wire; he needed to see the appliance it was connected to. With the freshly signed warrant in his pocket, he decided it was time to look inside Penwright's junction box.

The door to Arthur Penwright's flat opened into a sterile, silent world. The first thing that hit Ley was the smell: a harsh chemical cocktail of bleach and furniture polish, with the dry, dusty undertone of a room where the windows were never opened. There was no clutter. Not a single piece of paper on any surface, not a coat slung over a chair. The threadbare sofa cushions were plumped and perfectly aligned. In the small kitchenette, the tins in the cupboard were arranged in alphabetical order, their labels facing outwards with military precision. It was the polar opposite of chaos; it was the oppressive, suffocating order of a man desperately fighting to keep a universe of rage contained within the box of his own life.

Kendrick ran a gloved finger along a dust-free bookshelf. "Clean, I'll give him that." Ley didn't answer. He was standing in front of a small desk. On it, a cheap laptop was closed. Next to it, there was a single, perfectly square stack of papers. Ley picked them up. They were letters. Dozens of them, all addressed to the editor of the Derby Telegraph, the City Council, and various local businesses. They were complaints, written in a cramped, spidery hand, about litter, about noise, about the inefficiency of the bus service, about a chipped paving stone outside a bakery. None of them had been sent. They were a library of silent, impotent fury. "It's all here, Miles," Ley said quietly. "Every grudge. Every perceived slight. He wasn't wrapping ants. He was wrapping these."

A sharp buzz from Kendrick’s radio cut through the silence of the flat. "Target is on the move. Male, grey anorak, proceeding east on a bicycle from the location. He's carrying a plastic shopping bag." Ley looked at Kendrick, his eyes suddenly sharp, the weary detective replaced by the focused electrician. "He's going to his mother's. It's Thursday. It's on his schedule." They were out of the flat in seconds, the scent of bleach and fury clinging to their clothes. The circuit was live, and the current was flowing towards Wragley Crescent.

From the end of the street, they watched him arrive. The quiet, respectable anxieties of the crescent seemed to hold their breath as Arthur Penwright dismounted from his bicycle. He looked exactly as the patrol officer had described him: unmemorable. A grey man in a street of vibrant gardens and polished front doors. He paused for a moment, his gaze drifting across the road towards number 9, towards Mrs. Gable’s house, where a uniformed officer, disguised poorly as a gardener, was pretending to deadhead roses. Penwright’s face was a blank mask, but Ley could almost feel the heat of his contempt radiating across the tarmac. Then, he turned, walked up the path to number 15, and let himself in.

It was in that moment, watching the front door of number 15 click shut, that the final schematic clicked into place in Ley's mind. The current wasn't flowing towards Mrs. Gable. That was a diversion, a spark to draw the eye. Mrs. Gable and her rockery were the symbol of the world he resented, the pretty, ordered facade he was never a part of. But the source of the fault, the true ground of his bitterness, wasn't the stranger across the street. It was the life he had to return to, week after week. The epicentre was inside number 15. The cat hadn't just been a message to the police; it had been a dress rehearsal on home turf.

"Miles, round the back," Ley commanded, his voice barely a whisper. They moved swiftly, silently, using the manicured hedges for cover. The air was thick with the scent of cut grass and impending confrontation. Ley peered through the glass of the back kitchen door. Elspeth Penwright sat at her small formica table, a frail woman with thin, white hair, watching her son unpack the shopping. Arthur moved with a slow, methodical rhythm, placing milk in the fridge, bread in the bin. And then he placed the last two items on the counter next to the sink. A fresh, thick roll of toilet paper from the multipack he'd just bought. And a pair of his mother’s gardening secateurs.

Ley didn't wait. He eased the handle of the unlocked door and stepped inside, Kendrick a solid shadow behind him. The quiet domestic scene froze. Elspeth Penwright looked up, startled. Arthur turned slowly, his face not showing surprise or fear, but a kind of profound, weary disappointment, as if they had arrived too early for a performance he had spent a lifetime preparing. The shopping bag at his feet was open, and inside, nestled amongst the groceries, Ley could see them: a dozen tiny, intricate mummies. Not ants or mice this time. They were spiders. Each one painstakingly wrapped, their eight papery legs splayed out in a grotesque starburst. He was going to make another necklace.

"It's over, Arthur," Ley said, his voice calm and even. He felt no triumph, only a vast, mechanical sadness. "The circuit's broken." Arthur looked from Ley to his mother, and then to the secateurs on the counter. His gaze was hollow. "She doesn't see," he whispered, his voice thin and reedy, the voice of a ghost.
