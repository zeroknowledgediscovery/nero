The wind was a bully. It came hurtling off the flatlands, a brute of a thing that smelled of damp earth and distant industry, and slammed into Jenna Kirby’s chest as if it had a personal grudge. Her thighs burned, a deep, familiar ache that was both agony and reassurance. This was real. The protesting squeak of her derailleur, the grit flicking up from the tarmac to pepper her shins, the low, bruised-purple sky hanging over St Helens like a heavy blanket. She gritted her teeth, lowered her head, and pushed on, the rhythm of the pedals a stubborn counterpoint to the gale. One-two, one-two. A metronome for the miserable.

Ahead, the white head of The Dream rose from the spoil heap of an old colliery, its eyes closed against the weather. Some people thought it was beautiful, a serene monument to a new future. To Jenna, it mostly looked cold. A stark white face in a world of brick and grey. She preferred the view from the other side of town, the way the stained-glass museum caught the rare afternoon sun, or the looming, promise-filled shape of the Totally Wicked Stadium, home of the Saints. That felt like home. This hill, this wind, this was just the work. This was the penance.

Her house was one in a long row of red-brick terraces, their faces scrubbed clean by a century of rain. The moment she swung the back gate open, the smell hit her: bacon. Her dad’s answer to everything. Bad day at the glassworks? Bacon butty. Saints win? Bacon butty to celebrate. Daughter comes home looking like a drowned rat after a thirty-mile battle with the elements? Definitely bacon.

“Is that you, our Jen?” Mick’s voice, thick with the local accent, boomed from the kitchen.

“Who else would it be?” she called back, her own voice tight with the cold. She wheeled her bike into its dedicated space in the narrow yard, covering the saddle with a plastic bag. It was a good bike, a Trek she’d saved for a year to buy, and it was her one non-negotiable luxury.

Inside, the house was warm and cramped. Mick stood at the stove, a slab of a man in a faded Saints training top, prodding sizzling rashers with a fork. He had a kind, tired face and hands that looked like they could crush bricks. “Thought you’d be back sooner. That wind’s a nasty piece of work.”

“Tell me about it.” Jenna peeled off her wet gloves, her fingers stiff and numb.

“Get a brew in you. You look frozen solid.”

A flick of movement in the living room doorway caught her eye. Chloe. Her step-sister was draped over the frame, one hand holding her phone, the other languidly pushing her perfect blonde hair from her face. She was sixteen, two years younger than Jenna, but carried herself with the bored authority of a queen in exile.

“You smell of wet road,” Chloe announced, her nose wrinkled in disgust. She was immaculate in a fluffy white jumper and leggings that had never been within a mile of a puddle.

“And you smell of fake tan and desperation,” Jenna muttered, just loud enough for her dad not to hear.

“What was that?” Chloe’s eyes narrowed.

“Nothing.” Jenna pushed past her towards the stairs. “Gonna have a shower.”

Up in her small attic room, the world of wind and gears and burning muscles fell away. This was her other space. Tucked away under the eaves, it was half cyclist’s den, half secret ballroom. One wall was a collage of Tour de France posters and maps of local cycling routes marked with her personal best times. The other was dominated by a large, slightly warped mirror she’d salvaged from a skip. In front of it, the floorboards were scuffed and marked with tape.

She showered quickly, the hot water a blessed relief, and changed not into jogging bottoms, but into black footless tights and a thin vest. She tied her damp brown hair back, flicked on a small speaker, and the room filled with the dramatic, soaring strings of a paso doble.

She took a deep breath. Stood before the mirror. Her reflection stared back, critical. The shoulders were too broad, a legacy of hauling herself up hills. The legs were pure cyclist’s muscle, powerful and thick, not the long, elegant limbs of a dancer. But when the music swelled, something else took over. She moved, at first hesitantly, then with a growing, fierce concentration. She stamped her foot, her arm slicing the air, her face set in a mask of determined pride. She wasn’t graceful, not naturally. Every move was learned, practiced, hammered into her muscle memory through sheer, bloody-minded repetition. This wasn't like cycling, where you fought against an external force like the wind. This was a fight against herself, against her own body's inclinations.

Her mum had loved to dance. Before she got sick, she and Jenna would watch the big competition shows on telly, her mum commentating on every fleckerl and spin. "It's about the story, Jen," she'd say, her eyes shining. "Every dance tells a story." After she was gone, the television stayed dark on Saturday nights. It was Mick who, a year later, had quietly handed her a leaflet for a ballroom class at the local community centre. "Might be… you know," was all he’d managed. She’d gone, hidden at the back, all teenage awkwardness and grief. And she had never stopped.

The paso doble finished. She was breathing hard, sweat trickling between her shoulder blades. She cued up a rumba. Slow, sensual, difficult. She moved her hips, trying to find the fluid, continuous motion the online tutorials always talked about. It felt clunky. Forced.

A sharp rap on the door made her jump. “Jenna! Tea’s ready! And Sandra’s here.”

Her dad’s voice. Sandra. Chloe’s mum. Jenna’s stomach tightened. She switched off the music, a sudden flush of shame heating her cheeks. She never practiced when Sandra was in the house. Sandra had a way of looking at her, a polite, pitying smile that made Jenna feel like a charity case.

Downstairs, the kitchen was even more crowded. Sandra, a woman who seemed to be constructed entirely of sharp angles and expensive perfume, was perched on a stool, nursing a glass of white wine. She was telling a story about a problem with a client at her solicitor's office, her voice crisp and clear. She stopped when Jenna entered, her eyes doing a quick, dismissive scan of Jenna's practice clothes.

“Jenna, dear. Still in your… athletic wear?”

“Just got out of the shower,” Jenna said, grabbing a plate. The bacon butty Mick had made for her was already starting to congeal.

“I was just telling your father,” Sandra said, turning to Mick but keeping her eyes on Jenna, “that Chloe’s been invited to the most wonderful work experience opportunity. At a modelling agency in Manchester. Isn’t that thrilling?”

“Oh, wow,” Chloe said, though she’d clearly known all about it. She preened, flicking her hair again. “It’s for their social media team. It’s a massive deal.”

“That’s great, love,” Mick said, trying to sound enthusiastic. He glanced at Jenna, a silent question in his eyes. Jenna just shrugged and took a huge bite of her sandwich. Chewing gave her an excuse not to speak.

The conversation flowed around her, all about Chloe’s potential, the clothes she’d need, the train fares. Jenna felt a familiar sense of invisibility creep over her. She was the solid, dependable one. The one who got muddy and fixed her own bike punctures. Chloe was the project, the one destined for brighter, shinier things than St Helens could offer.

Jenna finished her food, mumbled her thanks, and escaped back to her room. She didn’t put the music back on. The mood was broken. Instead, she sat on her bed and opened her laptop. She navigated to her email, her heart starting to do a nervous little trip-hammer beat against her ribs. She’d sent the video in two weeks ago. Her final audition tape for ‘Northern Stars of Dance’. It was a long shot, a ridiculous, hopeful, secret little dream. The prize was a professional contract and a place in a touring show. She’d filmed her cha-cha-cha right here, in this room, clearing all the furniture against the walls, her dad holding his phone and trying not to breathe too loudly.

There were a dozen emails. Junk, a notification from her cycling app, a reminder about a dental appointment. And then… one. From an address she didn’t recognise. The subject line was just three words. ‘Northern Stars of Dance’.

Her hand trembled as she moved the cursor. Her breath caught in her throat. This was it. The rejection. The ‘thank you for your application, but the standard was exceptionally high this year’ email. She clicked.

Her eyes scanned the text, words blurring and then snapping into focus. ‘Dear Jenna Kirby… thrilled to inform you… thoroughly impressed by your power and passion… a unique quality… would be delighted to offer you a place in the Grand Final…’

The Grand Final. Not a heat. Not a semi-final. The final. Live on regional television. In three weeks.

A sound escaped her lips, a choked gasp that was half-laugh, half-sob. She read the email again, and then a third time, the words glowing on the screen like a benediction. It was real. She’d done it. She, Jenna Kirby, the muddy cyclist from a terrace in St Helens, was going to be a star of the dance.

She jumped up, her body buzzing with an energy that had nothing to do with cycling and everything to do with pure, unadulterated joy. She wanted to scream, to run down the stairs and wave the email in all their faces. In Sandra’s perfectly made-up face. In Chloe’s bored, beautiful face. See? I have a dream too. And it’s not a silly little hobby. It’s real.

But she didn’t. The secrecy was a habit too ingrained. This victory felt too fragile, too precious to expose to their world just yet. She stood in front of her warped mirror, her reflection looking back at her. The broad shoulders, the powerful legs. For the first time, she didn't see a cyclist trying to be a dancer. She just saw a dancer. A finalist. And she smiled, a huge, brilliant, uncontainable smile. The story, she thought, was just beginning.The smile remained, a private sunbeam in the cramped attic room. For a full minute, Jenna just stood there, letting the feeling wash over her. It was a dizzying, terrifying cocktail of triumph and panic. The Grand Final. The words seemed absurd, like a line from a film. People like her didn't get into grand finals. They got rained on, they fixed punctures, they earned their blisters and their bacon butties. They didn't get emails that promised spotlights and television cameras. She sank onto the edge of her bed, the laptop still warm on her thighs, and scrolled down, forcing her brain to process the practicalities buried beneath the blaze of the headline.

The final would be held at the Empress Ballroom in Blackpool. Her breath hitched. The home of British ballroom. A hallowed ground she’d only ever seen on television, all gilt and velvet and polished parquet. She would be paired with a professional dancer, one of the show’s resident troupe, to be assigned in the coming days. They would have less than three weeks to choreograph and perfect two routines: a ballroom dance of her choice and a ‘Judges’ Challenge’ Latin number, the style of which would be revealed a week before the show. A flurry of attachments contained contracts, schedules, and nondisclosure agreements. The secrecy she’d practiced for years was now not just a habit, but a contractual obligation. The thought was strangely comforting, a shield against having to explain herself before she was ready.

The sound of Sandra’s high, brittle laugh drifted up through the floorboards, followed by Chloe’s softer giggle. The sound pricked Jenna’s balloon of joy, letting a little of the air hiss out. Down there was the real world, the one where Chloe’s potential was a public project and her own ambitions were a weird hobby conducted in an attic. How could she possibly explain this? How could she walk into that kitchen and announce she needed to go to Blackpool to dance on television, when they all saw her as the girl who smelled of wet road and preferred bikes to boys? She couldn't. Not yet. She saved the email to a folder named ‘Bike Maintenance’, closed the laptop, and took a deep, steadying breath, schooling her features back into their usual neutral mask.

Later, fetching a glass of water, she found the kitchen dynamic unchanged. Sandra and Chloe were now huddled over a glossy magazine, pointing at hairstyles. Mick was at the sink, his broad back to the room, scrubbing at the frying pan with a quiet, methodical force. He was the only one who looked up as she came in, his brow furrowed with a familiar, gentle concern.
“You alright, Jen? You’ve been quiet.”
“Fine, Dad. Just tired. It was a long ride,” she said, the lie tasting like ash in her mouth. She wanted to tell him. More than anyone, she wanted to share the news with the man who had handed her that first leaflet, who had filmed her audition video with a trembling hand, who celebrated every small victory in her life with sizzling bacon. But she saw Sandra’s head tilt, her attention snagged by their conversation, and the words died in her throat.
“Right,” Mick said, turning back to the sink. He knew there was more, but he also knew not to push. He just scrubbed a little harder at a stubborn piece of grease.

Back in the sanctuary of her room, the full weight of the challenge began to settle in. Three weeks. It was an impossible timeline. Her rivals would be dancers who had spent their entire lives in studios, with private coaches and endless reserves of grace. She had an attic room, a warped mirror, and YouTube tutorials. She looked from the scuffed floorboards to the wall of cycling maps. The winding route up to Billinge Hill, marked in red ink. The brutal, leg-shredding climb past the Carr Mill Dam. For years, she had seen the bike as the work and the dancing as the escape. Now, looking at the contour lines on the map, she saw something else. She saw the source of her power. The judges hadn't praised her elegance or her technique; they had praised her 'power and passion'. That power wasn't born in a dance studio. It was forged out here, on the rain-lashed tarmac of St Helens, in a battle against the wind.

A new, fierce resolve solidified in her chest. This wasn't a fight against herself anymore. It was a fight for herself. The cycling wasn’t penance; it was training. Every hill she conquered was another round of stamina for a jive. Every long, grinding mile was building the endurance for a three-minute quickstep under blazing studio lights. Her two worlds, the gritty, real world of the cyclist and the secret, spangled world of the dancer, weren't in opposition. They were about to collide.

She walked to the window, pushing aside the thin curtain. The streetlights cast orange pools on the wet pavement. In the distance, the floodlights of the stadium cut through the dark, a beacon of local pride. For the first time, looking out at the town, she didn’t feel trapped by it. She felt fuelled by it. This place, with its wind, its rain, and its red-brick resilience, was in her blood. It was in her legs. And she was going to take it with her, all the way to the floor of the Empress Ballroom. The story, her mum had said, was everything. Well, her story was about to get a whole lot more interesting.The next morning, the alarm went off before the sun was even a rumour in the bruised sky. Usually, Jenna would hit snooze, bargaining for another ten minutes of warmth. But today, she was out of bed before the second beep, her movements crisp with purpose. She didn't dress for a ride; she dressed for training. Every piece of kit was chosen for efficiency, not comfort. As she wheeled her Trek out of the yard, the air was still and cold, the wind having sulked off sometime in the night. Mick was already up, the low murmur of the radio coming from the kitchen, but she slipped out without a word, needing the solitude.

The road was her gymnasium. The familiar thirty-mile loop wasn't a journey to be endured anymore, it was a sequence of exercises. The long, flat stretch alongside the canal became a drill for consistent cadence and controlled breathing, her mind focused on a steady 4/4 rhythm, like a foxtrot. The steep, winding climb up to Billinge, the one that always had her cursing under her breath, became an explosive interval session. She attacked it not with grim endurance, but with bursts of power, imagining the sharp, staccato stamps of a paso doble. Her legs burned with the effort, but the pain was different now. It wasn't the ache of penance; it was the satisfying fire of forging a weapon. She was a different person on the bike this morning, no longer a girl cycling away from her life, but a dancer hurtling towards it.

When she got home, breathless and drenched in sweat, Mick was waiting with the kettle already on. He watched her go through the familiar ritual of cleaning her bike chain, his expression unreadable. He’d noticed the change, the manic energy humming just beneath her skin. He didn't comment on the fact she’d left without her usual bacon butty being put on, but he poured her a mug of tea and left it on the counter, a silent offering. Jenna accepted it with a grateful nod, her mind already racing upstairs to her laptop, to the folder deceptively labelled ‘Bike Maintenance’. The joy from last night had now been joined by a thrumming, high-wire anxiety.

Another email had arrived overnight. The subject was ‘Your Professional Partner’. Her hands felt clumsy and thick as she clicked it open. A headshot appeared on the screen, a man who was the polar opposite of the slick, grinning dancers she’d seen on TV. He looked to be in his late thirties, with intense, dark eyes that seemed to see right through the camera lens. His dark hair was cropped short, and his face was all sharp angles and unforgiving lines, the hint of a scowl permanently etched around his mouth. The name below the picture was Leo Belikov. The biography was brief and brutal: three-time undefeated British National Latin Champion, known for his "uncompromising choreography and ferocious competitive spirit." He had a reputation. He was a genius, a tyrant, a star-maker, and a career-breaker. He was, the email stated, thrilled to be her partner. Jenna felt a jolt that was equal parts terror and elation. They hadn't given her the safe option. They’d given her a weapon of her own.

The logistics were laid out with military precision. Their first training session was in two days, at a private studio in Warrington, a neutral midpoint. She was to tell no one. Her phone buzzed with a text from an unknown number: *Jenna. Be on time. I don't wait. L.* The sheer arrogance of it almost made her laugh. It was so far removed from her world of 'please' and 'thank you' and waiting for the kettle to boil. A plan, a lie, began to form in her head, feeling both flimsy and audacious. She rehearsed it in the shower, repeating the words until they felt less like a falsehood and more like an alternate version of the truth.

That evening, as Sandra and Chloe discussed the merits of a particular Instagram filter, Jenna took a deep breath and walked into the centre of the living room, a place she usually tried to make herself invisible. She held a printout of a cycling article she’d found online about high-altitude training simulation.
“Dad,” she said, her voice louder than usual, cutting through their conversation. Mick looked up, startled. Sandra and Chloe glanced over, annoyed at the interruption. “I’ve been thinking. I’m hitting a plateau with my times. A few of the riders from the club are going on an intensive training week. It’s… it’s a special camp. Near the Peak District.”
Mick took the paper she offered, his brow furrowing as he tried to read the technical jargon. “A training camp? Now? It’s a bit sudden, isn’t it, love?”
“The opportunity just came up,” Jenna pushed on, her heart hammering. “It’s a big deal. They have proper coaches, performance analysis… all the stuff I can’t do on my own. It’s for three weeks. I’d have to stay over some nights.”
Sandra let out a small, disbelieving scoff. “A training camp? For cycling? How utterly… rustic. What does that even involve? Riding up more hills?”
“It’s about technique, Sandra,” Jenna said, her eyes fixed on her dad. “Building core strength. Endurance.” She was using words Leo might use. Words that felt solid and real.
Mick looked from the paper to his daughter's determined face. He saw the fire in her eyes, the same focused intensity she had when she was fighting the last mile home against a headwind. He didn’t understand the technicalities, and some part of him sensed there was a piece of the story he was missing, but he understood the drive. He recognised the ambition, a quality he worried had been scrubbed out of her by life.
“Well,” he said slowly, handing the paper back. “If you think it’ll help you. We’ll manage.”
“It will,” Jenna said, the relief so potent it almost buckled her knees. “It’s exactly what I need.”
Across the room, Chloe rolled her eyes and went back to her phone, utterly uninterested in Jenna’s muddy pursuits. Sandra gave a tight, dismissive smile and turned back to Chloe, resuming their conversation as if Jenna had never spoken. The invisibility cloak had settled back over her, but for once, Jenna was grateful for it. She had her cover. She had her secret. And in two days, she was going to meet the tyrant.The journey to Warrington was a masterpiece of clandestine travel. Jenna told Mick she was getting a lift with "one of the lads from the club," a vague figure named Dave who conveniently didn't have a surname. In reality, she cycled to the station, locked her Trek in a public rack with two different D-locks, and slunk onto the slow train, her dance shoes hidden in a battered rucksack along with a water bottle and a crushed cereal bar. The studio was on an anonymous industrial estate, a featureless grey cube that looked like it could house a company selling plastic widgets. The disconnect between its bland exterior and the world she was about to enter made her stomach clench.

Inside, the air smelled of lemon polish and faint, clean sweat. It was a single, vast room, one wall a floor-to-ceiling mirror, the other painted a stark, unforgiving white. The floor was a pale, polished wood that seemed to stretch for miles. There was no clutter, no personality, no scuffed floorboards or posters of cyclists. It was a laboratory for movement, and she felt like a specimen under a microscope. She checked her phone. 10:58. She was two minutes early. She put her rucksack down in a corner, feeling the need to make herself as small as possible, and waited, her reflection watching her, a tense, hunched figure in worn leggings and a faded vest.

The door opened at precisely 11:00. Leo Belikov was more intimidating in person. He was shorter than she’d expected, but he seemed to take up more space, his body a compact bundle of wire and muscle under a tight black t-shirt and tracksuit bottoms. He didn't look at her at first, his attention fixed on a tablet in his hand as he strode to the centre of the room and placed it on the floor. He tapped the screen, and the cavernous space filled with the low, expectant thrum of a bass line. Only then did his dark eyes lift and fix on her. The look wasn't welcoming or hostile; it was brutally analytical, like a mechanic assessing a faulty engine.

"Jenna Kirby," he said. It wasn’t a question. His voice was low, with a faint, unplaceable European accent that clipped the edges of his words. He circled her slowly, his gaze missing nothing, from her damp, scraped-back hair to the thick, calloused skin on her palms. "They told me you were a cyclist. They were not exaggerating." He stopped in front of her. "Your posture is appalling. Your shoulders are rolled forward from years of hunching over handlebars. Your centre of gravity is all wrong."

Jenna’s jaw tightened. She felt a hot flush of anger and humiliation. She’d fought headwinds that would have sent him flying, hauled herself up hills that would have snapped his delicate dancer’s legs, and he was dismissing her entire physical existence in under a minute. "I have good stamina," she said, the words coming out tight and defensive.

A flicker of something—amusement, maybe contempt—crossed his face. "Stamina is useless without technique. An engine is useless without a chassis. Your audition tape… the cha-cha. It had energy. Like a dog chasing a car. All power, no control. Why are you here, Jenna Kirby? What do you think you are doing in a ballroom competition?"

The directness of the question stole her breath. All her carefully constructed lies and evasions felt flimsy and ridiculous in the face of his searing honesty. She thought of her mum, of the Saturday nights watching television, of the secret ache in her heart that only dancing seemed to soothe. But she couldn't say any of that to him. He would shred it to pieces. So she fell back on the only other truth she knew. "I want to win," she said, her voice shaking slightly but her gaze firm.

He stared at her for a long moment, his head tilted. "Good. That is the only correct answer." He walked back to his tablet. "We have less than three weeks. Your ballroom choice is the waltz, because it will force you to fix your posture. The Judges' Challenge, when it comes, we will deal with. For now, I need to see the raw material. Show me your rumba."

Of course. The rumba. Her nemesis. The dance of slow, sensual connection that felt like trying to write poetry with a hammer. She felt a wave of pure panic, but his unwavering stare allowed for no refusal. It was a command. She took a deep breath, walked to the middle of the floor, and nodded. The music that filled the room was slow, sinuous, and mocking. She moved, trying to remember the tutorials, trying to find the hip action, the fluidity. Under his gaze, every flaw felt magnified. Her movements were jerky, her legs felt like lead, her attempt at sensuality felt like a parody. She was Jenna Kirby, from the red-brick terrace, smelling of wet road, and she was trying to be a Latin queen in a sterile white box. The absurdity of it all made her want to stop, to just walk out. But she didn't. She gritted her teeth, the same way she did on a hill, lowered her chin, and danced. She poured all her frustration, her secret hope, and her bloody-minded stubbornness into the clumsy, powerful movements.

He let her dance for a full minute, a minute that stretched into an eternity, before abruptly cutting the music. The silence crashed down, heavy and judgmental. She stood, breathing hard, her heart pounding, waiting for the inevitable dismissal. "That was," he said, his voice flat, "the most disconnected, mechanical, and agricultural rumba I have ever witnessed. Your hips move like a tractor's axle. You have no feeling for the music. You are fighting it, and you are fighting yourself." He walked towards her, and for a second she thought he was going to send her home. He stopped a foot away. "But," he said, and the word hung in the air, "you did not stop. And you did not look away. Your power comes from your legs, but your story comes from your spine. You hold it straight, even when you are afraid." He looked her dead in the eye. "It is a mess. But I can work with a mess. I cannot work with 'pretty' or 'afraid'. Be here tomorrow. Six a.m. We start with the waltz. And we will not stop until you learn how to stand."The next morning, the six a.m. alarm was a physical assault. Every muscle in Jenna’s body screamed in protest. The dull, familiar ache from cycling was a gentle lullaby compared to this new, sharp agony. Her shoulders, back, and arms felt like they had been systematically beaten. The industrial estate was shrouded in a cold, grey mist as she limped from the station, and when she pushed open the studio door, Leo was already there, stretching in the centre of the floor with the silent, fluid grace of a panther. He didn't acknowledge her arrival with a word, merely straightened up as she shed her jacket, his eyes already assessing, already finding fault.

"Frame," was the only word he said. He moved to stand in front of her, raising his arms. "Show me what you remember." Jenna hesitantly lifted her own, trying to replicate the position he had forced her into yesterday. Her left arm out, her right hand poised to rest on his shoulder. Before she had even settled, his hands were on her, cold and firm. One pressed between her shoulder blades, forcing them back and down. The other twisted her left elbow upwards, a sharp, burning sensation shooting through her joint. "Higher. Your arm is not a dying swan, it is a steel bar. You are presenting your partner to the world. You are not apologising for her."

For two solid hours, they didn't take a single step. The entire session was a static, excruciating exercise in posture. Leo prowled around her, making minute, painful adjustments. He tucked a finger under her chin to lift her head, tapped her ribs to remind her to engage her core, pressed down on her shoulders until she thought her spine would snap. Her muscles trembled with the sheer effort of holding the unnatural position. The powerful shoulders she had built on the hills of St Helens were now her greatest enemy, constantly wanting to roll forward, to hunch against the strain. The world shrank to the space between them, to the sound of her own ragged breathing and his low, constant stream of corrections.

The train ride home was a blur of detached misery. She huddled in a corner seat, her body thrumming with a pain so deep it felt cellular. Outside the window, the familiar landscape of terraced houses and old factory chimneys slid past, a world she no longer felt entirely a part of. She felt like a spy, moving between two realities, belonging to neither. When she finally unlocked her bike, swinging her leg over the frame felt like an act of supreme effort. The ride home, usually a mental release, was just more work, her aching back protesting every bump in the tarmac. Her lie to her dad about the 'training camp' had become a profound truth; this was the most intense training of her life, a brutal boot camp for the body and soul.

Back in the house, the smell was not of bacon, but of lamb chops and mint sauce. Mick had saved her a plate, and it sat on the counter under a piece of foil. He was watching a quiz show, the volume low. "Tough day at the camp?" he asked as she slumped onto a kitchen stool, her exhaustion too profound to hide. She just nodded, too tired to form words. He looked at the way she held herself, straighter than he’d ever seen her, even as she winced with every small movement. He saw the new, raw determination in her eyes, shadowed by a bone-deep weariness. His brow was a mess of worried lines, but he didn't pry, just pushed the plate towards her. "Get that in you. You look like you've been wrestling a bear."

The next few days bled into a gruelling, monotonous rhythm. Wake in the dark. Train to Warrington. Suffer Leo’s punishing perfectionism for hours that felt like weeks. Suffer the journey home. Cycle through the pain. Collapse into bed. The lie to her family became her shield. Her exhaustion was explained away by 'endurance drills', the new aches in her back and arms by 'core conditioning'. Chloe barely registered her presence, absorbed in her own drama of choosing an outfit for her work experience induction. For her, Jenna’s fatigue was just another boring part of the scenery, like the damp weather or the squeak of the back gate. In a way, their self-absorption was a gift. It allowed Jenna to move through the house like a ghost, her secret life wrapped tightly around her.

Leo was relentless. After mastering the static frame, he made her walk. Just walk, back and forth across the polished floor, for an entire session. He taught her how to place her heel first, how to use her standing leg, how to glide instead of clomp. He followed behind her, a hawk shadowing its prey, his voice a constant critique. "You walk like a farmer. You are on a ballroom floor, not a ploughed field." Then came the steps. The simple box step of the waltz became a geometric puzzle she couldn't solve. Her feet, so sure and powerful on the pedals of her bike, were clumsy, disobedient things on the dance floor. The natural turn, a foundational move, felt like a complex piece of engineering designed to make her fail.

On the fifth day, she broke. They were attempting a sequence of turns, and she stumbled, treading on his foot for what felt like the hundredth time. The music swelled, a beautiful, soaring melody that seemed to mock her clumsiness. He stopped, his body rigid with contained frustration. The road ahead was long and punishing, but for the first time since leaving St Helens, she felt sure she was heading in the right direction.The tour found its rhythm, a relentless, grinding beat that had nothing to do with music. It was the rhythm of coach wheels on a motorway, the identical beeps of hotel key cards, the muffled thump of a bassline from a distant stage. The cities bled into one another, a blur of grey skies and red-brick backstreets that looked disconcertingly like home but weren't. Manchester, Leeds, Newcastle. Each new theatre was a slight variation on a theme, each anonymous hotel room a sterile box for sleeping and studying. Jenna learned that the life of a touring dancer was a profoundly unglamorous exercise in logistics and pain management. She learned the best brand of instant porridge that could be made with a hotel kettle, the quickest way to find a launderette, and the precise combination of ibuprofen and deep heat rub that could get her legs functioning in the morning.

Her notebooks became her most treasured possession, their pages filled with a secret language only she could understand. Scrawled diagrams that looked more like engineering blueprints than dance notes detailed the physics of every routine. ‘Chaîné turn = maintain angular momentum, spot like a gyroscope.’ ‘Grand jeté = convert horizontal velocity to vertical lift, max power on takeoff.’ While other dancers possessed an innate kinesthetic intelligence, Jenna had to build it from the ground up, constructing a scaffold of logic and mechanics around every eight-count. After each show, she would sit on the floor of her hotel room, the glow of her laptop illuminating her tired face, and break down the day’s work, her pen moving with the methodical scratch of a student cramming for a final exam.

Her friendship with Ben was a small, warm pocket of sanity in the relentless grind. He was a tour veteran, his body a roadmap of old injuries and his mind a library of unwritten rules. He taught her how to sleep on the tour bus without waking up with a crick in her neck, which physiotherapists on their approved list were miracle workers and which were butchers, and most importantly, how to ration her energy. “It’s a marathon, not a sprint, Kirby,” he told her one afternoon as they nursed coffees in a drafty cafe in Leeds. “You can’t go at it a hundred percent every day, in every rehearsal. You’ll burn out or break. Learn to mark, to save the full-out performance for the paying crowd. Leo will kill you if he catches you, but he can’t be everywhere at once.” He was her translator, her guide, and his easy, uncomplicated friendship was the closest thing she had to the feeling of her dad’s kitchen.

The weekly phone calls home were both an anchor and a source of strange dislocation. Her dad’s questions were always the same, practical and loving. “Are you eating properly? It’s not all fancy salads, is it?” She would reassure him, painting a picture of a well-fed, well-rested life that was a gentle fiction, the pang of homesickness for a simple bacon butty surprisingly sharp. Her conversations with Chloe were another matter entirely. Her stepsister had embraced her self-appointed role as social media manager with a terrifying zeal. “Posted that rehearsal clip Ben sent me,” she’d announce via text. “Great engagement. Added a trending sound. The hashtag #SaintOfTheWaltz is still getting traction. We need to do a Q&A next. Keep it authentic but aspirational.” Jenna would stare at her own exhausted, un-aspirational face in the hotel mirror and feel a profound disconnect from the powerful, inspiring ‘brand’ that was being constructed in St Helens.

As the tour moved into its third week, Leo announced they would be adding a new transitional piece to the second act, a short, aggressive number designed to bridge two slower pieces. The music was a brutalist cacophony of drum solos and industrial noise. The choreography was fragmented, powerful, and utterly devoid of grace. In the centre was a brief, eight-count solo for a female dancer, a moment of contained, explosive power. Leo drilled the corps on the surrounding choreography, but his eyes kept scanning the dancers, looking for the right person for the solo. Sophie tried it, but her movements, though technically perfect, were too light, too balletic. She looked like a bird trying to impersonate a bulldozer. “No,” Leo snapped. “I don’t want a 'performance'. I need weight. I need an anchor.” His eyes fell on Eliza, who immediately stepped forward, her body tense with ambition. She executed the eight counts with a ferocious, cutting precision. It was technically flawless, but it was all sharp lines and anger, brittle and cold. Leo watched her, his expression unreadable, and shook his head almost imperceptibly. “Technique is not the same as power,” he said, his voice flat with disappointment.

A frustrated silence fell over the room. Leo paced in front of the mirrors, his mind clearly working. He seemed to have dismissed the idea. Then his gaze swept the back row and landed, with unnerving focus, on Jenna. She flinched, instinctively trying to make herself smaller. “Kirby,” he commanded. The entire company turned to look at her. “Front and centre.” Her heart hammered against her ribs as she walked the long, lonely path to the front of the room, feeling the heat of Eliza’s furious stare on her back. “Don't dance,” Leo ordered. “Show me a deadlift into a press. Not a dance move. The gym move.” Confused, but obedient, Jenna planted her feet, bent her knees, and mimed the movement, her body remembering the raw, functional strength from a thousand bike lifts and heavy gear bags. Her form was solid, grounded, powerful. “Again,” he said. “Focus on the drive from the floor.” She did it again, concentrating on the explosive power generated by her legs and glutes. He watched her for a long, silent moment. “You,” he said, the word a final judgment. “You do it. The eight-count from the drum solo. From the top.”

The silence in the room was no longer just respectful; it was thick with resentment and shock. Jenna stood, exposed and terrified, in the space Eliza had just vacated. She could feel the collective disbelief of the company. She wasn’t one of them. She was the TV girl, the cyclist, the amateur. She took a deep breath, trying to ignore the daggers being stared into her back. The drum solo began its brutal, percussive assault. She didn’t think of the steps as dance. She thought of them as the sequence of movements from her gym-move mime. The first count was the deadlift, a deep, powerful drive from her legs. The next counts were the press, her arms slicing the air not with grace but with force. The final movements were a controlled explosion, a spinning drop to the floor that was less a dance move and more a controlled demolition. She landed the final pose, crouched low to the ground like a sprinter in the blocks, her breath held tight in her chest. For a moment, no one moved. Then Leo gave a single, sharp nod. “That is the anchor,” he said to the room at large. “That is the weight. Kirby has the solo. Learn from it. Again, from the top.” The rehearsal moved on, but the atmosphere in the room had been irrevocably poisoned, and Jenna knew that her small victory had just painted a very large target on her back.The moment Leo’s command to repeat the number echoed through the mill, a chilling professionalism descended on the room. The company moved back to their starting positions with a fluid, resentful grace. They danced with a new, razor-sharp precision, every movement a silent, collective rebuke. No one looked at Jenna, yet she felt the weight of every gaze. She was a foreign body, a disruption to the established order, and they were closing ranks around her, their synchronised perfection a wall she could not penetrate. The rehearsal ended, and the room dissolved into its usual cliques with unnatural speed. Bags were packed, quiet conversations resumed, and a wave of dancers flowed out of the studio, leaving Jenna standing alone in a vast, echoing silence. Ben was the only one who lingered, his cheerful face clouded with concern as he watched her slowly pack her own bag. He didn’t say anything until they were out in the chilly Manchester street.

“Well,” he said, his breath pluming in the air. “You’ve officially kicked the hornet’s nest.” He glanced back at the dark windows of the mill. “It’s not just Eliza anymore, Jen. You’ve jumped the queue. In their eyes, you’re a tourist who’s just been handed the keys to the city, while they’ve been paying rent here their whole lives.” He kicked at a loose stone on the pavement, his expression grim. “They’re professionals. They won’t mess up the show, but they’ll make your life a misery in a thousand small ways you can’t complain about. Watch your back. Seriously.” The warning, delivered with his usual blunt kindness, was more terrifying than any of Eliza’s open hostility. He was describing a war to be fought in whispers and shadows, a conflict where the rules were unwritten and she was the only one who didn't know them.

That night, the generic hotel room felt less like a sanctuary and more like a prison cell. The loneliness was a physical presence, a heavy blanket that smothered the air. For a fleeting, desperate moment, she thought about calling her dad, wanting to hear the simple, uncomplicated reassurance of his voice. But how could she explain this intricate, venomous world? How could she describe the feeling of being hated for being chosen? It would only worry him, a problem he couldn't solve with a bacon butty or a quiet word of support. Instead, she opened her notebook. It was her only true ally, the one place she could be completely honest. She didn't write about her fear or her isolation. She drew. With the fierce concentration of a bomb disposal expert, she deconstructed the eight-count solo. She broke each movement down into vectors of force, angles of torque, points of balance. She wasn't just learning the choreography; she was reverse-engineering it, building it from the ground up in her own language of physics and mechanics. The notebook pages filled with furious, precise scribbles, a testament in ink and paper that this solo was hers not by luck, but by sheer, bloody-minded work.

The next few days were an education in psychological warfare. The hostility was no longer just a general atmosphere; it became specific, personal, and deniable. During a fast-paced group number, Eliza’s spin was a fraction of a second late, her arm extending just as Jenna passed, forcing her to stumble to avoid a collision. It was a tiny error, a professional misjudgment that could happen to anyone, but Jenna saw the flicker of satisfaction in Eliza’s eyes as she offered a cold, insincere, “So sorry, darling.” Later, during a partner-work rehearsal, Jenna’s usual partner, Tom, was suddenly switched out for a senior dancer who held her with a rigid, uncommunicative frame, making every lift and turn a struggle. Leo, overseeing a different section, didn't notice. The other dancers saw it all, their silence a form of quiet, collective punishment. Jenna felt like she was trying to run through water, every step meeting a thick, invisible resistance.

The tour moved on to Belfast. The night of the first show there, the night the new number was set to debut, the backstage air crackled with a malevolent energy. Jenna went to her designated spot on the costume rack, her hands trembling slightly as she reached for her shoes for the pre-show warm-up. They weren’t there. Her blood ran cold. She checked the bag again, then the floor around it. Nothing. A frantic, cold panic began to rise in her throat. This was not an accident. This was a deliberate act, a piece of petty, cruel sabotage designed to break her just minutes before her most exposed moment on stage. She started a frantic, quiet search, her breath catching in her chest, trying not to draw attention to her crisis. The five-minute call was announced over the PA system, the voice echoing like a death knell.

Ben found her, her face pale, frantically searching in a darkened corner. “What is it?” he hissed, seeing the panic in her eyes. “My shoes. They’re gone,” she whispered, the words choked. A grim understanding hardened his face. He didn’t waste time with questions. “Okay, think. Where’s the last place a cleaner would look?” he muttered, his mind working quickly. He started pulling aside stacked props and unused cable boxes. With less than two minutes to stage time, he let out a low curse of triumph. “Here.” Tucked behind a heavy flight case filled with spare sound equipment, almost invisible in the gloom, were her shoes. They were unharmed, just hidden. The point wasn't to stop her from dancing, but to send her on stage with her mind shattered and her confidence in ruins. Adrenaline, hot and sharp, surged through Jenna, burning away the cold panic and replacing it with a pure, white-hot rage.

She shoved her feet into the shoes, her hands lacing them with furious speed, and made it to the wings just as the lights went down for their entrance. She didn't feel nervous anymore. She felt lethal. She stood in the darkness, her breathing controlled, listening to the final notes of the preceding number. She wasn't thinking about the steps, or the audience, or the technical perfection. She was thinking of the petty, cowardly act of malice. She was thinking of Eliza’s smug, cold smile. She was thinking of Ben’s warning. *Watch your back.* The cue for the drum solo hit, a brutal, percussive shock. She walked from the darkness into a single, stark white spotlight, and the rage she felt became her fuel.

Her performance was not what she had rehearsed in her room. It was not a mechanical execution of a series of movements. It was a declaration of war. The first count, the drive from the floor, was a deep, guttural roar of defiance. Her arms sliced the air with a vicious precision, not as dance but as combat. Every movement was imbued with a controlled, dangerous fury, a story of a fight not against an invisible wind, but against a very real and present enemy. She threw herself into the final, spinning drop with a reckless, breathtaking abandon, landing in her crouched pose with a percussive thud that seemed to shake the stage. She held the pose for a beat longer than rehearsed, her head up, her eyes blazing with a challenger’s fire, staring out into the vast, silent darkness of the auditorium.

For a split-second after she hit her final pose, there was a profound, stunned silence. The audience, expecting a dance, had been given a confession, a raw, visceral display of athletic rage. Then, the applause came, not the polite clapping for a piece of competent choreography, but a sudden, explosive roar of appreciation for the sheer, raw power of it. It was the sound of a crowd that had just witnessed a knockout blow. Backstage, as she walked into the dim light of the wings, the other dancers parted for her, their faces a mixture of shock and a new, grudging respect. They avoided her eyes not out of disdain, but out of a sudden, unnerving awareness of the force they had provoked. Leo was standing exactly where she knew he would be, a dark silhouette against the stage lights. He watched her approach, his face an unreadable mask. He didn’t speak until she was right in front of him. He looked not at her face, but at her feet, still planted firmly on the ground. “Your landing,” he said, his voice a low, flat statement of fact, “was on the beat.” He held her gaze for a single, searing moment, and in his eyes she saw it: the ultimate validation. It wasn’t praise. It was a confirmation of her professional existence. He had thrown her to the wolves, and she had not only survived, she had become the alpha.The walk back to the dressing room was like moving through a different dimension. The air, which moments before had been electric with pre-show hostility, was now thick with a heavy, watchful stillness. The other dancers, flushed with the exertion of the first act, moved around her with a new, careful awareness, like predators reassessing a creature they had misjudged. They didn’t speak to her, but the silence was no longer contemptuous; it was wary, uncertain. Eliza was at her station, her back a rigid plank of fury, her movements as she reapplied her lipstick sharp and jerky. She did not look at Jenna, an act of pointed ignorance that was more telling than any glare. The war had moved to a new phase, from active aggression to a cold, tense stalemate. Jenna sat down at her own spot, her hands still trembling slightly, not from fear but from the residual adrenaline. She peeled off her sweaty practice top, and as she did, she caught the eye of a younger dancer, a girl named Maria, in the mirror. Maria quickly looked away, but not before giving a tiny, almost imperceptible nod of respect. It was the first crack in the wall.

In the days that followed, the atmosphere in rehearsal underwent a subtle but profound change. The petty sabotage stopped. Jenna's space was always clear, her equipment untouched. The invisible resistance she had felt in group numbers seemed to dissolve. It was not replaced by warmth or camaraderie, but by a cool, professional distance that was, in its own way, a form of acceptance. She was no longer the amateur to be tormented, but a colleague to be tolerated, a problem that had proven too difficult to dislodge. The senior dancers still moved in their own orbit, but their conversations no longer fell silent when she approached. They simply continued, acknowledging her presence with the same detached courtesy they afforded the lighting technicians or the stagehands. She was part of the machinery now, a specialised and somewhat dangerous component, but a component nonetheless.

Leo’s treatment of her also shifted. He no longer ignored her or addressed her only to correct a basic error. Now, his focus on her was as intense and demanding as it was for his lead dancers. He dissected her solo with a surgeon’s dispassionate precision, demanding more control in her explosive power, a cleaner line on her recovery, a more nuanced use of her core. "The rage was a useful tool, Kirby," he told her one afternoon, his voice low as he corrected her posture after a run-through. "But raw fuel is inefficient. You have refined it once. Now you will refine it again. I want the power of the explosion, but I want to see every shard of glass as it flies. Control. That is the difference between a weapon and a tantrum." The criticism was relentless, exhausting, and the greatest compliment he could possibly give her. He was no longer just teaching her; he was coaching her, pushing her from competence towards artistry.

The tour crossed the Irish Sea, the ferry cutting through choppy, grey water towards Scotland. The mood on the bus was lighter, the end of the Irish leg bringing a sense of accomplishment. Ben slumped into the seat next to her, a bag of crisps already open. "So, the Ice Queen hasn't tried to trip you all week," he observed, his tone casual. "I think you scared her. Not just with the dancing. You looked her dead in the eye and didn't blink. They respect that, this lot. They respect a survivor more than they respect talent." He offered her a crisp. "You’re not just the TV girl anymore. You're the girl who got her shoes stolen and then danced like her hair was on fire. That’s a better story. It's got more grit." Jenna took the crisp, the salty flavour sharp on her tongue. She had been so focused on surviving the fight that she hadn’t realised she’d won it, not by being accepted, but by forcing them to acknowledge she couldn’t be broken.

Glasgow felt different. It was a city carved from sandstone and determination, its grandeur laced with a tough, industrial spirit that reminded her achingly of home. On the morning of their first show at the Theatre Royal, Ben found her staring at her phone in the theatre's canteen, a rare, genuine smile on her face. "What's that?" he asked, peering over her shoulder. It was an article from a Belfast newspaper's website. A review of the show. He scanned the text, his eyes widening. The critic had praised the show's energy and precision, but had singled out one moment for specific mention. "…while the ensemble work is impeccable, the show finds its furious, beating heart in a brief, punk-rock solo from newcomer Jenna Kirby. A blistering, athletic statement of intent, it's a moment of raw, unpredictable power that elevates the entire second act. A star is not just born here; she is forged in fire." Ben let out a low whistle. "Blistering athletic statement of intent," he read aloud, grinning. "Leo's going to hate the flowery language, but he'll love the sentiment." At that moment, Eliza walked past their table on her way to the dressing rooms. Her eyes flickered to the phone screen, she saw the headline, and for the first time, Jenna saw not anger or hatred on her face, but something hollow and tired, the quiet, desolate look of final defeat. The war was over.

That evening, tucked away in yet another anonymous hotel room that smelled faintly of disinfectant, Jenna called home. The connection crackled, the distance between Glasgow and St Helens feeling both immense and insignificant. "Dad? It's me."
"Our Jen! How's Scotland? Saw a picture of you on Chloe's internet thing. You looked… fierce." There was a new confidence in his voice, the pride no longer laced with worry but worn as a badge of honour. Chloe had been posting videos of the new solo, short, grainy clips filmed from the wings by Ben, and her "brand strategy" had found its focus. The comments sections were filled with people from home, cheering on their warrior dancer.
"It's good, Dad. It's hard work."
"I know, love. But it’s good work, isn't it?" he said, and she could hear the smile in his voice. He understood the difference now. He didn't ask if she was eating her greens or if she was lonely. He asked about the stage, about the audience, about the work. Their conversation had found a new, shared language. He was no longer just her father; he was her most devoted fan.

After the call, Jenna lay on her bed, staring at the ceiling, the distant sounds of the city a low hum outside her window. The intense isolation she had felt in the beginning had receded, replaced by a quiet, self-contained solitude. She was still alone, but she was no longer lonely. She was a professional, part of a machine, a dancer with a solo, a girl with a review. The next morning, she had a rare few hours off before rehearsal. Instead of staying in her room to study, she pulled on her running shoes and went out into the city. She found herself drawn to the west end, to the steep, elegant streets that climbed up towards the university. She ran up a hill that made her lungs burn and her thighs scream with a deep, familiar fire. At the top, she stopped, leaning against a stone wall, her breath coming in ragged clouds, and looked out over the sprawling, grey, beautiful city. The pain in her legs was not an agony to be endured, but a reminder of where she had come from. It was the feeling of the spoil heap by The Dream, the climb past Carr Mill Dam, the long, brutal slog home against the wind. It was the feeling of St Helens. She looked out at the alien landscape of Glasgow, at the cranes and spires and tenements, and for the first time, she didn't feel like a visitor. She felt the solid ground beneath her feet, the strength in her own legs, and she realised that home was no longer just a place she had left behind. Home was the power she carried inside her.
