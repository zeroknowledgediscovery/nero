The smog of Greensboro had a taste. It was coppery, mostly, from the Scrap Spires that clawed at the perpetually grey sky, but it was layered with other, more subtle notes. There was the sour tang of the fungus ale brewing in a thousand basements, the sharp scent of hot oil from the clanking automatons that patrolled the lower streets, and, if you were high enough up, the damp, earthy perfume of moss reclaiming the rooftops. Fizzlewit, a goblin whose knuckles were permanently stained with grease and whose nose was a masterpiece of inquisitive angles, preferred the moss.

Her sanctuary was a patchwork of scavenged planters and cracked clay pots on the flat roof of a forgotten gear-house. Here, amidst the city's iron cacophony, she cultivated impossible things: whisper-thin moon-petal flowers that only bloomed in the reflection of the city's gaslights, hardy strains of moss that glowed with a soft, green phosphorescence, and a single, stubborn tomato plant that produced fruit as sour as a cog-master's frown. It was a secret rebellion of green against a world of rust and soot.

A low rumble, soft as distant thunder, vibrated through the tar-paper beneath her feet. Fizzlewit didn't look up from the delicate clockwork spider she was assembling. "I know, Barnaby. It’s almost time." The spider’s final leg, a length of re-bent copper wire, clicked into place. She held it up, a mechanical arachnid the size of her palm. It wiggled its new limb tentatively.

Barnaby rumbled again, a deeper, more insistent note this time. He was a creature of impossible scale in a city built for the small and wiry. An elephant. Not a shabby, circus-worn thing, but a magnificent beast whose hide was the color of worn slate and whose eyes held an intelligence that often felt older than Greensboro itself. His great, wrinkled form took up a full quarter of the rooftop, yet he moved with a silence that was a constant source of wonder. He nudged Fizzlewit’s shoulder with the tip of his trunk, a gesture of immense gentleness. His scent was clean, like dust after a rainstorm, a stark contrast to the city below.

"Alright, alright, you big worrywart," Fizzlewit sighed, setting the clockwork spider down. It immediately skittered over to a loose shingle and began attempting to secure it with a non-existent thread of silk. "It's just the midday steam-whistle. Same as every day." She patted the trunk, her small, green hand a stark contrast against its tough, creased skin.

But it wasn’t the same. The midday whistle, a shrieking exhalation of steam from the central Piston Tower that governed the city's rhythms, was late. Goblins were creatures of habit and mechanism. Late was not a concept that sat well with them. Below, in the tangled alleyways and cobbled streets, the usual clatter of industry was faltering, replaced by a questioning murmur. The rhythmic *thump-clank-thump-clank* of the Great Forges was stuttering, like a mechanical heart having an arrhythmia.

Fizzlewit scrambled onto Barnaby's broad back, her bare feet finding easy purchase on his familiar hide. He lifted his head, giving her a vantage point over the maze of pipes and corrugated tin roofs. The air, usually thick with predictable plumes of white and grey smoke, was now tainted. A new color was bleeding into the sky from the direction of the Scrap Spires. A sickly, yellowish haze. And with it, a new smell.

It was faint at first, easily mistaken for a spoiled batch of fungus-curd at the market. But it grew, carried on the sluggish breeze, a scent that was fundamentally, offensively *wrong*. It was the smell of dairy, yes, but a malevolent dairy. A smell of milk left to sour in a hot crankcase, of cheese that had grown feet and a bad attitude. It was a pungent, cloying odor that seemed to coat the back of the throat.

"Ugh," Fizzlewit grimaced, pinching her long nose. "What in the name of busted gears is that?"

Barnaby let out a low, unhappy trumpet, a sound of genuine distress. His large ears fanned the air, as if trying to wave the stench away. Down below, the murmuring had turned to shouts. A few goblins were running, not with purpose, but with the jittery, unfocused panic of a disturbed ant colony. Something was breaking the routine. Something was breaking the machine of the city.

Then they saw them. Marching from the shadow of the tallest Scrap Spire, the one they called Old Rusty, was a contingent of goblins. They weren't the usual City Guard in their dented bronze helms. These goblins wore black iron armor crudely welded together, and their helmets were fashioned into sneering, grotesque faces. On their breastplates, a new symbol was emblazoned, painted in that same queasy yellow: a circle with three wavy lines leaking from the bottom. A weeping cheese.

Leading them was a figure whose silhouette was unmistakable. He was broader than most goblins, with a swaggering gait that spoke of unearned confidence. Even from this distance, Fizzlewit could see the oily sheen on his green skin and the way he gestured with a jagged sword made from a sharpened length of rebar. Gorgon. A goblin who had risen from the sump-pits of the city's Underside with a reputation for cruelty and a bizarre, alchemical obsession with cheese. He had been a joke, a bogeyman whispered about in the alehouses. Now he was marching through the heart of Greensboro with an army at his back, and the air itself curdled around him.

His forces weren't attacking, not yet. They were securing intersections, blocking off the alleyways that led to the Spires. They moved with a chilling efficiency, their black iron forms a stark blight on the familiar rust-red of the city. The stuttering of the Great Forges stopped entirely, plunging Greensboro into an eerie, unnatural silence broken only by the spreading panic and the encroaching, foul odor.

"Gorgon," Fizzlewit whispered, her voice tight. She remembered him from her apprenticeship days at the Tinkerer's Guild, a lanky, unpleasant youth who was always trying to curdle the lubricating oil or ferment the polishing grease. He'd been exiled for turning the Guild Master's prized chronometer into a block of stinking, mold-riddled cheddar. They thought he was gone for good, another failure flushed into the Underside. They were wrong.

"He's going for the Spires," she realized aloud. The Scrap Spires weren't just landmarks; they were the source of everything. They were vast, vertical mines of compacted metal and forgotten technology, the raw material from which all of Greensboro was built and rebuilt. Control the Spires, and you controlled the city. "He can't do that. Old Man Zappcog is in there."

Zappcog was the Master Tinkerer, a goblin so old his skin had the texture of dried leather and his beard was more wire than hair. He was Fizzlewit's mentor, the one who had seen past her scrawny frame and recognized the spark of a true gear-head. He had taught her how to listen to the hum of a machine to diagnose its ills, how to coax life back into rusted-out automatons. He was the closest thing she had to a grandfather. And he lived in a workshop at the base of Old Rusty, a place that was probably now swarming with Gorgon’s cheese-reeking thugs.

A deep, mournful note came from Barnaby, and his trunk curled around Fizzlewit’s leg, a silent question. She looked down at her rooftop garden, her little patch of impossible peace. The green glow-moss seemed to dim in the yellowing light. Her clockwork spider had frozen mid-skitter, as if the very air had gummed up its tiny gears. This sanctuary wasn't safe. It was just a high shelf waiting for the rot to climb.

"No," she said, her voice finding a new hardness, a new edge of tempered steel. "We're not just going to sit here and watch him turn the whole city sour." She slid from Barnaby's back, landing silently on the rooftop. Her eyes, the color of amber, scanned the cityscape, no longer seeing pipes and rooftops, but pathways and possibilities. "Zappcog would have a plan. He always has a plan. But he's not getting out of there on his own."

She looked at Barnaby, whose wise, ancient eyes were fixed on her. In them, she saw no fear, only a steady, unwavering loyalty. He was a creature of the open plains and vast skies, yet he had chosen to stay with her in this cramped, clanking city. He had followed her into the darkest, rustiest corners of the Underside on scavenging runs without complaint. He would follow her now.

"First thing's first," Fizzlewit muttered, her mind already working, gears turning, plotting a course through the urban chaos. "We need to get a message to the other Guilds. The Alchemists, the Sump-Divers, even the blasted Cog-Masters. They won't listen to me, not a rooftop hermit. But they might listen to Zappcog's apprentice." She began gathering a few essentials into a leather satchel: a multi-tool with a dozen strange attachments, a pouch of dried, nutrient-rich fungus, a length of fine, strong wire, and a small tin containing her most reliable clockwork messenger-beetle. "And second," she said, slinging the satchel over her shoulder, "we need to get closer. I need to see what Gorgon's really up to."

A tremor of a different kind shook the building. It wasn’t a rumble from Barnaby, but a deep, structural groan from the city itself. A horrible, grinding shriek echoed from Old Rusty, the sound of tortured metal. A massive crane arm, one of the primary excavators of the Spire, tore loose from its moorings and plunged into the buildings below with a cataclysmic crash. Dust and debris, tinged with that awful yellow, billowed into the air. Gorgon wasn't just occupying the Spires. He was breaking them. He was poisoning the machine.

Fizzlewit didn't hesitate. She gave two sharp whistles, a signal Barnaby had known since she was a young goblin barely tall enough to reach his knee. It meant *let's go, and be quiet about it*. The great elephant lowered his head, allowing her to grab onto one of his tusks, a smooth, ivory-colored anchor in their world of grime. He turned, and with the impossible grace only he possessed, stepped from the roof onto the adjoining network of broad-shouldered steam pipes, his weight barely causing them to creak. Their quest had begun. Not with a grand proclamation or a call to arms, but with two friends stepping off a rooftop, leaving a small, secret garden to fend for itself in a city that was rapidly starting to stink of cheese.The city below was a hive kicked over. Goblins, normally so purposeful in their scurrying, now ran in frantic, crisscrossing patterns. A fruit vendor's cart lay overturned, its cargo of rust-oranges rolling into a gutter where they bobbed in a slick of iridescent oil. The usual symphony of industry had been replaced by a discordant chorus of shouts, clanging shutters, and the occasional, terrified shriek. Above it all, the yellowish haze thickened, catching the gaslights in its foul miasma and casting the entire district in a jaundiced, sickly glow. The smell was worse now, a physical presence that clung to the damp air and made Fizzlewit’s eyes water.

Barnaby’s steps were impossibly light on the wide-gauge steam pipe, his balance absolute. He was an island of calm in the churning sea of panic. Fizzlewit clung to his back, her mind racing. They navigated the rooftop arteries of the city, a high road of metal and tar known only to tinkers, chimney sweeps, and the desperate. From their perch, they saw the full extent of Gorgon’s initial strike. His "Cheese-mongers," as Fizzlewit had started to call them in her head, were not just a mob; they were organized. They had seized the Steam-Regulators, the Water-Valves, and the Gear-Exchanges. They weren't just taking the Spires; they were taking the city's entire circulatory system.

They reached the edge of the Tinkerer's district and paused in the shadow of a great, copper water tower. Below them lay the Cog-Grinder’s Promenade, a wide street that was usually a river of goblin couriers and lumbering cargo-automatons. It was now a staging ground for Gorgon's forces. They had wheeled out bizarre contraptions: tanks on sputtering iron wheels, with large brass nozzles aimed at the ornate facades of the Guild buildings. As Fizzlewit watched, one of the nozzles unleashed a thick, sputtering jet of the yellow substance. It wasn't just gas; it was a viscous, lumpy fluid that splattered against the bronze doors of the Alchemist's Guild. A horrific sizzling sound rose up, and the metal began to bubble and warp, turning a pocked, sickly green as if instantly overcome by a millennium of corrosion.

"It's an acid," Fizzlewit breathed, horrified. "A hyper-corrosive enzyme." It was alchemy, but a perverted, destructive form of it. Gorgon wasn't just conquering. He was dissolving their world. He had turned his foul obsession into a weapon that could eat through the very bones of Greensboro. Barnaby rumbled, a low, guttural sound of anger this time. He could smell the destruction, feel the city's pain as a vibration through the soles of his great feet. The clean, rainy scent of his skin was a comfort, a small pocket of sanity in the encroaching madness.

There was no way they could get to the Spires by the upper routes. Gorgon’s thugs were everywhere, their sneering iron masks seeming to leer up from every major intersection. They needed help, and they needed it now. Fizzlewit slid down Barnaby’s side into a sheltered recess behind the water tower, a space smelling of rust and pigeon droppings. She rummaged in her satchel and pulled out the small, tin box. Inside, nestled on a bed of velvet, was her prize-winning messenger beetle, 'Click'. It was a marvel of miniaturization, with a body of polished brass, legs of silver wire, and delicate, paper-thin wings made from shaved mica.

"Okay, Click," she whispered, carefully winding the tiny key in its abdomen. The beetle's antennae twitched, and a faint whirring sound came from within its delicate chassis. "You know the way to the Alchemist's Guild, top spire, Pipette's lab. Don't let anyone see you." She spoke into a tiny horn near its head, her words encoded into a series of vibrations that the beetle would replay upon arrival. "Gorgon. Corrosive acid. Looks like cheese. He's at the Spires. Zappcog is trapped. Need to unite the Guilds. Tell her… tell her the rust is winning." She sealed a tiny, written version of the message, coded in Tinkerer's shorthand, into a compartment on its back. She held the beetle aloft on her fingertip. It flexed its mica wings, which caught the dim light like fractured rainbows. Then, with a determined buzz, it launched into the foul air, a tiny, glittering spark of hope winging its way into the heart of the chaos.

Watching it disappear, Fizzlewit felt a sliver of her own hope return. It was a long shot, but Pipette was clever, and more importantly, she hated Gorgon almost as much as Fizzlewit did. Now for the next problem: Zappcog. The main paths were suicide. The rooftops were exposed. That left only one route. The one nobody took by choice. She looked down, past the streets and promenades, to the grated manholes and sewer entrances that dotted the alleyways like iron-rimmed eyes.

"We have to go down, Barnaby," she said grimly, patting his massive leg. "Into the Underside." Barnaby made a soft, questioning sound. The Underside was a maze of abandoned subway tunnels, forgotten cisterns, and maintenance conduits, a place of dripping water, strange fungi, and things best left undisturbed. It was also Gorgon's old stomping ground. He would have it mapped, perhaps even trapped. But it was also vast and labyrinthine. Big enough, she hoped, for a goblin and her elephant to pass through unseen. It was a fool's hope, but it was the only one they had.Finding an entrance to the Underside was the easy part; the city’s bowels had more outlets than a leaky automaton. Finding one large enough for Barnaby was the challenge. Fizzlewit guided him along the high pipes to the back of the old Piston-works, a district of colossal, slumbering machinery that had fallen silent for the first time in generations. Here, a massive storm drain outflow, a gaping maw of brick and iron, opened onto a steep, slime-slick ramp that descended into the darkness. It was designed to void entire districts of floodwater, and it smelled of rust, algae, and a deep, cold emptiness. For them, it was a doorway.

The descent was unnerving. The sounds of the panicked city above faded, replaced by the echo of Barnaby's careful footfalls and the steady drip-drip-drip of water from a thousand unseen leaks. The air grew cool and heavy, stripped of the coppery smog and its cheesy corruption, leaving only the pure, primal scent of damp earth and subterranean stone. Fizzlewit, perched just behind Barnaby's great head, activated a small lantern of her own design. It wasn't fueled by oil or gas, but by a captured glow-wasp, whose soft, pulsating bioluminescence cast long, dancing shadows down the cavernous tunnel. Barnaby, who could see surprisingly well in the dark, seemed to appreciate the familiar green light. He rumbled softly, a sound of cautious reassurance.

They moved through a world outside of time. The walls of the main conduit were a tapestry of goblin history, layered with pipes from different eras—gleaming copper next to verdigris-caked bronze, all held together with patches of pitted black iron. Phosphorescent moss grew in spiraling patterns, tracing veins of moisture along the curved brickwork and casting the tunnel in a faint, ghostly luminescence. This was the Underside Fizzlewit knew: vast, silent, and indifferent. But as they traveled deeper, following a route she had mapped on scavenging trips, she began to notice subtle, disturbing changes.

The usual scuttling creatures of the deep—the blind, pale cave-crickets and armored sewer-slugs—were absent. The tunnels were too quiet. Then she saw the first sign: a smear of that familiar, putrid yellow paste on the wall, as if a giant had wiped a cheesy finger along the bricks. Barnaby sniffed at it and recoiled with a snort of disgust, shaking his head. Gorgon had been here. His filth was seeping into the very foundations of Greensboro. Fizzlewit's hand tightened on the strap of her satchel. This was no longer a simple rescue mission; it was an extermination.

They came to a junction of three massive tunnels, a crossroads in the deep dark. The air here was strangely still. Fizzlewit's lantern-light reflected off something on the floor of the central passage, the most direct route to the foundations of the Scrap Spires. She signaled Barnaby to a halt, sliding silently to the ground. She crept forward, her bare feet making no sound on the damp stone. It was a puddle, but it wasn't water. It was a thick, whey-like liquid, unnaturally clear and shimmering with a faint, oily sheen. Kneeling beside it, she didn’t need to smell it to know what it was. A precursor to the corrosive agent, a trap waiting for a catalyst.

Her gaze followed the puddle to its edges, searching for the trigger. Her tinkerer's eyes, accustomed to spotting the tiniest misaligned gear or loose wire, saw it almost immediately: a nearly invisible filament of spun glass, stretched taut across the tunnel entrance at ankle-height. Had Barnaby lumbered through, his massive leg would have snapped the filament, releasing a chemical agent from a hidden nozzle in the ceiling into the puddle below, triggering a violent, acid-spewing reaction that would have flooded the tunnel. It was a devilishly simple, brutally effective trap. It was pure Gorgon.

As she was tracing the mechanism back to its source, a sound echoed from the tunnel to their right. Voices. Guttural, grumbling goblin-speech, accompanied by the metallic scrape of armored feet. A patrol. Fizzlewit’s blood ran cold. They were caught. Retreating was impossible; the patrol was too close. Moving forward meant navigating the trap, a task that would take precious minutes she didn't have. The tunnel to their left was smaller, a dark, uninviting tube half-choked with rubble and thick, web-like fungal growth. It might not even be big enough.

Barnaby didn't wait for a command. He seemed to understand the geometry of their predicament instantly. With a movement so swift and silent it defied his bulk, he backed into the left-hand tunnel, pushing aside rubble with his great head as if it were loose gravel. He squeezed his enormous frame into the opening, his slate-grey hide vanishing into the deeper shadows within. He then extended his trunk back out into the main junction, a silent invitation. Fizzlewit didn't hesitate. She took a running leap, grabbing the end of his trunk as it swung her upwards and into the cramped darkness of the side tunnel with him. He retracted his trunk just as the light from the patrol's lanterns washed over the junction.

Huddled in the darkness, the clean, earthy scent of her friend a calming presence, Fizzlewit held her breath. Two of Gorgon’s Cheese-mongers clomped into view. Their black iron armor was streaked with grime, and they carried poleaxes fashioned from sharpened gears welded to long pipes. "Nothin' again," one grumbled, his voice echoing in the chamber. "I hate this duty. It's damp. And it stinks down here."

"It stinks better than that slop Gorgon calls 'Fortified Cheddar'," the other replied, kicking at a loose stone. "My helm's still sour from the last briefing. So, what's the plan again? We just stand around until he melts the whole city?"

"He's not melting it," the first one snapped. "He's *improving* it. Purifying it. Getting rid of the rust and the rot. He said the Great Curdling is at hand. Once he gets the main core of Old Rusty attuned to his new formula, the whole city will be reborn in his image." He gestured vaguely upwards. "No more tinkering with useless cogs and springs. Just the pure, simple power of glorious cheese."

The second goblin shuddered. "Sounds sticky." They paused, their lanterns playing over the whey-like puddle. "Good thing nothing's big enough to come through here anyway," the first one said, blissfully unaware of the elephant-sized creature holding its breath twenty feet away. With a final grumble, they continued down the central passage, their lights and voices slowly fading into the oppressive silence.

Fizzlewit waited until the last echo had died. A slow, shuddering breath escaped her lips. Barnaby gently nudged her with his trunk, a silent 'all clear'. She patted him, her heart still hammering against her ribs. The patrol's chatter had been more terrifying than any trap. *The Great Curdling. Attuning the core of Old Rusty.* Gorgon wasn't just breaking the Spires; he had a much larger, much more insane plan. He was trying to turn the very heart of Greensboro's technology, the ancient archeotech core within the central Spire, into a giant alchemical reactor for his foul concoctions. He wasn't just poisoning the machine; he was trying to make it give birth to a monster.The revelation hung in the cold, damp air long after the patrol’s footsteps had faded, heavier and more chilling than the subterranean darkness. The Great Curdling. It was a phrase of such profound, grotesque absurdity that Fizzlewit’s mind struggled to give it shape. She had imagined Gorgon as a tyrant, a brute who wanted to rule over a pile of scrap. But this was something else entirely. It was a plan born of pure mania, a desire not to rule the machine of Greensboro, but to transform it into something monstrous and alien. To turn the city’s metal heart into a festering, alchemical cheese-vat was an act of such deep-seated wrongness it defied all principles of engineering and sanity.

With a new, cold resolve settling in her stomach, Fizzlewit slid down from their hiding spot. Barnaby followed, his huge form emerging from the constricted tunnel with a soft exhalation of breath. She approached the whey-puddle trap, her glow-wasp lantern casting a soft green light on the shimmering liquid. The glass filament was almost beautiful in its lethality, a simple tripwire for a complex chemical cascade. Gorgon’s work was crude, a mockery of the elegant chain reactions and precise mechanics she and Zappcog revered. It was the alchemy of a thug, all force and no finesse, using volatile compounds where a well-placed gear would suffice. There was no disarming it quickly, not without the risk of setting it off. The side tunnel was their only path forward.

That path was not a welcoming one. It was an ancient service conduit, barely wider than Barnaby’s shoulders, and the air within was stale and thick with the smell of petrified fungus. Rubble and the collapsed skeletons of long-dead pipes choked the passage at frequent intervals. Here, their partnership found its rhythm. Fizzlewit would scurry ahead, her small lantern held high, her tinkerer’s eye assessing the structural integrity of a blockage. She would tap a key stone or a rusted support beam. “There, Barnaby. The keystone is loose. A steady push, not a shove.” And Barnaby, with an intelligence that went beyond mere animal instinct, would obey. He would press his great, solid head against the exact point, and with a carefully modulated groan of effort, dislodge the rubble with minimal noise and no risk of a wider collapse. It was slow, arduous work, a claustrophobic journey through the city’s forgotten veins.

After what felt like an eternity of dust and darkness, the tight passage suddenly opened out, not into another tunnel, but into a space so vast their light was swallowed by it. The air changed, losing its musty closeness and taking on the cool, mineral scent of a deep cavern. They had broken through into one of the Great Cisterns, a colossal, bowl-shaped chamber carved from the bedrock in the city’s primordial past. It was supposed to be filled with emergency reserves of water, but it had been dry for centuries, a silent, empty amphitheater hidden beneath the streets. The floor was a mosaic of cracked mud, and rising from its center like the bones of a dead leviathan was the corroded architecture of an ancient water pump, its iron pipes thick as tree trunks.

The scale of the place was humbling, but it was the sight on the far side of the cistern that made Fizzlewit’s breath catch in her throat. A sickly yellow luminescence pulsed from a series of tunnels that exited the far wall, the same tunnels that would lead towards the foundations of the Scrap Spires. The light was organic, a biological glow that was a perversion of her own rooftop moss. It waxed and waned in a slow, queasy rhythm, like a diseased heart. Gorgon’s influence was stronger here, bleeding into the very rock. Worse still, the floor of the cistern was not as empty as she first thought. Dotted across the cracked mud were pale, lumpy shapes, each the size of a cargo crate. They looked like giant, malformed fungi, but as Barnaby took a hesitant step forward, one of the shapes stirred. It unfolded with a wet, tearing sound, revealing a grotesque parody of an automaton. Its chassis was not metal, but a hardened, cheese-like rind, and its limbs moved with a twitching, unnatural motion. It was a Sentry-bot, but one that had been dissolved and then hideously reborn.The cheese-bot lurched forward. Its movements were a horrible combination of a mechanical limp and an organic spasm, and each step made a wet, slapping sound on the cavern floor. A jagged 'lens' of what looked like hardened amber, embedded in its head-unit, began to glow, casting a cone of that same jaundiced, yellow light that swept slowly across the cistern. Fizzlewit instinctively yanked on Barnaby's ear, pulling them both deeper into the shadows of the tunnel mouth. The beam passed over their position, its light so thick and foul it seemed to leave a greasy film on the air itself. The sentry was blind to them for now, its programming either too crude or too degraded for peripheral vision.

"Okay," Fizzlewit whispered, her voice barely a puff of air. "The big pump in the middle. We move when the light moves away. One step at a time. No noise." Barnaby responded with a nearly imperceptible rumble, a vibration she felt more than heard. He understood. As the yellow beam began its slow sweep back across the cistern, they moved. It was a terrifying ballet in the dark. Barnaby, a creature of impossible mass, moved with the silence of a falling leaf, placing his huge, padded feet with preternatural care onto the cracked mud floor. Fizzlewit, a shadow at his side, kept her eyes locked on the lurching sentry, her hand held up to signal ‘stop’ or ‘go’. They froze as the beam swung back towards them, a long, agonizing moment where they were held breathless in a sliver of shadow, the squelching footsteps of the monstrous guard echoing in the vast, empty space.

After three such heart-stopping advances, they reached the relative safety of the ancient pump’s base. Crouching behind a rust-flaked iron cylinder as wide as a goblin is tall, Fizzlewit peered out. The situation was far worse than she'd feared. It wasn't just one sentry. As her eyes adjusted, she could make out at least a dozen of them, shambling in staggered, overlapping patrol routes across the cistern floor. Some were dormant, slumped like pale, lumpy boulders, while others twitched and lurched in a grotesque parody of their former function. On the far side of the chamber, near the glowing tunnel mouths, a group of Gorgon’s black-armored goblins were directing two of the cheese-bots as they hauled large, sloshing vats. They were creating a fortified chokepoint. There was no way to sneak past a dozen patrols and a full work crew.

Trapped. The word echoed in the sudden silence of Fizzlewit's mind. They were trapped between a single, narrow escape route and an army of abominations. Panic, cold and sharp, pricked at the edges of her composure. She fought it down, forcing her breathing to even out. A tinkerer did not panic. A tinkerer assessed the variables and found a new solution. Her gaze shifted from the enemy to her surroundings, from the problem to the potential tools at hand. She was crouched at the heart of a machine. A dead machine, yes, but a machine nonetheless. Its colossal iron limbs rose up into the darkness above them, a skeleton of gears, pistons, and counterweights, all frozen in a final, century-old tableau of inaction.

An idea sparked, a tiny flicker of defiance in the oppressive gloom. It was a stupid, reckless, and deeply satisfying idea. She wasn't an assassin. She wasn't a soldier. She was a tinkerer. And she was going to *fix* this. She tugged on Barnaby's trunk, pointing upwards into the labyrinth of rusted metal above them. High above, a massive counterweight, a solid block of iron the size of a small wagon, was held in place by a thick, chain-driven pulley system. The locking mechanism, a heavy toothed pawl latched onto a giant gear, was encrusted with rust but visibly intact. If that counterweight were to drop, the resulting crash would be cataclysmic. It would be loud enough to draw every single one of those twitching sentries to this spot.

"Barnaby," she whispered, her eyes gleaming with manic inspiration. "I need a distraction." She scrambled up the side of the pump, her bare feet and greasy hands finding purchase on pipes and brackets. She shimmied her way up to the main gear assembly, twenty feet above the cistern floor. The rust was thick, like a scab on the metal, but the underlying mechanics were sound. She pulled out her multi-tool and unfolded a long, needle-nosed nozzle attached to a small vial of 'Cog-Ease', her own potent blend of penetrating oil and mild acidic solvent. She began to carefully apply the liquid to the joints of the locking pawl, the oil hissing softly as it began to eat away at centuries of corrosion. The plan was simple: loosen the lock, then find a way to release it from a safe distance. That would give them the precious seconds they needed to cross the cistern while the cheese-bots were investigating the crash. It was their only way forward.The Cog-Ease worked with a satisfying hiss, the sound of entropy being briefly, locally reversed. And you, girl, will surrender all of Zappcog’s schematics, research notes, and Core-interface protocols to my engineers for a proper, mechanical analysis.” He tried to project an aura of control, of a return to the old, comfortable order, but his words rang hollow in the vast, quiet plaza.

Before Fizzlewit could even muster a response, Slyss, the Sump-Diver, took a step forward. He moved with the fluid economy of a creature accustomed to tight spaces and sudden dangers, and his quiet voice cut through Thwacket’s bluster like a sharpened knife through soft rust. “Your engineers, Thwacket, were watching the city melt along with the rest of us. Your ‘proper mechanical analysis’ failed. Your forges went cold. My divers in the deep conduits saw Gorgon’s rot firsthand, and it wasn’t stopped by a bigger gear or a stronger piston.” He turned his steady, dark eyes to Fizzlewit, ignoring Thwacket completely. “She says she corrected the flaw. The city is solid. The rot is gone. These are the only facts that matter. I don’t care if she did it by singing to it. I want to know if the fix is permanent, and I want to know what other monsters Gorgon left stirring in the deep places.”

The directness of the question, the sheer pragmatism of it, shifted the entire dynamic of the confrontation. It was no longer about authority and procedure; it was about survival and information. Fizzlewit felt a small measure of strength return. This was a language she understood. It was a diagnostic report. She took a deep breath, the coppery air a familiar comfort, and addressed not just Slyss, but the entire assembled council, her voice finding a new, solid timbre. “The fix is… a patch. Not a solution. I introduced a counter-frequency that forced a phase-shift in Gorgon’s enzyme, re-crystallizing it on a molecular level. "The Core is stable, but it's under immense stress. It's like a spring that's been wound too tight. It could hold for a hundred years, or it could snap tomorrow. The sheer audacity of it left the masters stunned. This was not a plea from a frightened girl, but a decree from the only expert left standing. Thwacket’s face went from red to a mottled purple, and he opened his mouth to unleash a tirade, but Fluxworth, the elderly Alchemist, held up a frail, steady hand. “She is correct, Thwacket. The girl—the young woman,” he corrected himself, his old eyes looking at Fizzlewit with a newfound respect, “holds the key. Thwacket, seeing his support evaporate, deflated, his rage replaced by a sullen, impotent silence. The motion was passed with a series of grim, reluctant nods. In the space of ten minutes, Fizzlewit had gone from a rooftop hermit to a member of Greensboro’s ruling body. The weight of it was dizzying. She felt an almost overwhelming urge to flee, to climb onto Barnaby’s back and disappear back into the quiet anonymity of her moss garden. But the image of Zappcog, lying silent on the floor of his ruined workshop, anchored her to the spot. This was his last project, and she would see it completed.

Having won the political battle she never wanted to fight, Fizzlewit made her first move. She did not issue an order about the Core or the forges. She turned to Slyss. “Master Slyss, I need a quiet place. A large one. Away from the center of the city. A place with clean water and room to move.” Her gaze flickered to Barnaby. “He has endured the worst of our city today. He deserves a sanctuary.” Slyss considered this for a moment, his gaze assessing the elephant not as a threat, but as a being. He nodded slowly. “The Old Reservoir, west of the Sump district. It’s been empty for a generation, but the aqueducts still feed it. It’s walled, quiet. Big enough for him to forget he is in a city. I will have it prepared.”

With a nod of thanks, Fizzlewit turned her back on the stunned and squabbling council. She placed a hand on Barnaby’s immense leg. “Come on, you big worrywart,” she murmured, her voice thick with an exhaustion so deep it was an ache in her bones. “Let’s go home. Or somewhere like it.” Without a backward glance, the two of them began to walk away, leaving the masters to begin the messy, noisy work of rebuilding their broken world. The crowd of goblins, who had watched the entire exchange in silent awe, parted for them, a path opening up through the city. This time, as they walked, the silence was different. It was filled not with questions or fear, but with a quiet, powerful, and growing respect for the goblin and the elephant who had walked into a nightmare and emerged as the city’s reluctant, weary saviors.The journey to the Old Reservoir was a pilgrimage through a city holding its breath. The crowds did not cheer or clamor; they simply watched, their silence a testament to the magnitude of what had transpired. They parted for the elephant and the goblin like iron filings before a magnet, their faces a gallery of shock, relief, and a deep, unsettling awe. The fine, yellow dust, the crystalline ghost of their near-annihilation, lay in a sparkling blanket over everything, muffling the sound of their passage until only the soft crunch of Barnaby’s footfalls and the distant, tentative hiss of a stabilizing steam-pipe could be heard. Fizzlewit walked in the bubble of silence that surrounded her friend, her gaze fixed forward, her mind a numb landscape of exhaustion. She felt like a clockwork automaton whose mainspring had been wound too tight and was now just ticking down its last, shuddering movements before stillness.

They were met at the edge of the Sump district by a pair of Slyss’s divers. They wore their slick, hardened-leather gear with a quiet professionalism, and they gave Fizzlewit a simple, respectful nod before turning to lead the way. They didn't speak, for which Fizzlewit was profoundly grateful. They led them through a maze of dripping alleyways and past the gaping mouths of sewer grates that smelled of clean, rushing water instead of the usual stagnant grime, a sign that Slyss’s guild was already hard at work flushing the city’s veins. Finally, they arrived at a colossal wall of dark, moss-stained stone, so tall it seemed to scrape the perpetually grey sky. It was a structure from another age, built with titanic, interlocking blocks that dwarfed the goblin-sized architecture of the surrounding district. A single, massive iron gate, green with centuries of verdigris, was being cranked open by a team of divers, its groan the first loud, mechanical sound they had heard since leaving the plaza.

The moment they stepped through, the city fell away. The air changed instantly, losing its coppery tang and the last phantom traces of Gorgon’s rot, replaced by the rich, damp perfume of wet earth, stone, and living green things. The Old Reservoir was a vast, sunken basin, a great stone bowl carved into the bedrock of Greensboro. A shallow lake of startlingly clear water filled its center, fed by a steady, gurgling stream that emerged from a Founder-era aqueduct high up in the far wall. Patches of tough, wild grass and resilient, flowering weeds grew in the cracks between the paving stones, a testament to nature’s stubborn refusal to be completely paved over. It was Fizzlewit’s rooftop sanctuary magnified a thousand times, a secret, untamed heart hidden within the city’s iron ribcage.

Barnaby let out a low, rumbling sigh that was pure, unadulterated relief. He walked to the edge of the lake, his massive form reflected perfectly in its still surface, and plunged his trunk in, drinking with long, slow, deep draughts. He then sprayed a great shower of the cool, clean water over his back, washing away the dust and grime and stress of the last day. Fizzlewit slid from his side, her legs barely holding her, and sank to the ground on a patch of surprisingly soft grass. She watched her friend rediscover a simple, profound joy, and as she watched, the iron bands of tension that had been holding her together since the midday whistle failed finally snapped. The full weight of Zappcog’s absence, a reality she had held at bay through sheer momentum, crashed down upon her. She didn't make a sound, but hot, silent tears began to carve fresh paths through the layers of grease and sorrow on her cheeks. He was gone. The cantankerous, brilliant, infuriating old goblin who had taught her to listen to the whispers of a machine was gone, and the city, for all its clatter, suddenly felt terribly quiet.

After a long while, her tears subsided, leaving behind a hollow ache. She pushed herself to her feet with a groan, her body protesting every movement. Duty, ingrained in her as deeply as any gear-train, took over. She waded into the shallows of the lake, the cool water a balm on her tired feet, and began the familiar ritual of caring for her friend. She checked each of his great, padded feet, gently cleaning away the sharp, crystalline dust that had embedded itself in the tough, wrinkled skin. He stood with perfect stillness, his trust in her absolute. When she was finished, he nudged her with his trunk, a soft inquiry, and she leaned her forehead against its rough, solid warmth, drawing strength from his unwavering presence. The sun, a pale disc in the grey sky, was beginning its descent, painting the clouds in bruised shades of purple and orange.

As twilight settled over the reservoir, casting long shadows from the ancient walls, Fizzlewit sat by the water's edge. She carefully unwrapped the leather cloth from her satchel. The fragments of Barnaby's tusk lay in her palm, their inner light extinguished, now just pieces of milky, shattered ivory. They were no longer a key or a weapon, just a reminder of the terrible cost. Barnaby, sensing her thoughts, came and stood over her. He lowered his head and gently touched the fragments in her hand with the blunted end of his tusk, a silent communion, a shared acknowledgement of the sacrifice. He had given a part of himself, and she had wielded it. They were bound together not just by friendship, but by the scars of the battle they had won.

Just as the first gaslights began to flicker to life atop the reservoir walls, casting a familiar orange glow onto their secluded peace, one of the Sump-Divers approached. He placed a small crate on the ground, filled with dried fungus, clean water flasks, and a small toolkit. Beside it, he laid a sealed scroll. "The council has convened. He gave a short, formal bow and retreated as silently as he had come. Fizzlewit looked from the quiet, life-giving water of the reservoir to the glowing gaslights, the mechanical stars of the city she was now charged with healing. The scroll on the ground felt as heavy as a foundry anvil. Her brief, precious respite was over. Her new life was waiting. She took one last, deep breath of the clean, earthy air, then stood up, her duty and her grief now forged into a new, unfamiliar alloy.Morning did not dawn in Greensboro; it seeped. The grey light bled slowly into the sky, revealing a city that was bruised but not broken. In the sanctuary of the Old Reservoir, the light was softer, filtered through the thick leaves of stubborn, wild trees and reflecting off the clear, still surface of the lake. Fizzlewit woke not to the shriek of a steam-whistle, but to the gentle sound of Barnaby drinking from the water’s edge. Her bed had been a patch of soft moss, her blanket a spare tarp left by the Sump-Divers, and she had slept with the dreamless, leaden exhaustion of a machine run to a full stop. Then her eyes fell on the sealed scroll lying beside the cold remains of her supply crate, and the weight of her new reality settled upon her like a shroud.

His presence was a solid, comforting anchor in the swirling currents of her new life. She patted his leg, her small hand a familiar fixture against his vastness, before reaching for the scroll. The wax seal was emblazoned with the symbol of the Piston Tower, a mark of the council’s authority. It felt alien in her grease-stained fingers. The contents were not a summons, but a battlefield report from a war she had only just begun. It was written in the precise, clipped hand of Pipette’s chief scribe. The Great Forges, while no longer in danger of dissolving, were running at dangerously inconsistent temperatures, producing brittle, unreliable metal. Master Thwacket’s faction was loudly proclaiming that Fizzlewit’s “resonant cascade” had destabilized the Core, demanding that his engineers be given immediate access to Zappcog’s workshop and the Core itself to implement “proper mechanical stabilization protocols.” A counter-motion, tabled by Slyss and seconded by Fluxworth, had narrowly passed, placing a temporary moratorium on all Core-level access until Fizzlewit provided her initial assessment. A hot, sharp anger, the first real emotion besides grief and exhaustion she had felt since waking, burned in Fizzlewit’s chest. Thwacket wasn't trying to fix the city; he was trying to own its breaking. His engineers, with their rigid, textbook understanding of mechanics, would treat the Core like a simple boiler, applying brute force where finesse was required. They would snap the overwound spring she had warned them about. She realized with a chilling certainty that Gorgon had been the immediate threat, but Thwacket’s arrogant ignorance was the true, long-term existential danger to Greensboro. She couldn't just fix the machine; she had to protect it from its own masters.

Her mind, once numb, began to fire on all cylinders, the familiar click and whir of a tinkerer formulating a plan. She needed data, schematics, and the freedom to work without having to justify her every move to a committee of blowhards. She was the Acting Master of the Core, a title they had granted her in a moment of crisis. Now, she would define what that title meant. Second, she required a new base of operations: the abandoned gear-house adjacent to the Old Reservoir’s western wall, to be granted to her under the sole authority of the Master of the Core, along with all tools and materials within. Her message was clear: this was not a negotiation. It was the only way forward.

As she finished writing, her eyes fell upon the leather pouch containing the fragments of Barnaby’s tusk. She emptied them carefully into her palm. They were just broken pieces of ivory now, their power spent. But Zappcog had always taught her that nothing was ever truly broken, it was just waiting for a new purpose. The Calibrator had worked because the ivory’s ancient, organic, crystalline structure could channel the Core’s energy in a way pure metal could not. It was a bridge between two worlds. A new idea began to form in her mind, a personal project amidst the coming chaos. She could not remake the Calibrator, but she could use these fragments. She could build a new kind of diagnostic tool, a handheld scanner that incorporated a piece of the ivory as a focusing lens. It wouldn't sing the Core’s song, but it might be able to listen to it, to see the stress fractures in the city’s resonant field. It would be a tool born of Zappcog’s science, Pipette’s alchemy, and Barnaby’s sacrifice. A tool for a new kind of tinkerer.

She rolled the parchment, sealing it not with the council's crest, but with a simple thumbprint of grease and charcoal. When the Sump-Diver returned at mid-morning to collect the previous day's empty water flasks, she handed him the scroll. "For the council," she said, her voice steady. The diver took it with a nod, his eyes showing a flicker of surprise at her swift, decisive response. He bowed and disappeared back into the city, a messenger carrying a declaration of a quiet, but absolute, revolution. Fizzlewit watched him go, then turned to Barnaby, who was patiently waiting. He was a master of gears and pistons, but Slyss was a master of flow and pressure, and had just demonstrated which was more fundamental. The abandoned gear-house stood on the western edge of the city, its back pressed against the ancient wall of the reservoir like a nervous child clinging to its parent. Fizzlewit stood in the entryway, a small, green figure dwarfed by the scale of her new domain, and felt a thrill that was equal parts terror and exhilaration. This was not a cozy rooftop or a cluttered workshop filled with another’s memories. This was a blank slate. A canvas. Barnaby pushed past her gently, his massive form fitting comfortably in the grand space. He let out a low, inquisitive rumble, his breath stirring up a cloud of dust that danced in a lone beam of light slanting down from a grimy window. A heavy, sliding gate on the far wall led directly out into the reservoir, a private entrance to his sanctuary. This place was a bridge between her two worlds, a place where the tinkerer and the friend of the elephant could be the same person.

Thwacket is screaming that it's sabotage, that the ‘resonant filth’ from the Core is corroding the infrastructure."

Pipette took the shard of metal, her gloved fingers tracing the strange cracks. She brought it to her nose, sniffing cautiously. "There's no chemical corrosive agent," she murmured, her brow furrowed in concentration. "This isn't alchemy. This is a structural failure on a molecular level. The re-crystallization wave must have created harmonic reverberations. Where the city's own resonant frequency was already weak or dissonant, it's causing the metal to become brittle." Slyss’s face was a grim mask. He issued a series of clipped orders to his second-in-command, dispatching teams to isolate the rupture and begin the slow, dangerous work of replacing the shattered section of pipe. He then turned to Fizzlewit, his dark eyes asking the question he didn't need to speak. She pulled off her goggles, her face pale under the harsh lights of the clean-room. "It's what I was afraid of," she said, her voice tight. "The spring is already starting to snap. We’re patching a leaking dam by staring at the cracks. We need to see the pressure. We need the resonoscope. Now."

The news of the ruptured pipe lit a fire under their work. The controlled, steady pace of the workshop accelerated into a frantic, focused race against time. Pipette, abandoning all other analysis, retrieved a velvet-lined case from her most secure cart. Inside were her personal focusing crystals, each one grown over years in a bath of rare earth salts and energized by carefully controlled lightning strikes. They shimmered with a faint, internal light, humming with latent energy. While Fizzlewit made the final, terrifyingly delicate polish of the ivory lens, Slyss’s top electricians worked to wire a self-contained, shielded power core, a device capable of delivering a perfectly stable current, isolated from the flickering city grid. Every component had to be perfect, every connection flawless. There would be no second chances.

Finally, the pieces were ready. On the wide, flat surface of the hydraulic press, under the anxious gaze of her two partners, Fizzlew-it began the final assembly. The shaped and polished ivory lens was carefully mounted in a brass housing cushioned by vibration-dampening gel. Pipette, using a set of impossibly fine silver tweezers, placed her shimmering crystals in a precise triangular formation around it. Fizzlewit then soldered the hair-thin copper wires that would channel the data, her touch so light it barely seemed to disturb the air. The grip, the dials for tuning the sensitivity, the connection to the shielded power core—each part clicked into place with a satisfying precision that was a small comfort amidst the looming chaos. The finished device looked like nothing Greensboro had ever seen: a robust, practical tool that held a heart of impossible magic. It was heavy in her hands, a weighted promise.

With a deep breath that did little to calm the frantic hammering in her chest, Fizzlew-it threw the activation switch. There was no grand surge of power, no flash of light. For a heart-stopping second, nothing happened. Then, a low, soft hum emanated from the device, a pure, clean tone that was the polar opposite of the Core’s discordant screaming. The ivory lens began to glow, not with the brilliant, violent light of the Calibrator, but with a soft, steady, internal luminescence, like a captured moonbeam. As Fizzlew-it aimed it at an empty patch of air in the center of the workshop, the resonoscope did something extraordinary. It did not display a reading on a gauge. It projected a holographic image, a faint, shimmering, three-dimensional latticework of light that floated in the dusty air. It was working. It was translating the invisible world of resonant energy into a language they could finally see.

The image was one of breathtaking, terrible beauty. It showed a vast, intricate web of silver-blue lines, constantly shifting and flowing, a map of the city’s healthy energy field, the song of the Archeo-Core made visible. But the web was damaged. Great, dark voids, like holes torn in a net, represented the districts where the power grid was failing. Jagged, angry-red lines of stress, like cracks in glass, radiated from a central point, showing the strain on the Core itself. It was worse than she had imagined. But as she adjusted the tuning dials, focusing on the signal coming from the direction of the shattered steam pipe, a new and more horrifying sight swam into view. It wasn't a crack or a void. It was a slow, pulsing bloom of deep, sickly violet light, a colour that seemed to absorb the light around it. It was a cancer on the grid, a harmonic corruption that was actively feeding on the surrounding energy, causing the crystalline brittleness in the metal. Gorgon’s cheese had been a loud, brutish poison. This was a quiet, insidious plague, an echo of his attack that had mutated into something new. They had defeated the monster, only to find its eggs hatching in the heart of the machine.The shimmering, holographic web hung in the dusty air of the workshop, a thing of terrible, mesmerizing beauty. Pipette stared, her alchemist’s mind, which thrived on quantifiable data and predictable reactions, utterly captivated and horrified. The silver-blue latticework of the city’s lifeblood was elegant and complex, but the pulsing, violet stain spreading from Sector Gamma was a thing of profound, cosmological wrongness. It did not just block the flow of energy; it seemed to feed on it, its edges fraying the healthy silver-blue lines, turning them brittle and grey. It was a disease made visible, a gangrenous wound on the soul of the city. Slyss, a goblin who had faced down cave-krakens and crushing pressure in the deep dark, took an involuntary step back, his hand resting on the hilt of the heavy wrench at his belt as if he could somehow beat the phantom light into submission. It was Fizzlewit who remained motionless, her knuckles white as she gripped the resonoscope. The dread she felt was cold and sharp. This was not the aftermath of an explosion; it was the slow, patient work of a cancer.

“Harmonic Blight,” Pipette breathed, the name coming to her unbidden. It was a term from obscure, theoretical alchemy, a concept for a plague that didn’t attack the body, but the very resonance that held matter together. “Gorgon’s enzyme was a catalyst, a blunt instrument. When we shattered it with the re-crystallization wave, the energy didn't just vanish. It seems a sliver of its chaotic signature was imprinted on the Core’s feedback loop. A scar. And now,” she gestured to the pulsing violet bloom, “the scar has become infected.” Her voice was a mixture of clinical fascination and raw fear. She understood the science of it, but the reality was a monster that had crawled out of her textbooks.

Slyss’s mind, ever the pragmatist, immediately translated the theory into a tactical reality. “So every time the Core pulses, it feeds this… Blight?” he asked, his gaze fixed on the ugly violet stain. Fizzlewit nodded, her throat tight. "Worse. The Blight is learning. It's adapting to the Core's frequency, attuning itself. The shattered pipe wasn't an accident. The Blight weakened the metal's resonant structure from the inside until the normal operational pressure was enough to shatter it. It's turning the city's own strength against it. The longer we wait, the more of the city’s infrastructure will become brittle. Power lines, support struts, the Great Forges themselves… anything connected to the Core’s network is a potential point of failure.” Slyss’s expression hardened. This was a language he understood. It was a siege from within, a war of attrition against the very bones of Greensboro.

There was no debate, no need for a council vote. A silent, grim consensus formed between the three of them in the heart of the reclaimed workshop. They had to go to the source. Fighting this plague from a distance was impossible. They had to see it, to understand its physical manifestation, to find a way to cut it out before it metastasized. Slyss became a whirlwind of quiet, efficient command. He dispatched two of his swiftest divers to scout the perimeter of Sector Gamma, not to engage, but to observe and report on any structural instabilities. Another team was tasked with preparing their gear: insulated suits to protect from stray energy discharges, heavy-duty cutting tools, and portable barricades. It was an insertion into a hostile, unpredictable environment, and the Sump-Divers prepared for it with the grim professionalism of seasoned veterans.

While Slyss managed the logistics of war, Pipette and Fizzlewit prepared for the work of surgeons. Pipette assembled a field kit of diagnostic tools that straddled the line between science and sorcery: vials that could capture ambient energy signatures, crystalline sensors that changed colour in the presence of harmonic dissonance, and a heavily shielded container for any physical samples they might retrieve. Fizzlewit, meanwhile, worked on the resonoscope. She couldn’t increase its power, but she could refine its focus. She carefully attached a secondary set of tuning dials and built a ruggedized harness that would allow her to carry it securely while keeping her hands free. Barnaby, who had been watching the frantic activity with a quiet intelligence, seemed to understand. When Fizzlewit finished the harness, he lowered his great head, allowing her to fit it over his broad shoulders, securing the resonoscope to his side like a piece of high-tech armor. He would be their transport, their mobile heavy support, and the one living creature among them whose very existence was a rebuke to the unnatural blight they were about to face.

Their journey through the city was a descent into a deepening sickness. The power fluctuations were no longer subtle flickers; whole blocks of gaslights would sputter and die as they passed, plunging streets into an unnerving gloom before flaring back to life with a sickly, over-bright intensity. A city-maintenance automaton, designed for sweeping the streets, stood frozen in the middle of a square, one of its articulated arms twitching uncontrollably, its gears grinding with a high-pitched, painful whine. Fizzlewit, riding high on Barnaby’s back, kept her hand on the resonoscope, feeling its low hum, a constant connection to the city’s ailing heart. As they drew closer to Sector Gamma, the hum of the device began to change, the pure note becoming marred by a faint, discordant undertone, like a beautiful voice straining against a wave of static. Barnaby felt it too. A low, unhappy rumble started deep in his chest, and his pace, usually so steady, became hesitant. He could sense the fundamental wrongness of the place they were approaching, a corruption that went deeper than sight or sound.

The Grand Confluence was not a room, but a cavernous, echoing nexus deep beneath the city, a place where the primary steam, water, and power conduits for half of Greensboro converged in a dizzying, three-dimensional tangle of iron, copper, and bronze. The air was a hot, wet blanket of steam, thick with the smell of hot metal and something else, a faint, acrid odor like burnt sugar and ozone. The source of the steam was immediately obvious: a colossal iron trunk, as thick as Barnaby’s body, had a gaping, crystalline wound in its side, from which superheated vapor billowed in a deafening, continuous roar. And swarming around the rupture, their bronze vests stark against the gloom, were a dozen of Thwacket’s Cog-Masters. They were under the command of a hulking, loud-mouthed foreman named Grolsch, who was bellowing orders as his crew struggled to position a massive, crude iron clamp over the shattered pipe.

The moment they saw Slyss’s divers emerge from the access tunnel, followed by the impossible apparition of Barnaby, the Cog-Masters froze, their tools held slack in their hands. Grolsch, his face red with exertion and fury, stormed towards them. “What in the name of the First Cog are you Sump-rats doing here? And you!” he jabbed a greasy finger at Fizzlewit. “This is a restricted engineering disaster zone! This is our jurisdiction! This failure is your fault, you and your magical light-show! Now take your pet and get out before you make things worse!” He spat on the ground, a gesture of profound contempt.

Fizzlewit ignored him. Her attention was captured by the resonoscope. She slid from Barnaby’s back and activated the projector. In the steam-filled air, the holographic image bloomed, more intense and more terrifying than it had been in the workshop. The violet blight was a raging, pulsating tumor of energy, centered directly on the shattered pipe. Its angry light seemed to drink the steam, and the jagged, red lines of stress in the surrounding energy field were multiplying with every passing second. With each impact, the violet bloom pulsed violently, and a new, fine crack spiderwebbed across the surface of the pipe, accompanied by a high-pitched, singing sound. Her blood ran cold. They weren’t fixing it. They were hitting it with a hammer. They were agitating the blight, feeding it with kinetic energy, and accelerating its growth at an exponential rate. “Stop!” she screamed, her voice barely audible over the roar of the steam. “Stop! You’re making it worse!”Grolsch’s meaty face, slick with steam and sweat, contorted into a sneer of utter contempt. He laughed, a harsh, barking sound that was almost lost in the deafening roar of the ruptured pipe. “Making it worse?” he bellowed, stepping so close Fizzlewit could smell the sour fungus-ale on his breath. “Listen to me, you little grease-rat. The Cog-Masters, their loyalty to their foreman and their procedures overriding the evidence of their own senses, obeyed. The rhythmic, brutal *thump-thump-thump* of the pneumatic hammers intensified, each impact sending a shuddering jolt through the metal floor. On the holographic display of the resonoscope, the violet bloom of the Harmonic Blight pulsed in agonized response to each blow. The angry red stress lines radiating from it multiplied, spreading through the silver-blue latticework like fire through a dry forest. That was the breaking point. Slyss didn’t shout an order or issue a challenge. He simply moved.
