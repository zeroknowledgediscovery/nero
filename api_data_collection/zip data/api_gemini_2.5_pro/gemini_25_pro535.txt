The singular, all-consuming thought that greeted Pipkin’s consciousness each morning was not of the rising sun, nor the sweet, cloying scent of honeysuckle that drifted through the dew-laced membrane of his window. Every tiny spoon, carved from mother-of-pearl, hung in a perfectly graded line. There were no stray crumbs on his moss-woven floor, no dust motes dancing in the shafts of light that pierced the canopy high above. A place for everything, and everything in its place. This was the creed by which Pipkin lived. Pipkin donned his flight cap, a polished beetle shell that gleamed like obsidian, and buzzed out of his front door, a perfectly round opening that could be sealed with a disc of dried mushroom.
