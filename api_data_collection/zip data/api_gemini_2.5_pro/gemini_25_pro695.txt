The clock was a tyrant. Not the gentle, ticking grandfather clock in his nan’s hallway, the one that smelled of beeswax and old paper, but a glaring digital monster, a merciless red eye counting down the seconds of his life. Finn O’Connell felt its glare on the back of his neck, a more potent force than the battery of studio lights heating the great white tent to the temperature of a low oven. His hands, usually steady on a thrashing tiller in a squall, trembled almost imperceptibly as he coaxed a final, delicate spiral of Italian meringue onto the peak of his creation. Flour and icing sugar had formed a fine white dust in the deep creases of his knuckles, a stark contrast to the permanent blue-black stain of engine grease under his nails that no amount of lemon juice and frantic scrubbing could ever fully vanquish. “One hour remaining, bakers!” Clara’s voice, a melody of forced cheerfulness, sliced through the thick, sugary air. He was deep in the zone, a state of hyper-focus he usually only found when plotting a course through treacherous currents, the salt spray on his face and the endless horizon his only companion. Here, the horizon was a wall of cameras and the current was a fickle sea of sea-salt caramel that threatened to split at the slightest provocation.

His semi-final showstopper was, perhaps predictably, a monument to the sea. He called it ‘The Farallon Lighthouse’. A towering croquembouche, not the traditional cone, but sculpted into the stout, whitewashed cylinder of the lighthouse that stood sentinel thirty miles off the San Francisco coast. Each choux bun was a perfect, golden brick; the spun sugar was the crystalline lantern room, and the caramel holding it all together was the colour of a Pacific sunset. Inside, a core of crème pâtissière, flavoured not just with vanilla but with a whisper of cardamom and orange blossom, a nod to the exotic spices he’d smelled in far-flung ports. It was ambitious. It was, according to Evelyn, the razor-sharp architect baking at the station to his left, “structurally unsound.” He’d simply grinned at her. He knew a thing or two about structural integrity. His fourteen-metre ketch, the *Sea Serpent*, had seen him through a tempest off Cape Horn. He was fairly certain he could handle a bit of pastry.

He was a sailor from San Jose. The sentence still sounded like a punchline to him. A mariner from the landlocked heart of Silicon Valley. It wasn’t strictly true, of course. He’d grown up spending weekends and summers in Santa Cruz, his father a part-time fisherman with a love for the ocean that was more religion than hobby. Finn had inherited it, magnified it. While his school friends were coding in their garages, dreaming of IPOs and stock options, Finn was dreaming of trade winds and archipelagos. He’d saved every penny from odd jobs—fixing sprinkler systems in the sprawling suburban lawns of tech billionaires, bussing tables at soulless chain restaurants—and bought the *Sea Serpent* when he was twenty-two. On long solo voyages, the meticulous, precise nature of baking was an anchor for his mind. Weighing flour, proving dough, the alchemy of turning simple ingredients into something warm and life-affirming… it was a small, controllable world on a vessel tossed about by an uncontrollable one. He’d gotten the apron.

“Bakers, your time is up! Step away from your benches!”

His heart hammered against his ribs. It was done. His lighthouse stood, a shimmering tower of pastry and hope, the golden crack in its lantern room catching the light. The judging was a blur. Dame Beatrice was first. “Yes, Alistair,” Finn said, his mouth dry. “A moment of instability.”

Finn felt his hopes sink. Adequate. The death knell of Bake Off. The four semi-finalists stood in a line, awaiting the verdict. Clara and Jude stood before them, their usual levity replaced with a practiced solemnity. “This has been,” Clara began, “one of the most difficult semi-finals we have ever had.”

Of course. A small, polite smile touched her lips. She gave a curt nod.

“The second person joining Evelyn in the final is…”

Finn braced himself. He pictured his boat, bobbing in its slip in the Berkeley marina. He could be there by the end of the week.

The world tilted on its axis. He heard a roaring in his ears that had nothing to do with the ocean. A sailor from San Jose was in the final of the Great Albion Bake-Off. A wide, incredulous grin spread across his face, a grin so big it felt like it might crack his jaw. The great white tent faded away, and in his mind, he was back in his workshop, planning the overhaul of a complex engine. The base layer, the dark chocolate and chili ocean, was baked and cooling. The dense, rum-soaked fruitcake that would form the hull of his ship was ready. He began the assembly, a feat of edible engineering. He panelled the fruitcake with dark gingerbread, carefully cut into planks. But under the hot studio lights, nothing was simple. One of the gingerbread panels cracked as he lifted it. His ganache, a little too warm, threatened to spill from between the layers, a black tide of chocolate. He felt a flicker of the old panic. Steady as she goes, O’Connell. He looked at the cracked plank and the oozing ganache, and saw not a disaster, but an opportunity. He wasn't building a pristine model; he was building the *Sea Serpent*, a vessel that bore the scars of a hundred storms. He used the dark ganache as caulking, pressing it into the gaps between the gingerbread planks, reinforcing the cracked piece. It looked rugged, authentic, and strong.

With an hour left, he began the most delicate part: the sky. He placed the light lemon and cardamom tier on top and began the painstaking work of piping the celestial map in royal icing. He chose the constellations of the Southern Cross and Orion, the stars that had guided him across hemispheres. His hand, usually so steady on a tiller, began to tremble with fatigue and adrenaline. The fine lines of icing wavered. He paused, closing his eyes, picturing himself on the deck of his boat at night, the immense, silent peace of the stars above. He took a breath, anchored himself in that memory, and continued to pipe. The lines weren't perfect, but they were true. They were his.

“Five minutes remaining, bakers!” Clara’s voice shattered his concentration. A final flurry of activity. He placed the last silver leaf star on his map, stood back, and took it all in. The dark, unpredictable ocean base; the sturdy, scarred fruitcake ship; the hopeful, star-dusted sky above. It wasn't just a cake. It was a voyage. It was patched-up, scarred, a little bit lopsided, and profoundly, undeniably his. The clock hit zero. "Time is up!" Finn laid his piping bag down, his hands trembling now with exhaustion.
