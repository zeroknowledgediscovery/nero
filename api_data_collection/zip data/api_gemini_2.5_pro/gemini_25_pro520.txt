The silence was the punchline. It always was, these days. Barnaby Glick stood on the grimy stage of "The Last Laugh," a club whose name had gone from ironic to prophetic in the space of a single, shimmering afternoon two years ago. The single spotlight cast his shadow long and gaunt against the brick wall, a dark smear that looked vaguely like a man who’d been told a terrible joke and was still waiting for the part that was supposed to be funny. His audience, a sparse collection of about thirty men scattered through the gloom, stared back at him with the collective energy of a wet bathmat.

"So, my toaster's been acting up," Barney said into the microphone, his voice a dry rasp. "It's got this setting, right? Goes from one to six. I used to keep it on four. Lena—my wife—she liked three. A gentle browning. Me, I like a bit of structural integrity. A bit of crunch. We'd have these little… toaster-based disagreements. You know the kind."

A man in the front row coughed, a wet, rattling sound that seemed to echo in the cavernous quiet. Barney flinched, but kept the rictus of a smile plastered on his face.

"Anyway, now… now I just crank it to six. Every time. I'm talking volcanic. Carbonized. I eat black squares of what used to be bread. And you know what? There's nobody there to tell me it's a fire hazard. Nobody to argue for the gentle browning." He paused, letting the silence hang, thick and heavy. "Freedom, fellas. It tastes like charcoal."

Nothing. Not a chuckle. Not a groan. Just the low hum of the beer fridge and the distant, lonely wail of a siren slicing through the night. The world outside was falling apart at the seams, and here he was, trying to get a laugh out of the dregs of mankind with bits about burnt toast. It was pathetic. His old material, the stuff about airline food and the DMV and the strange habits of his wife, it was all useless now. Every premise was predicated on a world that had a full complement of chromosomes. A world with women in it.

Two years, three months, and six days since the Great Unveiling. That’s what the news-drones called it. One moment, Lena was in the garden, trying to coax a rosebush back to life. The next, a flash of light so pure and absolute it bleached the sky white from horizon to horizon. When vision returned, she was still there, but not. A figure of incandescent light, her features softened into an expression of serene, terrifying indifference. Wings, vast and feathered with impossible luminescence, had unfurled from her back, tearing through her favourite cardigan. She had looked at him, not with recognition, but with a kind of distant pity, the way you might look at a bug trapped in amber. Then, she, and every other woman on the planet, from newborns to the ancient, had simply… ascended. They hadn't gone to a place. They’d become a place. A new, silent constellation in the sky, shimmering just beyond the veil of the day, and blazing like a million cold diamonds at night. They were there. They were beautiful. And they were gone.

They left behind angel-shaped holes in the world. And a lot of confused, angry, grieving men. Society, as it turned out, was a finely-tuned instrument that required both hands to play. With one hand gone, all that was left was a clumsy, discordant thumping. Power grids failed. Supply chains crumbled. Crime wasn't so much a wave as a new atmospheric condition. You just breathed it in now. The cops, or what was left of them, were too busy barricading their own precincts to bother with things like a mugging or a break-in. It was every man for himself, in a world that was suddenly, horrifically, full of them.

"Thank you, you've been… an audience," Barney mumbled, dropping the mic back into its stand with a clatter. He didn't wait for the smattering of confused applause. He just shuffled off stage, collected his threadbare coat, and pushed his way out the emergency exit into an alley that smelled of despair and stagnant water.

The city was a mess of shadows and flickering fires. Looting was a nightly ritual. Gangs of men, bonded by nothing more than desperation and proximity, roamed the streets, picking the bones of the old world clean. Barney kept his head down, his hands shoved deep in his pockets, his fingers tracing the outline of a single, heavy, cold key. It was a key to a problem, or maybe just a different, more interesting kind of disaster.

His house was a small, two-story affair in a neighborhood that was rapidly losing its battle with entropy. The garden where Lena had ascended was overgrown with triumphant weeds, the rosebush she’d tended now a thorny, skeletal wreck. He didn't go in the front door. He went around back, unlocked the thick metal-reinforced door to the basement, and descended into the humming darkness.

This was his real stage. His real audience. The Infinate Stabler.

It was a preposterous machine, a monument to hubris and bad soldering. Barney had built it over a decade with a theoretical physicist he'd met in a poker game, a man who had, with startling inconvenience, been married. The physicist’s wife was now a particularly bright speck of light somewhere over the South Pacific. The physicist himself had walked into the ocean a week after the Unveiling. But the machine remained.

It was supposed to be a probability engine. A way to peek into adjacent realities. The physicist had called it a "Quantum State Stabilizer." Barney, in a fit of drunken marketing genius, had scrawled "The Infinate Stabler" on the main console in permanent marker, misspelling and all. The name had stuck. It looked like the unholy lovechild of a mainframe computer and a scrap yard, a hulking behemoth of scavenged copper piping, cathode-ray tubes, and miles of tangled wiring that filled the entire back wall. It hummed with the energy of a thousand trapped wasps and smelled of ozone, burnt sugar, and a peculiar sort of metallic regret. The theory was simple, if insane: by pumping an enormous amount of energy into a localized point, you could momentarily weaken the walls between universes and ‘stabilize’ a quantum duplicate of a person or object from a nearby reality, pulling it into this one. It was hideously dangerous, monumentally unstable, and had an energy bill that could bankrupt a small nation, if small nations and banks were still a thing.

Barney hadn't touched it since the Unveiling. What was the point? To pull a version of Lena from a world where she hadn't turned into a celestial body? A Lena who wouldn’t know him, who would be terrified and alone? It felt like a violation. A desecration of his own grief.

But the world was ending in slow motion, and he was tired of just watching. If the angels wouldn't save them, if the government couldn't, if the police wouldn't… then someone had to. And a comedian wasn't exactly equipped for the job. But a comedian with a sidekick? That was a classic. Abbott and Costello. Martin and Lewis. A straight man and a funny man. A hero and… the other guy. Barney figured he could be the other guy. He just needed a hero.

He ran through the start-up sequence, the familiar thrum of the capacitors charging a grim comfort. He bypassed three different safety protocols that the physicist had insisted were "non-negotiable." The world was ending. Negotiating with physics felt redundant. The target parameters were a nightmare to calibrate. He didn't want a god. He didn't want a monster. He just needed someone… capable. He tweaked the dials, trying to isolate for variables like 'bravery,' 'physical fitness,' and 'a strong moral compass.' The machine whined, the numbers on the primary screen flickering erratically. It was like trying to tune a radio to a station that played nothing but screaming. Close enough, he figured.

He stood before the stabilization pad, a large, circular plate of scorched metal in the center of the room. He took a deep breath, the air thick with anticipation. He was a man standing on the precipice of a terrible idea, and the view was breathtaking. He slammed a large, red, comically oversized button on the console.

The Stabler roared. Not a hum, a full-throated, world-shaking roar. The lights in the basement flickered and died, replaced by a blinding, strobing flash from the stabilization pad. The smell of ozone was overpowering now, sharp and clean and violent. A column of shimmering, heat-hazy air coalesced over the metal plate, twisting and writhing like a captured ghost. Barney shielded his eyes, a lunatic’s grin spreading across his face. It was working. It was actually work—

And then, as quickly as it began, it stopped. The roar died. The light vanished. The emergency battery lights flickered on, casting the room in a sickly yellow glow. And standing on the stabilization pad, looking utterly bewildered, was a man.

He was short, round, and balding, dressed in a pristine, light-blue polo shirt and khaki pants. He blinked, adjusting to the dim light, and clutched a neatly folded piece of white fabric to his chest like a holy relic.

Barney stared. This was it? This was his hero? He looked like he'd just been pulled from a Sunday barbecue in a dimension where cholesterol was a myth. "Who… who are you?" Barney stammered.

The man looked down at his polo shirt, then back at Barney. "I'm Gregory," he said, his voice soft and placid. "I was just… I think I finally got it."

"Got what?" Barney asked, his heart sinking. "The call to adventure? The sacred duty to protect the innocent?"

Gregory’s face lit up with a beatific smile. He held up the folded fabric, presenting it with the reverence of a priest. "The fitted sheet," he said, his voice filled with a profound and unshakable sense of accomplishment. "I finally figured out how to fold a king-sized fitted sheet into a perfect, crisp square. The secret is the corners. You have to make little puppets with your hands…"

Barney stared at the man. At the neatly folded sheet. At the massive, reality-bending machine that had consumed his life's savings and a good portion of his sanity. He had tried to pull a knight in shining armor from the ether of infinite possibility. Instead, the universe, in its boundless and cruel sense of humor, had sent him a man whose only discernible skill was advanced laundry. The silence that followed was deeper and more profound than any he had ever experienced on stage. It wasn't a punchline. It was the whole damn joke.Barney did the only thing he could do. He laughed. It wasn't the practiced, rumbling chuckle he used on stage to coax a response from a dead room. It was a raw, hacking sound, torn from the depths of his chest, thin and full of holes. It was the sound of a man who had stared into the abyss of infinite possibility and the abyss had shown him its laundry basket. The laugh clawed its way up his throat and died in a wheezing gasp. He leaned against the humming console of the Infinate Stabler, the metal cool against his sweaty forehead, and stared at the mild-mannered man who was the universe's final, cruelest joke at his expense.

"The corners," Barney repeated, his voice flat. "You have to make puppets." He looked from Gregory's earnest, beaming face to the crisply folded sheet. It was, he had to admit, a masterpiece of domestic engineering. Not a wrinkle in sight. A perfect, cream-colored rectangle. It was the kind of thing Lena would have appreciated. The thought was a fresh stab of pain, sharp and unwelcome. He had tried to summon a champion, a warrior, a leader of men. The machine, in its infinite wisdom and shoddy construction, had instead given him a house husband from a dimension of supreme tranquility.

Gregory nodded enthusiastically, oblivious to the existential crisis unfolding before him. "Exactly! You put your hands in the two top corners, like little sock puppets, and then you bring them together and fold one over the other. It contains the elastic. Once you've contained the elastic, the rest is just simple geometry. I've been trying to get it right for years. My wife, Carol, she's a wizard at it. She'd just sort of… shake it, and it would fold itself. I always suspected witchcraft." He chuckled, a soft, pleasant sound that was utterly alien in the grim basement. "I can't wait to show her."

The words hung in the air. *Can't wait to show her.* Barney felt a wave of nausea. How did you explain to a man who measured his life in thread counts that his entire world, his Carol and her linen-witchcraft, was now on the other side of an impenetrable cosmic wall? How did you tell him he was a refugee, not of a country, but of a reality? "Gregory," Barney began, his voice cracking. "About your wife… about… everything. You're not in Kansas anymore. Or, you know, whatever equivalent of Kansas has nice barbecues and a thriving textile industry."

Before Gregory could ask what he meant, a new sound cut through the hum of the basement. A heavy thump from the floor above. Then another, followed by the splintering crack of wood. Barney froze, every muscle in his body tensing. The Stabler's roar had been a dinner bell. He’d known it, intellectually, but the sheer stupidity of his new reality had distracted him. Looters. Or worse, one of the organized gangs that had carved the city into a patchwork of predatory fiefdoms. He held a finger to his lips, his eyes wide with genuine fear, a stark contrast to the performative despair of his stand-up routine.

Gregory’s pleasant smile faltered, replaced by a look of mild concern. "Is someone upstairs? It sounds like they're being quite forceful with the door." He lowered his voice to a conspiratorial whisper. "Did you lock it? I find a good deadbolt is a wonderful deterrent. We had one installed last spring after a string of raccoon-related incidents in the neighborhood."

"It's not raccoons, Gregory," Barney hissed, grabbing the smaller man by the arm of his pristine polo shirt. Gregory felt surprisingly solid, soft but dense, like a well-made pillow. Barney’s mind was racing. The Stabler was useless now, a mountain of junk that needed hours, maybe days, to recharge, assuming it hadn't just fried itself into a permanent sculpture dedicated to failure. They couldn't stay here. His house was no longer a sanctuary; it was a target. The emergency exit he'd used to leave the club led to an alley. His basement had a similar escape route, a small, reinforced door leading to a crawlspace under the back porch that opened into the overgrown garden. It was their only chance.

"We have to go. Now," Barney said, tugging Gregory toward the back of the basement. "Don't talk. Don't make a sound. Just follow me." The crashing upstairs was louder now, accompanied by coarse laughter and the shattering of glass. They were inside. They were tearing his house apart, the house he and Lena had built, filled with memories that were now being trampled by muddy boots. For a moment, a hot, useless rage filled him, but fear was a more practical emotion. Fear kept you moving.

Gregory, however, was not moving. He was pulling back against Barney's grip, his face a mask of confusion. "But… my sheet," he said, his voice laced with panic as Barney's tugging threatened the integrity of his perfect fold. "And shouldn't we call the police? This seems like a matter for the authorities."

The question was so innocent, so profoundly, achingly stupid, that Barney nearly laughed again. "The authorities?" he whispered, his voice trembling with a cocktail of terror and black humor. "Gregory, the authorities are currently a guy named 'Spike' who's probably leading the charge upstairs to see if my Blu-ray player is worth trading for a can of beans." He pulled harder. "The sheet is not important. Your continued existence on this plane of reality is slightly more pressing. Now move!"

He half-dragged, half-shoved Gregory toward the small, steel-plated door at the far end of the basement. The key was in his pocket, the same cold, heavy key he'd been fiddling with in the alley. His hands shook as he fumbled with the lock, the sounds from upstairs growing more violent. A heavy object crashed to the floor, and he pictured the antique vase Lena's grandmother had given them, now a thousand shards of porcelain on the hardwood. He got the door open, revealing a dark, spiderweb-choked tunnel. The air that wafted out was damp and smelled of earth and decay. "In here," he ordered, giving Gregory a final, desperate shove into the darkness. Gregory stumbled forward, still clutching his folded sheet like a shield. Barney scrambled in after him, pulling the heavy door shut just as the basement door at the top of the stairs burst open, silhouetting three large, menacing figures against the flickering lights of the dying house. The last thing Barney saw before he plunged the crawlspace into absolute blackness was one of the men holding a lit torch. A real, honest-to-god torch, like a villager from a Frankenstein movie. Freedom, fellas. It tastes like charcoal and smells like a fire hazard.The crawlspace was the belly of the beast, a coffin of packed earth and stone foundations. It was a darkness so complete it felt like a physical pressure against Barney’s eyeballs. He could smell the damp soil, the faint, sweet rot of a century of dead insects, and the acrid tang of his own fear-sweat. Beside him, Gregory breathed with the placid rhythm of a man untroubled by anything more pressing than a poorly timed commercial break. A line of yellow light suddenly appeared under the heavy door they’d just sealed, followed by a low, hungry crackle. The smell of smoke, sharp and invasive, began to snake its way into their tomb.

"They're burning the house," Barney whispered, the words catching in his throat. It wasn’t a strategic move to flush them out; it was just what men did now. They broke things, and when they were done breaking them, they burned them. It was the only creative impulse left in the world.

"That seems terribly shortsighted," Gregory whispered back, his voice a gentle hum of disapproval. "Think of the property values." Barney didn't answer. He just grabbed the back of Gregory's polo shirt and shoved him forward, into the deeper dark. The tunnel was narrow, forcing them to a crab-like scuttle. Brittle cobwebs, thick as yarn, broke across their faces, and Barney felt the dry tickle of a spider scurrying down his neck. He slapped at it, his heart hammering against his ribs. Gregory, clumsy and unaccustomed to anything but level, polished floors, kept bumping into the earthen walls, sending little cascades of dirt showering down on them. He was a liability made of soft flesh and polite inquiries.

They reached the end of the tunnel, a latticework panel that served as a flimsy barrier to the outside world. Barney could see the skeletal fingers of overgrown weeds poking through the gaps, silhouetted against a flickering orange glow. His house was truly going up now. He pushed the panel, and it swung open with a protest of squealing, rusted hinges. The night air hit them, cool and thick with smoke. Above them, the impossible feminine constellation blazed, each point of light a stolen wife, a vanished daughter, a missing mother. They looked down on the world they had abandoned with the serene, infuriating beauty of a Renaissance painting. Barney dragged Gregory out into the chaos of what was once Lena's garden.

Gregory stood, meticulously brushing dirt from his khakis with his free hand, the other still clutching his precious folded sheet. Then he looked up and finally took in the scene. He saw the flames chewing at the frame of his interdimensional taxi. He saw the glow of other, more distant fires painting the bellies of the clouds. And he saw the sky, that impossible, silent audience of ascended women. His face, which had cycled through bewilderment and mild annoyance, finally settled on a deep, profound state of shock. "Oh, dear," he murmured, his voice barely a whisper. "This is going to be very difficult to explain to the homeowner's association."

Barney finally snapped. He spun Gregory around, his hands gripping the man’s soft shoulders. The fabric of the polo shirt was absurdly high-quality. "There is no homeowner's association!" he snarled, his voice a raw, desperate hiss. "There is no Carol! There are no police! There are no goddamn raccoons! They're all gone, Gregory! Gone!" He let go, one hand gesturing wildly at the fiery ruin of his home and the sky full of silent gods. "Two years ago, there was a flash of light. The Great Unveiling, the news-drones called it. Every living thing with a pair of X chromosomes just… left. They turned into that." He pointed a trembling finger at the sky. "They ascended. They became a place. And they left us here in the rubble to rot."

Gregory stared at Barney, then back up at the sky, his brow furrowed in intense concentration. He wasn’t screaming. He wasn’t crying. He was processing, the way a computer processes an impossible command, a "divide by zero" error in the code of reality. "Ascended," he repeated, the word alien in his mouth. "All of them? But… why? Was there a clerical error? A… a cosmic recall?" The question was so earnest, so rooted in a world of warranties and customer service, that Barney felt a fresh wave of despair wash over him. This wasn't a hero. This wasn't even a sidekick. This was the bewildered customer who had wandered onto the battlefield asking for a manager.

"Let's go with 'cosmic recall'," Barney grunted, the fight draining out of him. "Now, we have to move before the recalled products' angry husbands show up for the after-party." He grabbed Gregory’s arm again, pulling him through the thorny remains of the rosebush and into the alley. The only place he could think to go was the one place he'd been avoiding for two years: the apartment of the physicist who had helped him build the machine. Dr. Aris Thorne. A brilliant, broken man who had designed a universe-hopper and couldn't live in a world without his wife. His apartment was in a sturdier part of town, and maybe, just maybe, it was still untouched.

Navigating the nocturnal city was a masterclass in fear management. Barney led them through a rat's maze of darkened streets and stinking alleys, his every sense on high alert. He moved with a hunted animal's grace, born of two years of practice. Gregory, by contrast, moved like a piece of furniture being delivered. He stumbled over bags of trash, crunched loudly on broken glass, and marveled audibly at the sheer scale of the decay. "My goodness," he'd whisper, "The municipal sanitation services have really let things slide." Barney had to resist the urge to abandon him in a dumpster.

Their luck almost ran out on a wide boulevard littered with burned-out cars. A group of five men were huddled around a barrel fire, passing a bottle back and forth. Their faces were lean and predatory in the flickering light, their laughter like grinding stones. Barney yanked Gregory into the deep shadow of a defunct bank's ATM enclosure, clapping a hand over the smaller man's mouth before he could offer a cheerful greeting. Gregory’s eyes went wide over Barney's dirty fingers as he watched the men. One of them kicked a loose hubcap, sending it skittering across the pavement with a shriek of metal. The sound was casual, thoughtless, and more terrifying than any scream. They waited, hearts pounding in the suffocating silence of the doorway, until the gang ambled off in search of other things to break. When Barney finally released him, Gregory was trembling, his folded sheet held so tightly his knuckles were white.

They finally reached Aris Thorne’s building, a grim, concrete block of sixties-era optimism that had soured into a brutalist fortress. The main entrance was a gaping hole, but the reinforced inner lobby door, a relic of a more paranoid time, was still in place. Barney led Gregory around back to a service entrance he knew from long nights spent hauling equipment. A bit of work with a tire iron he’d been carrying for just such an occasion, and they were in. The air inside was stale and cold, thick with the undisturbed dust of years. They climbed six flights of stairs in pitch darkness, Gregory panting softly behind him, and found Thorne's apartment, 6B. Barney’s fingers, surprisingly nimble, worked the simple lock with a thin piece of metal from his wallet. The door clicked open.

He shoved it inward and they stumbled into the apartment. It was a time capsule. Books and papers lay exactly where the physicist had left them. A coffee cup sat on the desk, a disc of pale green mold blooming within it. The place was a mausoleum for a life interrupted. Barney leaned back against the door, his body screaming with exhaustion and adrenaline. He slid down to the floor, his head thunking against the wood. He looked over at his companion. Gregory, with the utmost care, was placing his perfectly folded, king-sized fitted sheet onto the center of a dusty, glass-topped coffee table. He spent a long moment smoothing its surface, his expression one of serene focus, as if this one small act of order could hold the encroaching chaos of a ruined universe at bay. Barney had wanted a savior. He’d gotten a curator for the apocalypse. He closed his eyes. The joke wasn't funny anymore. It was just long.Dawn filtered through the grime-caked windows of the apartment, a weak, grey light that seemed apologetic for its own existence. Barney hadn't moved. He lay on the floor, using his balled-up coat as a pillow, listening to the slow, steady drip of a faucet somewhere in the apartment. It was the only sound besides his own ragged breathing and the distant, mournful cry of the city. He felt hollowed out, a shell of a man in the shell of an apartment within the shell of a city. He had tried to play God with a junkyard and a half-baked theory, and his punishment was not fire and brimstone, but Gregory. Gregory, who had eventually fallen asleep in a plush armchair, his perfectly folded sheet resting on his lap like a sleeping cat.

A soft shuffling sound brought Barney’s attention back to the present. Gregory was awake, blinking in the dim light. He stood up, stretched with a small groan, and surveyed the dusty living room with the mild disapproval of a man discovering a smudge on his reading glasses. His eyes fell on the coffee table, where he had placed the sheet. A fine layer of dust had already settled on its pristine surface. He looked at Barney, his expression one of polite concern. “Excuse me,” he said, his voice a soft, morning whisper. “I don’t suppose Dr. Thorne kept any furniture polish under the sink? A nice lemon scent, perhaps? This place could really use a bit of a freshen-up.”

Barney stared at him from the floor. He didn’t have the energy for anger, or even for sarcasm. All he had was a vast, empty plain of exhaustion. “Gregory,” he said, his voice a gravelly ruin. “The man walked into the ocean. I doubt he was worried about dusting.” The statement seemed to give Gregory pause, not because of its tragic implications, but as if he were genuinely considering the logistical challenges of maintaining a tidy home while planning a suicide. He simply nodded, a thoughtful look on his face, and began to carefully refold his sheet, as if to protect it from the apartment’s ambient despair.

With a groan that seemed to emanate from his very soul, Barney pushed himself to his feet. Survival was a habit, and a hard one to break. He stumbled into the small kitchen. He twisted the tap and a stream of brownish water sputtered out. It smelled of rust and stagnation, but it was wet. He found two glasses, rinsed them with the dubious water, and filled them. He handed one to Gregory, who accepted it with a grateful nod before peering into it suspiciously. In the back of a cupboard, behind a box of fossilized cereal, Barney found his prize: three cans of cling peaches in heavy syrup and a can of cold-brew coffee concentrate. It was a breakfast of champions, if the championship was for staving off scurvy for another 72 hours. He punctured the lids with the tip of the tire iron and they sat at the dusty dining table, scooping peaches out of a can with their fingers.

“You know,” Gregory said thoughtfully, his chin glistening with syrup. “I find that canned peaches are always a bit mushy. For a real treat, you need them fresh, sliced over a nice vanilla bean ice cream. Carol—my wife—she makes a wonderful peach cobbler. The secret is a dash of nutmeg in the crust.” He smiled at the memory, a brief, sunny expression that was immediately swallowed by the gloom of the apartment. “She will be so worried. I was only meant to be in the laundry room.”

The simple, domestic image was so painful, so alien to Barney’s current existence, that he had to look away. He abandoned his peaches and walked into the physicist's study. This was the real reason he’d come here. Aris Thorne had been a brilliant man, a man who saw the universe not as a collection of things, but as a symphony of vibrating probabilities. His mind had been a beautiful, terrifying place, and his notes were the only map. The room was dominated by a large blackboard, a chaotic spiderweb of chalk equations that seemed to track the dying scream of a stable reality. Barney ran his hand over the desk, leaving a clean streak in the thick dust. He had to know if Aris had figured anything out before the end. He had to know if there was any point to any of this, beyond burnt toast and bad jokes.

He began his search, pulling books from shelves and sifting through stacks of papers. It was mostly academic jargon, quantum field theory and M-brane hypotheses that made his head spin. Gregory wandered in behind him, still holding his can of peaches. He peered at the blackboard, his head cocked to one side. "Goodness, that's a lot of math," he observed. "All those little symbols. It looks very official. I tried to help my nephew with his algebra once. It did not go well. So many letters pretending to be numbers. I find it fundamentally dishonest." He took another bite of peach. "This coffee is very strong."

Underneath the desk, half-hidden by a fallen stack of journals, was a heavy, olive-drab metal footlocker. It was secured with a serious-looking padlock. Barney felt a jolt of something that might have been hope. He fetched the tire iron. “Stand back,” he grunted, wedging the sharp end into the lock’s hasp. The noise was shocking in the silent apartment, a series of loud, metallic shrieks as he put his weight into it. Each groan of the metal was a fresh wave of anxiety, a sound that could carry for blocks in the dead city. Finally, with a sharp crack, the lock gave way.

He threw open the heavy lid. The contents were not what he expected. There was no doomsday device, no miracle weapon. There was a half-empty bottle of expensive scotch, a thick wool blanket, and a small, leather-bound journal. On top of the journal sat a single, thick envelope. Scrawled across the front in Thorne’s spiky, agitated handwriting was one word: "Barnaby." His blood ran cold. He picked it up, his fingers trembling slightly. Aris had known he would come. He had left him a final message. He tore it open. The letter inside was two pages, filled with the same frantic script as the blackboard.

*Barnaby,* it began, *If you are reading this, then you are a more sentimental fool than I gave you credit for, and the world is in an even worse state than I imagined. I couldn't stay. I saw her face in the numbers. I saw the cold, perfect math of her ascension. She's a constant now, a fixed point in a new celestial map. Beautiful, and utterly beyond reach. Trying to live with that knowledge is like trying to drink from a river of fire.*

*The Stabler was a child's toy, a blunt instrument. I was trying to knock on the door of the next apartment, and you, my friend, have apparently kicked it in and dragged the neighbor out in his pajamas. Do try to be a good host.*

*But I was wrong about the Unveiling. In my grief, I saw it only as a departure. It wasn't. It was a conversion. A transfiguration of unimaginable energy. And such an event, Barney, would not be clean. It would have a ground zero. It would leave a scar not on the world, but *in* the world. A place where the fabric of reality was worn thin, where the echoes of that transformation might still be audible. I ran the simulations. The energy release was not uniform; it followed a wave pattern that converged on a single terrestrial point. A place where the veil between what is, what was, and what could be, might be thin enough to whisper through.*

*I have no use for this information. My journey is in a different direction. But you... you always did have a perverse need for a punchline. Perhaps you'll find one there. Or perhaps you'll just find more silence. Either way, it's a destination. Something the world is in short supply of these days.*

Below the text was a string of coordinates. Barney’s heart hammered in his chest. A destination. A premise. He looked up from the letter, his eyes wild. He looked at the chaos of the study, the ruin of the city outside, and then at Gregory, who had finished his peaches and was now carefully trying to wipe his sticky fingers on a dusty curtain. For the first time in two years, three months, and seven days, Barney Glick felt the ghost of a purpose. It was a terrible, insane, and likely suicidal purpose, but it was his.

"Gregory," he said, his voice stronger than it had been in years. "How do you feel about a road trip?"

Gregory turned from the curtain, his hand still poised in mid-air. He blinked, considering the question with the solemnity it deserved. "A road trip?" he mused. "Well, that does sound like a bit of an adventure. I've always wanted to see the Grand Canyon. Will we be going near the Grand Canyon? And more importantly," he added, looking down at his slightly sticky fingers, "will there be facilities with adequate hand-washing stations along the way?"Barney looked at the man who was worried about hand-washing stations at the end of the world, and a strange calm settled over him. The universe hadn't just sent him a joke; it had sent him the straight man, too. It was a classic setup. The frantic, desperate schemer and the placid, oblivious partner. He was Abbott, and the universe had just delivered his Costello, albeit one obsessed with thread counts and stubborn stains. He let out a short, sharp bark of a laugh that held no humor, only a kind of weary recognition.

"No, Gregory. No Grand Canyon," he said, folding Thorne's letter and tucking it securely into his inside pocket. The paper felt warm against his chest, a fragile blueprint for an impossible journey. "We're going to a place… a place where the big thing happened. The Unveiling. Dr. Thorne thought it left a scar. A weak spot. I don't know what we'll find there. Maybe answers. Maybe a way to talk to them." He gestured vaguely toward the ceiling, toward the silent constellation of lost women. "Maybe it's just a crater in the middle of nowhere. But it's somewhere to go. And right now, that's better than here."

Gregory considered this, his head tilted. He processed the information not with fear or excitement, but with a quiet, organizational logic. "A scar," he repeated, testing the word. "Well, scars need tending. And a long drive requires preparation." He began to tick items off on his fingers. "We'll need a reliable vehicle, preferably with good lumbar support. Provisions, of course. Water is a priority. And a first-aid kit. I am quite particular about my first-aid kits. You can't just throw a few bandages in a tin and call it a day. You need antiseptic wipes, butterfly closures, a good pain reliever…" He trailed off, lost in the pleasant minutiae of emergency preparedness. Barney stared at him, a man planning a family vacation to the heart of the abyss.

Their first task was the vehicle. Barney knew better than to hope for anything on the street. The viable cars had been claimed or wrecked in the first few weeks; the rest had been cannibalized for parts, leaving skeletal remains picked over by the desperate. The only real hope lay underground, in the concrete catacombs of the city's multi-story car parks. He led Gregory to one such structure, a brutalist spiral plunging into the earth, its entrance choked with debris. The air grew colder as they descended, the silence amplifying the drip of water and the scuttling of unseen things. It was a mausoleum for a world obsessed with commuting.

They moved through the gloom, their footsteps echoing. Every car was a monument to a life cut short. A minivan with a 'Baby on Board' sticker, its windows smashed, the interior stripped bare. A sleek sports car, its tires slashed, its leather seats methodically shredded. For Barney, each one was a ghost. For Gregory, it was a slow-motion car show. "A sedan," he'd murmur, running a hand over a dusty hood. "Practical, but they never quite had the trunk space you'd want for a large grocery run." He peered into a shattered window. "Oh, and this model had the worst blind spot. Positively hazardous during a lane change." His commentary was a bizarre, mundane soundtrack to their descent into hell.

On the lowest level, P-4, where the air was thick with the smell of mold and old oil, they found it. Tucked away in a corner, behind a concrete pillar, sat a twenty-year-old Volvo station wagon. It was the color of faded mustard and shaped like the box it came in. It was also, miraculously, intact. No smashed windows, no slashed tires. It was so unassuming, so profoundly uncool, that it had been completely ignored. It was the automotive equivalent of a man who knew how to fold a fitted sheet. It was perfect. The driver's side door was locked. Barney grinned, a tight, feral expression, and produced the tire iron. With a sickening crunch of metal and a pop, the door was open.

The interior smelled of old coffee, stale dog, and the faint, lingering scent of a pine-tree air freshener. Barney slid into the driver's seat, the worn fabric rough against his back. He put his hands on the wheel. It felt solid. Real. He turned the key. Nothing. The dashboard remained dark, the engine silent. A quick look at the fuel gauge confirmed his fears: the needle was resting firmly on empty. A dead battery and an empty tank. It was a chariot waiting for two miracles. "Alright," Barney sighed, the brief flicker of victory extinguished. "Now the real work starts."

While Barney popped the hood and began contemplating the grim logistics of finding a live battery in a city of the dead, Gregory set about making the car their own. He found a crumpled bundle of fast-food napkins in the glove compartment and, after dabbing one with a bit of the brown water from their bottle, began methodically wiping down the dashboard. He hummed a soft, tuneless melody as he worked, a sound of such profound domesticity that it felt like an act of defiance against the surrounding decay. He cleared out old receipts, a dried-up pen, and a child’s plastic dinosaur, arranging them in a neat pile on the concrete floor. "You have to start with a clean space," he announced to Barney's back. "It promotes clarity of thought." His greatest discovery was a near-full bottle of hand sanitizer that had rolled under the passenger seat. He held it up like a holy grail, his face beaming. "Look! A gel-based sanitizer with aloe! The journey is off to an auspicious start."

Barney, meanwhile, had found a length of grimy plastic tubing in the trunk of a nearby wreck. Siphoning gas was a foul, desperate business he had been forced to learn. Gregory watched, aghast, as Barney fed one end of the tube into the tank of a derelict sedan. "You're not going to put the other end in your mouth, are you?" he asked, horrified. "The fumes are terribly toxic! And the taste… I can't imagine." Barney just gave him a look that could strip paint and got to work. After two false starts and a mouthful of gasoline that made him gag, he managed to get a sluggish, foul-smelling stream of fuel flowing into a hubcap he’d pried off the Volvo. It was slow, miserable work, and it took three different donor cars to half-fill the Volvo’s tank.

The battery was a stroke of luck. A delivery van, its back doors gaping open, had a heavy-duty battery that gave a faint, hopeful spark when Barney touched the tire iron to its terminals. It was monstrously heavy. Heaving it back to the Volvo, his muscles screaming, he felt a flicker of kinship with the ancient Egyptians, building their pyramids with brute force and desperation. Gregory, seeing him struggle, rushed to help, taking one side. The two of them, the comedian and the laundry savant, grunted and swore as they wrestled the power source into the station wagon’s engine bay and clamped on the corroded cables.

The moment of truth arrived cloaked in the stink of gasoline and rust. Barney slid back behind the wheel, his hands slick with grease and grime. Gregory stood by the open passenger door, his posture expectant, as if waiting for a chauffeur to begin a pleasant Sunday drive. Barney turned the key. The engine coughed, a single, pathetic sound. He tried again. A long, grinding whine, the sound of a machine in its death throes. "Dammit!" he snarled, slamming his fist on the dashboard. A cloud of dust puffed out of the vents. "Come on, you Swedish piece of…" Before he could finish, Gregory’s hand rested gently on his shoulder. "Try giving it just a little bit of gas as you turn the key," he suggested softly. "My father's old ride-on mower was always a bit temperamental in the damp. A little encouragement was all it needed."

The advice was so mundane, so absurdly helpful, that Barney was too stunned to argue. He pressed the accelerator just a fraction and turned the key one last time. The engine sputtered, caught, sputtered again, and then, with a deafening roar that echoed through the concrete tomb, it roared to life. The whole car shuddered, and the headlights flickered on, cutting two brilliant yellow paths through the darkness. The engine settled into a loud, uneven, asthmatic idle, but it was running. It was the most beautiful sound Barney had ever heard.

He didn't waste a second. He threw the car into drive, the transmission groaning in protest. "Get in!" he yelled at Gregory, who was already calmly seating himself and reaching for the seatbelt. Barney stomped on the accelerator. The tires squealed on the slick concrete as the old Volvo lurched forward, its rusty body rattling like a can full of bolts. They shot up the spiral ramp, bursting out of the garage's mouth and into the pale, indifferent daylight. The city, a panorama of ruin and silence, spread out before them. Gregory, having secured his seatbelt, began fastidiously adjusting his side-view mirror. Barney gripped the steering wheel, his knuckles white, and pointed the boxy, mustard-colored hood toward the highway, away from the smoldering ghost of his house and the city of the dead. He had a full tank of gas, a copilot from another dimension, and the coordinates for a miracle or a punchline. The road trip had begun.The highways were not empty. They were clogged. The arteries of the old world had suffered a massive, simultaneous coronary, and the asphalt was clotted with abandoned vehicles. Cars, trucks, and buses sat at odd angles, their doors ajar like slack-jawed mouths, their interiors picked clean by scavengers. Driving was less a matter of following a lane and more a slow, weaving slalom through a graveyard of steel and glass. Barney gripped the steering wheel, his eyes constantly scanning, navigating the gaps between a stalled semi-trailer and an overturned family van. The silence of it all was the most unnerving part. No horns. No radios. Just the asthmatic rattle of the Volvo’s engine and the crunch of broken glass under their tires.

Gregory, having meticulously cleaned his designated passenger area, had discovered an ancient, folded road atlas in the seat-back pocket. He spread it across his lap with an air of profound purpose. For ten minutes, he traced the faded lines with his finger, his brow furrowed in concentration, before clearing his throat. "Barnaby," he began, his tone that of a navigator announcing a critical course correction. "According to this, we should be approaching the turn-off for 'Prehistoric Putt-Putt World' in approximately three miles. It boasts a life-sized animatronic T-Rex on the ninth hole. Perhaps a worthy detour? For morale." He tapped the cartoonish drawing of a dinosaur wearing a visor.

Barney didn't take his eyes off the road. He had just swerved to avoid a sun-bleached mattress lying in the middle of the highway. "Gregory, that map was printed in 1998. The T-Rex is probably a pile of rust being used as a sniper's nest, and the only 'Prehistoric Putt-Putt World' is this one." He gestured with his chin at the ruined landscape. The world outside the Volvo's dusty windows was a monochrome of gray pavement, brown, dying grass, and the bleached skeletons of billboards advertising blockbuster movies that never opened and low-interest mortgages that would never be paid. One peeling sign showed a smiling family on a beach, the mother’s face ripped away by the wind, leaving a blank, white space. An angel-shaped hole in an advertisement for happiness.

As dusk began to bleed purple and orange across the sky, Barney pulled the car off the highway, tucking it into the relative concealment of a grove of withered pine trees beside a defunct rest stop. The building itself was a hollowed-out concrete shell, its windows gaping black holes, the walls a tapestry of graffiti that had devolved from angry slogans into crude, desperate scrawls. He killed the engine, and the ensuing silence was so total it rang in his ears. "We'll stay here for the night," he said, his voice low. "Don't get out. We eat in the car. We sleep in the car. We do not draw attention to ourselves."

Gregory nodded with solemn understanding, as if these were the established rules for a camping trip in a particularly stringent national park. While Barney cracked open one of the remaining cans of peaches with the tire iron, Gregory carefully retrieved his perfectly folded fitted sheet from the back. He laid it over the dashboard, smoothing it out with his palms until it formed a clean, cream-colored altar in the decaying cockpit of the car. It caught the last of the fading light, a small, defiant patch of order in a world of chaos. Barney watched him, a peach slice halfway to his mouth. The man was nesting. In the middle of the apocalypse, he was nesting. The sheer, unadulterated madness of it was almost comforting. Barney finished his peach and took the first watch, the useless tire iron resting across his lap, his eyes fixed on the shadows that lengthened and danced around them as the silent constellation of the Unveiling began to burn in the night sky.

They drove for two days, a slow, grinding crawl across the scarred face of the country. They saw other men, always from a distance. Solitary figures pushing shopping carts filled with scavenged goods along the shoulder of the interstate. Small, tense groups huddled around fires in the shells of burned-out gas stations, their faces suspicious and gaunt. There was no camaraderie in this world, only calculation. Every stranger was a potential threat, a competitor for the dwindling scraps of a dead civilization. Barney drove with a coiled knot of tension in his gut, while Gregory provided a running commentary on the collapse of civic infrastructure. "The weeds in that median are terribly overgrown," he'd cluck. "That's a haven for ticks. And look at the state of that guardrail. A clear safety violation."

It was on the third day, in the flat, sun-baked plains of what might have once been Kansas, that they encountered something new. Up ahead, the highway was completely blocked. A school bus had been dragged sideways across the road, and an assortment of junk—old tires, rusted bed frames, a decapitated lawn flamingo—had been piled up to form a crude but effective barricade. A large, hand-painted sign hung from the bus, the letters crude and dripping. It read: ‘PAY TOL’.

Barney’s blood went cold. He slowed the Volvo to a stop a hundred yards from the roadblock, his mind racing. This was organized. Not a gang, maybe, but a territorial claim. He looked in the rearview mirror. Turning around would be slow, exposing their backs. Ramming it was a gamble; the old Volvo might just crumple. "Okay," he breathed, his hands slick on the wheel. "Okay, we're just going to back away slowly."

"Why?" Gregory asked, peering over the dashboard. "It appears to be a toll plaza. We should pay the fee and be on our way. It's the civic thing to do." Before Barney could scream, Gregory had unbuckled his seatbelt. "I'll handle this," he said with an air of quiet confidence, and opened his door.

"Gregory, no!" Barney hissed, but it was too late. Gregory was already out of the car, walking toward the barricade with the steady, unhurried gait of a man about to dispute a cable bill. Four figures emerged from behind the bus. They were thin and sunburned, dressed in rags, and armed with a motley collection of sharpened pipes and baseball bats. They looked less like hardened killers and more like desperate, hungry farmers who had run out of options. Their leader, a tall man with a wild beard and crazed eyes, stepped forward, brandishing a rusty machete.

"That's far enough, pal!" the man yelled, his voice rough. "Toll's ten gallons of gas. Or all the food you got."

Gregory stopped a polite distance away, his hands clasped behind his back. He surveyed the man, then the barricade, then the misspelled sign. "Good afternoon," he said, his voice calm and clear. "I see you're managing this section of the highway now. A commendable initiative, given the circumstances. However, I have a few questions about your tolling structure. Firstly, your signage has a rather glaring spelling error, which could lead to legal ambiguities regarding the enforceability of the fee." He paused, letting this sink in. The men stared at him, utterly dumbfounded. The leader’s grip on his machete wavered.

"Secondly," Gregory continued, taking a small step forward, "ten gallons seems rather steep. There's been no road maintenance whatsoever for at least a hundred miles. The surface is littered with debris, and several bridges we crossed were of questionable structural integrity. What, precisely, is this toll paying for? It certainly isn't for the quality of the roadway." He looked the leader directly in the eye, his expression one of genuine, professional inquiry.

The leader opened his mouth, then closed it. He looked at his companions, who just shrugged in bewilderment. Barney watched from the car, his jaw on the floor. This wasn't a negotiation; it was a surrealist masterpiece. Gregory was auditing the apocalypse.

"We… we keep the… the bad guys away," the leader finally stammered, gesturing vaguely with his machete.

"The bad guys?" Gregory replied, his tone politely skeptical. "And how do you delineate between yourselves and these 'bad guys'? Do you have uniforms? A charter of some kind? From a consumer's perspective, the service you're offering is indistinguishable from the problem you claim to be solving." He reached into his polo shirt's breast pocket. The men tensed, raising their pipes. Barney’s hand flew to the gearshift. But Gregory didn't pull out a weapon. He pulled out the small, plastic dinosaur he'd found in the Volvo's glove box. He held it out. "Look, I understand you're trying to establish a micro-economy. It's admirable. But your business model is unsustainable. You're alienating your customer base. I'll tell you what." He placed the plastic dinosaur on a nearby oil drum. "Let's call this a down payment. You let us pass, and in return, I'll provide you with some complimentary consulting on how to improve your operation. For starters, a properly spelled sign and a tiered pricing system. Perhaps a frequent-traveler discount. Think about it."

He turned and walked back to the car, leaving the four men staring at the small, plastic stegosaurus as if it were a holy relic. Gregory got back in, buckled his seatbelt, and turned to Barney with a satisfied smile. "I believe they're considering my offer," he said. "They seem like reasonable men, just a bit misguided in their approach to customer relations."

One of the men hesitantly reached out and poked the dinosaur. The leader looked from the toy to the Volvo, his face a mask of utter confusion. He then looked at his men, and with a weary, defeated sigh, waved them through. As Barney gunned the engine and carefully navigated the Volvo around the edge of the barricade, he glanced at the toll-takers. They were huddled around the oil drum, staring at the plastic dinosaur, their weapons forgotten at their sides. They didn't look threatening anymore. They just looked lost.

They drove on in silence for another mile before Barney pulled over again, his hands shaking. He turned to the man beside him, a man who had just dismantled a hostile blockade with a lecture on business ethics and a child's toy. Barney had been trying to survive in this world. Gregory was still trying to fix it. He had tried to summon a hero, a knight in shining armor. What he'd gotten was infinitely more strange, and terrifyingly, maybe even more effective. "Gregory," Barney said, his voice barely a whisper. "Don't you ever do that again."

Gregory looked at him, his brow furrowed in genuine confusion. "Why not?" he asked. "It worked, didn't it?"Barney drove, his hands clenched so tightly on the plastic steering wheel that he could feel the molded seams digging into his palms. He was trying to explain the concept of imminent, violent death to a man who seemed to view it as a particularly aggressive form of customer complaint. He had tried logic. He had tried yelling. He was now bordering on a quiet, high-pitched plea that was doing nothing for his self-respect.

"They had a machete, Gregory. A big, rusty, tetanus-delivery-system of a machete. People with machetes don't want to discuss the finer points of their business model. They want to separate you from your possessions, and if they're having a bad day, your head from your shoulders." Barney's voice was strained, a thin wire of sound in the rattling car.

Gregory, who was attempting to clean a smudge off the passenger window with his sleeve, paused to consider this. "I suppose you're right," he conceded, much to Barney's shock. "But only in principle. That man, the one with the beard? He was a manager, not a killer. You can tell by the way he was holding it. All wrist, no follow-through. It was a prop, meant to intimidate. He was bluffing, and frankly, his bluff was predicated on a very poor understanding of his target demographic. He assumed we were scared. He failed to account for a consumer who simply wanted to see a fair return on his investment." He went back to polishing the window, a small, circular motion. "Besides," he added cheerfully, "he was holding it all wrong. You'd get a terrible blister on your thumb with a grip like that."

Barney surrendered. Arguing with Gregory was like trying to punch fog. He simply drove, sinking into a state of baffled resignation. His companion from another reality was not a warrior or a savior. He was a weapon of mass pedantry, a man capable of disarming bandits with the sheer, soul-crushing power of bureaucratic logic. It was a superpower so profoundly mundane it looped all the way back around to being terrifying. As the Volvo chewed up the miles, Barney found himself looking over at Gregory not with exasperation, but with a kind of clinical curiosity, the way a scientist might regard a creature that had evolved to survive in an ecosystem that shouldn't exist.

Their supplies were dwindling. The peaches were gone, the coffee concentrate was a few sludgy mouthfuls from empty, and the brownish water from Thorne’s apartment was starting to smell vaguely of pond life. They needed food. Late in the afternoon, on a lonely stretch of state highway, they saw it. A 'Megamart' superstore, a vast, featureless box of a building set back from the road, its massive parking lot a sea of silent, sun-faded cars. Its red and yellow sign was peeling, but it was still standing, a monument to a forgotten era of bulk purchasing.

"Okay," Barney said, pulling the car behind a listing delivery truck to hide it from the road. "New plan. We go in, we find the canned goods aisle, we fill a bag, and we leave. Five minutes. In and out. No browsing. No price comparisons. We are ghosts, understand?" He looked at Gregory, trying to impress the urgency of the mission upon him.

Gregory nodded, his expression serious. "Of course. Efficiency is key. But we'll need a cart. It’s terribly inefficient to carry everything by hand, and it increases the risk of dropping and damaging the product." Before Barney could protest, Gregory was out of the car and trotting toward the entrance, where a line of shopping carts stood welded together by rust. With a determined grunt, he managed to wrestle one free. It had one wobbly wheel, which gave it a drunken, lurching gait as he pushed it toward the shattered glass doors.

The inside of the Megamart was a cathedral of decay. Sunlight streamed through grime-covered skylights, illuminating dust motes dancing in the dead air. The shelves were in disarray, picked over and toppled, the floor a mosaic of crushed packaging, broken glass, and desiccated spills. It smelled of rot and dust and the faint, chemical scent of spoiled cleaning products. Barney moved quickly, his senses on high alert, his hand gripping the tire iron. Gregory, however, had come to a dead stop in the entranceway, his face a mask of profound disappointment. "Good heavens," he breathed, his voice echoing in the cavernous space. "The disorganization. It's simply appalling. The health and safety violations alone… someone could trip." He tutted, then carefully guided his wobbly cart around a puddle of what looked like ancient yogurt.

They made their way to the back of the store, to the canned goods aisle. It had been hit hard, but there were still treasures on the higher shelves. Barney, a veteran of this process, shimmied up a set of shelves like a desperate monkey, tossing down cans of beans, corn, and something labeled ‘luncheon meat’ to Gregory, who caught them with surprising dexterity and placed them carefully in the cart. The operation was going smoothly until Gregory held up a dented can of tomato soup. "Barnaby, this one's compromised," he said, his voice laced with concern. "There's a dent on the seam. That's a clear indicator of a potential botulism risk. We should leave it."

"Gregory, the whole world is a botulism risk," Barney hissed from his perch. "Just put it in the damn cart." Gregory looked at the can, then at Barney, his principles clearly at war with the direct order. After a moment of internal struggle, he sighed and placed the can at the very front of the cart, separate from the others, as if to quarantine it. He then proceeded to inspect every can Barney threw down, creating a small but growing pile of "unacceptable" goods on the floor.

As Barney was reaching for a promising-looking can of pineapple rings, a sound froze him in place. A slow, metallic scraping from the front of the store. It wasn't the wind. It was deliberate. He scrambled down the shelves, landing silently on the grimy linoleum. He grabbed Gregory's arm, his eyes wide. "Someone's here," he mouthed. Gregory’s eyes widened in return, not with fear, but with social anxiety. "Oh, dear," he whispered. "And the place is such a mess. It's terribly embarrassing."

Barney pulled him into the relative cover of the pet food aisle, crouching behind a pallet of fifty-pound bags of dog kibble. They peered through a gap in the shelving. A man had entered the store. He was huge, a mountain of a man with a shaved head and a thick, greasy beard, and he was dragging a large, empty metal drum behind him, the source of the scraping noise. He moved with a weary entitlement, as if he owned the place. He stopped, surveyed the canned goods aisle, and grunted in satisfaction when he saw the small pile of dented cans Gregory had left on the floor. He ambled over and began casually tossing them into his drum. He was a bottom-feeder, a hyena cleaning up the scraps others had left behind. He was also directly between them and the exit.

They waited in suffocating silence, the scent of processed meat and fish-flavored pellets thick in their nostrils. The man finished with the cans and began to wander down the next aisle, his drum scraping a slow, rhythmic beat of impending doom. This was their chance. Barney gave Gregory a slight push. "Go," he breathed. "Now. Quietly." They began a slow, crouched retreat, moving like burglars in their own heist. Gregory, even in his terror, took the time to carefully steer his wobbly cart to prevent it from rattling. They were halfway to the doors when Gregory’s foot caught on a discarded soda bottle. He stumbled, pinwheeling his arms for balance, and crashed directly into a towering display of empty cat litter boxes.

The sound was apocalyptic. A plastic avalanche that echoed through the silent Megamart like a gunshot. The scraping from the other aisle stopped. Heavy, thudding footsteps began moving toward them, fast. "Run," Barney croaked, his voice cracking. He abandoned all pretense of stealth, sprinting for the exit. He looked back and saw, to his horror, that Gregory was still trying to guide his shopping cart, its one bad wheel catching and jerking, slowing him down. The big man appeared at the end of the aisle, his face a mask of fury. He wasn't a bottom-feeder anymore; he was a bear whose territory had been invaded. With a roar, he charged. Barney didn't think. He spun around, raised the tire iron, and threw it with all his might. It was a pathetic, desperate throw, and it spun end over end, clattering harmlessly against the concrete floor ten feet in front of the charging man. It did nothing but annoy him.

But it had done something else. The clatter of the iron on the concrete was just loud enough to startle Gregory, who gave his cart one last, panicked shove. The wobbly wheel, which had been a hindrance for their entire shopping trip, finally found its moment of glorious, improbable purpose. It caught on a crack in the floor at the exact right angle, causing the cart to pivot violently and shoot sideways, directly into the path of the charging man. The man, whose eyes were fixed on Barney, didn't see it coming. He hit the cart at a full run, his shin connecting with the metal basket with a sickening crack of bone. He howled in pain and surprise, his momentum carrying him forward as he tumbled over the cart in a spectacular, ungraceful arc. He landed head-first in a large, wire bin full of dusty, discounted plush toys. His legs kicked in the air for a moment, and then he was still, a giant, furious baby in a nest of stuffed monkeys and bears.

Barney and Gregory stared, breathless. The giant lay motionless, buried to the waist in synthetic fur. The silence that descended was broken only by the faint, cheerful jingle of a plush toucan that had been activated by the impact. Barney looked at the shopping cart, then at the fallen giant, then at Gregory, who was meticulously straightening his polo shirt. The universe hadn't just given him a sidekick. It had given him a partner in a cosmic slapstick routine. He grabbed Gregory's arm, and this time, they ran. They abandoned the cart full of life-saving beans and botulism-free corn and sprinted out of the Megamart, leaving the unconscious giant to his plushy fate. They threw themselves into the Volvo, and Barney peeled out of the parking lot, not stopping until the Megamart was just a shimmering box in his rearview mirror. He drove with the toucan's cheerful, idiotic jingle still echoing in his head.The Volvo rattled down the empty highway, a mustard-yellow beetle fleeing a crime scene. The cheerful, moronic jingle of the plush toucan seemed to be imprinted on the inside of Barney’s skull, a maddeningly upbeat counterpoint to the frantic hammering of his own heart. He drove with a manic intensity, his eyes flitting between the road ahead and the rearview mirror, half-expecting to see a furious giant in a shopping cart bearing down on them. They had escaped. They had survived another encounter that should have ended with their skulls being used as soup bowls. And they had absolutely nothing to show for it but a fresh coating of terror.

Beside him, Gregory was the picture of contemplative calm. He had retrieved his folded sheet from the dashboard where it had been jostled during their hasty departure and was now carefully smoothing out a new wrinkle. When he was satisfied, he looked over at Barney, his expression one of polite disappointment. “A shame about the beans,” he said, his voice level. “And I do think that luncheon meat had potential, despite the rather unappealing name. It’s terribly inefficient to go through the entire procurement process only to abandon the goods at the point of sale. That wobbly wheel was a known issue. We should have reported it to management.”

The sheer, staggering disconnect of the statement was what finally broke Barney. He slammed on the brakes, pulling the car onto the gravel shoulder with a screech of tortured tires. He threw the car into park and turned in his seat, his face a mask of frayed nerves and disbelief. “Management?” he shrieked, his voice cracking. “There is no management, Gregory! There is only a seven-foot-tall man with a head like a bowling ball who was about to use your spine as a marimba! We almost died! Again! That is not a customer service issue!” His tirade ended in a series of ragged, gasping breaths. He felt lightheaded, his vision swimming with black spots.

Gregory listened patiently, his hands resting on his folded sheet. He did not look frightened or chastised. He looked like a man hearing a slightly overwrought but valid point during a heated board meeting. "I see what you're saying," he conceded, nodding slowly. "His approach to conflict resolution was certainly aggressive. And you're right, I hadn't considered the marimba analogy. It’s quite vivid.” He paused, his brow furrowed in thought. “Still, if the cart’s wheel had been properly maintained by the establishment, the entire confrontation could have been avoided. Proper maintenance schedules are the cornerstone of any successful enterprise. This is what happens when you let standards slip.” He sighed, a sound of genuine sorrow for the decline of retail best practices. Barney dropped his head against the steering wheel, which let out a long, mournful honk. The sound echoed across the empty plains, a lonely cry for a world that had a complaint department.

After a few minutes of listening to nothing but the ticking of the cooling engine and his own frantic pulse, Barney sat up. The anger had burned itself out, leaving behind the cold, familiar ash of desperation. They were foodless, their only weapon was lost, and they were in the middle of a vast, flat emptiness that offered no cover and no comfort. He uncrumpled the old road atlas. He found their approximate location, a meaningless speck on a grid of fading red lines. Then he located the coordinates from Thorne’s letter. They were still hundreds of miles away, on the other side of a mountain range that loomed on the map like a jagged spine. They would never make it without supplies. He folded the map, the worn paper feeling as fragile as his own hope. His eyes scanned the horizon, searching for anything, any sign of something other than dead grass and empty road. And then he saw it. Far off to the south, almost lost in the afternoon haze, was a single, straight line of darker green. A windbreak. Trees that hadn't just grown by accident. And where there were deliberate trees, there might be a farm.

"Change of plan," Barney muttered, turning the key. The Volvo’s engine roared back to life with its familiar, unhealthy rattle. He swung the car back onto the highway and took the next unmarked dirt turn-off, a path so overgrown it was more of a suggestion than a road. The car bucked and jostled, branches scraping against the windows like skeletal fingers. Gregory held his fitted sheet to his chest to protect it from the bumpy ride. “Are you certain about this, Barnaby?” he asked, his voice vibrating with the car's shuddering. “This road seems to be in a state of considerable disrepair. It doesn’t appear on the atlas.” Barney just grunted, his eyes fixed on the line of trees. It was a gamble. Farms had food, but they also had farmers, and the men who had been self-sufficient before the Unveiling were now the feudal lords of the new world, fiercely protective of their land and their resources. He was driving them into either a pantry or a grave.

The farmhouse, when they finally reached it, was a picture of weary endurance. It was a simple, two-story wooden structure, its white paint peeling back to reveal the weathered grey wood beneath. The fields around it were a riot of weeds, but a small, fenced-in garden near the house showed signs of life: rows of hardy-looking vegetables, green and vibrant against the brown earth. A few chickens scratched in a dusty patch near a coop. The place was quiet, but it wasn’t the dead quiet of the Megamart; it was the watchful quiet of a place that was lived in. Barney killed the engine, and they sat for a long moment, listening. The only sound was the wind whistling through a broken pane in an upstairs window.

Barney grabbed the tire iron from the floor, the last thing he had. Its weight felt pathetic. “Alright,” he whispered, his mouth dry. “New, new plan. I go to the door. I’ll be friendly. I’ll offer to… I don’t know, tell some jokes for a can of corn. You stay here. If you hear yelling or gunshots, you get in the driver's seat and you leave. Understand?” Gregory looked at him, his face uncharacteristically grave. He nodded slowly. “I understand, Barnaby. But try to lead with the one about the toaster. It has a certain pathos that I think a rural audience would appreciate.” Barney gave him a weak, terrified smile, got out of the car, and began walking toward the house, his heart a cold, heavy lump in his chest.

He was halfway across the overgrown yard when the screen door of the farmhouse creaked open. A man stepped out onto the porch, and Barney froze. He was old and wiry, his skin leathery from a lifetime of sun, his face a roadmap of wrinkles. He wore faded overalls and a grim expression. And he was holding a double-barreled shotgun, which he leveled squarely at Barney’s chest. The two black holes of the barrels looked as big as dinner plates. “That’s far enough,” the old man said, his voice a low, gravelly rasp. “I don’t know what you’re selling, but I ain’t buying. And I don’t know what you’re looking to take, but you’ll be taking a load of buckshot with it. Turn around and walk away.”

Barney’s throat closed up. He slowly raised his empty hands, the tire iron suddenly feeling impossibly heavy. His mind, which had once been a rapid-fire arsenal of quips and comebacks, was completely blank. All the practiced words, the routines, the carefully constructed jokes, they were all useless in the face of this simple, deadly equation. "We don't want any trouble," he managed to croak, the words sounding feeble and false even to his own ears. "We're just… hungry. We saw the farm. We were hoping maybe we could trade for some food." The old man’s eyes narrowed, his grip tightening on the shotgun. "Everyone's hungry," he growled. "Got nothing to trade. Nothing you'd have, anyway."

Just as the old man’s knuckle began to whiten on the trigger, the Volvo’s passenger door creaked open. Barney’s heart sank. "Gregory, no," he breathed. But it was too late. Gregory got out of the car and stood there, calmly brushing dust from his khaki pants. The farmer shifted the aim of the shotgun slightly, to cover them both. “I said get!” he barked, his voice rising. Gregory, however, seemed not to have heard him. His attention was fixed on the porch. He took a few steps forward, his head cocked. Barney wanted to scream, to tackle him, to do anything to stop the unfolding disaster. Instead, he just stood, paralyzed, a spectator at his own execution.

Gregory stopped beside Barney, completely ignoring the shotgun pointed at his face. He squinted at the old farmer on the porch, his expression one of polite, professional concern. “Pardon the intrusion,” Gregory said, his voice carrying clearly in the quiet yard. “And please forgive my friend, he’s not at his best when his blood sugar is low. I just couldn’t help but notice… that third step on your porch has a dangerous amount of wobble to it.” He pointed. “You can see the rot on the support joist. That’s a serious accident waiting to happen. A trip hazard of the highest order. If you have a hammer and some spare lumber, I could probably brace that for you. It wouldn’t take a moment.”

The old farmer stared. His grim, menacing expression faltered, replaced by a look of utter, slack-jawed bafflement. He glanced down at the step, then back at the small, round man in the pristine polo shirt who was offering home repair services at gunpoint. The shotgun, which had been held with deadly intent, lowered a few inches. The immediate threat of violence seemed to have been short-circuited by the sheer, unexpected banality of the offer. “The… the step?” the farmer stammered, his voice losing its gravelly edge.

"Precisely," Gregory said, nodding sagely. "And your gutters are full of leaves. That’s going to cause water damage to your fascia board come the first big rain. You really should have those cleared out before autumn.” He clasped his hands behind his back and surveyed the property with a critical eye, as if performing a home inspection. “It’s a lovely property, you know. Good bones. It just needs a little preventative maintenance to protect its long-term value.” The silence that followed was broken only by the caw of a distant crow. The farmer looked at his shotgun, then at Gregory, then back at his shotgun, as if he was no longer sure what it was for. Barney watched, breathless, as the universe, with its cruel and impeccable timing, prepared to deliver yet another impossible punchline.The old farmer, who Barney would later learn was named Jacob, blinked as if the sun had suddenly gotten twice as bright. He looked from Gregory’s earnest face to the shotgun in his own hands and then down at the offending step, as if seeing it for the first time. The weapon, a moment ago an extension of his will, now seemed like a cumbersome and slightly embarrassing accessory, like wearing a tuxedo to a barn raising. The absolute non-sequitur of Gregory's home-improvement offer had completely derailed his train of thought, which had been chugging steadily down the track toward justifiable homicide.

“The step,” Jacob repeated, the word coming out less as a threat and more as a bewildered question. He nudged it with the toe of his worn leather boot, and it wobbled obediently, letting out a pathetic little creak. A flicker of something—annoyance, recognition, perhaps even a hint of shame—crossed his weathered face. It was, undeniably, a wobbly step.

Barney seized the surreal, life-saving opportunity, stepping cautiously into the conversational void. "He's very observant," Barney said, his voice still shaky but now laced with the frantic energy of a man trying to sell a timeshare to his own executioner. "A real stickler for safety codes. We'd be happy to fix it for you. And the gutters. For a meal. And maybe some water. We're not looking for a handout, just a trade. Labor for supplies."

Jacob stared at them for a long, silent moment, the gears turning slowly behind his pale blue eyes. He was a man accustomed to a world of simple, brutal transactions: threats met with violence, weakness met with predation. He had no protocol for a man who met a shotgun with a critique of his property maintenance. The situation was so far outside his experience that it had reset his instincts. With a deep, weary sigh that seemed to carry the weight of the last two years, he lowered the shotgun, resting its stock on the porch floor. “Tools are in the shed out back,” he grunted, nodding his head toward a small, dilapidated outbuilding. “Don’t even think about trying anything stupid. I can field-dress a deer in under ten minutes. A man’s a hell of a lot simpler.” He turned and went back inside, leaving the screen door to slam shut behind him. The threat was still there, but it had been downgraded from imminent to conditional. It was the best deal Barney had been offered in years.

Gregory beamed, a man whose expertise had finally been recognized and appreciated. He turned to Barney, his expression one of pure, unadulterated purpose. “Right then,” he said briskly. “Let’s have a look at that shed. I hope he has a decent pry bar. You can’t do a proper joist repair without a good pry bar.” He trotted off toward the shed, leaving Barney to stand alone in the yard, tire iron dangling limply from his hand, feeling like a superfluous character in his own life-or-death drama.

The shed was a dusty treasure trove of practical capability. Gregory moved through the clutter of old engines and rusted farm implements with the discerning eye of a connoisseur, humming happily to himself. He located a hammer, a saw with a surprisingly sharp blade, and a heavy-duty pry bar that met his exacting standards. He even found a coffee can full of assorted nails, which he sorted by size with quiet efficiency. The work itself was a masterclass in mundane competence. Gregory diagnosed the problem with a series of authoritative prods and gentle taps, explaining the issue to Barney in excruciating detail. “You see, the end of the joist has succumbed to dry rot. It’s lost all its structural integrity. We’ll need to cut back to good wood and sister a new board alongside it. It’s the only proper way to do it.” Barney, feeling profoundly useless, was relegated to the role of surgeon’s assistant, holding boards steady and passing nails when requested. Under Gregory's cheerful and precise direction, they cut away the rot, measured and sawed a new piece of lumber from a stack behind the shed, and hammered it into place. An hour later, the step was as solid as the foundation of the house itself. Gregory tested it with his full weight, a satisfied smile on his face. “There,” he declared. “That should last another fifty years.”

As if summoned by the final hammer blow, the screen door creaked open again. Jacob stepped out, shotgun now leaning casually against the doorframe. He walked to the edge of the porch and stomped on the newly repaired step. It didn't move. It didn't creak. He stomped again, harder. Nothing. He looked at the clean cut of the new wood, the neat line of nails, and a grudging respect dawned in his eyes. He looked at Gregory, then at Barney, and grunted. “It’ll do,” he said, which from a man like Jacob was the equivalent of a glowing testimonial. “Wash up at the pump. Supper’s on.”

The inside of the farmhouse was spartan and immaculately clean, a stark contrast to the decay they had grown used to. The air smelled of woodsmoke and dried herbs. A simple wooden table stood in the center of the kitchen, already set with three mismatched plates. On a mantelpiece in the adjoining room, amidst a collection of old farm trophies, sat a single, silver-framed photograph of a smiling, round-faced woman with kind eyes. Jacob caught Barney looking at it. “My Martha,” he said, his voice suddenly softer. “She went up. Right from the garden, tending her tomatoes. Said she was going to make sauce.” He stared at the picture for a moment, lost in a memory. “She always hated that wobbly step. Said I was going to break my fool neck on it one day.” A sad, ghost of a smile touched his lips. “Guess she was right to worry.”

In that moment, the gulf between Barney and the old farmer vanished, replaced by a bridge of shared, silent grief. Barney thought of Lena, of her favourite cardigan torn by the impossible wings, of the rosebush that had become a thorny wreck. He knew this man’s silence. He lived in it every day. He simply nodded, unable to speak. The meal was a thick, savory stew of rabbit and vegetables from the garden, served with dense, crumbly cornbread. To Barney, who had been subsisting on canned fruit and desperation, it was the most magnificent feast he had ever eaten. They ate in a comfortable quiet, the only sounds the scrape of spoons against bowls. It was a temporary truce, a shared moment of warmth and nourishment in a cold, starving world.

When they had finished, Jacob rose from the table. “A deal’s a deal,” he said, his gruff demeanor returning, but without the hard edge of menace. He led them to a root cellar, a cool, subterranean space that smelled of damp earth and life. He began filling two sturdy canvas sacks with potatoes, onions, and carrots. He added six jars of preserved green beans and, to Barney’s profound gratitude, two jars of Martha’s tomato sauce. From a well outside, they filled every container they had with clean, cold water. It was a hoard of unimaginable wealth. As they loaded the supplies into the back of the Volvo, Jacob leaned against a fence post, watching them. “You heading for the mountains?” he asked. Barney nodded. "There's a place we need to get to." Jacob spat a stream of tobacco juice into the dirt. “Be careful. The passes are full of the desperate kind. Not like us. Not like those fools at the toll road. The ones who ran out of everything, including hope. They’ve got nothing left to lose, and that makes a man more dangerous than any animal.” He looked from Barney to Gregory, a flicker of something almost like concern in his eyes. “You,” he said, pointing a calloused finger at Gregory. “You keep talking. It seems to work for you.”

With the Volvo loaded down, its springs groaning under the weight of their new fortune, Barney turned the key. The engine sputtered to life. He looked at Jacob, who stood by the fence, a solitary, resilient figure against the vast, empty landscape. Barney raised a hand in a gesture of thanks and farewell. The old farmer just gave a short, sharp nod, then turned and walked back toward his solid, well-maintained porch. As they bumped back down the dirt track and onto the empty highway, Barney glanced over at his companion. Gregory was running a finger along the rim of a jar of tomato sauce, a look of deep satisfaction on his face. “A good trade,” Gregory pronounced. “Honest work for honest goods. And I have to say, that was an excellent stew. Though it could have used a bay leaf.” Barney didn't answer. He just drove, the weight of the food in the back a comforting pressure. They were fed. They were supplied. And they were heading for the mountains, into the territory of men who had nothing left to lose. For the first time, Barney felt that maybe, just maybe, they had a chance. The joke wasn't that the universe had sent him Gregory. The joke was that it knew he would need him.The bounty from Jacob’s farm shifted the entire atmosphere inside the Volvo. The oppressive scent of despair was replaced by the faint, earthy aroma of potatoes and the promise of Martha’s tomato sauce. For the first time in what felt like an eternity, Barney felt the unfamiliar sensation of a full stomach and a future that extended beyond the next six hours. The weight of the supplies in the back was a grounding force, a tangible anchor in a world that had come loose from its moorings. They drove towards the jagged teeth of the mountains on the horizon, a destination that now felt less like a fool's errand and more like a real, achievable goal. The road began to climb, and the flat, sun-bleached plains gave way to rolling foothills dotted with skeletal trees and stubborn, hardy scrub.

As they ascended, the Volvo began to complain. The steady, asthmatic rattle of the engine grew more pronounced on the inclines, and the transmission whined in protest on the steeper grades. The road narrowed, carving a precarious ledge out of the mountainside. To their right was a sheer rock face, streaked with mineral deposits that looked like dried blood. To their left, a dizzying drop into a hazy, pine-choked canyon. There were no guardrails. Whatever had existed before had long ago been scavenged for scrap metal. Gregory peered out the window, his expression one of professional disapproval. “The civil engineering in this region is frankly substandard,” he observed, his voice calm despite the thousand-foot drop a few feet away. “You’d never get this kind of gradient without proper terracing in a seismically active zone. And the lack of retaining walls is just asking for a rockslide.”

They rounded a sharp, blind curve, and Barney slammed on the brakes. The Volvo skidded on a patch of loose gravel, its rear end fishtailing perilously close to the canyon’s edge before coming to a stop. Blocking the narrow road ahead was another car, a battered blue sedan nosed directly into the rock wall. Steam or smoke drifted lazily from under its crumpled hood, and a figure was slumped motionless over the steering wheel. Barney’s entire body went rigid. His brief sense of security evaporated, replaced by the cold, familiar dread that had been his constant companion for two years. Every instinct, honed by paranoia and loss, screamed that this was wrong. Accidents didn’t just happen anymore. Nothing was that simple.

“Stay in the car,” Barney commanded, his voice a low, urgent whisper. He began to slowly, carefully, put the Volvo into reverse. “We’re backing out. Now.” But Gregory was already leaning forward, his face a mask of concern. “Good heavens, Barnaby, that person needs our help. There could be a head injury. We have to check on them. It’s our civic duty.” He reached for the door handle. Barney’s hand shot out and clamped down on Gregory’s arm like a vise. “Gregory, listen to me. There are no civic duties. There are no accidents. That is a trap.” Their eyes met, a silent battle of wills between two years of brutal experience and a lifetime of unshakeable decency.

The argument was rendered moot a second later. From the rocks high above them, two figures emerged, stepping out from the shadows with the unnerving suddenness of apparitions. They were not like the desperate toll-takers or the lumbering giant of the Megamart. These men were lean and wiry, moving with a predatory economy of motion. Their clothes were functional, their faces hard and empty, and they carried rifles, not makeshift clubs or rusty machetes. This was a different class of survivor. One of them, with a fluid, practiced movement, slid down a scree slope to the road behind the Volvo, blocking their only retreat. The other, clearly the leader, remained on the ledge above, his rifle held in a casual, competent grip that was far more terrifying than any overt threat. He raised a hand and pointed, first at the Volvo’s tires, then at Barney. The message was unambiguous. They were caught.

“Okay,” Barney breathed, releasing Gregory’s arm. His mind was a frantic cacophony of failed plans. There was nowhere to run. They were outgunned, outmaneuvered, and utterly exposed on the narrow mountain road. The leader gestured with the barrel of his rifle, a sharp, impatient flick of the wrist. *Get out.* “Gregory, whatever happens, stay behind me. Don’t talk. Don’t offer to fix anything. Just let me handle this.” He opened his door, his hands raised in a gesture of universal surrender, and stepped out into the cold mountain air. The silence was absolute, broken only by the wind whistling down the canyon. The two men on the road began to approach, their boots crunching on the gravel, their eyes as cold and gray as the rock around them.

But Gregory, predictably, did not stay behind him. He exited from the passenger side, his movements deliberate and unhurried. He didn’t look at the men with the guns. His attention was completely fixed on the staged accident. He walked towards the blue sedan, a man on a mission of mercy, ignoring the rifles that were now trained on his chest. “Excuse me!” he called out to the slumped figure in the driver’s seat. “Are you injured? We have water and a first-aid kit. Though I must confess,” he added, his voice taking on a slightly critical tone, “our kit is somewhat lacking in burn cream.” The gunmen stopped, their predatory advance momentarily thrown off by this bizarre new development. The man behind the Volvo exchanged a confused glance with the leader, who remained impassive on the ledge above.

“Get away from the car,” the gunman in front of them snarled, his voice a low rasp. Gregory turned to face him, his expression not of fear, but of mild annoyance at being interrupted. “I will in a moment, but this gentleman’s well-being is the immediate priority. And frankly, I have some concerns about this entire situation.” He gestured toward the front of the crashed sedan with the confidence of a forensic investigator. “The pattern of damage is entirely inconsistent with the supposed cause of the accident. A vehicle striking a rock wall at this angle would exhibit significant front-end compression and likely a ruptured radiator. This vehicle has neither. The hood is crumpled, yes, but the tire is merely scuffed, suggesting it was rolled into place. It’s a very poorly staged tableau, I must say. If you’re going to feign an emergency, a little attention to detail goes a long way toward selling the narrative.”

The gunman stared, his simple, violent worldview completely unable to process this lecture. His anger, however, was easy enough to access. “What did you say?” he growled, taking a step forward and raising his rifle. The decoy in the car stirred, lifting his head. The ruse was over. But Gregory’s insane, pedantic deconstruction had done something Barney never could have: it had created an opening. It was a crack in the wall of their confidence, a split-second of disbelief and fury where there should have been cold, efficient violence. And in that split second, Barney acted. He dove back into the Volvo, slammed the door, and stomped on the accelerator. The engine roared, not in retreat, but in a suicidal, desperate charge forward.

The Volvo didn't aim for the men. It aimed for the back fender of the blue sedan. The impact was a deafening shriek of metal on metal. The sedan, not properly braked, was shoved forward a few feet, creating a gap between its rear and the rock wall—a gap barely wider than the Volvo itself. The gunmen, startled by the sudden violence, instinctively jumped back. Barney threw the car into reverse, the tires spinning and spitting gravel, then slammed it into drive again, aiming for the sliver of daylight he had created. “Gregory, get in!” he screamed, as the car shot forward. The passenger-side door was still swinging open. With a speed and agility that seemed impossible for a man of his build, Gregory launched himself into the moving vehicle, tumbling onto the passenger seat just as the Volvo hit the gap.

The sound was the world being torn apart. The entire right side of the station wagon screamed as it scraped along the jagged rock face. Sparks flew like fireworks, and the noise of groaning, ripping metal was deafening. The car shuddered violently, fighting to tear itself apart, but Barney kept his foot pressed to the floor, his knuckles white on the steering wheel. For a terrifying second, he thought they were stuck, but then with a final, horrible lurch, they were through. A shot rang out from behind them, and the rear window shattered, showering the back seat and their precious supplies with a cascade of glittering glass. Barney didn't look back. He pushed the battered, screaming Volvo down the mountain pass, the wind whipping through the broken window, the sound of their escape echoing through the canyon. He glanced at Gregory, who was sitting up, calmly brushing slivers of safety glass from the sleeve of his polo shirt. “Well,” Gregory said, his voice only slightly breathless. “I must say, Barnaby, that was a highly unorthodox solution. You’ve severely compromised the vehicle’s structural integrity.” Barney didn’t respond. He just laughed, a wild, ragged, and utterly triumphant sound. They were alive. And the dark, wounded heart of the mountains awaited.The laughter died in Barney’s throat, replaced by a painful, hacking cough. The adrenaline that had flooded his system, turning fear into fuel, was draining away, leaving behind a shaky, nauseous exhaustion. The Volvo, a noble and grievously wounded beast, protested every rotation of its tires. The scream of metal against rock had been replaced by a chorus of groans, rattles, and a high-pitched whistling from the mauled right side. Wind whipped through the shattered rear window, a cold, mocking gale that swirled dust and slivers of glass around the cabin. His hands, slick with sweat, trembled on the steering wheel. He had done it. They had escaped. And in doing so, he had likely beaten their only means of survival to death.

He drove for another mile, the road mercifully straightening out, before he saw a small, paved turnout—a scenic overlook from a time when people had the luxury of stopping to admire the view. He pulled the car in, the tortured squeal of the brakes echoing in the sudden silence as he killed the engine. For a long moment, they just sat there, listening to the ticking of hot metal and the lonely moan of the wind. Barney rested his forehead against the steering wheel, his whole body humming with the aftershock of the violence. They were alive, but the victory felt thin and temporary, like a paper bandage on a gaping wound. He took a deep, shuddering breath and forced himself to face the consequences.

Getting out of the car was a new challenge. His driver-side door, spared the worst of the impact, opened with a groan. Gregory’s, however, was jammed shut, the metal of the doorframe bent and twisted. He had to clamber awkwardly over the center console and out Barney’s side. Once free, they stood and surveyed the damage in silence. The Volvo was a wreck. It was no longer a car; it was a scar on wheels. The entire right side, from the front fender to the rear, was a single, continuous wound of peeled-back metal, deep gouges, and shattered plastic. The side-view mirror was gone, sheared off completely. The rubber on the right-side tires was flayed and scored, and the rear tire was canted at a sickeningly unnatural angle. It was a miracle they were still rolling at all.

Gregory circled the vehicle with the grave solemnity of a doctor examining a patient with a catastrophic injury. He ran his hand gently over the torn metal, his brow furrowed. He crouched to inspect the damaged tire, then stood and peered at the buckled roofline. He was silent for a full minute before he delivered his diagnosis. "The unibody construction has been severely compromised," he announced, his voice flat. "The door pillars are bent, which means the frame is likely twisted. That will affect the alignment and cause uneven tire wear. Furthermore, the drag coefficient has been catastrophically altered, which will have a significant negative impact on our fuel efficiency. We've lost at least five, maybe six miles to the gallon." Barney just stared at him. Only Gregory could watch a replay of their own near-death and emerge with concerns about the drag coefficient.

The most immediate problem, however, was the gaping, jagged hole where the rear window had been. The canvas sacks of potatoes and Jacob's precious jars were completely exposed. The wind had already blown a fine layer of road dust over everything. "We might as well be driving a convertible pickup truck," Barney said, his voice hollow with defeat. "Everything we have is on display. The next person who sees us won't even need to stage an accident. They’ll just follow us until we run out of gas and pick us clean." He kicked at a loose piece of gravel, the small, violent act doing nothing to dispel the immense cloud of despair that was settling over him.

Gregory looked from the exposed supplies to the jagged hole, then down at the neatly folded, cream-colored square he had been clutching to his chest throughout the entire ordeal. For a moment, he held it tighter, a man clinging to the last remnant of a world that made sense. Then, with a quiet sigh of resignation, he seemed to make a decision. "A temporary, non-adhesive covering," he murmured, more to himself than to Barney. "It won’t be weatherproof, but it will obscure the view and provide a rudimentary barrier against debris." He carefully, reverently, unfolded his king-sized fitted sheet. The pristine rectangle, his personal masterpiece of domestic engineering, billowed out in the mountain wind, a flag of truce in a world that only understood war.

"What are you doing?" Barney asked, though he already knew. "You can't. That's your… thing." He couldn't bring himself to say what it was: a shield, a memory, a piece of portable sanity. Gregory looked at him, his expression gentle but resolute. "Its primary function was to protect a mattress, Barnaby. This is merely a redeployment of assets to a higher-priority task." The logic was so absurdly, perfectly Gregory that Barney couldn't argue. He just watched as his strange companion assessed the broken window frame, his mind already working on the problem of installation. "We'll need to wedge the elasticated corners into the remaining window seal," Gregory explained. "It will require a great deal of tension to hold it in place. You'll need to pull from the inside while I secure the edges."

The process was a clumsy, desperate ballet. Barney climbed into the back of the station wagon, kneeling amidst the potatoes, and pulled the flapping sheet taut while Gregory, with the focused precision of a brain surgeon, used his fingertips and the edge of a stray piece of plastic trim to force the elastic deep into the cracked rubber gasket. They worked in silence, the flapping of the sheet and the grunts of their effort the only sounds. It was ridiculous. It was pathetic. It was two men trying to patch a mortal wound on a dying machine with bed linen. But as the last corner was wedged into place, the frantic flapping stopped. The sheet held, a taut, opaque barrier that transformed the gaping hole into a strange, blank canvas.

Barney crawled back out of the car, his knees covered in dust. He looked at their handiwork. The Volvo was now even more of a spectacle. A mustard-yellow wreck with a cream-colored, fabric patch billowing slightly from its rear end. It looked like a clown car that had lost a fight with a tiger. Gregory stood back, a smudge of grease now prominent on his cheek, and surveyed their work with a nod of profound satisfaction. "It's not ideal," he pronounced. "The tension is uneven, and it will vibrate terribly at speed. But it will serve its purpose." He looked at his empty hands, a brief, fleeting expression of loss crossing his face before it was replaced by his usual placid determination. He had sacrificed his personal totem for the good of their shared, insane mission.

They climbed back into the battered car, Gregory once again having to perform the awkward scramble over the center console. Barney stared at the table, then at the radio, and a new thought, a terrible and brilliant idea, began to spark in the darkness of his mind.
