From every recess and walkway, the Bedrockers attacked. It was not a charge, but an infestation. They swarmed from the darkness, armed with hissing pneumatic drivers that punched through armor plating and heavy, sharpened pry-bars that were brutally effective in the close-quarters chaos. The fight was a sensory explosion in Elara’s mind, a fragmented hell of the lead gorilla’s panic, his pain as a steel pipe shattered his knee, his confusion as his comrades fell around him, overwhelmed not by superior firepower, but by sheer, brutal ingenuity.

The true battle was at the pylon. As the strobing lights disoriented the guards, Joric and her handpicked team of two ignored them, their sole focus the orangutan and his drill. Elara gathered every ounce of her will, every dreg of her strength, and screamed into the network, a raw, psychic bolt of pure warning aimed directly at Joric. *LEFT!*

The mental effort was cataclysmic. A blinding, white-hot pain exploded behind her eyes, and she felt the warm, metallic trickle of blood from her nose. Her body convulsed on the cot, but the warning landed. Joric, her movements preternaturally fast, did not dodge. With practiced efficiency, she slammed the emergency shut-off on the Core-Breaker’s panel. The high-pitched whine of the drill died. The countdown in Elara’s head vanished, leaving behind a silence more profound than any sound.

They had faced the hammer of the Primacy and had not just survived; they had won. Joric stood over the silent Core-Breaker, her knuckles raw, her chest heaving. She looked at the neutralized device, then her gaze lifted, staring into the darkness in the direction of the infirmary, her expression a complex mixture of awe, respect, and a dawning, terrible understanding of the true power she now had at her disposal.

Back in the small, quiet room, Elara knew none of this. The psychic backlash from her final, desperate scream had been a circuit breaker for her mind. The Maelstrom, the battle, the voices—all of it dissolved into a welcome, painless nothingness. We’ll watch over you.* She fell into a deep, dreamless sleep, not the passive, empty slumber of a prisoner, but the profound, earned exhaustion of a soldier who had fought her first battle and, against all odds, had emerged victorious.The darkness receded not like a curtain being drawn, but like a tide going out, leaving behind a shoreline of profound stillness. In its place was a silence so deep and pure it felt like a tangible presence, a soft, heavy blanket tucking her in. It was a managed quiet, she realized, a walled garden built in the center of her own mind. And the gardener was Liam. His consciousness was no longer a fragile thread or a desperate whisper, but a steady, warm current flowing alongside her own. He was a silent companion, a gatekeeper holding back the storm so she could heal.

She opened her eyes. The single bulb in the infirmary still glowed, casting long shadows that seemed softer than before. Her body was a territory of dull aches and sharp, specific pains, but the frantic, wired exhaustion was gone, replaced by a deep, bone-weary languor. The saline drip was gone. The bowl of broth on the crate beside her was empty, though she had no memory of finishing it. Instead, there was a mug of water and a piece of hard, unleavened bread. The essentials for recovery. Joric’s pragmatism was its own form of care. As she sat up, a wave of dizziness passed, but it was a simple, physical symptom, not the psychic vertigo she had grown accustomed to. The blood from her nose had been cleaned away. Someone had tended to her while she was lost in the dark.

*Welcome back,* Liam’s voice echoed in her mind, clean and clear, stripped of all static. *You did well. The Maelstrom is… different. They felt the victory. They felt the trap spring. They felt your focus. The fear is still there, but it has a shape now. You’ve given them a spine.* Elara reached for a glass of water, her hand surprisingly steady. She was changing. The connection was no longer a parasitic drain but a symbiotic extension of her own senses. She could still feel the distant hum of the million minds, but it was an ocean heard from a high cliff, its power immense but no longer threatening to drown her. *What about Bedrock?* she asked, her thought a simple, direct query. *What happened after I passed out?*

The request was a cold shard of ice in Elara’s gut. The psychic connection with the Primacy guard had been a desperate, passive act of listening. What Joric was asking for was an invasion. A deliberate, targeted violation of another being’s consciousness. “You want me to… to read his mind?” Elara whispered, the concept feeling obscene. “That’s not how it works. I don’t read, I connect. It hardened into something resembling disappointment.

“Morality is a luxury purchased by security,” Joric stated, her voice dangerously quiet. “Your feelings on the matter are noted, but they are irrelevant when weighed against the survival of my people. The information in that ape’s head is the only thing that will keep us alive for the next week. Thrax will not accept this failure. He will not send another survey team. He will send an army. The only choice you have is whether you do it now, while we have time to prepare, or later, as our homes are burning around us.”

The curtain fell, leaving Elara alone in the silence of the infirmary, the weight of Joric’s ultimatum pressing down on her. She felt sick. She was a weapon, and her own conscience was being dismissed as a sentimental malfunction. *She’s not wrong, Elara,* Liam’s voice surfaced, gentle but firm. *From a purely tactical standpoint, the data is necessary.* Elara recoiled from her own brother’s logic. *You want me to do this? To violate him?* she shot back, her thought a spike of angry betrayal. *No,* Liam replied, his mental voice soothing her with a practiced calm. *I don’t want you to. But the Maelstrom does. A new sensation bloomed in her mind. It was a complex chord of thoughts and feelings from the collective. It was the silence of a demand made and an answer expected, the silence of a gun being cocked in a quiet room. Elara sat on the edge of the cot, the coarse blanket rough against her skin, and felt the immense, terrifying weight of the choice before her. To become a monster to fight monsters. To violate the sanctity of a mind, the very thing she had fought to liberate, in the name of survival. The Maelstrom hummed its unified, logical argument in the back of her consciousness, a million voices whispering that the needs of the many outweighed the rights of the one. They offered her power, precision, a clean and guiltless vivisection of a captive soul. It was this offer, this promise of a guilt-free atrocity, that terrified her the most. She could feel herself standing on a precipice, in danger of becoming the very thing she had rebelled against.

She pushed herself off the cot, her legs still unsteady, and walked out of the infirmary, her decision made. It was not a capitulation, but a negotiation.
