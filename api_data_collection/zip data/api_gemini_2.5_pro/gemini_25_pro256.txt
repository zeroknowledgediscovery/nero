The water was a solace, a thick blue silence that swallowed the world whole. Before the sun had even thought about cresting the volcanic ridges that cradled Bandung, Lady Cohen was already a creature of the pool. She cleaved the surface in a rhythm that was more than discipline; it was a prayer. Her long limbs, sculpted by a million repetitions, pulled her through the chlorinated cathedral, each stroke a silent syllable against the encroaching noise of the day. Down here, beneath the shimmering skin of the water, there were no expectations. There was only the burn in her shoulders, the controlled explosion of her kick, and the metronomic thrum of her own heart in her ears. The world above was a distorted wobble of light, a place of sharp edges and sharper words. The world below was fluid, forgiving, eternal.

She was not a lady in the titled sense; it was the name her Anglophile father had given her, a strange and formal inheritance in the heart of West Java. But she had grown into it, or perhaps the name had shaped her. There was a reserve to her, a quiet dignity that her teammates sometimes mistook for aloofness. They saw the relentless focus, the way she could shut out the locker room chatter and find her zone, but they didn't see the reason for it. They didn't know that for Lady, the world was a constant, low-grade hum of anxiety, and the water was the only thing that could ever truly turn the volume down. She swam not just to compete, but to survive the silence between her own thoughts.

Her turn at the wall was a compact, violent unfolding, a burst of power that sent her rocketing back the other way. Forty more lengths. Her mind counted automatically, a human abacus. The water rushed past her ears, a familiar roar. It was here, in this state of suspended effort, that she felt most herself. Or perhaps, least herself, which was even better. A body in motion, a machine of muscle and bone and will, untroubled by the ghosts that haunted the dry, static world. Ghosts of expectation, of failure, of a grief so vast and old it felt like it belonged to the very soil of the city. She didn't have a name for her grief, not like the giant did. Hers was a quiet, internal haunting. His, she was beginning to learn, was a spectacle.

After practice, the world came rushing back in. The slap of sandals on wet tile, the hiss of the showers, the bright, brittle chatter of the younger girls. The air grew heavy with the sweet scent of jasmine-scented soap mingling with the ever-present chlorine. Lady wrapped a towel around her waist, her skin still cool from her long immersion. Her friend and training partner, Maya, was already dressed, furiously texting on her phone, a small smile playing on her lips. Maya was her opposite—all bright colours and quick laughter, a creature of the sunlit world Lady so often felt excluded from.

“You were flying today, Lady,” Maya said without looking up from her screen. “Coach said your splits on the last hundred were almost your personal best.”

“The water felt good,” Lady replied, her voice still raspy from the effort. It was the most she ever offered. She pulled on a simple t-shirt and loose pants, her movements economical and precise. She didn’t like to linger. The locker room felt like a cage after the freedom of the pool.

“Good? It was incredible. You’re a machine.” Maya finally pocketed her phone. “Come on, I’m starving. Pak Agus’s *bubur ayam* is calling my name.”

They walked out into the cool Bandung morning. The city was waking up, a symphony of revving motorbike engines and the calls of street vendors. A fine mist, the tail end of the night’s rain, still clung to the broad leaves of the banana trees and gave the air a clean, earthy smell. It was a city of contrasts, of Dutch colonial architecture slowly crumbling beside gleaming new shopping malls, of ancient traditions and hyper-modern youth culture. They navigated the crowded pavement, a river of people flowing to work and school. Lady felt the familiar tension return to her shoulders. Too many people, too much noise. She focused on Maya’s bright yellow hijab bobbing ahead of her, a small beacon in the chaos.

The *warung* was a simple affair, a wooden cart with a few plastic stools set out under a tattered blue tarp. Pak Agus, a man whose face was a roadmap of wrinkles, greeted them with a familiar nod. He was already scooping steaming rice porridge into two bowls, his movements practiced and efficient.

“Nona Lady, Nona Maya,” he grunted, his voice thick with a Sundanese accent. “Extra fried onions for you.”

They sat on the low stools, the comforting warmth of the bowls seeping into their hands. This was a ritual, their small island of peace after the rigors of training. They ate in comfortable silence for a few minutes, the savoury porridge a balm. Lady watched the city go by, the endless parade of faces, each one a story she would never know. She felt a pang of something—not quite loneliness, but a profound sense of separation, as if she were watching it all through a pane of thick glass.

Then came the buzz. It wasn't Maya’s usual notification chime, a cheerful little trill. It was a series of rapid-fire vibrations, angry and insistent. Maya set her spoon down with a clatter, a frown creasing her brow as she pulled out her phone.

Lady watched her friend’s face change. It was a slow-motion collapse. The smile vanished, replaced by confusion, then a dawning horror. The colour drained from her cheeks, leaving her skin a sickly, ashen grey. Her eyes, wide and unblinking, were fixed on the screen. Her breath hitched.

“Maya?” Lady asked, her voice quiet. “What is it?”

Maya didn’t answer. She just held the phone out, her hand trembling so violently the screen was a blur. Lady took it from her. The first thing she saw was a group chat. *Sahabat Selamanya*—Friends Forever. A group with Maya and a dozen of her closest friends from university. Then she saw the post.

It was a picture. A close-up. Two feet, propped up on a wooden bench. They were Maya’s feet. Lady recognized the small, crescent-shaped scar on the left ankle from a childhood fall. They were bare, the nails painted a cheerful coral pink. It was an intimate, innocuous image, made grotesque by its context. And beneath it, a flood of messages from her friends. *Maya, what is this?* *Is this a joke?* *OMG, are you okay?*

Then Lady scrolled up to the post itself. The picture had been sent from Maya’s own account, which meant her account had been hacked. And there was a caption, a single, horrifying line sent to the entire group. *She liked the sound it made.* Below that, a name. *—Abhishek.*

A cold wave, far colder than the pool, washed over Lady. The noise in her head went completely silent. In its place was a sharp, terrifying clarity. Abhishek. The name had been whispered in news reports and frantic social media posts for weeks. A phantom who haunted the alleyways and quiet streets of the city. The stories were all the same, bizarre and violating. A woman walking alone. A sudden, looming shadow. A huge man, a giant, they said. He would get terrifyingly close, lean in, and blow a wet, explosive raspberry against their cheek or neck. And then he would vanish. The physical act was over in a second, but the humiliation was the real weapon. The next day, a picture of their feet, taken somehow in that fleeting moment, would be posted to their friends, their family, their colleagues. An intimate part of them, turned into a public spectacle, a trophy.

“He… he was there,” Maya whispered, her voice a thread of sound. She was staring at her own feet, tucked under the plastic stool, as if they were alien things. “Last night. I was walking home from the library. In the alley behind my house.”

Lady put the phone down, her own hands now perfectly steady. A strange calm had settled over her. It was the same calm she felt on the starting block before a championship race. All the nervous energy, all the anxiety, compressed into a single point of diamond-hard focus.

“Tell me,” she said. Her voice was flat, devoid of emotion. It was the voice she used with her coach when discussing fractions of a second.

Maya shook her head, tears finally beginning to spill down her cheeks, tracing paths through the condensation on her skin. “I didn't see his face. It was so fast. He was just… huge. He smelled like rain and… and something bitter. Like old grief. He blocked the light. And then… the sound.” She flinched, a full-body shudder. “Right in my ear. It was so loud. And wet. I screamed and he was gone. I just ran. I thought… I thought it was just a weirdo, a prank.”

Lady looked from Maya’s terrified face to the bowl of half-eaten porridge, now cold and congealed. The comforting ritual was shattered. The safe little island had been swallowed by a tidal wave of calculated cruelty. She looked at Maya’s feet again, the feet that propelled her through the water, that carried her through the world. Now they were a symbol of her violation, broadcast to everyone she loved.

The giant fuelled by grief. That’s what one journalist had called him. A man so broken he had to break others in the most bizarre way imaginable. He didn’t steal. He didn’t hit. He stole their sense of safety. He hit them with shame.

Lady reached across the small table and put her hand over Maya’s. Her hand was still cold from the pool, but it was steady. A rock. Maya’s hand was a frightened bird, trembling and slick with sweat. In that moment, something shifted inside Lady Cohen. The vague, shapeless anxieties that had plagued her for years suddenly had a target. The noise she had always tried to swim away from now had a name. Abhishek. The water had taught her many things. It had taught her discipline. It had taught her how to hold her breath in the crushing pressure of the deep end. It had taught her that the only way to get to the other side was to push through the resistance, to make the element your own. The giant had brought his noise into her silent, ordered world. He had hurt her friend. He had trespassed. And Lady Cohen, swimmer, daughter of Bandung, a woman who knew the deep and silent places, knew with an absolute and terrifying certainty that she had to be the one to make him stop. The race had begun.They left the *bubur ayam* unfinished, the congealing porridge a monument to a morning that had broken in two. Lady tucked a few crumpled rupiah notes under her empty bowl, her movements deft and final. She helped Maya to her feet, her friend’s body pliant and unresisting, like someone being pulled from a wreck. Pak Agus watched them go, his wrinkled face unreadable, but he wiped the counter with a slow, deliberate motion, as if erasing the moment from his small corner of the world. The chaos of the street seemed louder now, more menacing. The roar of a passing *angkota*, its sides painted with garish eagles, made Maya jump. Lady’s arm around her was a firm, unyielding brace.

“We’ll get a Gojek,” Lady said, her voice low and even. She was already navigating her own phone, her thumb tapping the screen with practiced speed. The world she usually held at arm’s length was now something she had to engage with, to command. There was no retreating beneath the water. The threat was up here, on the hot, crowded pavement, breathing the same jasmine-scented, exhaust-filled air. As they waited for their ride, Lady’s eyes scanned the crowd. She saw him everywhere and nowhere. Every tall man, every broad back, every shadow that stretched and warped in the morning light was a potential echo of the giant. The low-grade hum of her anxiety had sharpened into a high-frequency signal, an active sonar pinging against the shapes around her.

Maya’s apartment was on the third floor of a narrow building painted a cheerful, if peeling, shade of sky blue. Inside, it was a reflection of her personality: brightly coloured cushions on the floor, walls covered in framed photos of smiling friends and concert ticket stubs, a collection of tiny, whimsical succulents on the windowsill. It was a space built for light and laughter, which made the silence that fell as Maya collapsed onto her sofa all the more profound. She curled into a tight ball, her vibrant yellow hijab a splash of colour against the grey fabric, and began to cry in earnest—not the quiet tears from before, but ragged, wrenching sobs that shook her entire body.

Lady did not offer platitudes. She didn't say it would be okay, because she didn't know if that was true. Instead, she moved through the apartment with the quiet efficiency of a ghost. She filled a kettle and set it on the stove. She found a box of ginger tea in a cupboard filled with brightly patterned mugs. She dampened a washcloth with cool water and gently placed it on the back of Maya’s neck. They were small, mechanical acts, the dry-land equivalent of a perfectly executed turn at the wall. They were anchors. While the kettle hissed and whistled, Lady sat on the floor beside the sofa, not touching Maya, but simply being present, a solid, unmoving object in the turbulent room. She was a sea wall, built to withstand a storm.

When the sobs subsided into shuddering breaths, Lady spoke. "The alley behind your house," she stated, not a question. "The one that connects Jalan Merpati to the main road?" Maya nodded against the cushion, her face hidden. "What time?"

"I don't know... late," Maya mumbled. "Maybe ten. The library closed at nine-thirty. I stayed to talk to Rina."

"Was it dark? Were the lights working?" Lady’s mind was building a schematic of the scene. The dimensions of the pool, the temperature of the water, the exact placement of the starting blocks—these were the variables she controlled. Now, the variables were light, time, and shadow.

"One was flickering," Maya whispered. "The one halfway down. It made the shadows jump. I was rushing. I had my headphones in, but I wasn't listening to anything. I just... I like the quiet." The irony of her own words seemed to strike her, and a fresh wave of tears threatened. "He came from the recessed doorway. The old batik shop that closed down. I didn't see him until he was right there. He filled the whole alley. I looked up and all I could see was his chest."

“His smell,” Lady prompted gently. “You said he smelled like rain and something bitter.”

“Yes. Petrichor. Like the smell of the earth after a storm. And… cloves. No, not sweet like cigarettes. Sharper. Like clove oil. The kind my *nenek* uses for a toothache. Old medicine. Old pain.” Maya’s voice was detached now, her mind replaying the memory from a clinical distance, a self-protective reflex Lady knew all too well. “I never saw his face. He had a hood up. A dark hoodie. It was his size that I remember. I’m not small, Lady, you know that. But I felt like a child. He leaned down, and I felt his breath before… before the sound. It was hot. And then he was gone. Like he just dissolved back into the shadows.”

Lady stored every detail. The flickering light. The doorway. The smell of petrichor and clove oil. The sheer size of him. These were the new split times, the data points she needed. While Maya finally drifted into a fitful, exhausted sleep on the sofa, Lady sat at the small kitchen table, her friend's laptop open before her. She typed 'Abhishek Bandung' into the search bar. The screen filled with a mosaic of fear and morbid curiosity. News articles from local blogs, speculative threads on Kaskus, and dozens of frantic, angry posts on Twitter and Instagram. There were at least seven other documented victims in the past two months. All women. All walking alone. All in quiet, transitional spaces—alleys, parking garages, dimly lit residential streets.

She began to cross-reference. She pulled up a map of Bandung and started dropping pins on the locations of the attacks. A pattern began to emerge. A loose cluster around the northern part of the city, near the university campuses and the more affluent, leafy neighbourhoods. But it was spreading. She read the victims' accounts, when she could find them. They all repeated the same elements: the sudden, immense shadow, the speed, the bizarre, humiliating act, and then the digital violation the next day. The posting of the feet. Always the feet. A strange, almost fetishistic signature. But it was the caption that struck Lady the most. *She liked the sound it made.* It wasn't just an assault; it was a rewriting of the victim's experience. It was a lie, designed to compound the shame, to isolate them from their own friends with a veneer of perverse complicity. He wasn’t just a giant; he was a gaslighter. He didn't just haunt the alleys; he poisoned the wells of friendship and trust. Lady’s jaw tightened. She opened a new document, a blank white page, and began to type. *Victim 1: Anisa, March 12, Parking Garage Cikapundung. Victim 2: Dewi, March 21, Alley near Dago Tea House.* The abacus in her mind was clicking, the numbers and locations arranging themselves into a grim constellation. The water was her sanctuary because it was predictable, its rules immutable. This world, his world, was chaotic. But chaos, she knew, was often just a pattern you hadn't understood yet.Her list grew into a grim litany of violated spaces. She worked for hours, the only sounds the soft clicks of the keyboard and Maya’s shallow, sleeping breaths. The city outside moved from the frantic energy of the morning commute to the slower, heavier rhythm of midday. Lady didn’t notice. She was submerged again, but this time, the deep was made of data: dates, times, street names, and the fragmented, fearful testimonies of strangers. The pins on her digital map formed a loose, predatory arc across the northern suburbs, a hunting ground defined by shadows and solitude. She saw that the locations were not random. They were all places of transition, non-places where a person is neither here nor there, caught between origin and destination. They were moments of inherent vulnerability, and he knew it. He was a student of it.

She couldn’t stay behind the screen. The data was a skeleton, but she needed to feel its flesh. She needed to stand in those spaces and understand their grammar. She changed into her running clothes, the familiar stretch of spandex a thin armor. Maya was still on the sofa, wrapped in a blanket, staring blankly at the wall. "Going for a run," Lady said, her voice softer than she intended. It was a lie of omission. This was not about cardio. It was reconnaissance. Maya just nodded, not really seeing her. The bright light in her friend’s eyes had been completely extinguished.

Her first stop was not an alley but a multi-story parking garage near Cikapundung River, the site of the first reported attack. The air was thick and still, tasting of damp concrete, oil, and decay. She paid the attendant a few thousand rupiah and walked up the echoing ramps, her running shoes silent on the grimy floor. She found the spot from the news report—level 3B, a corner far from the stairwell, perpetually in the gloom cast by a massive concrete pillar. The lights up here hummed and flickered, casting long, distorted shadows. She stood where the victim, Anisa, must have stood, her back to the pillar, fumbling for her car keys. Lady closed her eyes. She imagined the sudden eclipse of the weak fluorescent light. The feeling of being cornered, of a presence so large it changed the air pressure. She tried to imagine the sound, the wet, percussive shock of it. There was no fear in her, only a cold, analytical curiosity. She was memorizing the terrain.

From the garage, she began to run. It was a new kind of training. Not for time, but for territory. Her route was a jagged line connecting the pins on her map. She ran with a swimmer’s endurance, her breathing deep and even, her gaze sweeping, absorbing details. She passed the Dago Tea House and found the narrow service alley behind it. She ran through the quiet residential lane in Gegerkalong where a university student had been accosted. Each location was a verse in the same ugly poem: poor lighting, blind corners, places to hide. They were flaws in the city’s design, and he was exploiting them.

Finally, she arrived at the alley behind Maya’s building. It was narrower than she’d imagined, the brick walls on either side stained with damp. Halfway down, a single fluorescent tube flickered erratically, just as Maya had described, making the shadows writhe and dance. She found the recessed doorway of the defunct batik shop. It was a dark mouth, set back a full meter from the alley wall, deep enough to completely conceal a large man from anyone approaching. She stepped inside it. The world of the alley shrank to a narrow slice of light. Anyone walking past would be completely oblivious until they were right on top of this spot. She looked out from the darkness, seeing the world as he must have seen it: a series of unsuspecting targets, framed and isolated. As she stood there, a scent snagged in her awareness, faint but distinct beneath the dominant odours of garbage and wet earth. It was sharp, medicinal, and deeply familiar. Clove oil. The smell of old medicine, of a pain being numbed. It was real. A tangible clue left behind by a ghost.

When she returned to the apartment, Maya was sitting up, scrolling through her phone. The digital part of the assault was continuing its insidious work. A cousin had sent a private message: *Maya, I saw that post. Are you hanging out with weird people? Be careful.* Another friend had simply sent a single question mark. Each message was a small cut, reminding her that her humiliation was a public event, a topic for gossip and concern-trolling. The giant’s caption, *She liked the sound it made,* was doing exactly what it was designed to do: planting a seed of doubt, making her friends question her, making her question herself. Lady took a bottle of water from the fridge and handed it to Maya, her fingers brushing her friend’s. Maya’s skin was cold.

The next morning, the water was waiting. The chlorine smell was sharper in the pre-dawn chill, a sterile promise of order. As Lady pushed off the wall and fell into her rhythm, she realized the pool had changed for her. It was no longer a sanctuary from the world, but a crucible in which to forge a response to it. The rhythmic pull, kick, glide, breathe was no longer a mantra to quiet her mind, but the turning of a massive engine of thought. The constant, low-grade hum of her anxiety was gone, replaced by the sharp, clear signal of her purpose. The distorted wobble of light from the world above no longer seemed threatening. It was just a problem to be solved, a code to be broken. She was not swimming away from the noise anymore. She was swimming towards its source. Each stroke was a calculation, each lap a tightening of the net. The giant had made a terrible mistake. He had brought his chaotic, ugly grief into her world of measured lengths and perfect lines. He had started a race with Lady Cohen, not realizing she had been training for it her entire life.That evening, Maya was a ghost in her own brightly-coloured apartment. She had pulled the curtains tight against the afternoon sun, plunging the rooms into a gloomy twilight. The television was on, but the volume was muted, the frantic, flashing images of a Korean drama playing out in an absurd pantomime. She hadn’t touched the food Lady had brought her, a container of *soto Bandung* from her favourite stall. It sat on the coffee table, a small, cold offering. The storm of her grief had passed, leaving behind a dead, unnerving calm. She moved slowly, deliberately, as if the air itself were thick and heavy. She checked the lock on her front door for the third time in an hour.

Lady watched her from the kitchen, methodically cleaning the dishes they had used. The small, repetitive act of scrubbing a plate, rinsing it, and placing it in the drying rack was a comfort. It was a problem with a simple, tangible solution. “They’re creating a new group chat,” Maya said, her voice monotone, her eyes fixed on her phone. “Without me. Rina told me. She said it was just so they could talk about the trip next month without… without it being awkward.”

The word hung in the air. *Awkward.* The lie he had told—*She liked the sound it made*—was a corrosive agent, eating away at the structures of her life. It made her friends’ pity feel tinged with suspicion. It made their concern feel like an interrogation. He had made her not just a victim, but a social liability. Lady dried her hands, her knuckles white. The cold, clear focus she’d felt at the porridge stand was hardening into something else, something heavier and more dangerous. It was one thing to hunt a shadow. It was another to watch your best friend be erased by it, one well-meaning, cowardly text at a time.

“Do you remember,” Lady began, her voice quiet but clear in the still room, “when we were kids, at the provincial championships? The girl from Tasikmalaya, the one who kept false starting in the backstroke to throw you off?” Maya didn't look up, but a slight flicker in her posture told Lady she was listening. “You were so rattled. You almost scratched from the final. Coach said to ignore her, to focus on your own lane. But that wasn’t right. You can’t ignore a shark in the water. You have to watch it. You have to know its habits. You have to be smarter than it.”

Lady walked over and sat on the floor opposite the sofa, placing herself directly in Maya’s line of sight. “I told you to watch her breath. To start your own pull the second she inhaled for her start. You used her trick against her. You beat her by a full body length.” She leaned forward, her gaze intense. “This is the same. He has habits. He has a pattern. He thinks he’s the only one watching. He’s wrong.” Maya finally looked at her, and for the first time all day, a glimmer of something other than despair flickered in her eyes. It was the faintest spark of the competitor she was, the girl who knew what it meant to race.

The next day, Lady’s mission had a name: *Minyak Cengkeh*. Clove oil. The smell of old medicine and numbed pain. It was the only tangible thing the giant had left behind, a faint olfactory signature in a crime built on ephemera. After her morning practice, where she swam with a cold, relentless fury that left the younger swimmers staring in awe, she didn’t join the rush for the showers. She pulled out her phone and searched for *toko jamu*—traditional medicine shops—near the cluster of attack sites. There were dozens, from slick, modern herbalists in the malls to dusty, hole-in-the-wall counters in the labyrinthine alleys of the city’s older quarters. She started with the oldest and most traditional, the places that felt like they belonged to the same soil as the giant’s grief.

Her first two stops were fruitless. The shopkeepers were busy, the air thick with the scent of ginger, turmeric, and a hundred other roots and leaves. They sold tiny bottles of clove oil, the kind a person would buy for a toothache. They barely glanced at her when she asked if they’d had any unusual customers, any particularly large men. She was just another face in the endless stream of the city. But at the third shop, tucked away in a narrow street behind the sprawling Pasar Baru market, the atmosphere was different. It was less a shop and more a dim, fragrant cavern, the walls lined with dark wood cabinets and glass jars filled with dried, mysterious things. An old woman with skin like crumpled parchment and eyes that seemed to see more than the visible world sat on a low stool, meticulously sorting galangal root.

Lady approached quietly, her swimmer’s frame feeling oversized and clumsy in the cramped space. The woman, Ibu Yati, looked up. "Looking for something to make you faster, Nona?" she rasped, a ghost of a smile on her lips. "I have something for endurance. The Dutch soldiers used to buy it."

Lady smiled back, a small, rare thing. “Something for inflammation, maybe. Too much training.” She bought a packet of pre-mixed herbs, paying for it and making small talk about the rain. Then, as she was about to leave, she paused. “Ibu, a strange question. My uncle has a terrible pain. In his shoulders. He’s a very big man. He uses a lot of clove oil for it, for massage. Do you sell it in large bottles?”

Ibu Yati’s sharp eyes narrowed, not with suspicion, but with recollection. She gestured with a gnarled finger toward a dusty shelf. “Not many use it for massage. It is too… hot. Too aggressive. For the teeth, yes. For a deep muscle ache? Perhaps.” She paused, her gaze turning inward. “There is one. A quiet man. Big, yes. Like a mountain that walks. He does not talk much. He buys the large bottle. Every few weeks.” A chill, separate from the pool’s cold, traced its way down Lady’s spine.

“Does he live around here?” Lady asked, trying to keep her voice casual, the way one might inquire about a mutual acquaintance.

“I do not know where he lives,” Ibu Yati said, turning back to her roots, the conversation seemingly over. “He is not from here. He has the look of a man carrying a heavy stone inside him. A sorrowful giant.” The shopkeeper’s words echoed the journalist’s description. She picked up a piece of galangal and inhaled its earthy scent. "He pays with new bills. Always crisp. And he smells of the rain, even on a sunny day." The final detail landed like a hammer blow. It was him. This was not a guess; it was a certainty. He had a source. He had a routine. The ghost now had a shadow. Lady thanked the old woman, her heart a metronomic thrum against her ribs, the same rhythm she felt on the starting block. The race was no longer just a map of his attacks, but a trail leading from a single, fragrant source.The knowledge settled in her not like a victory, but like a weight. A tangible, physical certainty that anchored her in the chaotic world. He was real. He was knowable. He had a routine. The ghost was starting to cast a shadow. Lady walked away from the fragrant, dim-lit shop, the packet of herbs a useless prop in her hand. The noise of the Pasar Baru market, a cacophony of haggling vendors and honking scooters, seemed to fade into a distant hum. All she could hear was Ibu Yati’s raspy voice: *A mountain that walks. A sorrowful giant.* She had found the source of the clove oil, the spring from which his strange ritual flowed. Now she just had to wait by the water’s edge.

She returned to Maya’s apartment to find it changed. The heavy curtains had been thrown open, letting the harsh, bright light of the late afternoon flood the room. Maya was still on the sofa, but she was sitting up straight, her laptop balanced on her knees. On the screen was Lady’s own grim spreadsheet of dates and locations. Maya had added a new column, colour-coded and annotated. She had been working. She looked up as Lady entered, and the vacant, haunted look in her eyes had been replaced by a small, hard spark of anger. It was the best thing Lady had seen all day.

“Every few weeks, she said,” Maya stated, her voice tight. “So I checked. Anisa was attacked on March 12th. Rini was April 2nd. That’s twenty-one days. Three weeks exactly. The next one, Farida, was April 26th. Twenty-four days. Then you, May 19th. Twenty-three days.” She pointed a trembling but determined finger at the screen. “He’s on a cycle. A three-to-four-week cycle. The last bottle of oil would have been bought sometime before Anisa. If he comes back every few weeks…”

Lady moved to stand behind her, looking at the data. The abacus in her own mind was clicking in sync with Maya’s furious typing. The pattern was undeniable. It was the rhythm of an obsession, the schedule of a man tending to a chronic wound. “When was the last attack before you?” Lady asked, her eyes scanning the list.

“May 8th. A girl near the ITB campus,” Maya answered without looking. “Eleven days before me. He’s speeding up. Or becoming more… needy.” She scrolled down to a new section of the spreadsheet. It was a list of names, the other victims. Beside each name was a string of social media links. “I messaged them,” Maya said, her voice dropping to a near-whisper. She finally looked at Lady, her expression a mixture of shame and defiance. “I sent a message to Anisa, the first one. I told her it happened to me, too. I told her I believed her. I asked her… I asked her about his smell.”

It was an act of profound bravery. For Maya, a creature of bright connections and effortless sociability, to deliberately reach back into the trauma and connect with a stranger over their shared humiliation was like diving into the deep end without knowing how to swim. It was a rejection of the isolation he had tried to impose on her.

“She just wrote back,” Maya said, turning back to the screen and clicking open a messaging app. A simple reply was stark against the white background. *Yes. Rain. And cloves. Like a dentist's office. How did you know?*

They worked through the evening, a strange new partnership forged in the wreckage of the old one. Lady, the strategist, mapped the city, calculated the probabilities, and sketched out the geography of his hunting ground. Maya, the communicator, navigated the intricate digital webs that connected his victims. She coaxed out details with a quiet empathy that Lady, with her blunt efficiency, could never possess. They learned that the giant always approached from the left. That he never spoke a word. That two of the victims, besides Maya, remembered the detail of him wearing a dark hoodie, even on warm nights. They were building a profile not of a monster, but of a man, a creature of habit as predictable as the tide.

Based on his cycle of attacks and Ibu Yati’s vague timeline, they came to a stark conclusion. He was due for a refill. Soon. Possibly within the next forty-eight hours. The trap had to be set now. The next evening, they were parked in a borrowed car a hundred meters down the road from Ibu Yati’s *toko jamu*. The car, a battered Toyota, belonged to Maya’s cousin and smelled faintly of baby formula and stale french fries. It was an absurdly domestic vessel for the hunt they were on. Lady sat in the driver’s seat, her posture perfectly still, her gaze fixed on the narrow entrance to the shop. She had the eerie calm of a swimmer in the moments of silence before the starting pistol fires. All the training, all the preparation, had led to this single point.

Maya fidgeted in the passenger seat, her phone a useless weight in her hands. The digital world she usually inhabited was gone, replaced by the gritty, analogue reality of the street outside. The sun had set, and the streetlights cast a sickly orange glow, turning the evening mist into a luminous haze. The sounds of the city were muted through the glass: the whine of motorbikes, the distant clang of a seller’s cart, the murmur of voices from a nearby food stall. For an hour, nothing happened. The waiting was a physical presence in the car, a heavy blanket.

“What if we’re wrong?” Maya whispered, her voice cracking. “What if he came yesterday? What if he’s not coming at all?”

“He’ll come,” Lady said, her voice flat and certain. Her certainty wasn’t based on hope, but on data. On the rigid patterns of obsession. “People like him, they don’t break their routines. The routine is all they have.”

And then, she saw him. He didn’t emerge from the crowd so much as coalesce out of the evening shadows. One moment the street was a river of ordinary people, the next, he was there, walking toward the *toko jamu*. He was immense. The word ‘giant’ was not an exaggeration. He was a head taller than anyone else on the street, with shoulders so broad they seemed to block the light behind him. He wore a dark, hooded sweatshirt, the hood pulled low, and loose cargo pants. He moved with a slow, heavy gait, like his body was a burden to him. There was nothing overtly menacing in his walk, but his sheer scale was an act of intimidation. People seemed to unconsciously give him a wider berth, a silent, instinctual recognition of his mass. Lady’s heart didn’t race; it settled into a slow, powerful thrum. The metronome.

He paused just before the entrance to the shop, and for a terrifying second, he turned his head, his face still lost in the deep shadow of his hood, and seemed to look directly at their car. Maya gasped and shrank down in her seat, a reflexive motion of pure fear. But Lady didn’t flinch. She met the unseen gaze, her focus absolute. It was the moment the swimmer sees the far wall, the target. He was no longer a ghost or a data point. He was a man in a dark hoodie, smelling of rain and clove oil, standing twenty meters away. The giant had a face, even if she couldn’t see it yet. The real race had just begun.The moment stretched, taut and thin as a starting line. The giant’s hooded head turned away from their car as if pulled by an invisible string, his attention returning to the small, fragrant shop. He moved with a heavy finality, disappearing into the dim doorway and eclipsing the light within. The breath Maya had been holding escaped in a ragged, shaky puff, fogging a small patch on the passenger-side window. She wiped it away with the heel of her hand, her eyes still wide with a primal fear. Lady didn’t move. Her hands rested lightly on the steering wheel, her posture unchanged, her gaze locked on the entrance. She was in the silent, breath-held moment after the ‘take your marks’ command, waiting for the gun.

“He saw us,” Maya whispered, her voice a reedy thread. “Lady, he looked right at us.”

“He saw a car,” Lady replied, her tone as even and placid as the surface of the pool on a windless morning. “One of a thousand cars on this street. He’s not looking for us. He doesn’t know we exist.” The words were meant to be a comfort, but they came out as a cold statement of fact, a strategic assessment. In his world, he was the hunter, the watcher. The idea that he himself could be the prey was a possibility that had likely never occurred to him. That was his weakness. That was her advantage.

They waited. Each second was a slow drip of dread for Maya, a measured beat of the metronome for Lady. After what felt like an eternity but was only two minutes, the giant re-emerged. In his large hand, he clutched a small, plain brown paper bag. The clove oil. Their theory made manifest. He didn’t linger. He turned and began to walk, heading north, his heavy, rhythmic footsteps carrying him back into the river of people, his immense form a moving island in the current. Lady turned the key. The car’s engine coughed to life with a quiet hum. She switched on the headlights, two anonymous beams in a sea of light, and pulled smoothly away from the curb. The hunt was on.

Navigating the evening traffic of Bandung was its own form of combat. A chaotic ballet of motorbikes weaving through impossibly small gaps, of angkot vans stopping abruptly to disgorge passengers, of cars inching forward bumper-to-bumper. It was the antithesis of Lady’s ordered world, a symphony of noise and unpredictable motion. Yet she drove with an unnatural calm, her focus narrowed to a single point: the dark, hooded figure walking two hundred meters ahead. She kept two cars and a swarm of scooters between them, using the city’s chaos as camouflage. Maya, despite her terror, became her eyes and ears, her fear sharpening her senses.

“He’s turning right onto Jalan Cihampelas,” Maya said, her voice steadier now that she had a task. “It’s going to be packed. We’ll lose him.”

“No, we won’t,” Lady said. She hung back, letting the traffic build, then shot through a gap, making a hard right turn, her movements economical and precise. She was using the flow, predicting its eddies and currents. They caught sight of him again, his broad back moving past the garish storefronts of the famous ‘Jeans Street.’ He ignored the brightly lit chaos, the calls of vendors, the throngs of teenage shoppers. He was a man walking through a world he was not part of, his gaze fixed on some internal horizon. He moved with a purpose that was entirely his own.

He led them north, away from the commercial heart of the city and into the quieter, older district of Ciumbuleuit. Here, the streets grew wider, flanked by high walls of moss-stained stone and wrought iron. Behind them lay the sprawling ghosts of Dutch colonial architecture, great villas set back from the road in vast, overgrown gardens. The air grew cooler, heavier, smelling of damp earth and fragrant night-blooming flowers. The noise of the city receded, replaced by the chirping of crickets and the distant bark of a dog. It was a neighbourhood of secrets, a place where the past lingered like the evening mist.

The giant turned off the main road and onto a narrow, tree-lined lane. The branches of ancient flame trees formed a dense canopy overhead, plunging the street into near-total darkness, broken only by the occasional lonely streetlamp. Lady killed her headlights, the car coasting forward in shadow. He was slowing down. They were close. He stopped before a tall, rusted gate, half-swallowed by a riot of bougainvillea. With a grating squeal of metal that scraped at the silence, he pushed it open and disappeared inside. Lady pulled the car to the curb a hundred meters down, parking under the deep shadow of a banyan tree, and cut the engine. The silence that filled the car was absolute.

Before them, set far back from the road at the end of a cracked and weed-choked driveway, stood his house. It was a monument to decay. A two-story Dutch villa, once a place of grandeur, now in a state of terminal decline. The white paint was peeling away in long strips, revealing the dark, stained plaster beneath like rotting flesh. Shutters hung crookedly from the windows, and the vast garden was a wild, untamed jungle. It wasn't just a house; it was a grief made of stone and peeling paint, a sorrowful giant standing sentinel over its own ruin. After a moment, a single light flickered on in a second-story window, a pale, lonely yellow square against the immense darkness of the facade.

They sat for a long time, watching that single square of light. It was the only sign of life. They had found him. They had tracked the ghost to his grave. Maya finally broke the silence, her voice barely a whisper. “We have his address. We can go to the police now, Lady. We can tell them everything.”

Lady’s gaze remained on the house, on that solitary window. The police. They would ask for proof. They would take statements. There would be reports and procedures. They would ask Maya to relive her humiliation for a skeptical male officer. They might come here, knock on the door, and the giant would deny everything. His word against theirs. What was the crime, after all? Blowing a raspberry? Posting a picture of feet? It was a crime of shame, and shame was a wound the law had no way to stitch. He had built his entire reign of terror on that simple, terrible fact. He knew the system couldn't touch him.

“No,” Lady said, and the single word was hard and cold and final. The abacus in her mind had finished its calculation. The race wasn’t over. This was just the final turn. The police couldn’t give Maya back her friends, or her peace of mind, or the feeling of safety in her own city. The system couldn’t make the noise stop. She looked away from the house and turned to her friend, her eyes, accustomed to the gloom, seeming to gleam with a chilling intensity. “He brought the noise into our world, Maya. I’m going to go into his and turn it off.”Maya stared at her, the interior of the car suddenly feeling small and suffocating. The single yellow window of the giant’s house seemed to press in on them, a malevolent eye. “Turn it off? What does that mean, Lady? What are you going to do?” Her voice was thin, laced with a new kind of fear, a fear not for herself, but for her friend. “We found him. We did the impossible part. Now we hand it over. To the police. To people who handle monsters.”

“He’s not a monster,” Lady said, her voice still unnervingly calm. She finally turned her gaze from the house to look at Maya, and in the dim light, her friend’s face was a mask of terror. “He’s a man. A creature of habit. Monsters are unpredictable. He’s the most predictable person I’ve ever seen. The police will file a report. They will ask you questions that will make you feel dirty all over again. They might get a warrant, and when they do, he will stand there in his giant sadness and say nothing. What is the charge? Humiliation? There is no law for that. And all the while, the pictures are still out there. The whispers in your group chat will get louder. They can’t fix what he broke, Maya. They can only put him in a cage. They can’t make the world quiet for you again.”

The logic was cold and sharp and terrifyingly clear. It bypassed emotion and went straight to the structural truth of their situation. Maya felt a wave of helpless anger. Anger at the giant for his cruelty, at her friends for their weakness, and now at Lady for her impossible, reckless certainty. “So what? You’re going to break into his house? And do what? Take his picture? Post it to his friends? He probably doesn’t have any friends, Lady! He’s a ghost in a tomb! He will break you in half.”

Lady reached over and took the keys from the ignition, the small jingle of metal a final, decisive sound in the charged silence. She dropped them into Maya’s lap. “Go home, Maya. Get some sleep.” It wasn’t a suggestion; it was a dismissal. A gentle but firm partitioning of their worlds. From this point on, Lady would swim in a lane of her own. For a long moment, Maya couldn’t move, caught between the urge to grab Lady and shake her back to sanity and the overwhelming instinct to flee. The single yellow window was still watching. Finally, her hands trembling, she slid over to the driver’s seat, her movements stiff and clumsy. She didn’t say goodbye. There were no words for this. As she pulled away, the last thing she saw in her rearview mirror was Lady’s solitary figure, a statue of purpose, walking calmly into the deep shadows that lined the crumbling villa’s walls.

The next morning, the water was a confession. Lady cleaved its surface before anyone else had arrived, the only sounds the rhythmic splash of her strokes and the hollow echo of her own breathing in the vast, empty pool hall. The water was no longer a cathedral; it was a whetstone, and with every lap she was sharpening her focus, grinding away any feeling that wasn't pure, cold intent. She didn’t count lengths. She visualized the house. The high stone wall, its surface a treacherous map of moss and missing mortar. The overgrown garden, a jungle of defensive thorns and tangled roots. The single lit window on the second floor. Her mind, the same mind that could calculate split times to the hundredth of a second, was now mapping entry points, calculating risks, planning a route through a different kind of resistance. She was not escaping the world today. She was rehearsing her invasion of it.

That night, she returned. She wore all black, the colour of the deep end, of the water after the lights have been turned off. She moved through the sleeping Ciumbuleuit streets like a phantom, her runner’s gait eating up the pavement in silence. The house was as she had left it, a hulking shape of deeper black against the starless sky. This time, there were no lights on. He was asleep. Or he was waiting. It didn’t matter. She found the spot she had marked in her mind, a section of the perimeter wall where a great banyan tree had forced its roots through the stone, creating a series of rough handholds and footholds. She tested her weight, her muscles, sculpted by a million repetitions, bunching and releasing with practiced efficiency. It was a climb, not a swim, but the principle was the same: leverage, power, and a complete disregard for the burn. She swung a leg over the top, her body folding and unfolding with the same compact violence as her turn at the wall, and dropped silently into the untamed garden on the other side.

The air inside the walls was different. It was heavy, stagnant, thick with the smell of jasmine and decay, of wet earth and something else—the faint, pervasive medicinal scent of clove oil that seemed to seep from the very pores of the house. The garden was a labyrinth of thorny bushes and thick, grasping vines. She moved through it slowly, her senses on high alert, placing each foot with the care of a bomb disposal expert. She reached the house itself, running her hands along the peeling plaster. She found what she was looking for: a service window for the kitchen, its wooden frame warped by years of rain and neglect. The latch was old and rusted. Using the thin, strong blade of a diver’s knife she’d brought, she worked it into the gap, leveraging it with steady pressure. For a heart-stopping moment it held fast, and then with a low groan of protesting wood, it gave way. She slid the window open, hoisted herself through the narrow opening, and dropped into the absolute darkness of the giant’s kitchen.

The silence inside was a living thing. It was heavier than the water at the bottom of the deep end, and it pressed in on her ears. The air was a physical presence, thick with the dust of forgotten decades and the sharp, medicinal tang of his obsession. Using a small penlight, its beam a tiny, trembling spear in the vast darkness, she began to move. The kitchen was a wasteland of broken tiles and grimy surfaces. She moved through a formal dining room where a great mahogany table lay shrouded in a dusty white sheet, a ghostly banquet hall. She ascended a wide, sweeping staircase, its wooden steps creaking under her weight with agonizing slowness, each sound an explosion in the stillness. She was inside his world now, breathing his air, walking his floors. She was a virus entering the bloodstream.

She followed the scent of cloves, which grew stronger as she moved down the second-floor hallway. It led her not to the room with the window she had seen, but to a smaller room at the end of the hall. The door was slightly ajar. Pushing it open, she found the source. It was a small study, and it was here that he lived. A single mattress lay on the floor in the corner. A laptop sat open on a simple desk, its screen dark. And on a small table, next to a large bottle of clove oil and a pile of clean, crisp banknotes, was a camera. But it was the walls that made her breath catch in her throat. They were covered, floor to ceiling, with photographs. Dozens and dozens of them. They were all pictures of feet. Anisa’s, Dewi’s, Maya’s. All of his victims. Intimate, innocuous images turned into a grotesque gallery, a testament to his campaign of shame. She saw Maya’s feet, the nails painted coral pink, and a cold, pure rage unlike anything she had ever known washed through her.

But then her light found a different section of the wall, set apart from the others. Here, there was only one photograph, much older, its colours faded. It was not of a victim. It was a family portrait. A proud-looking man with the giant’s broad shoulders, a smiling woman, and a little girl of about seven, with bright, laughing eyes, standing between them. Next to the photo was a small pair of children’s shoes, red and scuffed at the toes. And below that, pinned to the wall with a single thumbtack, was a drawing, done in a child’s unsteady hand. It was a picture of a little girl with a huge smile, holding hands with a giant stick figure. Above it, scrawled in crayon, was a single word: *Ayah*. Father. Lady moved her light back to the photograph, to the face of the little girl. The face was familiar. With a sickening lurch, she realized she had seen it before, in a news report from years ago. A hit-and-run. A child killed on her way home from school. A driver who was never found. And the girl’s name, she remembered, was Diya. Diya Abhishek. His daughter. The giant’s grief wasn’t a spectacle. It was a shrine. And his bizarre, cruel ritual was not random. It was a broken, twisted attempt to recreate the last thing he ever saw of his daughter: her feet, sticking out from under the blanket they had used to cover her on the street.The cold, sharp clarity of Lady’s rage, the diamond-hard point of focus that had guided her through the dark house, shattered against the fragile, faded photograph. It was a silent, internal explosion, leaving behind not heat, but a profound, hollowing cold. The abacus in her mind stopped clicking. The metronomic thrum of her heart faltered. The world, which for the past forty-eight hours had simplified into a race track with a clear villain and a finish line, suddenly dissolved into a gray, murky fog of incomprehensible sorrow. *Ayah*. Father. Diya Abhishek. The name echoed from a half-forgotten news report, a brief tragedy in the city’s endless cycle of life and death. Now it was the key, the terrible, Rosetta Stone that translated his bizarre cruelty into a language of unbearable grief. The feet. He wasn't collecting trophies. He was trying to see his daughter again.

A sound, low and guttural, drifted from the adjacent room. A groan of someone turning in a troubled sleep. Lady froze, her penlight snapping off, plunging the room back into absolute blackness. She was a statue in the suffocating dark, every nerve ending screaming. The sound came again, and this time it formed a word, a broken whisper thick with sleep and sorrow. "Diya." The name was not a memory; it was an open wound. He was not a monster sleeping in his lair; he was a father trapped in a nightmare from which he could never wake. The malice she had come to punish felt like a phantom limb; the real presence in this house was a grief so vast and suffocating it had become the very architecture of the place.

Her original plan, a vague but violent notion of turning his own methods against him, of exposing him with his own camera, now seemed grotesquely inadequate, like trying to cure a plague with a slap on the wrist. To hurt him would be to add another meaningless echo to a room already screaming with pain. To expose him to the police felt just as hollow. They would cage the body, but his spirit was already imprisoned in a cell of his own making, a cell whose walls were papered with the faces of his daughter and the feet of strangers. Her mission had been to turn the volume down, to restore the silence he had shattered for Maya and the others. But how could she silence a scream that had been ringing for years?

She switched the penlight back on, the beam now feeling like a sacrilegious intrusion. Her gaze fell again upon the wall of victims. Maya’s feet, her cheerful coral-pink nail polish a small, defiant spark of life in this mausoleum of sorrow. The anger for her friend was still there, a hot coal buried in the cold ash of this new understanding. The giant’s pain did not excuse the pain he had inflicted. It did not magically un-send the messages or erase the shame. But it changed the equation. This was not a punishment. It had to be a correction. An intervention. With a resolve that came from the deepest, quietest part of herself, the part that knew the immutable rules of the water, she knew what she had to do. She would not destroy his shrine. She would edit it.

With the delicate precision of a surgeon, she began to dismantle the gallery of victims. Her fingers, strong and sure from a lifetime of gripping the starting block, carefully worked the thumbtacks from the plaster. She did not rip them down. She curated them, lifting each photograph from the wall as if removing a shard of glass from a wound. Each picture was a small weight in her hand, a stolen moment of intimacy. She worked in a focused silence, her movements economical and swift, a dry-land version of her perfect, water-slicing stroke. One by one, she liberated the images from the wall, stacking them in a neat, orderly pile on his desk. She was imposing her world of clean lines and measured discipline onto his world of chaotic, spiraling grief.

A floorboard creaked heavily in the bedroom. A thick, congested cough. The giant was stirring. Adrenaline, cold and sharp, lanced through her. Her heart began to pound, no longer a steady metronome but a frantic, panicked drum. She swept the remaining photos from the wall, her movements now faster, a controlled flurry of motion. The wall was nearly bare. She took one last look, her light scanning the room. She had left his daughter. The faded family portrait, the scuffed red shoes, the crayon drawing of *Ayah*. That part of his grief was his alone. She had only removed the parts that had spilled out and poisoned the world. She scooped up the thick stack of photographs, a damning and tragic manuscript, and slid them into the empty pouch on her belt.

Her escape was a masterclass in controlled fear. She moved back through the house, a ghost in reverse. Every creak of the grand staircase, every rustle of the wind against the broken shutters, was the sound of his approach. The air no longer felt just tragic; it was now electric with menace, the sorrowful giant’s potential for violence a palpable threat. She slipped through the kitchen window, her body folding back into the night, and dropped onto the soft, damp earth of the garden. She didn't look back. She navigated the tangled growth, scaled the banyan-choked wall, and melted back into the silent, sleeping streets of Ciumbuleuit, the stack of photographs a cold, heavy weight against her hip. She had not won. She had not lost. She had simply finished her race, and the finish line felt nothing like she had expected.

The sun, when it came, felt different. It crept into the dusty, second-floor room of the decaying villa, its gentle light falling on the hulking form of Abhishek as he slowly awoke. He sat up, his massive frame seeming to suck the very air from the room. His movements were slow, burdened, the actions of a man who had not rested. His morning was a ritual as fixed as the sun’s rising. He walked his heavy tread down the hall to the small study, the room that was both his heart and his tomb. He entered, his eyes still clouded with sleep and sorrow, expecting to see the familiar gallery, the faces of his grief.

And then he stopped. The heavy, shuffling momentum of his body ceased so abruptly it was as if he had run into an invisible wall. He stood in the doorway, his huge form utterly still, and stared. The walls were bare. The constellation of stolen intimacy, the mosaic of his shared shame, was gone. Wiped clean. His gaze darted around the room. The camera. The oil. The mattress. All there. His eyes fixed on the small, lonely shrine in the corner. Diya’s faded smile, her scuffed red shoes, her childish drawing. Untouched. Perfect. His gaze drifted back to the vast, empty expanse of plasterboard, pockmarked with the tiny wounds of a hundred thumbtacks. He took a hesitant step forward and reached out a hand, his thick fingers, large enough to palm a basketball, touching the empty wall with a strange, delicate tenderness. He traced the pinholes, his brow furrowed not in anger, but in a profound, bottomless confusion. The loop was broken. The echo was gone. Someone had crept into the heart of his madness and imposed a terrifying, inexplicable silence.For Abhishek, the empty wall was a physical blow, a concussive force that left a ringing in his ears louder than any silence he had ever known. The world tilted, the familiar geography of his sanctuary suddenly rendered alien. He stumbled forward and pressed his palms flat against the cool plaster, as if he could feel the ghost of the images through his skin. The pinholes were a constellation of absences, a map of what was lost. His frantic, terrified gaze swept the room. The laptop was there. The money was there. The bottle of clove oil, his dark sacrament, sat untouched. His eyes locked on the corner of the wall he had not dared to look at directly. Diya’s smiling face, the scuffed red shoes, her childish drawing of *Ayah*. They were all still there, a tiny, sacred island in an ocean of inexplicable emptiness.

The precision of the act was its most terrifying feature. This was not a robbery. This was not vandalism. This was a targeted, silent, devastatingly intimate act. Someone had walked through his fortress of grief, breathed the air of his obsession, and looked upon its most sacred and profane artifacts. They had seen his victims, and they had seen his daughter. And they had made a choice. They had judged him. They had dissected his madness, separating the part that belonged only to him from the part he had inflicted upon the world, and they had surgically removed the latter. The sheer understanding implicit in the act was more violating than any physical assault. It was the violation of being truly, completely seen. He sank to his knees, his massive frame folding in on itself, the sorrowful giant brought low not by a show of force, but by a quiet, critical gaze. The rage he expected to feel was absent, replaced by a cold, primal fear. The hunter, for the first time in years, felt the unmistakable sensation of being watched.

The next morning, the water of the Cikapayang pool was a baptism. As Lady’s body cut through the cool, chlorinated blue, it felt less like a prayer and more like a confession. She was washing the dust of the villa from her skin, the scent of clove oil from her memory, the weight of the giant’s grief from her soul. With every powerful stroke, every controlled explosion of her kick, she was pushing the night away, seeking the clean, uncomplicated burn of exhaustion. But the sanctuary had been compromised. She had brought the ghost of that house back with her. She could feel it in the unusual tension in her shoulders, in the way she held her breath a fraction of a second too long before each turn. The vague hum of anxiety she had spent a lifetime swimming away from was gone, but in its place was something sharper, more defined: the knowledge that she had not ended something, but had merely changed its shape. She had broken his ritual. She had poked the eye of a sleeping beast. And she had no idea what it would do when it fully awoke.

She found Maya in the exact same position she had been in two days prior, curled on the sofa in her bright apartment, but this time the energy was not one of collapse, but of frantic, coiled tension. Her eyes were red-rimmed and wide, her phone clutched in her hand like a talisman that had failed to work. She shot to her feet the moment Lady walked in, her face a mask of terror and recrimination.
“Where have you been? I called you twenty times! I thought… I thought he’d found you. I thought you were lying in a ditch somewhere. What did you do, Lady? For God’s sake, what did you do?”

Lady didn’t answer immediately. She walked to the kitchen, her movements slow and deliberate, and poured two glasses of water. She handed one to Maya, whose hand was trembling too much to take it securely. Lady wrapped her friend’s fingers around the cool glass. “His name is Abhishek,” Lady said, her voice low and even. “His daughter was killed seven years ago. A hit-and-run. Her name was Diya. He’s not a monster, Maya. He’s a memory. A broken one.”

Maya stared at her, uncomprehending. The narrative she had built, the one with a clear villain to hate, was being rewritten in a language she didn’t understand. “What are you talking about? What does that have to do with anything? He attacked me. He attacked all of us.”

“I went inside,” Lady said, and the simple statement landed with the force of a physical impact. “To his house. To the room. He had a shrine to her. A picture, her old shoes. And on all the other walls… he had you. All of you. The photos.” She paused, letting the reality of it sink in. “I took them.”
Maya’s mouth opened, but no sound came out. She sank back onto the sofa, the glass of water tipping precariously in her hand. “You… you took the pictures? That’s it? You faced a giant and you… you did housekeeping?” The question was absurd, born of a night of terror that had imagined a dozen violent, cinematic conclusions, none of which involved an act of quiet, meticulous theft.

From a simple tote bag, Lady pulled out a thick, rectangular packet wrapped in plain cloth. She placed it on the coffee table between them. The stack of photographs. The physical manifestation of his crimes, the collected poison of his grief. “He isn’t just hurting women, Maya. He’s trying to see his daughter again. The last way he saw her. It’s twisted, it’s sick, but it’s not just malice. To hurt him back in the same way felt… wrong. It wouldn’t have made the noise stop. It would have just made it louder.”

Hesitantly, Maya reached out and unwrapped the cloth. She stared at the top photograph. It was of a woman’s feet in sensible flats, standing on the tarmac of a parking garage. Anisa. She flipped to the next, and the next. Each image was a small, stolen piece of a life, made grotesque only by the context in which they had been displayed. She finally got to her own. The coral-pink toenails, the crescent-shaped scar on her ankle. Seeing it here, in her own living room, stripped from the wall of his madness, was a profoundly disorienting experience. It was just a picture of her feet. He had weaponized it, but here, now, its power was diminished.

“What do we do with them?” Maya whispered, looking up at Lady. The fear in her eyes was finally being replaced by something else, something closer to resolve.
“We finish it,” Lady said. “The way we should have from the start. Together.”

That evening, they took the small bundle to a quiet stretch of hillside overlooking the city. They didn’t need a bucket or an accelerant. They used a simple metal biscuit tin from Maya’s kitchen. In the cool night air, with the glittering lights of Bandung spread out below them like a fallen constellation, they performed their own ritual. One by one, they took the photographs and lit them from the flame of a single candle. They watched as the images of their stolen intimacy curled, blackened, and turned to weightless, gray ash. They watched Anisa’s sensible flats and Dewi’s fashionable heels and Maya’s coral-pink toenails dissolve into nothing. It was not a violent act, but a quiet, deliberate erasure. They were not destroying evidence; they were releasing ghosts. When the last picture had turned to ash, Maya tipped the tin, and the night breeze caught the fragile remains, scattering them into the darkness over the city he had haunted. The noise wasn't gone, not completely, but for the first time in a long time, Maya could feel the edges of a profound and welcome silence. The race was over. Lady Cohen had won, not by being the fastest or the strongest, but by understanding the one rule of the deep that also applied to the world above: sometimes the only way to beat the monster is to teach it how to be quiet again.The day after the fire was unnervingly normal. The world did not acknowledge the tectonic shift that had occurred in its hidden corners. The sun rose over the volcanic ridges of Bandung with its usual indifferent glory, the symphony of motorbike engines began on schedule, and at the Cikapayang pool, the water was the same temperature it always was. Yet for Lady Cohen, everything was different. As she cleaved the surface, the familiar blue silence was no longer a refuge from the world, but a medium through which she could finally hear it. The quiet in her mind was no longer a forced absence of noise, but a true and profound stillness, the kind that follows a storm. The burn in her shoulders felt clean, the burn of exertion, not of escape. For the first time in years, she was not swimming away from her own thoughts; she was simply swimming.

After practice, the locker room felt less like a cage and more like a simple room. The bright, brittle chatter of the younger swimmers was just sound, no longer a grating intrusion on her frequency. Maya was waiting, not furiously texting, but methodically packing her bag, her movements calm and measured. The vibrant colours were still there—a turquoise hijab, a bright pink sports bottle—but they seemed less like a shield and more like a simple statement of self. They walked out into the cool morning, and Lady found she could breathe the city’s chaotic air without the familiar tension seizing her shoulders. The endless parade of faces flowing down the pavement was no longer a source of anxiety, but simply a river of which she was now a part, no longer watching from behind a pane of thick glass.

They returned to Pak Agus’s *warung*. It was a deliberate act, a quiet reclamation of a space that had been violated. They sat on the same low plastic stools, and the old man with the roadmap face wordlessly placed two steaming bowls of *bubur ayam* before them. He added the extra fried onions to Lady’s bowl without being asked, but as he turned away, his gaze lingered on them for a fraction of a second longer than usual, a silent acknowledgment that the two girls who had fled his stall in a panic days ago were not the same two who sat before him now. They ate in a comfortable silence, the savoury warmth of the porridge a balm, the sounds of the city a neutral backdrop to their shared, unspoken victory. It was Maya who finally broke the quiet.

“Rina called me last night,” she said, her voice soft but steady. She didn’t look up from her bowl, but a small smile touched her lips. “She apologized. For the new group chat, for being a coward. She said everyone was just scared and confused and they didn’t know what to do. She asked me if I would… rejoin the old group.” Maya took a spoonful of porridge, a simple act that felt momentous. “I told her I’d think about it.” She finally looked at Lady, and her eyes were clear, the haunted, ashen look completely gone, replaced by a deep, sober strength. “What he stole… it wasn’t the picture of my feet. It was them. My friends. You gave them back to me, Lady.”

A week passed. A fragile, tentative peace settled over their lives. The whispers about the bizarre phantom of Bandung, the sorrowful giant, began to fade from social media, replaced by new dramas and fleeting outrages. The city forgot, as cities always do. For Lady, the quiet was an unfamiliar luxury. She began to train with a purity of focus she hadn’t felt since she was a child, driven only by the pull of the water and the ticking of the clock. The hum of anxiety that had been the soundtrack to her life for as long as she could remember was gone, and in its absence, she felt lighter, faster. She allowed herself to believe it was over. She allowed herself to believe she had won.

It was this belief that left her unguarded. It happened during a late afternoon practice, the kind the elite swimmers used for endurance sets. The main lights in the vast hall had been turned down to save energy, and the setting sun slanted through the high, grimy windows, casting long, wavering golden pillars into the water. The pool was mostly empty, the air thick with steam and the echoing slap of water against tile. Lady was on her final lap of a punishing two-thousand-meter set, her body a finely tuned machine running on the fumes of her will. She was in a state of pure physical sensation, her mind blissfully empty, aware only of the ache in her muscles and the burning in her lungs. She executed a final, flawless turn and powered through the last twenty-five meters, her arms feeling like lead weights, and slammed her hand against the touchpad on the wall.

She hung there for a moment, gasping, her heart hammering against her ribs, the sound a frantic drum in her own ears. Water streamed down her face as she ripped off her swim cap and pushed her goggles up onto her forehead, blinking to clear her vision. She took a deep, shuddering breath and her gaze drifted upward, a habitual glance toward the public viewing gallery that ran along the second story. It was usually empty at this hour, a row of vacant plastic seats in the gloom. But it was not empty. A figure was standing there, in the center of the gallery, silhouetted against the blazing orange of the setting sun. His form was a solid block of darkness, immense and utterly still. He was not wearing a dark hoodie. He wore a simple short-sleeved shirt that did nothing to hide the impossible breadth of his shoulders. He wasn’t looking at the pool. He was not observing the swimmers. His entire presence, his stillness, his focus, was trained on one single point. On her. Abhishek had come to the cathedral.The water streaming from her face went cold against her skin, a sudden, chilling counterpoint to the heat in her blood. Time, for Lady Cohen, slowed to the syrupy viscosity of a dream. The echoing shouts of her coach giving instructions to a younger swimmer, the rhythmic splashing from the other lanes, the scent of chlorine—it all receded, fading into a distant, irrelevant hum. The world compressed into the hundred-meter distance between the water’s edge and the shadowed figure in the gallery. It was him. There was no doubt. The impossible scale of him, the sheer solidity of his presence, was as unique as a fingerprint. But this was not the same man she had followed from the *toko jamu*. The hesitant, heavy gait was gone. The shroud of his hoodie was gone. This figure was perfectly still, planted, his posture radiating a terrifying, patient certainty. He had not stumbled into her world; he had arrived.

For a full minute, neither of them moved. It was a standoff conducted in absolute silence, a conversation held across a vast, echoing space. He was delivering his response to her invasion. *You came into my house,* his stillness seemed to say. *You walked through my memories and you laid your hands on my grief. You showed me that I could be found. Now I am showing you the same.* He had breached the one place on earth she had ever considered holy, her sanctuary of measured lengths and blue silence. He had taken her place of solace and turned it into the finish line of a race she now realized she had lost. The quiet she had fought so hard to reclaim, the quiet he had momentarily given her by his own absence, was a lie. It was merely the intake of breath before the scream.

With a will that felt dredged up from the deepest part of her, Lady forced her limbs to move. She pushed off the wall and swam to the side ladder, her movements stiff, robotic. Every instinct screamed at her to stay in the water, in her element, but she knew that would be a concession, an admission of fear. She had to get out. She had to walk through his gaze. Her bare feet slapped against the wet, cold tiles, each step an agony of exposure. She felt his eyes on her, a physical pressure against her back, tracking her as she walked the long, open expanse toward the women’s locker room. She was acutely aware of her vulnerability—clad only in a thin layer of spandex, her skin dripping, her exhaustion a palpable aura. She did not run. Her swimmer’s discipline, the iron control over her own body, was the only armor she had left. She walked with her head held high, her gaze fixed on the locker room door, a swimmer finishing a race she knew was already lost.

Just before she pushed through the door, she allowed herself one last glance over her shoulder. The gallery was empty. The space where he had stood was now just a collection of vacant plastic seats, backlit by the dying sun. For a disorienting second, she wondered if she had imagined it, if the punishing set had conjured a hallucination from the depths of her exhaustion and guilt. But she knew it was real. His absence was more terrifying than his presence. He had not come to confront her. He had come to make a statement, to plant a flag. He had dissolved back into the city’s shadows, but this time, she knew he was not returning to his tomb. He was out, and he was watching. The hum was back, no longer a low-grade internal static, but a clear, external signal, a sonar ping that was locked directly onto her.

Inside the locker room, the familiar sounds of hissing showers and slamming metal doors did nothing to ground her. The space felt alien, its security a fragile illusion. She leaned against the cool tile wall, her body trembling not from cold or exhaustion, but from a deep, seismic shock. The quiet she had cherished for a week had been a gift, and he had just brutally taken it back. She fumbled for her phone in her bag, her fingers clumsy and slick. She typed a short, stark message to Maya, her thumb shaking so badly she had to correct it twice. *He knows. He was here.*

They met an hour later, not at a brightly lit food stall, but on a secluded stone bench in the Taman Lansia, the ‘Elderly Park,’ a quiet green space where the city’s chaos felt buffered by ancient, sprawling trees. Night had fallen completely, and the park was lit by elegant, old-fashioned lamps that cast pools of soft, insufficient light. Maya had arrived first, and she stood pacing, a nervous energy radiating from her. When she saw Lady approach, she didn’t speak, but simply stared, her eyes searching Lady’s face for an explanation that could possibly make sense of her cryptic text.

“He was at the pool,” Lady said, her voice flat, devoid of the emotion churning inside her. “In the gallery. He just stood there. Watching me.” She sank onto the bench, the cold stone seeping through her thin pants. “I don’t know how he found me.”

“How do you think he found you?” Maya’s voice was sharp, a new and unfamiliar edge of anger in it. “You’re Lady Cohen! You’re one of the most recognizable swimmers in West Java! Your picture is in the paper, your practice schedule is public knowledge for anyone in the league! You thought you were a ghost, but you have a name and a face and a life! Of course he found you!” The anger was a thin shield for the terror underneath. Maya sat down, but not close, leaving a gap between them. “We didn’t win, did we? We didn’t fix him. We just… gave him a new target.”

The truth of her words was a cold weight in Lady’s stomach. She had been arrogant. She had believed her world of disciplined silence was stronger than his world of chaotic grief. She had trespassed, thinking she was the arbiter, the one who could edit his story. But she had only succeeded in making herself a character in it. He was no longer a phantom haunting the city’s margins, reacting to a wound from the past. He was now an active, intelligent adversary, responding to a threat in the present. The race had changed. It was no longer a marathon of attrition against a predictable opponent. It was now a duel. And he had just made the first move on a board of his own choosing.The weight of Maya’s words settled over Lady in the quiet darkness of the park. *You have a name and a face and a life.* It was a simple, brutal truth she had managed to ignore her entire life. She had always treated her public persona as a swimmer like an ill-fitting coat she was forced to wear on occasion; she never considered that a predator could see it as a map leading directly to her door. Her strategy had been born of the pool, of the anonymous, submerged world where only effort and time mattered. She had treated Abhishek like a physics problem, a set of variables to be solved. She had never accounted for the fact that he could read the newspaper. He hadn’t just found her; he had researched her. The chilling deliberateness of it changed everything.

The walk back to her small apartment was a gauntlet of imagined gazes. Every shadowed doorway, every pair of headlights that swept across her, every figure lingering by a street-side stall was him. The anonymity of the city, once a source of low-grade anxiety, was now a source of acute terror. It was a perfect hiding place, not for her, but for him. She double-locked her door, something she rarely did, and leaned against it, the cool wood a poor substitute for the impenetrable blue of the water. The silence in her apartment was no longer peaceful. It was expectant. It was the silence of a held breath. He had not only breached her sanctuary, he had followed her home and laid siege to her solitude.

The next morning, practice was torture. The moment she stepped onto the pool deck, she felt exposed, the vast, open space of the hall a stage. Her eyes were drawn irresistibly to the public gallery, scanning the rows of empty plastic seats, her muscles tensing at every flicker of movement in the periphery. When she pushed off the wall, the water itself felt different. It was no longer a forgiving, eternal medium, but a transparent trap, offering no cover from the eyes that could be watching from above. Her rhythm was gone. Her strokes were ragged, her timing off. She mistimed a turn, her hand grazing the wall instead of striking it, the small miscalculation sending a jolt of failure through her.

Her coach, a wiry, no-nonsense man named Budi, blew his whistle, the sharp sound cutting through the water. “Cohen! What was that? Your head is somewhere else today. Are you on this deck or are you on the moon?”
She didn’t answer, just clung to the lane line, gasping. She was neither on the deck nor on the moon. She was in a second-floor gallery, seeing the world through a giant’s eyes, watching a girl in the water who had nowhere to hide.

The psychological assault continued, subtle and relentless. Two days later, a package arrived at her apartment. It was a small, square box, wrapped in plain brown paper, with no return address. Her name and address were written on the front in neat, blocky capital letters. Lady stared at it, her heart a cold, heavy stone in her chest. She took it to the kitchen and placed it on the counter as if it were an unexploded bomb. Maya, whom she had called in a quiet panic, arrived within minutes, her face pale.
“Don’t open it,” Maya pleaded, her voice trembling. “Just call them. Call the police, Lady. Please. This has gone too far. This isn’t a game.”

But Lady knew it was a game. That was exactly what it had become. A game of escalating intimacy, of psychological chess. He wasn't going to send her a threat he couldn't take back. That wasn't his style. His weapon was shame, violation, the twisting of the intimate into the grotesque. She had to know his move. With a steadiness that belied the tremor in her soul, she sliced the tape with a kitchen knife and opened the box. Inside, nestled on a bed of tissue paper, was a single, perfect, impossibly detailed scale model of a swimming pool starting block. It was an expensive, architectural-grade model, the kind used for planning new athletic facilities. And on the top, where a swimmer’s feet would be, two tiny footprints had been painted in a familiar shade of coral pink.

Maya let out a small, strangled gasp and stumbled backward, her hand flying to her mouth. It was a message of breathtaking cruelty and precision. It wasn't about Maya's feet anymore. The coral pink was a reference, a shared secret between him and Lady. *I remember what you took from me,* the model screamed in its perfect silence. *And I can get to the very heart of your world. I can stand where you stand. I can see what you see.* He had taken the symbol of her power, the starting block from which she launched herself into her element, and turned it into a token of his invasion.

“That’s it. I’m calling,” Maya said, her voice shaking with a fury born of sheer terror. She snatched her phone, her thumb already jabbing at the screen. Lady reached out and gently placed her hand over Maya’s, stopping her.
“And what will you tell them, Maya?” Lady’s voice was hollow, defeated. “That a man sent me a model of a starting block? The same police who wouldn’t know what to do with a bizarre ritual of humiliation won’t know what to do with a bizarre gift. They will put it in an evidence bag, file a report under ‘Harassment, Vague,’ and tell me to be careful. He knows that. He is operating in the space between their laws.”
“Then what, Lady? What do we do? Wait for him to send you a doll with your face on it? Wait for him to show up outside your door?”

Lady looked from Maya’s terrified face to the miniature starting block on her counter. He had responded to her move—the removal of the photos—with a move of his own. He had stolen the feeling of safety from her home and her sanctuary. He had demonstrated his reach. Now it was her turn again. The duel was real, and the only way to survive it was to play. Her fear, a cold, paralyzing ocean, began to recede, replaced by the icy tide of her resolve. She had gone into his world to understand his grief. Now she had to go back to understand his strategy.

“I’m going back to the house,” she said, the words falling into the tense silence of the room.
“No,” Maya said, the word a flat command. “Absolutely not. He’ll be waiting for you this time.”
“I’m not going in,” Lady clarified, her mind already working, the abacus clicking once more. “I’m not a target anymore. I’m his opponent. I need to know how he’s changed. The house… the house is his starting block. I need to see what he does now that the race has changed.” She was no longer a swimmer trying to quiet the noise. She was a strategist, analyzing her adversary, and her next move had to be to return to the place where it all began.The argument that followed was short, brutal, and left a scar. Maya, for the first time in their long friendship, looked at Lady not with concern or fear, but with a hot, unforgiving anger. It was the anger of the powerless, of someone watching a loved one walk calmly and deliberately into a fire.

“This is insane, Lady,” Maya hissed, her body coiled with a furious energy. “This isn’t a race. There is no strategy. He’s a psychopath who knows where you live, and you want to go back to his front door? For what? To see if he’s redecorated his murder shrine? He is baiting you, and you are swimming right into the net!”

“It’s not a net, it’s a chessboard,” Lady countered, her own voice dangerously quiet. She hated the hurt she was causing her friend, but to stop now felt like a betrayal of her own nature, like willingly drowning. “He made a move. I have to see the board to make mine. To go to the police now, to hide… that’s conceding the game. I can’t do that. I don’t know how.”

“Then learn!” Maya cried out, her voice cracking, tears of frustration and terror finally breaking free. “For once in your life, learn how to lose! Learn how to quit! It might be the only thing that keeps you alive.” She grabbed her bag and went to the door, pausing with her hand on the knob. She looked back at Lady, her face a ruin of love and despair. “I can’t watch you do this. I won’t. When he hurts you, Lady… I can’t be the one to find you.” The door clicked shut behind her, and the silence that rushed in to fill the void was the loneliest Lady had ever known.

This time, her preparation was different. There was no need for the black armour of a covert operative, no diver’s knife, no intent to breach. This was reconnaissance. She dressed in muted grays and dark blues, the colours of the urban twilight, clothes that would allow her to blend into the evening shadows. From the back of a dusty closet, she retrieved a heavy pair of binoculars in a worn leather case, a relic from her Anglophile father’s brief and unsuccessful bird-watching phase. Holding them, she felt a strange connection to him, another solitary watcher, though he had only ever hunted the flash of a sunbird’s wing. She was hunting a far more dangerous creature.

Returning to the hushed, tree-lined streets of Ciumbuleuit felt like rereading a terrifying book. The route was familiar, but every detail was now imbued with a sinister new meaning. The high, moss-stained walls were not just quaintly historic; they were fortifications. The deep shadows under the ancient flame trees were not just cool patches of night; they were ambushes in waiting. When she arrived on the lane where the villa stood, she didn’t park. She left her scooter two streets away and approached on foot, moving with a fluid silence that was the dry-land expression of her grace in the water.

The house loomed at the end of its weed-choked drive, a black hole in the fabric of the night. It looked exactly the same—a monument of decay, silent and seemingly inert. But Lady knew it was no longer a tomb. It was an active fortress. She needed a vantage point, somewhere she could watch without being seen. Across the street and slightly down the lane, a new villa was in the early stages of construction. It was a concrete skeleton, open to the elements, a chaotic mess of rebar, stacked bricks, and scaffolding. It was perfect. She slipped through a gap in the temporary fencing and climbed the rough concrete stairs to the second floor, the soles of her running shoes finding purchase on the dusty, unfinished surface. From behind a thick concrete pillar, she had a clear, elevated view of the giant’s house and a wide swath of its overgrown garden. She raised the binoculars to her eyes, adjusted the focus, and began her vigil.

For hours, nothing. The house remained dark. The silence was absolute, broken only by the chirping of crickets and the distant whine of a motorbike on the main road. Doubt began to creep in, cold and sharp. Had she been wrong? Was Maya right? Was he out there right now, circling her own apartment while she was here, watching an empty house? The waiting was a form of psychological warfare, a battle against her own imagination. She forced herself into the mindset of a long-distance swim, finding a rhythm in her breathing, letting her thoughts recede, focusing only on the target. Then, a light flicked on. It wasn’t the single, lonely yellow square in the upstairs window. It was a harsh, bare bulb on the ground floor, at the rear of the house, casting a wide, stark trapezoid of light into the jungle of the back garden.

A moment later, he stepped into the light. Lady’s breath caught in her throat, the binoculars trembling slightly in her grip. He was shirtless. In the harsh glare of the single bulb, his torso was a terrifying landscape of muscle and mass, the skin glistening with a film of sweat. The sheer physicality of him, unobscured by the concealing hoodie, was overwhelming. He was not just a large man; he was a creature of immense, condensed power. He moved to a section of the garden that had been cleared, a rough circle of bare earth amidst the chaos of weeds. And he began to train.

It was nothing like she had imagined. He wasn’t lifting heavy objects or punching at the air. His movements were a strange and disturbing synthesis of grace and brutality. He performed deep, explosive squats, launching his massive frame into the air with a speed that seemed impossible for his size. He moved through a series of complex, flowing exercises that looked like a violent form of yoga, his body folding and unfolding, stretching and coiling like a great python. It was the training of a predator, designed for explosive speed, balance, and silent movement. A sickening wave of recognition washed over Lady. He was doing what she did. In his own dark, twisted way, he was conditioning his body, honing his physical instrument, disciplining his muscles for a singular purpose. He was a swimmer in a different element, and his element was violence.

She watched, mesmerized and horrified, for nearly an hour. She saw the iron discipline in his repetition, the way he pushed his body past the point of exhaustion, the same way she did in the final, punishing laps of her own sets. A chilling symmetry locked them together, a dark mirroring she could not deny. He was not just playing mind games. He was not just sending her threatening little gifts. He was preparing his body for a physical resolution. The model starting block was not a metaphor. It was a promise of a physical contest, a challenge issued from his world to hers. He saw her not just as a mind to be broken, but as a body to be overcome. He was getting ready for the final race. Lady lowered the binoculars, her hands cold and numb. She slipped away from her concrete hiding place, her retreat a silent admission of a terrible new reality. She had poked the giant, and he was not just awake; he was training.The retreat from the construction site was a descent back into a world that had become thin and brittle. Lady moved through the sleeping streets, the binoculars a cold, heavy weight against her chest, a talisman of a horrifying new gospel. The symmetry she had witnessed was a profanity. She had always viewed her own discipline as a form of purification, a way to impose a clean, prayerful order on the chaos of her mind and the world. To see that same iron will, that same devotion to repetition and physical perfection, mirrored in his dark garden and bent toward a purpose of predatory violence felt like a fundamental corruption of her own soul. He wasn't just her opposite; he was her dark reflection, a version of herself born from a different, more terrible crucible. The thought was a toxin, spreading through her with an icy burn.

Back in her apartment, the small, perfect model of the starting block no longer looked like a threat. It looked like a training manual. She picked it up, her swimmer’s hands, so attuned to the feel of water, now assessing the cold, hard lines of the miniature platform. It was a declaration of intent, a piece of his world deliberately placed in hers, and she had failed to read its true meaning. It wasn't about shame; it was about physicality. It was about the contest. He had studied her, identified the source of her power, and was now building his own to match it. The graceful, brutal motions she had witnessed through the binoculars played over and over in her mind. He was honing his body not with the brute force of a brawler, but with the terrifying efficiency of a fellow athlete, an athlete of ambush and assault.

The next morning, the water knew. When Lady dived in, the clean, familiar entry was gone. It was a collision. Her body, usually so attuned to the liquid embrace of the pool, felt like a blunt instrument. She drove herself forward with a raw, punishing force, her technique dissolving into a sequence of controlled explosions. The silent syllables of her prayer were gone, replaced by a guttural litany of effort and rage. Her kicks were no longer a controlled flutter but a furious churning, her arms not pulling her through the water but tearing a path. She was no longer seeking grace or silence. She was forging a weapon. The pool was no longer a cathedral for worship; it had become a forge, and she was hammering her own body into something harder, something capable of withstanding impact.

“Cohen!” The sharp crack of Coach Budi’s voice over the water was a familiar sound, but the tone was not. It held a new, serrated edge of alarm. He paced the side of the pool, his stopwatch hanging forgotten from his neck, as Lady finished a length with a messy, explosive turn that threw water halfway across the deck. “What is this? Are we training for the national team or a back-alley brawl? Your form is garbage! You’re fighting the water, not working with it. You look like you’re trying to drown it.” He was right. She was. She was trying to drown the ghost of a man training in a dark garden, trying to match his brutal grace with a fury of her own. She ignored him, pushed off the wall, and threw herself into another length of raw, undisciplined power.

After practice, as she was throwing her gear into her bag with jerky, angry movements, Budi cornered her by the lockers. His usual gruffness was gone, replaced by a deep, weary concern that unsettled her more than his anger. “I’ve coached you for ten years, Lady,” he said, his voice low. “I know your rhythm better than I know my own heartbeat. Something is broken. I see you in the water, and you are training for an injury. You are tearing at your own shoulders, fighting your own body. Whatever is happening on dry land,” he gestured vaguely toward the world beyond the pool’s walls, “you solve it there. You don’t bring it in here to die.” The word hung in the humid air between them, stark and terrible. She flinched, pulling away, offering no explanation. She couldn’t tell him that for the first time in her life, the thing she was training for had nothing to do with a finish line.

The chasm between her and Maya was a constant, physical ache. Lady ate her post-practice *bubur ayam* alone, the empty plastic stool beside her a testament to a loss she couldn’t fix. The world of easy laughter and shared secrets felt a million miles away, a sunlit country to which her passport had been revoked. Her phone felt impossibly heavy in her pocket. She knew she should call, should apologize, should heed her friend’s panicked, loving advice and go to the police. But what would be the point? It would be an admission of defeat. It would be quitting the race. And her entire life, her entire being, had been sculpted by one unwavering principle: you never, ever quit the race. You swim through the pain. You finish what you started.

Three days of this brutal new regimen passed. Three days of tearing at the water, of fighting her own body, of living in a state of high, predatory alert. Her muscles screamed in protest, a deep, satisfying ache that was the only thing that felt real. Then, he made his next move. She came back from a late run, the sun having already set, her body slick with sweat. As she entered the locker room, now empty and echoing in the evening gloom, she felt it immediately. A change in the air. A wrongness. Her locker, number 42, was closed and locked, just as she had left it. She spun the combination, her fingers stiff with a sudden, icy dread. She pulled the door open. Inside, her neatly folded towel, her change of clothes, her shampoo bottle—all were in their place. But on top of her towel sat a small, perfect, glistening pile of something dark. It was a cluster of wilted jasmine flowers, their petals brown and decaying at the edges, exactly like the ones that grew in a tangled riot near the back of his decaying villa. Their sweet, funereal scent, the scent of his garden, mingled with the sterile smell of chlorine. The message was as clear and as brutal as a punch to the gut. *There is no safe place. Your sanctuary is my garden now. I can get to your most private space. I can touch your things. I can leave my decay in the heart of your clean, ordered world.* The fear was a white-hot flash, but it was instantly consumed by a rage so pure and absolute it cleared her mind of everything else. The race was no longer about a finish line. It was about survival. He had brought his dirt and his sorrow and his rot into her cathedral, and he had left it on her altar.
