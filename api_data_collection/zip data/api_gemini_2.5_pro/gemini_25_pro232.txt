The rain fell on Loughborough as if it meant to scour the bricks and mortar from the bone of the town. A fine, persistent Midlands drizzle that slicked the slate roofs and turned the night air into a damp shroud. From his perch atop the Carillon Tower, Prince Alistair felt the wet seep into the seams of his collar. He ignored it. The cold was a familiar companion, a whetstone for the senses. Below, the market square was a smear of sodium orange and blurred headlights, a mundane world utterly oblivious to the silent predator in its eyrie.

His target was a man named Gregor, a mid-level functionary in the Belgian embassy with a weakness for bad debt and worse women. Neither of those things interested Alistair. What interested him was the small, encrypted data-slate Gregor was carrying, a slate that had been passed to him by a ghost from the East in a pub lavatory not twenty minutes prior. Information was the true currency of the modern age, and Alistair was a master purveyor. His particular set of skills, as his guardian Sir Kaelan dryly called them, had little to do with cutting ribbons or christening ships. They were skills of shadow and whisper, of leverage and lock-picks, of knowing precisely where to apply pressure to make a man, or a government, fracture. A prince for the new, dark age.

Gregor scurried from the amber warmth of The Swan in the Rushes, his trench coat collar pulled high. A pathetic figure. Alistair tracked his movement, a fluid shift of weight on the balls of his feet. He had mapped the route ten times in his head. Gregor would cut through the Queen's Park, a shortcut to his rented flat on Ashby Road. Foolish. Parks at night were for lovers or muggers. Tonight, Gregor would meet something else entirely.

Alistair descended, not with the clatter of a soldier, but with the silence of a falling leaf. He used the ancient, pitted stonework as a ladder, his gloved fingers finding holds invisible to the untrained eye. He dropped the last fifteen feet into a narrow alleyway, his impact a soft thump absorbed by bent knees. The stench of stale beer and refuse filled his nostrils. He was a creature of two worlds: the rarefied air of his title, and the filth of the gutters where real power flowed. He moved out of the alley, a shadow detaching itself from deeper shadows, and melted into the treeline at the park’s edge.

"Gregor," Alistair’s voice was low, calm, devoid of threat. That was the key. Fear made people unpredictable.
