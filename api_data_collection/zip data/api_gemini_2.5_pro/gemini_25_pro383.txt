What if Anya Sharma was a ghost, a fiction created by Vance to lure them into the open?

Just as the first tendrils of despair began to take hold, a flicker of movement caught her eye near the base of the giant crane. Prove you aren't dross.” It was a challenge, not a greeting, devoid of any pleasantry.

Liyana motioned for her family to stay hidden, her heart pounding a steady, defiant rhythm. She stepped out from behind the pylon and walked into the open, meeting the woman halfway. Up close, Anya Sharma was younger than Liyana had expected, her face sharp and intelligent, framed by short, dark hair. A web of faint, silvery scars traced a pattern from her left temple to her cheekbone, the ghost of an old, violent injury. Her gaze wasn't on Liyana’s face, but on her hands, checking for a weapon, for a trap. “So did you,” Liyana replied, her voice steady. She wouldn’t be intimidated. She had faced down armed men in her own living room an hour ago. A paranoid journalist was nothing.

Simultaneously, from the landward entrance to the pier, the roar of multiple heavy engines echoed through the container yard. Headlights flared to life, cutting off their only escape route. Now!” But it was too late. Figures were already deploying from the trucks, fanning out, their dark forms silhouetted against the blinding headlights. They were boxed in, exposed, and utterly outnumbered.The world dissolved into a maelstrom of harsh, tactical light and overwhelming noise. Anya Sharma’s survival instincts were flawless; she yanked Liyana toward the deep shadows of the container yard, her body already low to the ground, her mind calculating angles and escape routes. But Liyana was not a lone operative. She was an anchor, tied to the four most precious lives in her universe, who were huddled behind a flimsy wall of rotting wood. She would not run. She could not run. Her family was here.

From behind the pallets, her father’s voice was a low, desperate command. “Zayne, grab your mother! Get to the back of the containers! Go!” He was trying to create a deeper layer of cover, to put more distance between his family and the unfolding violence. They were a liability, a weakness, and Liyana loved them with a ferocity that burned away her own fear. Anya swore, pulling the collar of her jacket over her mouth and nose. “Tear gas! We have to move!” But Liyana knew it wouldn’t be that simple. Stepping forward, away from her family, away from Anya, she drew their fire, their attention. She planted her feet on the cracked concrete, a solitary figure against the oncoming tide. It was a statement to be made.

Anya Sharma, recovering from her own shock, understood the tactical situation in an instant. Anya had reached the crane’s control cab, a glass-walled box suspended a hundred feet in the air, and with the desperate genius of a cornered animal, she had found the crane’s powerful external PA system and had done the one thing she could think of to sow chaos: she had started playing a track from Julian’s own album, a pirated copy from her tablet now blasting across the pier.
