The chlorine-sharp air was the first thing Leo Vance knew each morning, a chemical tang that tasted of purpose. It clung to the damp tiles of the Windsor & Eton Leisure Centre, a ghost in the machine of the humming filtration systems. He sliced through the surface, the entry almost silent, a practised tear in the fabric of the water. This was his sanctuary and his stage. Underwater, the world became a muted, beautiful blur. The thud of his own heart was the bass drum, the rush of water past his ears a constant, soothing hiss. He wasn't just a swimmer; he was an aquatic storyteller. His limbs carved shapes, telling tales of struggle and flight, of being lost and found. He'd spent a decade perfecting this, turning the brutal mechanics of competitive swimming into something that bordered on art. It was a lonely pursuit. Artistic swimming for men wasn't exactly a mainstream sport. It was usually met with a confused tilt of the head, or worse, a smirk.
