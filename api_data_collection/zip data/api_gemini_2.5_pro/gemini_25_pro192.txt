The water was a slap of liquid blue, a cool shock against skin that had been baking under the Highveld sun all morning. A Pretoria sky, wide and unforgivingly bright, beat down on the tiled surrounds, but here, in the chlorinated embrace of the Hillcrest municipal pool, Riaan de Villiers was a world away. He was not in Pretoria. He was in London. The roar of the suburban traffic on Duxbury Road was the roar of a crowd in the Hammersmith Apollo. The sharp scent of chlorine was the electric tang of stage fog.

This was not swimming. Not really. This was hydro-choreography. His own invention. A ballet of limbs and lungs, a story told in ripples and spray. He kicked off the wall, a torpedo of pale skin and taut muscle, not aiming for speed but for grace. His mind was a symphony, the soaring crescendo of the Britain's Got Talent theme music playing on a loop that had been spinning for three weeks straight. The final. The words tasted like honey and electricity in his mouth, a secret mantra he’d been chanting inwardly since the call.

He could still feel the phantom buzz of the phone in his hand, the cheap plastic suddenly feeling like a holy relic. He’d been at his desk, staring at a spreadsheet of municipal water consumption data, the numbers blurring into a meaningless grey smear. Then, a withheld number. He almost ignored it. He always ignored them. But something made him answer. A crisp, British voice. Impossibly British. The kind you only heard on the BBC.

“Is this Mr Riaan de Villiers?”

“Ja. Speaking.” His own voice sounded rough, thick with the dust of the office.

“This is Charlotte, from Syco Entertainment. I’m calling on behalf of Mr Cowell regarding your audition for Britain’s Got Talent.”

Riaan’s heart had done a strange, painful lurch. His audition. The video he’d sent in months ago, a desperate, hopeful act filmed on his mother’s phone by a grudging neighbour. He’d almost forgotten. You send those things out into the ether and expect nothing. A message in a bottle tossed into a digital ocean.

“I… yes?”

“We’re delighted to inform you,” the voice had chirped, a sound like tiny silver bells, “that you have not only progressed but have been selected as a wildcard act directly for the grand final.”

The world had dissolved. The grey spreadsheet, the humming air-conditioner, the picture of his mother’s cat blu-tacked to the partition wall. All of it had just… melted away, replaced by a blinding white light of pure, unadulterated joy. A wildcard. A direct pass to the final. Simon had seen it. He’d seen the genius in the shaky, poorly-lit video of a man splashing artistically in a public pool thousands of miles away. He’d recognised a pioneer.

Now, in the water, Riaan became the pioneer. He breached the surface, arms scything, creating a pattern of concentric circles that he imagined a top-down camera was capturing in breathtaking high definition. This was his opening. ‘The Awakening of the Vaal’. It started slow, a representation of the great river’s sluggish beginnings, then built into a frenzy of movement, a thrashing, chaotic ballet that symbolised the industry and life it supported. To anyone watching from the side, it might have looked like a man having a mild, rhythmic seizure. To Riaan, it was art. It was his ticket.

He finished the routine with a final, dramatic submersion, holding his breath until his lungs burned, staying under for a full minute before rising slowly, theatrically, water streaming from his face and hair. He broke the surface to silence. The only sound was the splat of a hadeda ibis on the nearby rugby field and the distant drone of a lawnmower. No applause. Not yet. The applause was coming.

“Riaan!”

He blinked, the harsh sunlight making him squint. His mother, Elna, stood at the edge of the pool, a faded floral towel in one hand and a steaming mug in the other. She had a permanent look of gentle, weary concern etched around her eyes, a roadmap of a mother’s love and worry.

“You’ll catch a chill,” she said, her voice soft. “Come have your rooibos.”

He heaved himself out of the water, his body feeling heavy and clumsy on land. He was a different creature here. An amphibian forced onto dry, unforgiving concrete. He took the towel, the rough fabric a familiar comfort. “Ma, did you see that? The ending? I think I’ve got the timing perfect. A full sixty-five seconds under. It’s for the suspense, you see. Ant and Dec will be losing their minds.”

Elna smiled, a faint, watery twitch of her lips. “It was very… powerful, my boy. Very energetic. Now drink your tea before it’s cold.”

He took the mug, the warmth seeping into his cold fingers. He loved his mother, but she didn’t understand. How could she? She saw her son, Riaan, the clerk from the water board who still lived in his childhood bedroom. She didn’t see the artist, the visionary who was about to take Britain by storm. He’d tried to explain it to her, showing her YouTube clips of past winners, of Susan Boyle’s astonishing reveal, of Diversity’s stunning choreography. “It’s a platform, Ma,” he’d said, his voice fervent. “It changes lives.” She had just patted his hand and said, “Of course, liefie. Just be careful.”

Back in his room, the world of the competition closed in around him again, a much more comfortable reality than the quiet, sun-drenched predictability of his mother’s house. The walls were a collage of his ambition. A faded poster of Ryk Neethling from the 2004 Olympics was half-covered by a glossy printout of the BGT logo. A map of the London Underground was tacked next to his window, a route from Heathrow to Hammersmith marked in red koki pen.

His suitcase lay open on the bed. It was almost entirely packed. Three pairs of Speedos, each a different, vibrant colour for different moods and routines. His competition pair, a sleek, jet-black garment he had ordered online from the UK, lay on top, shrouded in tissue paper like a holy shroud. Goggles. Nose clips. A special, quick-dry towel. He had even packed a small South African flag to wave during the victory lap. He picked up the black Speedo, the material smooth and cool. He could almost feel the weight of a nation’s hope in its delicate Lycra weave. He was not just doing this for himself. He was doing this for Pretoria. For South Africa. A country known for rugby and braais was about to be known for hydro-choreography.

He imagined the judges’ faces. Amanda, her eyes welling with tears. Alesha, nodding, a look of stunned respect. And Simon. The great man himself. He would be leaning back, a small, slow smile playing on his lips, the smile of a man who had struck gold. “Riaan,” he’d say, his voice resonating through the auditorium. “In all my years of doing this, I have never seen anything like that. It was brave, it was original, it was… magic.”

A shiver of pure ecstasy ran down Riaan’s spine. He hugged the Speedo to his chest. This was real. It had to be. The alternative was unthinkable. The alternative was a lifetime of water consumption spreadsheets and the quiet, crushing weight of his mother’s worried eyes. No. This was his escape. His destiny. The flight was in two days. He had the email with the ticket confirmation printed out and folded in his wallet. He checked it ten times a day. British Airways, flight BA56, departing O. R. Tambo International. The details were seared into his brain. Soon the Pretoria sun would be a memory, replaced by the glare of a million London lights. And he would be ready. He would be magnificent.The two days leading up to his departure passed in a blur of surreal anticipation. Riaan moved through his life like a ghost, his body performing the familiar tasks of his job while his mind was already across the hemisphere, bathed in the glow of television studio lights. He filed his last water consumption report, the numbers looking more alien than ever, like symbols from a forgotten language. He smiled politely at his colleagues’ well-wishes, their talk of "good luck" and "have a jol" sounding like faint echoes from a world he had already outgrown. They thought he was going on holiday. He let them. How could he explain that this was not a vacation, but an ascension?

The drive to the airport was a quiet, tense affair. Elna gripped the steering wheel of her beige Toyota Tazz so tightly her knuckles were white. She kept glancing at him, her brow furrowed in that familiar pattern of concern. The highveld landscape scrolled past the window – acacia trees, dusty verges, the distant, geometric shapes of Johannesburg’s mine dumps glinting in the afternoon sun. To Riaan, it was a farewell tour of a life he was shedding like a skin.

“You’ve got the flight details I printed for you?” Elna asked for the third time, her voice tight.

“Yes, Ma. In my wallet. Right next to the printout from the hotel. The one Syco booked for me.” He said the word ‘Syco’ with a reverence usually reserved for the church. It was the name of his saviours.

“And you have enough pounds? I know it’s not much, what I could get for you, but…”

“It’s fine, Ma. They’re covering my expenses. Per diems, you know. It’s professional.” He patted her arm, a gesture he intended to be reassuring but which felt condescending even to him. He was already a different person, someone who knew about per diems and professional entertainment contracts. His mother, with her worries about bus fare and the price of a sandwich at Heathrow, was a relic of his past.

“Just… be careful of the Tube, Riaan. I’ve seen on the news, it gets so crowded. And don’t talk to strangers. Not everyone is as friendly as they are here.”

He simply nodded, his mind elsewhere. He was picturing his arrival. He would be met by a driver holding a sign: MR DE VILLIERS. He would be whisked away to a hotel where a welcome package from the BGT team would be waiting for him. There would be a schedule, rehearsal times, a press junket. He would need to be charming and humble, but with an underlying steeliness. The Simon Cowell of hydro-choreography.

At the international departures drop-off, the reality of the moment finally landed with a thud. The air was thick with the smell of jet fuel and the nervous energy of a hundred goodbyes. Elna pulled the car to a stop and put it in park, but made no move to get out. For a long moment, she just stared ahead.

“Well,” she finally said, her voice thick. “This is it.” She turned to him, and her eyes, the same pale blue as the Hillcrest pool, were swimming with unshed tears. “I packed a bag of biltong in your carry-on. In case you get hungry.”

A lump formed in Riaan’s throat. Biltong. The taste of home, of Saturday afternoons watching rugby, of a life so small and simple he was desperate to escape it. He leaned over and hugged her, feeling the frailness of her shoulders, smelling the familiar scent of her rose-scented hand cream and rooibos tea. It was the smell of his entire childhood.

“I’ll be fine, Ma. I’ll make you proud,” he whispered into her hair.

“Oh, liefie,” she squeezed him tight, a lifetime of love and fear in the embrace. “You already do.”

She let go, fumbling in her handbag for a tissue. “Go now, before you miss your flight. Phone me when you land. No matter what time it is. Promise?”

“I promise.” He got out of the car and pulled his suitcase from the boot. He gave her one last wave, a bright, manufactured smile plastered on his face. He watched the little beige Tazz pull away, a small, sad vessel disappearing into the chaotic stream of airport traffic. For a fleeting second, he felt a terrifying pang of loneliness, a dizzying sense of being untethered. He was utterly, terrifyingly on his own.

He shook it off, turning to face the vast glass and steel facade of the terminal. This was no time for sentiment. He squared his shoulders, adjusted his grip on his suitcase, and walked through the automatic doors. The cool, conditioned air hit him, a different world. He was no longer Elna de Villiers’ son. He was Riaan de Villiers, Britain’s next great hope. The roar of the crowd in the Hammersmith Apollo was waiting for him, and he had a plane to catch.Inside the terminal, the scale of the operation was immense. A cathedral of polished floors and soaring ceilings, filled with the constant, rolling thunder of suitcases and the polyglot murmur of a thousand intersecting journeys. For a moment, Riaan felt small, an insignificant plankton in this vast ocean of humanity. He clutched the handle of his suitcase, the cheap plastic a grounding, familiar texture. Then he straightened up, reminding himself that he was not just another traveller. He was a chosen one. He was on a mission.

He found the British Airways check-in desks, a long, snaking queue already formed before them. He waited, his leg jiggling with a nervous energy that he tried to disguise as impatience. When it was finally his turn, he approached the counter with what he hoped was an air of quiet importance. The woman behind the desk was brisk and professional, her name tag reading ‘Patience’. She did not live up to the name.

“Passport and booking reference,” she said, her eyes fixed on her computer screen. Riaan slid his documents across the counter. He half-expected her to look up, for a flicker of recognition to cross her features. Perhaps his name was on a special list. ‘V.I.P. – Handle with care. Future celebrity’.

Patience simply typed, her nails clicking rhythmically on the keyboard. “One bag for the hold?” she asked, not looking up.

“Yes,” Riaan said. He lifted his heavy case onto the scale. It contained his entire future. He wanted to tell her to be careful with it, that it held the sacred vestments of a new art form, but he bit his tongue. The number on the scale flashed green. “Window or aisle?” Patience asked.

“Window, please,” Riaan replied, imagining himself staring down at the clouds, soaring towards his new life. She printed the boarding pass and the luggage tag with a series of loud whirs and rips, slapped the sticker onto his suitcase handle, and sent it trundling away down the conveyor belt. “Gate A14. Boarding commences at 20:45. Enjoy your flight.” It was a dismissal. He was processed. He took his passport and boarding pass, a faint sense of anti-climax pricking at him. He reasoned that they had to be discreet. Of course. It was all part of the game.

Passport control was even more anonymous. He handed his documents to a man in a glass booth who looked as though he hadn’t smiled since the last time the Springboks had lost a World Cup. The man grunted, scanned the passport, and then brought a heavy rubber stamp down with a percussive, definitive *thunk*. It was the sound of a door closing on his old life. He was officially an export. He walked through into the international departures lounge feeling lighter, the bureaucratic hurdles cleared, the finality of it all settling in his bones like a deep, satisfying warmth.

He found a seat overlooking the airfield, where planes sat like colossal, sleeping birds under the deepening indigo sky. The last vestiges of the sunset were a smear of fiery orange on the horizon. He bought a bottle of water, the price of which made his eyes widen, and sat down to wait. He took out his phone, the battery already worryingly low, and opened the email from Syco Entertainment for what must have been the hundredth time. He read the crisp, perfect sentences, the words a balm to his soul. *Mr Cowell… delighted to inform you… wildcard act… grand final*. The digital text felt more real to him than the hard plastic chair he was sitting on, more tangible than the distant lights of Kempton Park blinking outside the window. This was his reality now.

A boarding announcement crackled over the PA system, first in clipped English, then in Sesotho and Zulu. The call was for his flight. A jolt, pure and potent, shot through him. This was it. He gathered his things, his heart thumping a frantic rhythm against his ribs. He joined the queue, a diverse collection of people heading for London – a family with young children buzzing with excitement, a group of businessmen in suits already looking weary, a young couple holding hands, their faces glowing with the promise of adventure. Riaan felt a sense of kinship with them, yet also apart. Their journeys were finite, holidays or business trips. His was a transformation.

He handed his boarding pass to the attendant, who tore it with a practised flick of the wrist. He walked down the narrow, articulated tunnel of the jet bridge, the muffled sounds and strange, filtered air a passage between worlds. He stepped onto the aircraft, the flight attendants greeting him with practiced smiles. He found his seat, 23A, a window seat as requested. The cabin was warm and smelled faintly of disinfectant and coffee. He squeezed past his seatmate, a large man who was already deeply engrossed in a sudoku puzzle, and sank into his chair. It was real. He was on the plane.

He buckled his seatbelt, the click of the mechanism sounding like the locking of a vault. A vault that contained his destiny. He pressed his face to the cool triple-glazed window, watching the ground crew scurrying below like ants. The cabin doors closed with a solid, pressurised hiss, sealing them in. The safety demonstration began, but Riaan didn’t watch. His mind was on the Hammersmith Apollo stage. The engines spooled up with a rising whine, a sound that vibrated through the very core of his being. The plane began to move, rolling away from the terminal, a slow and lumbering beast. Then, after a pause that felt an eternity long, the engines roared to life. He was pushed back into his seat as the aircraft accelerated, the runway lights a blur of white and blue. Then came the moment of grace, the impossible lightness as tons of metal lifted into the air. The rumble of the wheels vanished, replaced by the smooth, powerful hum of flight. Below him, the lights of Johannesburg spread out, a glittering carpet of human endeavour, and then began to shrink, to fade, to become nothing more than a memory. He was airborne. He was free.The first few hours of the flight were a state of suspended bliss. Riaan felt as though he had shed gravity itself, not just the pull of the earth but the weight of his entire previous existence. Below him, the continent of Africa was a vast, dark shape stippled with occasional clusters of light, each one a city or a town full of lives he would never know, full of spreadsheets and municipal concerns he had now transcended. The constant, deep hum of the Rolls-Royce engines was a sacred mantra, a drone that vibrated through his seat and into his bones, rearranging his very cellular structure into that of an international artist. The old Riaan, the one who worried about his mother and the price of petrol, was down there, shrinking with every passing minute.

The cabin lights dimmed, and the small screens on the seatbacks flickered to life, offering a hundred different distractions. Riaan scrolled through the blockbuster movies and television series with disinterest. They were pale fictions compared to the technicolour epic unfolding in his own mind. He closed his eyes, ignoring the screen, and let the Hammersmith Apollo materialise behind his eyelids. He saw the stage, not as it was, but as it would be for him: a gigantic, glass-walled tank, purpose-built for his debut. He choreographed a new routine in his head, this one titled ‘Drought and Deluge,’ a piece that would explore the stark duality of the South African climate, full of tense, rigid movements giving way to a sudden, explosive release of fluid motion. He practised the finger-tutting section under his complimentary blanket, his hands twitching with contained energy.

The spell was broken by the clatter of the meal trolley and a flight attendant asking him, “Chicken or beef?” The question felt absurdly mundane. He was wrestling with the sublime, and here was a choice between two foil-covered rectangles of protein. He chose the chicken, which turned out to be a pallid breast in a watery sauce, accompanied by overcooked peas and a block of rice. He ate mechanically, the plastic cutlery feeling flimsy and foolish in his hands. It was fuel, nothing more. His seatmate, the large man with the sudoku puzzle, finished his beef with gusto and wiped his mouth with a napkin. “Frik,” he said, extending a meaty hand. “Heading over for a conference. Logistics. Mind-numbingly dull, but the company’s paying.”

Riaan shook the hand, feeling his own slender fingers swallowed by Frik’s firm grip. “Riaan,” he replied. This was it, the first time he would introduce his new self to a stranger, an impartial observer. “Holiday or work?” Frik asked, his gaze friendly but direct. Riaan took a breath, puffing out his chest slightly. “It’s work, in a way. I’m a performer. I’m in the final of Britain’s Got Talent.” The words hung in the air between them, feeling both monumental and terrifyingly fragile. Frik’s eyebrows shot up. “No kidding? What do you do? A singer? My wife loves the singers.”

“Not a singer, no,” Riaan said, trying to modulate his tone to sound humble yet brilliant. “I’m a hydro-choreographer. It’s a new art form. It’s a… a narrative ballet, performed aquatically.” He watched Frik’s face for the dawn of understanding, the flash of impressed awe. Instead, Frik’s brow furrowed. “Hydro-what now? Is that like… synchronised swimming?” The comparison was like a splash of cold water. “No, not at all,” Riaan said quickly, a defensive edge to his voice. “Synchronised swimming is about patterns, about unison. This is about story. About emotion. It’s solo. It’s raw.” Frik nodded slowly, though his eyes remained blank. “Right. Like a man, swimming… with feeling. Well, good luck with that, hey. Takes all sorts.” He then turned back to his sudoku puzzle, pencil poised, and Riaan was dismissed. The conversation was over.

For a moment, a hot flush of indignation and doubt crept up Riaan’s neck. *Swimming with feeling.* The man had reduced his life’s work, his soul’s expression, to a simpleton’s phrase. He felt the urge to grab Frik by the shoulders, to explain the nuances of ‘The Awakening of the Vaal’, to describe the intricate ripple-patterns that represented the socio-economic turmoil of the Gauteng province. But he didn't. Frik was the audience he was leaving behind. He was the past. Simon Cowell was the future. Simon would understand. People like Frik were the reason visionaries like him needed a global platform. He was not here for them. He was here for the millions who were ready for something new.

He tried to sleep, but his mind was a fizzing, churning cauldron of hope and anxiety. He reclined his seat as far as it would go, which was not very far at all, and shut his eyes. But instead of the cheering crowds of London, he saw his mother’s face as she drove away, her small frame lost in the driver’s seat of the old Tazz. He saw his desk at the water board, the grey smear of numbers waiting for him. The alternative. The life he would have to return to if this all turned out to be… what? A mistake? A dream? He pushed the thought away violently. He fumbled in his carry-on bag, past the biltong his mother had packed, and pulled out his wallet. From it, he extracted the folded, creased printout of the email from ‘Charlotte at Syco Entertainment’. He angled the tiny overhead light and read it again, the tenth time since boarding. Hours later, he woke with a stiff neck to the gentle brightening of the cabin. A new day was dawning, a European day. He scrambled to lift his window shade. Outside, the world was a blanket of thick, bruised-looking cloud. It was a city built on a different scale, in a different colour palette. The plane banked, its wing seeming to slice through the tops of the clouds, and he saw the snaking, brown ribbon of the Thames. He felt a shiver, not of cold, but of arrival. They worked in silence for a few minutes, the only sounds the rustle of leaves and the distant drone of a lawnmower. The unspoken weight of London, of the money, of his spectacular failure, had been a constant presence between them, a ghost in every shared room. Riaan knew he had to be the one to exorcise it. “Ma,” he began, his voice quiet. “I’m going to pay you back. All of it. I’ll give you a bit from my salary each month.” Elna stopped weeding and looked at her hands, caked with rich, dark soil. “That’s not necessary, Riaan.” “It is,” he insisted, looking at her directly for the first time in what felt like weeks. “It’s necessary for me.” He saw her understand. He saw the shift in her eyes from worry to a quiet, affirming pride. “Alright, liefie,” she said softly. Then, he said the words he had been practicing in the silence of his room. “And thank you, Ma. For… for everything. For not… for just being here when I got back.” A single tear escaped the corner of her eye and traced a clean path through the dust on her cheek. She reached out and patted his hand, her touch light and firm. “Always,” she said.

That afternoon, he finally dealt with the last remaining evidence. He went to his room and gathered the neat pile of papers from his desk: the printout of the BGT logo, the map of the Underground with its mocking red line, the creased and folded copy of the scam email from ‘Charlotte’. He looked at them not with the hot shame of his return, but with a kind of weary detachment. They were artifacts from a fever dream, relics of a man he barely recognised anymore. He found an old shoebox at the back of his wardrobe, the brand faded and forgotten. He placed the papers inside, along with the small, folded South African flag. He hesitated for a moment, then went to his suitcase, which he had unpacked but left open in a corner. From the bottom, he took the neatly folded swimsuits—the sunshine yellow, the electric green, the fiery red, and the sleek, jet-black competition suit. He placed them in the shoebox on top of the papers. He put the lid on the box, slid it to the very back of the wardrobe, and closed the door. It was not an act of erasure, but of acceptance. The story was a part of him, but it would no longer be the whole of him.

A few weeks later, a new kind of curiosity began to stir. He was staring at a hydrological map of the region at work, tracing the intricate blue lines that represented the river systems feeding the province. His eyes settled on the name: Vaal. He remembered the arrogant title of his imagined routine, ‘The Awakening of the Vaal’. He had presumed to tell the story of a river he had never even seen, a river he knew only as a line on a map and a name on a spreadsheet. A quiet, genuine question formed in his mind: what did it actually look like? That Saturday, instead of driving to the pool, he took a different route. He drove south out of the city, past the mine dumps and the sprawling townships, onto the open highway. He followed the signs for Vereeniging, the name itself synonymous with industry and water. He didn't have a specific destination. He just drove towards the river. He eventually found a dusty turn-off that led down to a quiet, public picnic spot on the riverbank. He got out of his car and walked to the water's edge. It was not a turquoise stage. It was a vast, slow-moving body of brown water, wide and powerful, its surface shimmering under the unfiltered Highveld sun. Reeds grew thick along the banks, and in the distance, the geometric shapes of a power station broke the horizon. It was not beautiful in a classical sense. It was raw, functional, and immense. He stood there for a long time, just looking, the breeze cool off the water. He was not an artist seeking inspiration. He was just a man from the water board, finally paying his respects to the source.The visit to the Vaal had shifted something deep within Riaan. When he returned to the office on Monday, the rows of numbers on his spreadsheet no longer looked like an abstract code for boredom; they looked like a map. He could see the great, slow-moving river behind the data, the vast pumping stations, the sprawling reservoirs, the intricate web of pipes branching out like a circulatory system under the skin of the city. He had spent years trying to impose a story onto the water, a dramatic, self-serving narrative of his own creation. Now, he began to realise that the water had been telling its own story all along, a vast, complex epic of consumption and scarcity, of engineering and need, and his job was not to be its artist, but one of its humble translators.

This new perspective transformed the mundane task of data verification into something akin to detective work. He began to notice the small currents and eddies in the flow of information that he had previously ignored. One afternoon, while cross-referencing consumption reports from the northern sectors, he found a small, stubborn anomaly. In a specific quadrant of Soshanguve, a historically lower-consumption area, the weekly data showed a sustained, significant spike that had started about two months prior. It was the kind of statistical blip that was usually dismissed as a series of faulty meter readings or a data-entry error. Old Riaan would have flagged it with a query, passed it on, and forgotten about it. But the new, quieter Riaan was intrigued. He felt a familiar pull, the same instinct that used to guide him towards a new choreographic sequence, but now it was grounded in fact, not fantasy.

He didn't mention it to anyone. After work, instead of driving straight home, he began to stay an hour or two later, the quiet hum of the deserted office a cocoon for his concentration. He pulled up archived maintenance logs for the area, cross-referencing the dates with the start of the consumption spike. He found three separate low-pressure complaints from residents in that quadrant, all of which had been closed with the note ‘No fault found at property line’. On his own initiative, he pulled up geological surveys and old municipal plans of the area from the digital archive. He printed out maps, spreading them across his small desk, and began to highlight the main arterial water lines in yellow koki pen. A theory began to form, a ghost story told in data. He wasn't looking at a collection of faulty meters. He was looking at a massive, unreported leak, a primary pipe haemorrhaging millions of litres of treated water directly into the porous dolomite rock beneath the suburb.

The following Monday, he approached the office of his supervisor, Mr. Kriel, a man whose weary cynicism was a legendary force within the department. Kriel was a classic bureaucrat, a man who saw initiative not as a virtue, but as the potential for more paperwork. Riaan knocked softly on the open doorframe. He was not the fervent visionary pleading for a chance; he was a junior clerk with a query. In his hand, he held not a prop for a routine, but a thin file containing his printed charts and highlighted maps. “Mr. Kriel? Do you have a moment?” Kriel looked up from his newspaper, his expression a well-practiced mask of tired impatience. “What is it, de Villiers? The coffee machine broken again?”

“No, sir,” Riaan said, his voice calm and even. “It’s about the consumption data for Soshanguve, Zone F. I think I’ve found a significant discrepancy.” He stepped forward and laid his primary chart on the desk. It showed two lines: the historical average water use for the area, and the current usage, which had diverged sharply two months ago and remained stubbornly high. Kriel glanced at it with disinterest. “It’s meter drift, de Villiers. Or they’ve got a batch of faulty readings. Send a query to billing, they’ll sort it out.” “I thought so too, sir,” Riaan said, not allowing himself to be dismissed. He laid a second page next to the first, a map of the zone with three red dots on it. “But the spike correlates with these three separate low-pressure complaints, all logged within the same week. And the total volume of the discrepancy, if my calculations are right, is in the region of three megalitres a week. That’s too much for simple meter error.”

Mr. Kriel sighed, a long, theatrical exhalation of a man burdened by the foolishness of the world. He picked up the charts, his skepticism palpable. He clearly expected to find a simple flaw in Riaan’s logic, an error that would allow him to dismiss the matter and return to his newspaper. But Riaan had been thorough. He had triple-checked his calculations. He had anticipated the objections. For once in his life, he was not improvising a fantasy; he was presenting a carefully constructed case built on a foundation of verifiable fact. He didn’t try to oversell it. He simply pointed to his final map, where a yellow line representing a major water main intersected the cluster of low-pressure complaints. “The pipe here is over forty years old, sir. And it runs through a dolomite-heavy substrate. If there’s a major fracture, the water wouldn't surface. It would just disappear.”

There was a long silence. Kriel stared at the papers, his brow furrowed. He looked from the consumption chart to the map, then back again. Riaan watched him, his heart beating a steady, calm rhythm. He was not waiting for applause or a golden buzzer. He was waiting for a logical assessment. Finally, Kriel grunted, a sound deep in his chest. He pushed the papers back across the desk towards Riaan, though he did not meet his eye. "It's a long shot, de Villiers," he said, his voice gruff. "A hell of a long shot. But the paperwork is tidy." He leaned back in his chair, the springs groaning in protest. "Lodge a formal infrastructure query. Cite the data. I want a copy on my desk. I'll forward it to Engineering with a priority flag." He picked up his newspaper again, a clear signal that the audience was over. "Don't waste my time."

Riaan gathered his papers, his hands steady. “Thank you, sir,” he said. He walked back to his cubicle, the short distance feeling strangely momentous. There was no surge of ecstasy, no blinding white light of joy. Instead, there was a small, solid kernel of satisfaction warming his chest. It was a feeling as quiet and profound as the slow, powerful movement of the river he had driven so far to see. He had found a story hidden in the numbers, a real story about a real problem. He had given it a voice. And someone had listened. He sat down at his desk, pulled up a blank query form, and began to type.The formal query, once submitted, was absorbed into the vast, silent machinery of the municipality. For the first few days, Riaan felt a low-grade hum of anticipation, finding himself checking his email with a frequency that reminded him, uncomfortably, of his London obsession. But no response came. The priority flag Mr. Kriel had promised seemed to mean little in the grand, unhurried timeline of civic administration. The initial spark of purpose began to fade, replaced by the familiar, steady rhythm of his daily work. The anomaly in Soshanguve Zone F became just another submitted file number, a task completed. He returned his focus to other sectors, other spreadsheets, and the quiet satisfaction he had felt in Kriel’s office settled into a dormant, half-forgotten memory. Life resumed its new, placid shape: the methodical work, the cleansing anonymity of his swims, the slow, steady repayment of the debt to his mother, each electronic transfer a small, concrete act of atonement.

Almost three weeks after he had submitted the report, a perfunctory email appeared in his inbox. The subject line was the query number, a sterile string of letters and digits. The body of the message was a single, bureaucratic sentence: “Your query is under preliminary review by the Engineering Department.” It was a non-update, a form letter designed to confirm that his report had not been lost entirely, but had merely reached a new level of institutional purgatory. Yet, seeing the words ‘Engineering Department’ in black and white sent a small, surprising jolt through him. It was real. Someone, somewhere, was at least looking at it. He felt a flicker of the old hope, but he tamped it down immediately, cautioning himself against the dangerous pull of expectation. He had learned, the hard way, the vast distance between a preliminary review and a tangible outcome. He filed the email and forced himself to forget it.

The following afternoon, his desk phone rang, a rare occurrence in an office that communicated mostly through email and shouted conversations over cubicle walls. He picked it up, expecting a query from another department. “Riaan de Villiers, please.” The voice was young, energetic, and professional. “Speaking,” Riaan replied. “Hi, my name is Sibusiso Ndlovu, I’m a junior hydro-engineer with the city. I’m calling about your infrastructure query for Soshanguve. The one Mr. Kriel flagged.” Riaan’s stomach gave a small, nervous lurch. An engineer. A real one. “Yes?” he said, his voice tighter than he intended. “Listen, man, I just wanted to say, this is a very tidy piece of work,” Sibusiso said, his tone one of genuine, peer-to-peer respect. “Most of the stuff we get from Data is a mess. But you’ve correlated consumption spikes, consumer complaints, and geological substrates. You’ve even calculated the potential volume loss. Nobody does that.”

Praise, Riaan discovered, felt entirely different when it was earned through methodical labour rather than imagined genius. It wasn’t a dizzying high, but a warm, solid feeling of competence. “I just noticed the numbers didn’t make sense,” Riaan said, downplaying his effort. “Well, they make a lot of sense when you lay them out like this,” Sibusiso continued. “Look, officially, this is still on a long list. Dispatching an acoustic survey team is expensive, and my boss needs a mountain of justification to sign off on a ‘potential’ leak, especially one that isn't showing any surface water. But your report… this is the best justification I’ve seen all year. I’m going to push it up the chain. I can’t promise anything, but I wanted you to know someone is taking it seriously.” The call ended a few moments later, but Riaan sat holding the receiver for a long time. *Someone is taking it seriously.* The words resonated deeper than any imagined applause from a faceless crowd. This was a different kind of recognition, a quiet validation from a world of tangible things, of pipes and pressures and the solid, unyielding logic of physics.

Another week passed, and the silence returned. Riaan almost convinced himself that Sibusiso’s enthusiasm had been ground down by the slow, immense friction of bureaucracy. He was at his desk on a Thursday morning, trying to reconcile a conflicting report from the Garsfontein reservoir, when Mr. Kriel appeared at his cubicle. He didn’t speak, but simply stood there, arms crossed, until Riaan looked up. “De Villiers,” Kriel said, his voice its usual gruff monotone. “Engineering is sending an acoustic survey team out to Soshanguve tomorrow morning. Seven a.m. They want the clerk who wrote the report on-site to brief the foreman.” Riaan stared at him, his mind blank for a second. “Me, sir?” Kriel gave a small, almost imperceptible nod. “It’s your anomaly. You drive. Get the exact GPS coordinates from your file. Don't be late.” He turned and walked back to his office without another word, leaving Riaan sitting in stunned silence. He was being sent into the field. Kriel had called it *his* anomaly. The sense of ownership, of responsibility, was terrifying and exhilarating in equal measure.

The next morning, Riaan drove through the pre-dawn darkness, his small Tazz feeling utterly insignificant on the wide, empty highway heading north. He felt a nervous flutter in his stomach that was nauseatingly similar to the feeling he’d had on the plane to London. He clutched the steering wheel, his knuckles white. What if he was wrong? What if this whole thing was just a statistical ghost, a phantom he had conjured from the data? The humiliation would be immense, a public re-enactment of his London folly, this time played out in front of gruff engineers and his own cynical boss. He followed the GPS on his phone to a dusty, unremarkable stretch of road on the outskirts of Soshanguve. A large municipal truck was already parked on the gravel verge, its yellow lights flashing in the pale dawn light. A team of four men in blue overalls were gathered around it, drinking coffee from steaming flasks. They looked up as Riaan’s car pulled up, their expressions impassive. He suddenly felt ridiculous in his office chinos and polished shoes, a pen-pusher straying into the world of real work.

A burly, grey-haired foreman detached himself from the group and ambled over. “You the data guy?” he asked, his voice a low rumble. “Riaan de Villiers,” Riaan said, offering a hand that the foreman ignored. “Ja, okay. So where’s this river you think you’ve found?” The foreman’s tone was laced with a weary skepticism that made Riaan’s blood run cold. He was being humoured. This was a box-ticking exercise. He took a breath, unfolded his printed map, and pointed to a spot about fifty metres from the road, a patch of dry, winter-brown grass distinguished by nothing at all. The foreman glanced at it, grunted, and waved his team over. The work began with a slow, methodical precision that was agonizing to watch. They laid out sensitive microphones on the ground, connecting them with a web of cables back to a console in the truck. The foreman, whose name Riaan learned was Piet, put on a large pair of industrial headphones and began to walk a slow, deliberate grid pattern, his eyes closed in concentration, listening for a sound that only he could hear.

Riaan could do nothing but stand by the side of the road, feeling useless and exposed. The sun climbed higher, beating down on the veld, and the traffic on the nearby road picked up. An hour passed. Then another. Piet said nothing, his face an unreadable mask. The other crew members sat in the shade of the truck, smoking and talking in low voices. With every silent, passing minute, Riaan’s certainty withered. The numbers on his spreadsheet seemed like a distant, foolish fantasy. He was just a clerk who had wasted a crew’s morning and a department’s money chasing a ghost. He was already composing his apology to Mr. Kriel in his head. Just as he was about to give up all hope, Piet stopped dead in his tracks. He stood perfectly still for a full minute, tilting his head slightly. He then held up a hand for silence, though no one was speaking. He knelt down, pressing one side of the headphones firmly against his ear, his eyes shut tight. He stayed like that for what felt like an eternity. Finally, he rose slowly, took off the headphones, and wiped a slick of sweat from his brow. He looked over at his crew, then his eyes found Riaan. He walked over, his expression no longer skeptical, but one of grim, professional awe. He pointed with his thumb to the patch of unremarkable ground where he had been kneeling. “It’s there,” he said, his voice low and gravelly. “Deep. Sounds like the whole bloody Vaal is running under our feet.”The words hung in the hot, still air, as solid and real as the ground beneath his feet. Riaan didn't feel a surge of elation, no blinding white light of victory. Instead, the feeling was one of profound, quiet gravity, as if a great weight had been gently lowered onto his shoulders, a weight not of burden, but of substance. The frantic, high-pitched frequency of hope and shame that had been the soundtrack to his life for months simply ceased, replaced by a deep, resonant hum of reality. He had been right. The numbers had been right. The story they told was true. He looked from Piet's grim, sweat-streaked face to the unremarkable patch of brown grass, now transformed into a place of immense significance, a hidden wound in the earth that he alone had been able to diagnose from nine kilometers away, through a screen. It was a magic trick far more profound than anything he had ever tried to conjure in a swimming pool.

Piet did not wait for a response. The moment of revelation immediately gave way to the blunt poetry of action. He turned to his crew, his voice a gravelly bark that cut through the morning stillness. “Right! Get the markers out. We’ll need a fifty-metre exclusion zone. Jannie, get on the horn to central dispatch, tell them we have a confirmed sub-surface primary main fracture. Tell them it’s a big one. They need to start rerouting pressure from the Soshanguve East reservoir now, or we’ll lose the whole quadrant when we cut the pipe. Stefan, get the requisition forms for the TLB and a ten-tonne tipper. Tell them we need it yesterday.” The crew, their earlier bored indifference replaced by a focused, professional urgency, moved without a word. Spray cans appeared, and soon a large, fluorescent orange circle was drawn on the ground, a target marking the hidden disaster. Riaan stood on the edge of the activity, a civilian observer at the scene of a crisis he had uncovered, watching the practical, physical consequences of his desk work unfold in real time.

A clean white municipal bakkie crunched to a stop on the gravel verge, and a young man in crisp jeans and a work shirt jumped out. It was Sibusiso Ndlovu, the engineer from the phone, and his face was alight with a brilliant, infectious grin. He strode straight towards Riaan, his hand outstretched. “I knew it! I knew it!” he said, his handshake firm and energetic. “I saw the crew dispatched this morning and I had to come see for myself. My boss is going to have a fit when he sees the requisition costs, but man, you’ve just saved his career.” He looked over at the flurry of activity with the excited eyes of a scientist witnessing a successful experiment. “They’ll have to excavate down at least five metres. It’s a messy, expensive job. But you have no idea the cascade of problems this prevents. We’re talking about more than just lost water. A leak this size, in this substrate? It creates voids. You get subsidence, road collapse, foundation damage. You haven’t just found a leak, Riaan. You’ve stopped this whole area from quietly sinking.”

The drive back to the office was a strange, dreamlike journey. Riaan’s hands were steady on the steering wheel, his mind a quiet landscape. He replayed the morning’s events: the tense, silent waiting; Piet’s gruff pronouncement; Sibusiso’s beaming, triumphant face. He felt tired, a deep, cellular exhaustion that was a world away from the anxious, jittery fatigue of his London trip. This was the weariness of meaningful effort, of a problem confronted and confirmed. His shoes were coated in a fine red dust, and he could smell the dry, earthy scent of the veld on his clothes. When he walked back into the air-conditioned hush of the municipal building, he felt like a visitor from another, more tangible world. He didn't bother to wipe his feet. He walked straight to Mr. Kriel's office, the dust a silent testament to his morning's work.

Kriel was at his desk, scowling at a memo. He looked up as Riaan appeared in the doorway, his eyes taking in the dusty shoes and the weary set of Riaan’s shoulders. He raised a skeptical eyebrow. “Well?” he grunted. Riaan stood straight, his hands by his sides. He did not present charts or offer explanations. He simply delivered the fact. “The leak was confirmed, sir. It’s a primary main fracture. The engineering crew is on-site.” Kriel’s face remained a stony mask, but Riaan saw a flicker, a brief, unguarded expression of genuine surprise in his eyes before the curtain of cynicism fell again. He stared at Riaan for a long, silent moment, as if re-evaluating the very substance of the man in front of him. Finally, he gave a slow, deliberate nod. “Good work, de Villiers,” he said, his voice low and devoid of any warmth, yet carrying an undeniable weight of professional respect. He then looked down at the memo on his desk. “Now get back to your desk. You have a report from the Garsfontein reservoir that’s overdue.” The audience was over. Riaan turned and walked back to his cubicle, the two simple words, ‘Good work,’ echoing in his mind, not as a thunderous ovation, but as a quiet, solid, and deeply satisfying foundation being laid, stone by careful stone.The news of the Soshanguve leak did not sweep through the department like a fire, but seeped into it like the very water Riaan had tracked, moving through the quiet, unseen channels of office gossip. There was no formal announcement, no memo celebrating his discovery. Instead, there was a subtle shift in the atmosphere around his cubicle. Conversations would dip slightly as he walked past, only to resume with a new, speculative energy. Derek from Accounts attempted a joke about him being a ‘water diviner’, but it lacked his usual booming confidence and trailed off awkwardly. People looked at him differently. He was no longer Riaan the quiet clerk who had returned strangely subdued from a holiday; he was now Riaan the man who had found the monster leak, a semi-mythical figure in the mundane lore of the department. He deflected the tentative questions with the same bland, non-committal answers he had perfected since his return, but the shield of his anonymity had been breached, not by his own foolish ambition, but by the quiet, undeniable weight of a single, well-researched fact.

Mr. Kriel’s brand of recognition was even more subtle, and far more significant. The day after the on-site confirmation, a thick, formidable-looking file landed on Riaan’s desk with a dull thud. It contained a decade’s worth of pressure and flow-rate data from the entire Moot water district, an area notorious for its aging infrastructure and baffling inconsistencies. There was no note attached, no word of explanation from Kriel. There didn't need to be. The file was a message in itself. It was not a reward; it was a promotion in all but name, a battlefield commission handed out on the quiet front lines of data analysis. Kriel was giving him the department's most stubborn problems, the tangled knots of data that everyone else had given up on. It was a gruelling, thankless task, and it was the highest compliment the cynical supervisor knew how to pay. Riaan opened the file, and as he looked at the first dense page of charts and figures, he felt not dread, but a calm, energizing sense of purpose. He was being trusted with the difficult things.

A few days later, an email from Sibusiso Ndlovu arrived. There was no text, just a single, powerful image attached. It was a photograph taken from the edge of a vast, muddy excavation pit. The hole was enormous, a raw, ochre wound in the earth easily ten metres across. At the bottom, looking like tiny, brightly coloured toys, workers stood beside the exposed pipe. Riaan could clearly see the fracture, a jagged, catastrophic tear in the concrete casing, from which a steady stream of water still gushed, feeding a small, muddy lake at the bottom of the pit. The sheer scale of it was breathtaking. The numbers on his spreadsheet had been abstract symbols of loss; this was the brutal, physical reality. He stared at the picture for a long time, the roar of the machinery and the shouts of the men almost audible. He saved the image to his desktop, replacing a scenic default wallpaper of a Swiss mountain he had never seen. This muddy hole in Soshanguve was his mountain. It was real.

The following week, Sibusiso invited him to the site during a lunch break. “The new pipe section is going in today,” he’d said on the phone. “You should see it.” When Riaan arrived, the scene was one of controlled chaos. The roar of a massive crane dominated the soundscape as it carefully lowered a colossal section of new, deep blue piping into the pit. Riaan stood with Sibusiso on the edge, the dust kicked up by the activity coating his shoes. He watched, mesmerised, as the workers below, guided by Piet’s shouted instructions, expertly manoeuvred the new section into place. It was a ballet of heavy machinery and human skill, a choreography of immense power and precision. Sibusiso pointed down, explaining the mechanics of the custom-made flex-seal joints and the new cathodic protection system that would prevent corrosion. Riaan listened, absorbing the new vocabulary, but his focus was on the raw, elemental nature of the work. This was the opposite of his fantasy in the pool. It was not about him. It was a collective, tangible act of mending, of making something broken whole again. He was a small, quiet part of that act, and the feeling of connection to it was more profound than any standing ovation he had ever imagined.

That Saturday, as he and his mother were sitting on the stoep drinking their afternoon tea, he finally found the words. He did not build up to it. He simply took out his phone and showed her the picture Sibusiso had sent him of the vast excavation. “That’s the leak I was telling you about, Ma,” he said quietly. “The one I found in the data.” Elna took the phone, her brow furrowed as she tried to comprehend the scale of the image. He explained, simply and without embellishment, what the picture showed: the broken pipe, the lost water, the work they were now doing to fix it. He told her about Sibusiso, and Mr. Kriel, and the report he had written. He spoke not with the feverish, self-important energy he had used to describe his BGT dreams, but with the calm, measured tone of a man describing his job. When he finished, Elna looked up from the phone, not at the image, but at him. Her eyes were shining, and the smile that spread across her face was one he had not seen in years. It was not the gentle, worried smile of a mother trying to be supportive. It was a beam of pure, radiant, uncomplicated pride. “Oh, Riaan,” she said, her voice thick with emotion. “That’s not just good work. That’s a good thing. A very good thing you’ve done.” In that moment, surrounded by the familiar quiet of his mother’s garden, Riaan finally received the validation he had chased across the globe, and it was warmer and more real than he could ever have imagined.

The next time he went to the Hillcrest pool, something had fundamentally changed. As he slid into the cool, clear water, he felt a new and profound connection to the medium. This water was not a prop or a stage. It was a destination. He thought of its long journey: from the vast expanse of the Vaal Dam, through the roaring pumps of the purification plants, travelling hundreds of kilometres through the great, hidden arteries of the city, one of which he now knew intimately, all the way to this quiet suburban pool. It had a story, an immense and vital story, and he was no longer an outsider trying to impose his own narrative upon it. He was a part of its journey, a guardian of its flow. He pushed off the wall, his stroke smooth and strong, his body cutting cleanly through the water. He swam his laps with a quiet, meditative focus, the black line on the bottom of the pool a steady, guiding presence. He was not a hydro-choreographer. He was not a failed artist. He was Riaan de Villiers, from the water board. And as he moved through the silent, buoyant world, he felt an overwhelming, bone-deep sense of peace. He was exactly where he was supposed to be.The Moot file was a beast. It was a slab of departmental history bound in faded blue, filled with a decade of raw, un-sanitised data that was as dense and forbidding as a tropical jungle. To the old Riaan, it would have been a punishment, a soul-crushing sentence to a lifetime of incomprehensible figures. To the new Riaan, it was a landscape. He spent his days exploring its terrain, not with dread, but with the patient focus of a cartographer. He learned the rhythm of its seasons: the predictable surge in consumption during the dry winter months as gardens were watered, the dip during the summer rains. He identified its landmarks: the steady, thrumming pulse of a large hospital, the erratic gulps of a car wash, the gentle, constant draw of a quiet residential street. This was a new kind of choreography, a ballet of data points where a sudden dip could signify a burst pipe and a slow, creeping rise could tell a story of urban densification. He was no longer trying to force a narrative onto the water; he was learning to listen to the one it was already telling.

He had lunch with Sibusiso once a week, a new ritual that grounded him in his new reality. They would go to a small Portuguese place near the office that served prego rolls on floury buns, the air thick with the smell of garlic and grilled meat. Their conversation was a language of pressures and flows, of pipe diameters and soil substrates. Sibusiso would describe the practical challenges of his work – the difficulty of getting a permit for a road closure, the frustration of dealing with a contractor who cut corners. For the first time, Riaan had a friend who understood the intricate, invisible world he now inhabited. Sibusiso, in turn, treated Riaan’s work with a respect that was still startling. “You’re like a doctor,” he said one afternoon, wiping garlic butter from his chin. “I’m just the surgeon who goes in and cuts. You’re the one reading the scans, finding the tumour before it kills the patient. What you did in Soshanguve… the pressure is stable there for the first time in years. People are noticing.” The words landed without fanfare, a simple statement of fact that meant more to Riaan than any standing ovation ever could.

It was during a deep dive into the Moot file that he found the next whisper. It wasn't a roar, like the Soshanguve leak, but a quiet, inconsistent murmur in a small industrial park near Wonderboom. It was not an error. It was a secret.

Mr. Kriel was silent for a long time, tapping a thick finger on the chart showing the nocturnal spikes. He looked up at Riaan, his expression unreadable. “This isn’t an engineering problem, de Villiers. This is a compliance and revenue issue. It’s messy.” Riaan braced himself for a dismissal, for the instruction to hand the file over to another department where it would languish for months. Put together a joint site inspection proposal. I want it on my desk by Friday.” He paused, then added, his voice a low grumble, “And de Villiers… from now on, you don’t need to knock.”

That evening, Riaan went to the pool. The sun was low, casting long, deep shadows across the water. He swam his laps, his body moving through the familiar, comforting resistance, but his mind was not blank. It was clear and focused, turning over the details of the Wonderboom file, planning the structure of his meeting with Susan and Mark. The water was not an escape, but a thinking space, a fluid, quiet office where the complex problems of his day could settle and arrange themselves into clear, logical patterns. After his swim, he didn’t rush out. He just floated on his back in the deep end, his arms outstretched, his ears just below the surface, the sounds of the world muffled to a distant, gentle thrum. He looked up at the vast, darkening expanse of the Pretoria sky, a canvas of deep violet and fading orange. He felt small beneath its immensity, a tiny, buoyant point of consciousness. But he was not insignificant. He was anchored, connected by a thousand invisible threads to the city, to his work, to the very water that was holding him. There was no applause, no roar of a crowd. There was only the gentle lapping of the water against the sides of the pool and the steady, quiet beat of his own heart, a rhythm that was finally, perfectly, in sync with the world.Leading a meeting was a foreign concept, a function Riaan had always associated with other people, with the Mr. Kriels of the world who occupied offices with doors. The meeting was held in one of the building’s small, windowless boardrooms, a beige box that smelled faintly of stale coffee and whiteboard cleaner. She gave Riaan a brisk, evaluative nod and immediately began scrolling through emails on her phone. Their tariff is based on low-level office and sanitation use. If they’re using this much water, they’re mis-categorised at best, and fraudulent at worst.” He then pointed to his final projection. “Based on the tariff for a medium industrial user, and not including potential effluent charges, the city has lost an estimated two hundred thousand rand in revenue from these three units in the last year alone.”

The room went quiet. The number, stark and concrete, hung in the air. Susan had stopped tapping. She picked up the projection sheet, her eyes scanning the calculations, searching for a misplaced decimal point, a flawed assumption. Mark, for his part, had sat up a little straighter, his weary posture replaced by a glimmer of interest. The problem had moved from a trivial anomaly to a significant compliance issue with a clear financial stake. Riaan stayed silent, letting them process. He was not selling a vision; he was presenting an audit. “I agree,” Riaan said, turning to his final page. “That’s why I’m proposing a two-stage approach. Riaan walked out of the beige box of a boardroom feeling not triumphant, but profoundly, satisfyingly solid. He had been given a problem, and he was solving it. It was as simple, and as deeply gratifying, as that.The week following the meeting passed in a state of suspended animation. The act itself was anticlimactic—a few clicks on a keyboard, a generated tracking number—but Riaan felt its significance resonate through the quiet hum of the office. He had nudged a sleeping giant, and now he had to wait for it to stir. The waiting was different this time. It was not the desperate, frantic hope he had felt waiting for a call from London, nor the anxious dread he had experienced waiting for the flight home. He adjusted his routine. Each morning, before even opening his email, he would log into the raw data feed for the Wonderboom sector. He pulled up the consumption graph for the three targeted units, the familiar, rhythmic spikes of the past few months a baseline against which he would measure the future. On the first morning after the letters were delivered, he felt a familiar tremor of anticipation as the graph loaded. The timeline scrolled to the previous night, between the hours of two and four a.m. The line was flat. A perfect, unwavering, horizontal line indicating nothing more than the passive, infinitesimal draw of the meter itself. Riaan stared at it, a slow smile spreading across his face. It was not the smile of triumph, but of confirmation. The animal had heard the rustle in the bushes and had frozen.

The pattern, or rather, the lack of it, held for the rest of the week. Every morning, Riaan would check the feed, and every morning he would be greeted by the same beautiful, damning flatline. The secret factory had shut down its nocturnal operations. The silence in the data was louder than any alarm bell. He meticulously documented the change, creating a new chart that showed the consumption pattern before the query letter and after. The contrast was stark and irrefutable. After five days of this digital silence, he scheduled a follow-up conference call with Susan and Mark. He knew this would be the critical test. “So, what you’re telling us,” Mark from Licensing said, his voice tinny and weary over the speakerphone, “is that the problem has stopped. It’s thin, but it’s defensible.” “I agree,” Susan chimed in, her voice decisive. “The sudden drop-off is evidence of irregularity in itself. Let’s schedule the inspection for next week. Mark, you’ll take the lead on-site. De Villiers, you’ll come along as a technical advisor from Data. We’re going to see what they’re hiding.”

The week leading up to the inspection was a tense, quiet period of preparation.
