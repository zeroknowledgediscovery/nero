The air tasted of rust and recycled breath. Always. Leo Vance inhaled it, held it, let it out in a controlled plume. He ignored the faint, ever-present scent of sweat and damp concrete that clung to everything in the Warren, the sprawling underground city that was now humanity’s cramped, grimy womb. He focused on the rhythm. Inhale for four, hold for seven, exhale for eight. A box of breath in a world made of boxes.

“I can’t,” Anya gasped, her knuckles white where she gripped the worn resistance bands. Her face, usually pale in the wan glow of the lumens, was flushed a blotchy red. “Leo, I just… can’t.”

“You can,” he said, his voice even, a calibrated tool. “The fatigue is a liar. Your muscles have the memory. Your mind is the weak link. Push through the lie.”

His ‘gym’ was a repurposed maintenance alcove in Sector Gamma-7, a pocket of slightly-less-cramped space carved out between a hydroponics overflow conduit and the primary ventilation shaft for the lower habs. The equipment was scavenged, welded, and jury-rigged. Rusted plates for weights, frayed bands for resistance, a floor padded with discarded nutrient-paste transport mats. It wasn't much, but it was his. Here, he wasn’t just another body in the flow. He was a sculptor of sinew, a priest of physical betterment in a world where the body was the last thing anyone truly owned.

Anya groaned, a sound of genuine despair, and her arms trembled, the bands going slack. “What’s the point? Strong for what? To haul another crate of fungal protein? To pass the quarterly fitness mandate so I don’t get reassigned to the reclamation crews?” She slumped onto the mats, her thin shoulders shaking. “I remember the sun, Leo. Sometimes, at night, when the cyclers are quiet, I can almost feel it on my skin. And then I wake up, and there’s just… this.” She gestured vaguely at the curved, sweating walls, the low ceiling, the inescapable grayness of their existence.

Leo knelt, his own powerfully built frame moving with a quiet efficiency that belied its bulk. He didn’t offer platitudes. They were useless currency in the Warren. “The point,” he said, his voice softening just a fraction, “is that tomorrow the air cyclers might fail. The point is that the mag-lift to Delta Sector could seize up and you’ll have to take the six hundred emergency stairs. The point is that your body is the one machine you have to maintain yourself. Everything else is provisional.”

He reached into the pocket of his worn canvas trousers and his fingers closed around it. A nervous habit. The pen. It was cool to the touch, made of a dark, smooth metal that seemed to drink the light. It was heavier than it looked, perfectly balanced. An inheritance from a grandfather he’d barely known, a relic from the Before Times, from the Scorch. A 'writer's pen', his mother had called it, given to him in a dusty box just before they’d sealed the main blast doors of the Warren for what they all thought would be the last time. It was useless now. A memento of a world that no longer valued such things. But he carried it. Always. The cap had a small, intricate sigil etched into its crown, a spiral that coiled into itself. He often traced it with his thumb, a mindless, repetitive motion that centered him.

Anya wiped her eyes with the back of her hand. “You make it sound so simple.”

“It’s the only simple thing left,” he replied, standing up. “Get a water ration. We’ll work on core stability next.”

As she shuffled toward the public dispensary spigot, the low, omnipresent hum of the Warren’s life support changed pitch. It wasn’t the familiar groan of a system under strain. It was a sharp, rising whine, an electronic shriek that clawed at the ears. Then, the klaxons began. Not the monotonous blare of a ration-time announcement or a shift change. This was the other one. The one they hadn't heard in Gamma-7 for three years. The rapid, gut-churning pulse of a Breach Alert.

The air didn't just taste of rust anymore. It tasted of pure, metallic fear.

Anya froze, the metal cup trembling in her hand. Her eyes, wide and stark, found Leo’s. People in the narrow causeway outside the alcove started to scream. The sound was thin at first, then it grew into a chorus of raw panic. The heavy-booted tread of the Wardens, the Warren’s security force, echoed down the corridor, their shouts lost in the cacophony.

“Leo?” Anya’s voice was a tiny, fragile thing.

“Get behind me,” he said, his own voice tight. He pushed her back into the far corner of the alcove, behind a stack of rusted weight plates. His heart was a frantic hammer against his ribs. He had the pen in his hand, his grip so tight his knuckles ached. A useless, stupid comfort object. He cursed himself for the false security it offered.

The sounds from the corridor were changing. The human screams were being punctuated by something else. A high-pitched, chittering shriek that sounded like a recording of a primate house played at triple speed and fed through a distortion pedal. It was a sound that scraped at the primitive hindbrain, a symphony of wrongness. He’d heard recordings of them during Warden briefings. The Changed. The Scourge. The things the monkeys had become.

A blur of motion flickered at the edge of the alcove. A Warden, his gray uniform dark with sweat and something else, stumbled past, one arm missing at the shoulder, his face a mask of disbelief. He collapsed twenty feet down the corridor. And then it was there, framed in the entrance to his gym.

It was worse than the recordings. So much worse. It had once been a capuchin, maybe. The thought was absurd. That small, clever creature was now a nightmare of anatomy. Its limbs were elongated, bent at impossible angles, allowing it to scuttle along the ceiling as easily as the floor. Its skin was like scorched leather, tight over taut muscle, and its face was a horror. The familiar monkey features were stretched, distorted. It had four eyes, small and black and glittering with a hateful intelligence that was not animalistic. It was personal. A thin, drooling maw opened in a silent scream, revealing needle-like teeth. The smell hit him then, a mix of ozone, singed hair, and rotting fruit.

The creature’s head swiveled, its multiple eyes locking onto him. It dropped from the ceiling, landing on the mats with a soft, sickening thud. It was low, coiled, its long, multi-jointed fingers flexing, each tipped with a black, jagged claw. Anya let out a choked sob from behind him.

There was nowhere to run. The alcove was a dead end. His mind raced, cataloging his "weapons." A forty-pound iron plate? Too slow. A resistance band? A joke. The creature took a skittering step forward, its chittering rising in pitch, a sound that felt like it was trying to unspool his sanity. It was playing with him. The malevolent intelligence in its gaze confirmed it.

It lunged.

Leo didn't think. He reacted. He threw himself to the side, stumbling over a loose mat. The creature was impossibly fast, its clawed hand raking the air where his throat had been. It hissed in frustration, a dry, rasping sound, and pivoted for another attack. Leo was on the ground, scrambling backward, his hand still clenched around the heavy metal pen. It was all he had. A final, pathetic act of defiance.

As the demon-monkey leaped again, a screeching, black-limbed horror filling his vision, Leo thrust his hand up in a desperate, reflexive warding gesture. He wasn't trying to stab it. He was just trying to keep it away. The nib of the pen, the sharp, metallic point, scraped across the creature’s outstretched forearm.

The effect was instantaneous and utterly impossible.

A flash of brilliant, searing white light erupted from the point of contact, silent but blinding. The creature’s chittering shriek was cut short, replaced by a sound that was not of this world—a high, ethereal wail that was equal parts agony and disbelief. The air crackled with energy, and the smell of ozone intensified until it was a physical blow. Where the pen had touched it, a line of pure, silver fire bloomed, spreading across its leathery skin like cracks in ice. The intricate sigil on the pen's cap glowed with the same inner light, pulsing in time with Leo’s frantic heartbeat.

The demon did not bleed. It did not burn in any conventional sense. It unraveled. Its form flickered, the scorched skin dissolving into motes of gray ash, the malformed bones turning to dust. The hateful intelligence in its four eyes dissolved into confusion, then nothingness. Within three seconds, the entire creature, the screeching, clawing, terrifying reality of it, had simply ceased to be. A small pile of fine, dark gray dust settled on the mat where it had stood. Then, silence. A profound, ringing silence that was deeper than the absence of the klaxons, which had suddenly stopped.

Leo was on his back, propped up on one elbow, breathing in huge, shuddering gasps. His hand was shaking violently. The pen was still clutched in his fist, now feeling impossibly heavy. It was warm, humming with a faint, residual energy that vibrated up his arm. He stared at the dust, then at the pen, then back at the dust. His mind refused to connect the two events. It was a coincidence. A power surge. A localized system failure. Anything but the truth that was staring him in the face.

Anya was staring, her face a mask of stunned awe. "What… what did you do?"

Before he could even begin to formulate an answer, two figures in the heavy gray armor of the Wardens appeared at the entrance, their pulse rifles leveled. They swept the alcove with their weapon lights, the beams cutting through the gloom. They saw Leo on the floor, the terrified woman behind the weights, and the small, inexplicable pile of dust on the mats.

The lead Warden, a woman with a hard, scarred face and captain’s bars on her collar, lowered her rifle slightly. Her eyes narrowed, taking in the scene with a practiced, analytical gaze. She looked at the dust, then at Leo. Her eyes locked on the pen still clutched in his hand.

“On your feet,” she commanded, her voice flat and devoid of emotion. “Slowly. And drop the pen.”

Leo didn't move. He couldn't. He was still trying to make his lungs work properly. He looked at the object in his hand. It wasn't a memento. It wasn't a useless relic. It was something else. Something impossible. Something that had just saved his life.

The second Warden took a step forward, his rifle aimed squarely at Leo's chest. "You heard the Captain. Drop it. Now."

Slowly, his fingers protesting, Leo opened his hand. The pen fell to the mat with a soft thud. It looked inert again. Just a piece of metal. Just a pen. But the warmth was still radiating from it, and in the low light of the Warren, he could have sworn the sigil on its cap gave one last, faint pulse of silver light before fading completely. The Captain’s gaze didn’t leave his face, and Leo knew, with a certainty that chilled him more than the demon had, that his life as a personal trainer in a quiet corner of the Warren was over. His world, already confined to a box, had just become infinitely smaller and infinitely more dangerous.The Captain’s command hung in the air, sharper than any blade. For a long moment, Leo’s world was a tiny, focused vignette: the two armed Wardens, the glint of light on their polished visors, the oppressive weight of their presence filling his small sanctuary. The familiar smell of sweat and rust was being scrubbed away by the clean, sterile scent of their armor’s ozone scrubbers. He pushed himself up from the floor, his muscles aching with a fatigue that had nothing to do with exercise. Every instinct screamed at him to snatch the pen back, to hold onto the one impossible thing that had worked, but the cold, unwavering aim of the pulse rifles was a more compelling argument. His hand opened, a betrayal of that instinct, and the pen dropped. It made a small, insignificant sound against the padded mat, a final note in the symphony of his former life.

The Warden Captain, moving with the same efficient grace as Leo, stepped into the alcove. She didn't spare him more than a cursory glance. Her focus was entirely on the objects on the floor. First, her boot nudged the pile of dust, her expression unreadable behind her helmet’s visor. There was no surprise, no confusion, just a grim assessment. Then, she knelt, her armored knee joint hissing softly, and picked up the pen. She didn't hold it like a weapon or a curiosity. She held it with a strange, almost clinical reverence, her gauntleted fingers turning it over and over. Her gaze lingered on the spiral sigil at its cap, and Leo could feel the intensity of her scrutiny even from ten feet away.

“Kael, secure the civilian,” the Captain ordered, her voice still a monotone, never taking her eyes off the pen. The second Warden moved forward, producing a pair of mag-cuffs from his belt. The magnetic locks clicked shut around Leo's wrists with a cold finality. He didn't resist. His strength, the very thing he'd built his life around, was useless here. “Ma'am, the witness?” Warden Kael asked, gesturing with his rifle toward Anya, who was still pressed into the corner, watching with wide, terrified eyes.

“Take her statement and send her to Med-Bay for a psych eval. Standard procedure,” the Captain replied dismissively. She finally looked up, her hard eyes meeting Leo’s. “This one comes with me.” She slid the pen into a sealed pouch on her tactical vest, a motion that felt to Leo like a lock turning on a cage. A cage he was just realizing he was now inside. The Warden named Kael gave him a rough shove toward the corridor, and as he stumbled out of his gym for the last time, he glanced back at Anya. Her face was a pale blur of fear and confusion. There was nothing he could do for her. He was no longer a trainer, a neighbor, or a friend. He was evidence.

The causeway was a scene of controlled chaos. The piercing wail of the Breach Alert had been replaced by the clipped orders of Warden squads and the quiet, desperate whimpers of residents huddled in their doorways. Med-techs in white overalls were tending to a man with a deep gash on his arm, while another team was solemnly zipping a black bag around the Warden who had collapsed. The air, thick with the smell of fear-sweat and antiseptic, was a testament to the violence that had just torn through their predictable lives. As Leo was marched down the corridor, his hands cuffed behind him, people stared. They didn’t see Leo Vance, the fitness guy from Gamma-7. They saw a prisoner, flanked by the Warren’s ultimate authority, being led away from the epicenter of the horror. Their fear of the creatures was eclipsed, for a moment, by their fear of the Wardens and whatever trouble he was in.

They bypassed the crowded mag-lifts, the Captain leading him toward a service elevator he’d never seen used. Its doors were unmarked, made of the same drab, reinforced steel as the walls, completely invisible to the uninitiated. The Captain pressed her thumb to a recessed panel, and a section of the wall slid open with a near-silent hiss, revealing a small, brightly lit lift. It was clean, unlike everything else in the lower sectors. Inside, there were only two buttons, one lit, one dark. The descent was smooth and fast, creating a disorienting pressure in his ears. When the doors opened again, the air that greeted him was different. It was cold, dry, and truly clean, lacking the familiar undertones of rust and humanity. He was in a corridor of polished white polymer, the light so bright it made him squint. The grim utility of the Warren had been replaced by a stark, sterile functionality. This was a place of secrets, a part of the city that didn’t appear on any public schematic. The Captain pushed him forward into an empty, white room, bare except for a single metal chair bolted to the floor. The door slid shut behind him, the sound echoing in the utter silence. He was alone, unarmed, and a prisoner in a box far whiter and colder than the one he had just left.The silence was the first assault. In the Warren, silence was a myth, a forgotten luxury. The ever-present hum of life support, the distant rumble of the mag-trains, the murmur of a million souls breathing in unison—it was the background radiation of their existence. Here, in this seamless white box, the absence of sound was a physical pressure. It pushed in on him, forcing him to listen to the frantic, internal rhythm of his own blood and the chaotic scramble of his thoughts. He sat bolted to the floor, the magnetic cuffs a cold, heavy weight on his wrists, a constant reminder of his utter lack of control. He was a master of his own physiology, yet he couldn't even wipe the sweat from his brow.

His mind kept replaying the moment. The chittering horror, the impossible speed, the desperate upward thrust of his hand. And the light. The silent, silver-white fire that had unmade the creature. He tried to rationalize it, to fit it into the logical, physical world he understood. A gas pocket ignited by static electricity? A freak discharge from a nearby power conduit that just happened to coincide with his movement? The explanations were flimsy, pathetic attempts to patch the gaping hole that had been torn in his reality. The truth was the heavy, warm hum he’d felt in his hand, the impossible connection between the sigil on the cap and the creature’s dissolution. It wasn't a coincidence. The pen was not a pen. And he was not who he thought he was.

The door slid open with a soft hiss that made him flinch. The Warden Captain entered, her heavy outer armor gone. She now wore a simple, severe gray duty uniform that did little to soften her presence. The scars on her face, a pale webwork across one cheek, seemed more prominent in the harsh, even light. She held the pen. She walked to the wall opposite him and a section of it recessed, producing a small, metallic table. She placed the pen on it, just out of his reach. A trophy. A leash. Her eyes, the color of worn slate, met his. They were devoid of anger or accusation, holding only a deep, unsettling weariness, as if she had seen this scene play out a thousand times before.

“Leo Vance. Sector Gamma-7. Occupation: physical conditioning specialist,” she stated, not asked. She consulted a slim datapad she held in her other hand. “Born in the Warren, thirty-two standard years ago. Mother, Elara Vance, deceased. Father, unknown. One registered next-of-kin, a grandfather, Marcus Vance, recorded as 'lost, presumed dead' during the Scorch.” She looked up from the datapad, her gaze pinning him to the chair. “Let’s talk about your grandfather.”

The question threw him. He had expected accusations, demands for an explanation of the impossible, not a query about a man who was little more than a ghost. “I never knew him,” Leo said, his voice raspy. “He was Before. My mother… she didn’t talk about him much. Said he was a scholar, a historian. She said he loved books and old things.” He gestured with his head toward the pen on the table. “She gave me that before the Doors were sealed. It was his. She said he was a writer and that he would have wanted me to have it. That’s all I know.”

The Captain listened without a flicker of expression, her stillness a form of intimidation more effective than any threat. She made a small notation on her datapad with her thumb. “She told you he was a writer. Did she tell you what he wrote about?” Leo shook his head, the movement stiff and awkward. “No. Just… things. History. The world that was lost.” The words felt foolish and hollow in the sterile room. He had always imagined his grandfather hunched over books, chronicling the fall of civilization. Now, that image felt like a children’s story.

“The creature you encountered,” the Captain said, abruptly changing the subject, her voice dropping a fraction, “the official designation is Aberration. The information you receive in Warden briefings is a sanitized, heavily redacted version of the truth. It is designed to foster compliance, not understanding. What you witnessed, what you *did*… that is not in any public file.” She took a step closer, her shadow falling over him. “Did you know the pen could do that, Vance? Did your mother tell you what it truly was when she gave it to you?”

“No!” The denial was explosive, torn from him with raw honesty. “I thought it was junk! A memento. A useless piece of metal I carried for luck. I was on the ground, it was going to kill me. I just… threw my hand up. It was all I had.” As he spoke the words, the memory flared behind his eyes—the silver fire, the ethereal wail. A shudder wracked his body. “I don’t understand what happened. It wasn’t me.” He looked at his cuffed hands, the instruments of his life’s work, now agents of something arcane and terrifying.

The Captain watched him, her analytical gaze searching his face, and for the first time, he saw a flicker of something other than cold authority in her eyes. It might have been confirmation. “You are correct. It wasn’t you,” she said, her tone clinical once more. “Not entirely. That pen is a Relic. A remnant of a war you know nothing about, fought with weapons that defy our understanding of physics. They are exceedingly rare. More importantly, they are inert for most people. Just pieces of metal, as you believed.” She paused, letting the weight of her next words settle on him. “But for a select few, they are not inert. They resonate. They respond. The Relic didn't act on its own. It acted through you.”

She turned and walked back toward the door, her part in this conversation seemingly concluded. Leo’s mind reeled. A Relic. A war. Acted *through* him. It wasn't an answer; it was a thousand new questions, each more terrifying than the last. He felt a surge of panic, a desperate need to not be left alone with these revelations. "Wait," he called out, his voice cracking. "What does that mean? What happens to me now? Am I a prisoner?"

The Captain paused with her hand on the panel to open the door. She didn’t turn around. “Your life as Leo Vance, trainer of sinew in Gamma-7, is over,” she said, the words as final as a closing blast door. “You are no longer a resident. You are an asset. Your body is no longer simply your own machine to maintain; it is now a resource of the Warren Defense Council, Special Operations Division.” She was twisting his own words, his own philosophy, and turning it into the bars of a new cage.

“An asset for what?” he demanded, pulling against the cuffs. “What am I supposed to be?”

She finally turned her head, her profile sharp and unforgiving in the stark light. “You are a man who can kill an Aberration with a touch. In a world where our best pulse rifles often fail to stop them, that makes you the most valuable weapon we have.” The door hissed open. “As for what happens now… your training begins tomorrow.” The door slid shut, plunging him back into the profound, echoing silence. But it was no longer an empty silence. It was filled with the monstrous, undeniable truth, and the chilling promise of what he was about to become.The silence left in the Captain’s wake was a weapon in itself. It was a suffocating void that amplified the frantic thumping of his own heart, the rasp of his breathing, the soft click of the magnetic cuffs as he tested their strength in a futile gesture of defiance. His muscles, trained to obey, bunched and strained against the unyielding metal, a familiar process that yielded no results. He was a master of a domain that no longer mattered. The words echoed in the white emptiness, stripping him down layer by layer. *Asset. Resource. Weapon.* He had spent his life teaching others to reclaim ownership of their bodies from the crushing apathy of the Warren, only to have his own so completely and utterly expropriated.

He squeezed his eyes shut, trying to force the chaos in his head into some semblance of order, but the image of the silver fire kept burning through his thoughts. It was not a memory of violence, but of erasure. A clean, silent deletion of something that should not have been. He thought of his grandfather, the faceless scholar. Was this his history? Not chronicles of kings and empires, but of Aberrations and Relics? Was ‘writer’ a code word for something else, a euphemism passed down by a mother who was either lying to protect him or was as ignorant as he had been? The weight of a history he never knew he had settled on him, heavier than any iron plate he had ever lifted. He wasn’t just Leo Vance anymore; he was the terminus of a bloodline, a legacy of impossible power he had carried his whole life like a dormant disease.

Hours bled into each other. He had no way to mark their passing in the perpetual, shadowless light. He dozed fitfully in the chair, his dreams a feverish mash of chittering limbs and the scent of ozone. When the door finally hissed open again, it was not the Captain who entered. Two others, a man and a woman, stood in the doorway. They wore the same gray uniforms but without the armor or overt weaponry. Their movements were quiet and precise, like medical orderlies or technicians, and they regarded him with a detached curiosity, as if he were a complex and volatile machine they had been assigned to transport. The man held a small device and with a click, the mag-cuffs on Leo’s wrists fell open. His hands were free, but the feeling of liberation was fleeting, replaced by the deep, phantom ache of their presence.

"On your feet," the woman said, her voice as flat and sterile as their surroundings. There was no room for argument. They flanked him as they led him from the interrogation room, their silence more unnerving than the Warden’s shouted commands. The journey took them deeper into the white labyrinth. This was not a place of habitation; it was a laboratory, a barracks, and a prison all rolled into one. Through a thick viewport in one wall, he caught a glimpse of a vast, cavern-like space where figures in padded gear sparred with training drones that spat sparks of contained plasma. In another room, a scientist in a clean-suit manipulated a shimmering, unstable wisp of light held within a magnetic field. This was the Warren’s secret heart, the engine of its true war, running silently beneath the mundane struggles of the city above.

His new quarters were at the end of a long, silent corridor. The room was larger than his maintenance alcove, but infinitely more sterile. A slab-like bed was recessed into one wall, its mattress a self-molding gel. A sanitation unit stood in the corner, and a small, bare desk was bolted to the floor. There were no personal effects, no scuff marks on the floor, no lingering scent of humanity. On the bed lay a folded uniform of soft gray fabric, a stark contrast to his own worn canvas and synth-cotton. The door slid shut behind him, and a soft click indicated it had locked. He was alone, but the sense of being constantly watched was a palpable film on his skin. He had been moved from one box to another, this one cleaner, brighter, and far more inescapable.

He paced the length of the cell, seven steps one way, seven steps back, the familiar rhythm a small comfort in the alien environment. He ran his hands over the walls; they were smooth, seamless, and cool to the touch. There were no vents to pry open, no conduits to follow, no weaknesses to exploit. This was a cage designed by experts. After some time, a panel he hadn't noticed near the floor slid open, and a tray emerged. On it was a steaming bowl of what looked like actual broth with recognizable vegetable chunks and a piece of bread that wasn't made from processed algae. Beside it was a glass of water, clear and cold. It was the most luxurious meal he’d seen in his life. He ate, not because he was hungry, but because the alternative was a weakness he could not afford. The message was clear: cooperate, and the cage would be comfortable.

He had just finished the last of the broth when the door opened to admit the Captain. She was alone, and she carried the pen. She walked to the desk and placed it in the center, the dark metal a stark slash against the white surface. Her slate-gray eyes were unblinking, fixed on his. She didn’t sit, maintaining her position of authority. "The incident in the alcove was a reaction," she began, her voice crisp and direct. "An uncontrolled, instinctual response to a lethal threat. That is useless to us. An unguided missile is as much a danger to its allies as its enemies. Your training begins with the most fundamental principle: control."

Leo looked from her face to the pen. He could feel a faint thrum of anticipation in the air, or perhaps it was just in his own blood. "Control what? You said it wasn't me." He flexed his hands, the only instruments of control he had ever truly understood. "What do you want me to do?" The Captain’s expression remained impassive, a mask of cold professionalism. She gestured toward the desk. "Pick it up. Feel it. In the alcove, you were terrified. Your adrenaline was surging. Your entire being was focused on one singular, desperate thought: survival. That emotional intensity, that focus, is the key that turned the lock. I want you to find that key again, but without a monster's claws at your throat. I want you to connect with it. Make the sigil glow."

He approached the desk slowly, his heart hammering. This was it. The first test. He reached out and his fingers closed around the familiar, heavy barrel of the pen. It was cool to the touch, inert. There was no hum, no warmth, no echo of the impossible power it had unleashed. It was just a pen. He closed his eyes, summoning the memory of the alcove: the stench of ozone and rot, Anya’s choked sob, the sight of those four glittering black eyes. He tried to stoke the embers of that terror, to fan it into the inferno it had been. He focused all his will on the object in his hand, pouring his concentration into it like he would a final, desperate lift. He clenched his fist, his knuckles turning white. Nothing happened. The pen remained cold. The sigil remained dark. A frustrated grunt escaped his lips. The chasm between the panicked boy on the floor and the man standing here now felt impossibly wide. "I can't," he said, the words tasting like ash, an echo of Anya’s despair. "It's not working." The Captain’s gaze didn’t waver. "Of course it isn't," she said, her voice devoid of sympathy. "Fear is a crude, unreliable fuel. You will learn to use a better one. Try again."“Try again,” the Captain commanded, her voice an instrument that tolerated no dissent. Her words were not an encouragement, but a statement of fact. There was no other option.

Leo’s jaw tightened. He closed his fist around the pen, his knuckles white with the strain. He tried to force the connection, to will the power into existence through sheer physical effort, as if he could squeeze it out of the inert metal. He pictured the rusted plates in his gym, the burn of a muscle pushed to its absolute limit, the moment of triumph when a weight was finally lifted. He channeled all of that familiar strain, that lifetime of disciplined exertion, into the object in his hand. The result was a tremor in his own arm and a bead of sweat that traced a path down his temple. The pen remained a cold, dead weight, a mocking testament to his powerlessness. He let out a breath, a hiss of pure frustration. "It's a rock," he growled, opening his hand and glaring at it as it lay on his palm. "It's a dead, useless rock."

“You are attempting to lift a mountain with a lever made of straw,” the Captain said, her tone cutting. She took a single step forward, her presence seeming to shrink the already small room. “Your entire life, your philosophy, has been built on the tangible. Muscle, sinew, bone. You believe in what you can measure, what you can break down and rebuild. But this power does not respond to brute force. It responds to clarity.” She paused, and her eyes seemed to bore into him, seeing more than he wanted. “You have a mantra. A box of breath. Inhale four, hold seven, exhale eight. You use it to master your body. You believe you are controlling your lungs, your heart rate. You are mistaken. You are controlling your mind. The physical response is merely a byproduct. The fear you felt in the alcove did not power the Relic. The absolute, singular focus that fear gave you is what opened the door. You had one thought, one purpose: to live. All else was stripped away. Find that place again, Vance. Not the terror. The focus.”

Her words hung in the sterile air, dissecting his own methods and presenting them back to him in a new, unnerving light. She was right.
