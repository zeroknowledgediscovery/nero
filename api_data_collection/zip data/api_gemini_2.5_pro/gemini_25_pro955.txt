The cursor blinked, a tiny, insistent heartbeat on a blank page of a history assignment she was supposed to have finished last Tuesday. Elara Vance stared at it, her brain a slush of half-formed thoughts about the Emu War and the peculiar bitterness of instant coffee gone cold. Outside, a Toowoomba winter was asserting itself, the wind whistling a low, mournful tune through the gaps in the window frame of their old Queenslander. The world felt grey. The assignment was grey. Her future, stretching out into an endless vista of university applications and sensible career choices, felt like the most profound shade of grey imaginable.

She sighed, a puff of air that barely disturbed the dust motes dancing in the slanted afternoon light. Procrastination, her most loyal companion, nudged her elbow. Just a quick check. One last look before she committed herself to another two hundred words on Australia's feathered foes. Her fingers, numb with cold and boredom, tapped the trackpad, pulling up the email inbox she’d already refreshed seventeen times that day. Junk. A notification from her uni portal. A newsletter from a bookshop she’d visited once. And then… it.

The subject line was deceptively simple: *Starlight Spectacular – Finalist Notification*.

It didn't seem real. The words floated, detached from meaning. Her heart gave a single, hard thud against her ribs, like a drum kick announcing a song's beginning. She clicked. The logo, a gaudy explosion of gold and purple stars, took a moment to load, pixel by painful pixel. Her breath hitched.

*Dear Elara Vance,*

*On behalf of the entire judging panel and production team, it is our immense pleasure to congratulate you on being selected as one of the ten finalists for this year’s Starlight Spectacular!*

The rest of the text blurred. Words like ‘Brisbane’, ‘live broadcast’, ‘sound check’, and ‘accommodation details to follow’ swam in her vision, but they were just noise. The core message had landed, a meteorite striking the placid surface of her life and sending out shockwaves that made the very air in her bedroom tremble. A finalist. She, Elara Vance, from a drafty house on a quiet street in a city famous for its flowers and its cold, was a finalist. A laugh escaped her, a strange, choked sound that was half-sob, half-gasp. She pushed her chair back so fast it screeched in protest, the sound swallowed by the sudden, roaring ocean in her ears.

She had to tell someone. Mum was at work, Dad was out in the shed tinkering with some ancient lawnmower, and her brother Toby was in the digital netherworld of his gaming headset. It had to be Liam.

Her phone felt slick in her suddenly sweaty palm. She fumbled his number, her thumb jabbing at the screen. He answered on the second ring, his voice a familiar, laconic drawl. "Vance. You've finally decided to surrender to the overwhelming evidence and admit that the Fender Telecaster is aesthetically superior to the Stratocaster?"

"I'm in," she breathed, the words barely a whisper.

"In? In what? In trouble for that history paper?"

"The final, Liam. The *Starlight Spectacular*. I'm in the final."

There was a pause on the other end of the line. She could picture him perfectly: leaning against the counter of the music shop where he worked, a halo of dust motes around his unruly dark hair, his brow furrowed. "Are you serious?" he said, the teasing tone gone, replaced by a low thrum of disbelief. "Ellie, are you for real?"

"They sent the email. I'm looking at it right now. It's got the logo and everything. Oh my god, Liam, I think I'm going to throw up."

"Don't you dare," he commanded, a smile creeping into his voice now. "This is it. This is the thing. I'm closing up shop. Meet me at the lookout in twenty minutes. We're celebrating."

The call ended, and Elara was left standing in the middle of her room, vibrating like one of her own guitar strings. The grey was gone, incinerated. The world was a kaleidoscope of impossible, brilliant colour. She grabbed her worn denim jacket, shoved her feet into her scuffed boots, and ran out of the house, leaving the blinking cursor and the Emu War to fight another day.

The air at Picnic Point was sharp, cutting at her cheeks, but she didn't feel the cold. The view stretched out before them, the whole of the Lockyer Valley a patchwork quilt of greens and browns under a vast, pale blue sky. It was a view she’d seen a thousand times, a view so familiar it was practically etched onto the inside of her eyelids. But today it looked different. It looked like a promise. A launching point.

Liam was already there, leaning against the railing with two takeaway cups of hot chocolate that were probably more sugar than anything else. He handed one to her, his eyes crinkling at the corners. "So. The big time."

She took a sip, the warmth spreading through her chest. "It doesn't feel real. It feels like a dream I'm about to wake up from."

"Nah, it's real," he said, bumping her shoulder with his. "I always said that song of yours was something else. 'Cobblestone Heart'. They'd have been idiots not to pick you." He said it with such simple conviction, as if it were an undeniable law of physics. It made her believe it, just for a second.

"'Cobblestone Heart' got me through the heats, maybe," she mused, staring out at the valley. "But the final? In Brisbane? On television? Liam, the other people… they're not like me. They have vocal coaches and professional headshots and thousands of followers. I saw one of them, this girl Seraphina Stone from the Gold Coast. She looks like she was genetically engineered in a lab to be a pop star."

"So? You've got a guitar held together with stubbornness and a voice that can make old blokes cry into their beer," he countered. "You've got stories. Seraphina Stone probably sings songs written by a committee of Swedes about dancing in a club she's not even old enough to get into. You sing about the frost on the grass and the way the mountains look at sunset. It's different. It's better."

She wanted to believe him. She really did. She leaned her head on his shoulder, the rough fabric of his jacket scratching her cheek. For a few minutes, they just stood there in comfortable silence, watching the cars crawl along the highway far below, tiny specks of light carrying people to and from their own ordinary lives. Her own life had, in the space of a single email, ceased to be ordinary.

The news landed at the family dinner table with a mixture of excitement and low-grade parental anxiety. Her mum, Sharon, immediately began a mental checklist of practicalities, her fork hovering over her shepherd's pie. "Brisbane? For how long? Will they put you up somewhere safe? What about your classes? You can't afford to fall behind, Elara. And what will you wear? You can't go on television in that ratty jumper."

"Sharon, let the girl breathe," her dad, Mark, said, a slow smile spreading across his face. He was a man of few words, but his pride was evident in the way he looked at her, his eyes shining. "This is fantastic, Ellie. Really fantastic."

Toby, her fourteen-year-old brother, looked up from his phone for the first time all evening. "Wait, you're gonna be on TV? Like, for real TV?"

"Looks like it," Elara said, a grin playing on her lips.

"Cool. Can you say my gamer tag on air? It's 'VoidSlayer99'."

"No," came the chorus from Elara and both her parents.

Later that night, long after the house had fallen silent, Elara sat on the edge of her bed, her old Maton guitar resting in her lap. The wood was worn smooth in all the places her hands and arms had rested over the years. It smelled of old wood, finger oil, and a thousand half-finished songs. She’d written ‘Cobblestone Heart’ on this guitar, sitting right here in this room, on a night much like this one. It was a song about feeling stuck, about having a heart that was tough and resilient but also chipped and weathered by a small-town life, dreaming of something more. The irony was not lost on her. The song about being stuck was her ticket out.

Her fingers found the familiar shapes of the chords, the notes spilling out into the quiet room, soft and clear. The melody was simple, melancholic but with an undercurrent of hope that lifted it at the end of each verse. This song was a part of her. It was her quiet nights, her walks through Queens Park when the camellias were in bloom, her view from Picnic Point.

But as she played, a new feeling began to creep in, a cold little snake of doubt winding its way around the warmth of her excitement. Liam was right. Seraphina Stone and her ilk were a different species. Elara pulled up the Starlight Spectacular finalist page on her phone. There they all were. A slick R&B singer from Brisbane. A country duo of brothers who looked like they’d stepped out of a catalogue. A dancer whose body seemed to defy gravity. And there, Seraphina. Her profile picture was professionally lit, her smile wide and perfect, her eyes holding a fierce, polished ambition. Her bio listed previous competition wins, a single already on Spotify with ten thousand streams, and training at a prestigious performing arts academy.

Elara’s own profile picture was a selfie Liam had taken of her in the park, her hair a bit messy from the wind, her smile genuine but crooked. Her bio just said: *Elara Vance. Singer-songwriter from Toowoomba. Hopes you like her songs.*

A chasm seemed to open up between her and them. A vast, terrifying gap. She was just a girl with a guitar. A girl from the quiet city on the hill. For the first time, the sheer scale of what was happening hit her not as a thrill, but as a weight. The pressure was immense, a physical thing pressing down on her chest. All those people watching. The judges. Her family. Her friends. The whole of Toowoomba, probably. She would be representing them all.

She put the guitar down, her hands trembling slightly. The elation from the afternoon felt a long way away now, replaced by the gnawing dread of potential failure. She lay back on her bed, staring up at the ceiling, the shadows from the trees outside dancing across it like spectres. She had one song. Three and a half minutes to prove that she belonged on that stage. Three and a half minutes to justify this impossible, terrifying, wonderful chance. The blinking cursor on her history assignment seemed like a relic from another lifetime. She had a new war to fight now.The next morning, the world had not returned to grey. Instead, it was a blinding, over-saturated white, the kind that follows a flashbulb going off in a dark room. The finalist email was still open on her laptop, a glowing testament to the fact that it hadn't been a fever dream. The feeling in her stomach was no longer the joyous flutter of butterflies but the frantic beating of a trapped bird’s wings. It was real. All of it. The potential for dizzying success and the even more potent potential for humiliating, public failure. She managed to get dressed and force down a piece of toast under the watchful, excited eyes of her mother, who had already started a list on a notepad by the kettle titled ‘Starlight Spectacular - Things To Do’.

Her university lecture on post-war Australian politics felt like a transmission from another planet. The professor’s voice droned on about the Chifley government and the Snowy Mountains Scheme, grand projects that shaped a nation. Elara sat in the back row, her notebook open to a fresh page, but the only thing she wrote was a frantic, spiralling doodle around the words ‘Seraphina Stone’. She imagined Seraphina wouldn’t be sitting in a chilly lecture hall worrying about a history credit. She’d be in a sun-drenched studio, working with a vocal coach named something exotic like ‘Jean-Pierre’, perfecting a high note that would make angels weep. Elara, meanwhile, was trying to remember the key points of the 1949 coal strike while her own future felt like it was teetering on a knife’s edge.

The real bombshell landed in her inbox later that afternoon, just as she was contemplating giving the Emu War a second, half-hearted attempt. A new email from the Starlight Spectacular production team. The subject: *Finalist Information Pack & Song Requirements*. This one was less fanfare, more business. A dense PDF attachment detailed a dizzying schedule of rehearsals, media calls, and wardrobe fittings. But it was section 3b that made her blood run cold. *Finalists are required to perform two songs for the live broadcast: their original submission and one cover song. The cover must be selected from the approved list (see Appendix C) and submitted for arrangement no later than 5:00 PM Friday.*

Friday. It was Wednesday. Appendix C was a sprawling list of over a hundred songs, ranging from timeless rock anthems to current chart-toppers. It was a minefield. Choosing the wrong song could be disastrous. A song that was too big for her voice, too cheesy, too cliché, or worst of all, a song that would invite direct comparison to a legendary artist she could never hope to match. She felt a fresh wave of panic, hotter and more acidic than before. This was no longer just about showing up and singing her heart out with ‘Cobblestone Heart’. This was a strategic choice. It was a test. And she had two days to get it right.

Without a second thought, she was back in her denim jacket, her phone already pressed to her ear as she hurried down the street towards the town centre. The wind had a bitter edge to it, whipping her hair across her face. "Liam, code red," she said, the moment he picked up. "Appendix C."

"Is that a secret government program you've uncovered in your history research?" he asked, his voice laced with amusement.

"Worse. It's the approved cover song list. I have to pick one by Friday. And it's huge. There's Queen, and Adele, and Taylor Swift, and some guy I've never even heard of who sounds like a robot gargling autotune. I'm going to pick the wrong one. I'm going to get up on national television and sound like a dying cat trying to sing 'Bohemian Rhapsody'."

"Okay, deep breaths, Vance. Don't self-destruct before you've even left the launchpad," he said, his tone shifting to something more serious. "Come down to the shop. Bring the list. We'll figure it out."

The music shop was her sanctuary. It smelled of lemon oil, rosin, and old carpet, a scent that always managed to soothe her frayed nerves. Liam was restringing a glossy acoustic guitar, his brow furrowed in concentration. He looked up as she came in, the little bell over the door announcing her frantic arrival. He wiped his hands on a rag and gestured for her phone. "Let's see the damage."

She handed it over, and he scrolled through the list, letting out a low whistle. "Wow. That's a mixed bag. Everything from Johnny Cash to Harry Styles. It's like a wedding DJ's panic dream." He scrolled for a few minutes in silence while Elara paced between a display of guitar pedals and a rack of ukuleles.

"See? It's impossible," she said, running a hand through her hair. "Seraphina will probably pick some massive power ballad and hire a gospel choir. The country brothers will do a perfect, twangy version of a Garth Brooks classic. What do I do? What song on that list is 'girl with a beat-up guitar from Toowoomba'?"

"That's exactly what we need to find," Liam said, his eyes scanning the screen. "Not the biggest song. Not the most popular song. The most *you* song. Something you can break down and make your own. Something with a story." He stopped scrolling. "What about this one?" He turned the phone to her. The title on the screen was ‘Landslide’ by Fleetwood Mac.

Elara stared at it. She knew the song, of course. It was one of her dad’s favourites. A quiet, fingerpicked ballad full of gentle melancholy and profound questions about time and change. It was beautiful. It was terrifying. "Stevie Nicks? Liam, that's like trying to repaint the Mona Lisa. People will kill me."

"No one's going to kill you," he said patiently. "Listen to the words, Ellie. 'I've been afraid of changing, 'cause I've built my life around you. But time makes you bolder...' It's literally what you're going through right now. It's not a pop song about a club. It's a song about being scared and growing up. It's a Cobblestone Heart kind of song." He was right. The lyrics hit her with an unexpected force, speaking directly to the fear coiling in her gut. It was a song she could sing without pretending to be someone else.

Just then, the bell above the door chimed again. A middle-aged woman with a kind face and a Queens Park rose badge pinned to her coat stepped inside, followed by her teenage daughter. The woman's eyes scanned the shop and then landed on Elara, widening in recognition. "Oh, my goodness," she said, her voice a loud whisper. "You're Elara Vance, aren't you? My sister-in-law's cousin is friends with your mum on Facebook. We saw the post! The Starlight Spectacular! That's just wonderful! The whole town will be watching, you know. We're all so proud of you."

Elara felt a hot blush creep up her neck. She managed a weak, slightly strangled smile. "Oh. Wow. Thanks. That's… really nice of you."

"We'll be cheering you on, dear," the woman beamed, before turning her attention to the sheet music. The moment passed, but it left an indelible mark. It wasn't just on Facebook anymore. It was real, spoken out loud by strangers in the one place that always felt like her own private world. The pride in the woman's eyes was genuine, but it felt like another tonne of weight being carefully placed on her shoulders. The whole town. Watching. Waiting. She looked from the woman to Liam, and then back to the song title on her phone. 'Landslide'. The word had never seemed more appropriate.The woman’s cheerful departure left a vacuum in the small shop, sucking the air from Elara’s lungs. The bell's gentle chime sounded like a starting pistol. The whole town. A knot of anxiety, tight and cold, formed in her stomach. It was one thing for her parents and Liam to be proud of her; it was another thing entirely for her success or failure to become a matter of public interest, a topic of conversation for strangers over coffee. The pressure was no longer an internal monologue; it was an external force, pressing in from all sides.

Liam watched her, his expression uncharacteristically serious. He leaned over the counter, lowering his voice. "Hey. Don't let that get in your head. It's just noise. All that matters is the music. So, are we doing this? Are we taking on Stevie Nicks?" He tapped the phone screen again, his finger resting on the word 'Landslide'. He wasn't asking if she could win. He was asking if she was brave enough to try.

She looked from his steady gaze to the song title. 'Landslide'. It felt less like a song choice and more like a declaration of intent. It was about facing the terrifying prospect of change head-on. It was about admitting fear and finding strength anyway. It was her song, even if someone else had written it. She took a deep, shaky breath, the scent of lemon oil and old wood filling her senses, grounding her. "Okay," she said, the word small but firm. "Yeah. Let's do it." A slow smile spread across Liam's face. "Good. Don't try to be her. Don't even listen to the original again until after the show. Find your own way through it. Find the chords that feel right under your fingers. Sing it like you wrote it about the view from Picnic Point."

That night, after staring at the draft email for a full hour, her finger hovering over the send button, Elara committed. *To: Starlight Spectacular Production Team. Subject: Re: Finalist Information Pack & Song Requirements. Dear Production Team, For my cover song selection, I would like to perform 'Landslide' by Fleetwood Mac. Kind regards, Elara Vance.* The moment she clicked send, a wave of irreversible finality washed over her. There was no going back. She had stepped off the ledge. Now all she could do was hope she'd learn to fly on the way down.

The next forty-eight hours were a frantic, caffeine-fuelled blur of music. She barricaded herself in her room, the world shrinking to the six strings of her Maton and the scrawled lyrics on a legal pad. As instructed, she avoided the Fleetwood Mac version, trying to unearth the song’s core from memory and intuition alone. At first, it was a clumsy, awkward affair. Her fingers stumbled over the chord changes, and her voice felt thin, a pale imitation of the ghost of Stevie Nicks's performance she held in her head. The snake of doubt returned, whispering that she'd made a terrible mistake, that she was an imposter playing dress-up with a masterpiece.

Liam came over on Thursday evening, letting himself in and navigating the obstacle course of her scattered notes and empty coffee mugs. She took the guitar back, and as she played his version, something clicked. The song started to feel less like a museum piece and more like something she could inhabit. It started to sound like her.

The family dinner table became mission control. Her mum’s list had spawned sub-lists. A new, sensible suitcase sat in the hallway. "I've packed three cardigans, Elara, Brisbane air conditioning can be ferocious. And I put in those throat lozenges. And you must call us every night. Not a text, a call." The true gut-punch arrived on Friday afternoon, in the form of a slickly produced "Meet The Finalists!" video posted on the Starlight Spectacular’s social media channels. Elara watched it on her phone, huddled on her bed. One by one, the finalists appeared in thirty-second vignettes. There was the R&B singer, smooth and confident. The country brothers, charming and wholesome. And then there was Seraphina Stone. The clip showed her in a recording studio, headphones on, laughing with a producer. She was luminous, her charisma practically pouring through the screen. "I'm just so incredibly grateful for this opportunity," she said to the camera, her smile dazzling. She threw her phone onto her quilt, the image of Seraphina's perfect smile burned into her mind. It was hopeless. She was a folk singer with a chipped guitar and one good song about being sad in a small town. Seraphina was a star in waiting, a fully-formed pop product. Liam was wrong. It wasn't different, it was amateur versus professional. The chasm between them was no longer a fear; it was a fact, documented in high-definition video and set to an upbeat, generic backing track.

She didn't hear Liam come in until he sat beside her and nudged her shoulder. "Just saw the video," he said quietly. He’d seen the look on her face. "So what? She wants to make people dance. The night before she left for Brisbane, a strange calm settled over the house. Her bags were packed, her guitar case standing by the door like a silent, loyal bodyguard. A week ago, she was a girl with a history assignment, a cold cup of coffee, and a future that felt like a long, grey road. The fear was still there, a low hum beneath the surface, but for the first time, it was matched by a fierce, protective love for her own music. She had to see it through. The song, born in the quiet of her bedroom, unfurled in the massive theatre, and instead of being lost, it seemed to fill every corner, every heart that had ever felt stuck. When the last, hopeful chord faded, the applause that rose up to meet her was different. It wasn't just loud; it was warm, an exhalation of shared feeling.

The host jogged back on stage, his professional smile tinged with genuine warmth. "Just beautiful, Elara. Absolutely beautiful. Folks, she's going to sing one more for us, a timeless classic. This is 'Landslide'." He jogged off, and the lights shifted, bathing her in a softer, more intimate blue. The feedback crackle from rehearsal echoed in her memory, a ghost of panic. But then she remembered Seraphina's nod. She wasn't alone. Taking a deep, supported breath, she began to play.

The fingerpicked melody was a fragile web of sound in the sudden hush. She sang the first lines with a hushed reverence, a quiet confession whispered into the microphone. *'Well, I've been afraid of changing, 'cause I've built my life around you.'* She let her voice tremble just slightly, not from nerves, but from honesty. As the chorus arrived, she let the sound swell, her voice gaining strength and clarity, the cracks she’d fought to keep adding a layer of raw, aching truth. On the screen, the director got his wish. The camera pushed in for a tight close-up, and she knew she was singing not just to the room, but to the nation. She sang of the fear and the boldness, of the child in her heart and the mountains that made her feel small. It was no longer a cover song. It was her testimony.

The final line, *'And I'm getting older, too,'* was barely a whisper. She held the final, ringing chord, letting it hang in the air until it decayed into absolute silence. For a second, a single, eternal second, there was nothing. No coughs, no whispers, just the collective held breath of thousands of people. Then, the theatre exploded. It was not just applause; it was a roar, a tidal wave of sound and emotion that washed over her, so powerful it felt like a physical impact. People were on their feet. In the front row, a woman was openly weeping. On the judges' panel, the toughest critic, a man known for his stoic demeanor, was wiping his eye. Elara let out a breath she didn't realize she'd been holding, a choked gasp of disbelief and release. She had reached them. She had been heard.The wave of sound was a living thing, a physical presence that pushed against her, buoying her up. Elara could only sit there on the stool, her fingers frozen on the strings of her guitar, her breath caught somewhere in her chest. The host, whose name she couldn't remember, jogged back to her side, his own professional smile looking a little stunned, a little softer around the edges. He put a hand on her shoulder, a gesture that felt surprisingly human. "Elara Vance, everyone!" he shouted into his microphone, and the roar from the crowd somehow found another level. "How about that? Give it up for Toowoomba's finest!" He leaned in, his voice for her ears only. "Kid, I've been doing this for ten years. I've never seen anything like that."

He guided her to stand and face the judges' table. They were all on their feet, their applause no longer a polite duty but a genuine ovation. The toughest of them, a notoriously cynical record producer named Marcus Thorne, was the first to speak when the noise finally subsided enough for him to be heard. His voice, usually a tool for delivering withering critiques, was thick with emotion. "Elara," he began, shaking his head slightly as if in disbelief. "In this business, we spend millions of dollars trying to manufacture authenticity. We assemble teams of writers to create 'stories'. We use algorithms to predict what will connect with an audience." He paused, looking directly at her, his gaze piercing. "And then a girl from Toowoomba comes on stage with a beat-up guitar and in six minutes, with two songs, reminds us all that the real thing… the actual, honest, break-your-heart-and-put-it-back-together-again real thing… is priceless. That wasn't just a performance. That was a testimony."

The walk backstage was a disorienting blur. The blinding lights gave way to the shadowy maze of cables and rolling equipment cases. Her legs felt disconnected from her body, moving on autopilot. The thunder of applause faded, replaced by the quiet hum of machinery and the murmur of the stagehands. And then she saw them, huddled just beyond the curtain, their faces a mess of tears and ecstatic pride. Her mum was first, wrapping her in a hug so tight it nearly squeezed the air from her lungs, sobbing "Oh, my baby, my brilliant baby," into her hair. Her dad followed, his own eyes wet, crushing her in a hug that smelled of his familiar aftershave and unspoken love. Toby, for once without his phone, just stared at her, his jaw slack. "Whoa," he breathed. "You were… actually good." Liam stood behind them, not pushing his way forward, just watching her with a quiet, knowing smile that said everything. It was the "I told you so" she had been waiting her whole life to hear, delivered without a single word.

As her family enveloped her, she caught sight of the other finalists. Jesse McRae, whose own brilliant performance felt like a lifetime ago, caught her eye and gave her a wide, sincere grin and two thumbs up. Even Jax, who had been watching on a monitor nearby, gave a slow, surprised nod of respect before turning away, as if caught showing an uncool emotion. But it was Seraphina’s reaction that stopped her breath. She was standing alone, her silver dress a shimmering mirage in the dim light. She hadn't been called back for her own judges’ comments yet, but her face held no trace of anxiety. As Elara’s gaze met hers, Seraphina gave a small, almost imperceptible shake of her head, a look of profound, weary admiration in her eyes. "You made them feel something," she murmured, so quietly that Elara almost didn't hear her over her mother's happy sobs. "That's the part they can't teach you."

Before the feeling could fully land, the stage manager was back, his headset buzzing. "Alright people, we need everyone for the final announcement! Let's go, let's go! Line up on stage, please!" The ten finalists were herded back into the light, forming a nervous, glittering line across the front of the stage. Elara found herself standing between Clay McRae and a dancer whose lithe body seemed to hum with nervous energy. The host stood centre stage, holding a gold envelope, basking in the suspense. The lights, the noise, the sheer, overwhelming scale of it all returned. Elara felt Liam’s quiet grin and her dad’s note in her pocket, two small anchors in a raging sea. She didn't dare to hope. She had already won. She had sung her songs, and they had listened. That was more than she had ever dreamed of.

The host began the agonizing process of elimination, his voice booming over the tense silence. "In no particular order, the judges have made their decision. Our top three finalists for this year's Starlight Spectacular are..." He paused for an eternity, letting the camera pan across their anxious faces. "The McRae Brothers!" The two brothers hugged, their faces a picture of joyous disbelief. "Seraphina Stone!" Seraphina’s professional smile clicked into place, a mask of perfect composure as she nodded graciously. "And Elara Vance!" Elara's heart jumped into her throat. She hadn't even registered the possibility. Top three. Her, with her worn Maton and her songs about the frost, was in the top three alongside the Nashville-ready brothers and the pop-goddess-in-waiting.

The final moments passed in a dizzying, heart-pounding rush. The host announced the third-place finishers, the brilliant country duo who had wowed everyone with their harmonies. The McRae brothers accepted their placement with smiles and grace, waving to their cheering family in the crowd. And then there were two. Elara and Seraphina. The small-town songwriter and the pop star prodigy. The 'authentic' and the 'perfect'. The camera switched between their faces, a study in contrasts. Elara stood there, her hands clenched, trying to remember how to breathe. Seraphina stood perfectly still, her posture immaculate, a silver statue awaiting its fate. "And the winner of this year's Starlight Spectacular, taking home the recording contract and a hundred thousand dollars, is..." The host ripped open the envelope, the sound like a gunshot in the silent theatre. He milked the pause, a wide grin on his face. The entire world seemed to hang on his next words. Elara felt a strange sense of peace descend. It didn't matter. She had done what she came to do.

"ELARA VANCE!"

The name echoed through the theatre, but for a moment, it didn't register. The words were just sounds, detached from meaning, like the finalist email a lifetime ago. Then Seraphina turned to her. There was no disappointment in her eyes, only a tired, genuine smile. She stepped forward and gave Elara a brief, surprising hug. "You earned it," she whispered, and the touch, the words, finally broke the spell. The world came crashing back in a deafening, dazzling explosion. Confetti cannons erupted, showering the stage in a blizzard of gold and purple paper. The theme music swelled, and the crowd’s roar became a physical entity, shaking the floor beneath her feet. Her knees buckled, and she would have fallen if the host hadn't grabbed her arm, pressing a large, heavy glass trophy into her hands. Through the swirling confetti, she saw them. Her mum, her dad, Toby, and Liam, running onto the stage, their faces incandescent with joy. They surrounded her, a tangle of loving arms, their shouts of pride lost in the glorious noise. Elara clutched the trophy to her chest, her other hand gripping the neck of her guitar, tears streaming down her face, washing away the makeup and the lights and the last lingering traces of grey. She looked out at the impossible, brilliant kaleidoscope of it all, not as the Toowoomba songbird, but as Elara Vance, a girl who had found her voice and, in doing so, had changed the colour of her entire world.The confetti was still falling, a silent, glittering snowstorm under the hot stage lights, when the world began to move again. The roaring ocean of sound slowly resolved back into individual applause and shouts, and the human tide of her family and Liam swept her into a vortex of hugs and happy tears. Her cheek was pressed against the rough denim of Liam’s jacket, and she could smell the familiar scent of the music shop on him, a tiny patch of home in the heart of this dazzling, alien landscape. He pulled back, his hands on her shoulders, his eyes shining with a fierce pride that mirrored her dad’s. "See?" he said, his voice thick with emotion but his grin as wide and crooked as ever. "Told you. The Fender Telecaster of contestants." It was so stupid, so perfectly Liam, that a real laugh, a genuine, bubbling-up-from-the-soul laugh, escaped her for the first time.

The dreamlike state was shattered by the arrival of the press. A tight phalanx of reporters and photographers, held back until the broadcast officially ended, surged forward. Flashes popped like miniature supernovas, momentarily bleaching the world white. Microphones were thrust into her face, a bouquet of black foam and logos. "Elara, how does it feel?" "What's the first thing you're going to do with the money?" "Are you ready to be Australia's next big star?" The questions came in a dizzying torrent. Elara blinked, speechless, clutching the heavy glass trophy to her chest like a shield. It was her dad who stepped forward, putting a protective arm around her. "She's a bit overwhelmed, folks. We're just so incredibly proud of her." His simple, honest statement did more to calm the frenzy than any rehearsed answer could have. Elara found her voice, a small, wavering thing. "I... I think I'm going to call my grandmother," she said, an answer so un-starlike, so mundane, that a few of the reporters actually chuckled.

Just as the chaos threatened to engulf them again, a calm, authoritative voice cut through the noise. "Give the kid some air." Marcus Thorne stood there, flanked by Chloe the producer, who now looked at Elara with a dazed, proprietary awe. The press parted for the record executive like he was a shark and they were a school of pilot fish. He walked right up to Elara, ignoring the still-flashing cameras. He looked not at the trophy, but at the Maton still slung over her shoulder. "That guitar has a few stories in it," he said, his voice a low rumble. "We're about to add a few more." He gave her a look that was part appraisal, part warning. "My office will call you on Monday morning. We'll book a flight to Sydney. Get some rest this weekend. The real work starts now." And with that, he turned and was gone, leaving a wake of silence and the heavy weight of his words.

Later, in the surreal quiet of her sky suite, the celebration felt more real. The gaudy trophy sat on the marble coffee table, catching the city lights and refracting them into tiny rainbows on the ceiling. Toby was taking a series of reverent selfies with it. Her mum, having finally stopped crying, was now attempting to brush confetti out of Elara’s hair while simultaneously trying to get her to drink a cup of tea. Her dad stood by the vast window, looking out at the sprawling, glittering city, a small, proud smile on his face. Liam was on the couch, scrolling through his phone with a stunned expression. "Ellie," he said, looking up. "You're trending. Like, number one in the country. Above a political scandal and a cat that can play the keyboard." The information was too large to process. It floated in the air, abstract and weightless, refusing to land.

After her family, buzzing with an energy that would surely last the entire drive back to Toowoomba, finally retreated to their own room, Liam stayed behind. The wild, celebratory joy in the suite subsided, leaving a comfortable, humming quiet. "Well," he said, leaning back against the couch and gesturing with his tea cup at the trophy. "That's a bit shinier than the 'Best New Talent' award from the pub battle of the bands." She sank onto the couch beside him, the exhaustion of the week finally hitting her in a tidal wave. "I think I'm going to wake up tomorrow and it'll be Tuesday and I'll still have that history assignment to write." He nudged her foot with his. "Nah. The Emu War is officially over, Vance. You won." He was quiet for a moment, looking at her, his usual teasing manner gone. "You were incredible tonight, Ellie. Not because of all this," he waved a hand at the room, "but because you were just you. You didn't let them turn you into the 'Toowoomba songbird'. You just sang your songs." He finally said the words she needed to hear. "I'm so proud of you." She leaned her head on his shoulder, just as she had at Picnic Point what felt like a hundred years ago, and closed her eyes, the glittering city outside the window finally feeling less like a foreign planet and more like a promise.

She woke the next morning to the unfamiliar sensation of absolute silence, the thick hotel carpets and heavy doors swallowing all sound. For a blissful, groggy second, she was just Elara, in a bed, on a Sunday. Then she opened her eyes. The first thing she saw was the trophy on the bedside table, a solid, glass-and-gold refutation of any notion that it had all been a dream. The second thing she saw, when she finally summoned the courage to look at her phone, was her own face. It was everywhere. On the homepage of every major news site. In thumbnails for a dozen different reaction videos on YouTube. Her Instagram, which had 347 followers a week ago, was now a cascade of blue verification ticks and a number so large it looked like a misprint. It was dizzying, a vertiginous plunge into a new reality. She was a headline. A public fact. A commodity. A cold sliver of the old fear returned, a different strain of the virus: the fear not of failure, but of this overwhelming, all-consuming success.

The drive home was a slow, surreal journey back across the border from fantasy to reality. As they left the sprawling haze of Brisbane behind and began the winding climb up the Toowoomba Range, the familiar patchwork of the Lockyer Valley spread out below them, a comforting sight. Yet it looked different. The last time she had seen this view, she had been a girl with a secret, a hope, a one-in-a-million shot. Now, she was returning as a victor, a prizewinner, a local hero. She felt like an astronaut re-entering the atmosphere, unsure if the quiet world she had left behind would still feel like home. When they pulled into their street, a quiet suburban road that was usually sleepy on a Sunday afternoon, they found it lined with cars. Neighbours were out on their lawns, and a handmade sign, painted on an old bedsheet and strung between two trees, flapped in the breeze. It read: *WELCOME HOME, ELARA! OUR STAR!* As she stepped out of the car, a smattering of applause and cheers broke out. It was her world, but it had tilted on its axis.

She spent the rest of the day in a gentle, hazy cocoon, fielding phone calls from relatives she hadn’t spoken to in years and politely declining requests from the local paper for an in-depth, at-home interview. The weight of Marcus Thorne’s words—*The real work starts now*—was a constant, low hum beneath the surface of the congratulations. The Starlight Spectacular was over. The prize had been won. But the blinking cursor on a blank page had simply been replaced by a signed, sealed recording contract, a document that represented a new, far more intimidating kind of blank page. On Monday morning, as promised, her phone rang. The number was unlisted, from Sydney. "Good morning, is this Elara Vance?" The voice was crisp, female, and radiated an efficiency that Elara could feel through the phone line. "This is Amelia from Marcus Thorne’s office at Phoenix Records. Mr. Thorne would like to meet with you tomorrow to discuss the next steps. We’ve booked you a flight out of Brisbane at 9:00 AM. A car will be at your house at six. We look forward to seeing you, Elara. Welcome to the team." The line clicked dead. Elara stood in the kitchen of her quiet Toowoomba house, the phone still pressed to her ear, the familiar scent of her mum’s toast in the air. Sydney. Phoenix Records. A car at six. The kaleidoscope had stopped spinning, and the pieces were beginning to fall into a new, terrifyingly clear pattern. The war was over. The campaign was about to begin.The silence that followed the phone call was more profound than any applause. Elara stood in the middle of her familiar kitchen, the receiver still cool against her ear, listening to the hum of the refrigerator and the gentle tick of the clock on the wall. These were the mundane, bedrock sounds of her life, and they suddenly seemed fragile, like a recording from a distant past. Her mother, who had been listening with an expression of rapt attention, immediately snapped into action, her face a battleground of fierce pride and logistical anxiety. She grabbed the notepad that still bore the heading ‘Starlight Spectacular’ and forcefully crossed it out, writing ‘Sydney!!!’ underneath with three emphatic exclamation points. Her dad simply walked over and took the phone from Elara’s limp hand, placing it back in its cradle before enveloping her in a hug. He didn’t say anything, but she could feel the thrum of his heartbeat, steady and sure against her own frantic pulse. The future was no longer an abstract concept or a distant city on the horizon; it was a black car arriving in less than twenty-four hours to carry her away.

That evening, she packed. It felt less like preparing for a trip and more like curating a museum exhibit of her former life. She laid her worn denim jacket, the one that had been her second skin, carefully into the sensible suitcase her mum had bought. She folded her favourite band t-shirts, soft and faded from a hundred washes. On an impulse, she dug out the ratty, grey jumper her mother so despised, the one she’d been wearing the day the finalist email arrived, and tucked it into the bottom of the case like a secret. This wasn't just clothing; it was armour, a collection of talismans to ward off the sterilising gloss of her new world. Every object held a memory, a piece of the girl who wrote songs in a drafty room, and she was terrified of leaving any part of that girl behind. The thought was a cold stone in her gut: she was going to Sydney to become a recording artist, but she felt like she was preparing to be remade, her own story sanded down and re-varnished to suit a new owner.

Liam found her on the front porch swing, pushing off gently with the toe of her boot, making the old chains groan in a familiar rhythm. The cool Toowoomba night air was sharp and clean, smelling of damp earth and blooming jasmine from the neighbour's fence. He didn't say anything for a long time, just sat on the top step, nursing a bottle of ginger beer. The comfortable silence was a balm on her frayed nerves. Finally, she was the one to speak, her voice low. "They're going to try and change me, aren't they? They'll give me different clothes, a different sound. They'll want me to write songs about dancing in clubs with people I don't know." He took a long swig from his bottle before answering, his eyes fixed on the dark shape of the mountains on the horizon. "Probably," he said, with a bluntness that was both jarring and comforting. "They're a business. They're going to try and turn you into a product they think they can sell. They'll look at you and see a checklist: small-town girl, authentic, heartfelt. They'll try to bottle that."

She stopped swinging, the silence now filled with the low hum of her anxiety. "So what do I do?" she whispered. He turned to look at her then, his expression serious under the dim porch light. "You do what you've always done. You be stubborn. Your stubbornness is your superpower, Vance. You were stubborn enough to keep writing songs when no one was listening. You were stubborn enough to argue with that vocal coach about the meaning of a song. You just have to be stubborn enough to remember that they didn't sign a product. They signed a songwriter. They signed the girl who wrote 'Cobblestone Heart' because her heart actually felt like that. Don't you ever let them make you forget it." He reached out and tapped the worn headstock of her Maton, which she’d brought out with her. "This is your compass," he said. "If the music ever starts to feel like it's not coming from you, you'll know you're lost. Just find your way back to it."

The morning was a watercolour wash of pre-dawn grey, the air cold and still. True to Amelia's word, at precisely six o'clock, a sleek black sedan, so polished it seemed to absorb the dim light, glided to a stop at their curb. It was utterly silent, its presence on their quiet, unpretentious street as alien as a fallen spaceship. The farewell on the driveway was a clumsy, heartbreaking ballet of last-minute advice from her mum, another bone-crushing hug from her dad, and a surprisingly heartfelt "Don't get famous and weird" from Toby. As the driver, a man in a crisp suit who didn't say a word, held the door for her, Elara slid into the plush leather interior. The car smelled of nothing, a clean, sterile void. The door closed with a soft, expensive thud, sealing her in and the sounds of her life—the chirping of the morning birds, her mother's final, wavering "Be safe!"—out. As the car pulled away, she looked through the tinted rear window at the three figures standing on the footpath, getting smaller and smaller, until the car turned the corner and they were gone.

The flight was a disembodying experience. Elara had a window seat, and as the plane climbed, she watched the world she knew shrink and flatten into a map of itself. The patchwork of green and brown farmland, the winding roads she had driven a thousand times, the familiar hump of the ranges—it all became a geometric pattern, an abstraction. Then the plane banked and climbed through a thick blanket of cloud, and for a long, surreal moment, there was nothing but an endless expanse of brilliant white, a blank page stretched from horizon to horizon. She was suspended between two worlds, the one she had left behind and the one that was waiting for her, invisible, below. When they finally descended, the landscape that emerged was utterly different. A vast, sprawling geometry of concrete and terracotta, threaded with the glittering blue of the harbour. It was immense, overwhelming, and utterly impersonal.

In the bustling arrivals hall of Sydney Airport, a young woman with a sharp bob and a tablet computer bearing the Phoenix Records logo found her instantly. "Elara Vance? I'm Sarah, from the A&R department. Welcome to Sydney. The car's just this way." Her smile was bright and efficient, and she talked non-stop as she guided Elara through the throng, a stream of cheerful chatter about the weather, the traffic, and how excited everyone at the label was. It was a practiced, professional warmth, designed to put her at ease, but it only made Elara feel more like a package being processed. The drive into the city was a sensory assault. The sounds were louder, the light harsher, the sheer number of people a stark contrast to the comfortable spaces of Toowoomba. The car navigated the dense traffic, flanked on all sides by towering buildings of glass and steel that reflected the sky in cold, distorted fragments.

Phoenix Records was located in a skyscraper that seemed to pierce the very clouds she had just flown through. It was a monument of smoked glass and gleaming chrome, its lobby a cavernous, minimalist space with a marble floor so polished she could see her own nervous reflection in it. The air was cool and smelled faintly of expensive cleaning products and ambition. The friendly receptionist directed her to the executive elevator, and Elara rode up alone, the silence amplifying the frantic beat of her heart. The elevator opened onto the 34th floor. The corridor was hushed, lined with platinum records in gleaming frames, a gallery of past successes. At the very end of the hall was a set of double doors in dark, polished wood. A simple, elegant silver plaque was affixed to it. It read: *Marcus Thorne, CEO*.

She stood before the doors for a long moment, her hand hovering in the air. This was it. The blinking cursor on a new blank page. The Emu War was a forgotten skirmish from a previous life; this was the precipice of a new, unknown campaign. Her entire future was waiting on the other side of that door. She thought of Liam's words—*Be stubborn*—and her dad’s note, a warm, secret weight in her pocket. Taking a deep, shuddering breath that did little to calm the roaring in her ears, she finally raised her hand and knocked. A moment passed, and then a deep, familiar voice from within called out, "Come in." Elara pushed the heavy door open and stepped across the threshold, leaving the last vestiges of the girl she had been on the other side.The office was not a room; it was a territory. The space was vast and minimalist, a study in quiet, absolute power. One entire wall was a seamless pane of glass that framed a breathtaking, god-like view of Sydney Harbour, the bridge a familiar steel arch in the distance, the Opera House a cluster of white sails on the glittering blue water. The view was so spectacular it felt aggressive, a declaration that from this vantage point, the entire world was just a pretty picture. The furniture was sparse and sculptural: a huge desk of dark, polished timber that seemed to float above the floor, and two low-slung leather chairs that looked more like art installations than places for humans to sit. There was no clutter, no personal photos, only the silent, framed platinum records on the wall, a graveyard of past triumphs.

Marcus Thorne was not sitting at his desk. He stood with his back to the door, hands clasped behind him, looking out at the view as if he owned it. He was a silhouette against the blinding sky. "Close the door, Elara," he said, his voice calm and resonant, not turning around. She did as she was told, the heavy door clicking shut with an air of finality, sealing her inside the quiet, pressurised space. He turned slowly, his face unreadable. In the sharp, unforgiving light of his office, he looked less like a TV personality and more like a predator in his natural habitat. "Sit down." He gestured to one of the leather chairs. It was as cold and unwelcoming as it looked, forcing her into a posture that was both alert and slightly uncomfortable. He didn't sit at his desk, but perched on the edge of it, a subtle power move that made him seem both casual and completely in command.

"Congratulations again," he began, his voice devoid of the emotion it had held on the night of the final. This was business. "You gave us a great television moment. Crying woman in the front row, judges on their feet. The network was ecstatic. Your social media metrics are through the roof. It's a publicist's dream." He spoke of her raw, emotional breakthrough as if it were a well-executed marketing strategy. The coldness of it was a shock, but it was also clarifying. This was the reality behind the confetti. "But that's over now," he continued, leaning forward slightly. "The fairytale is a great story for the magazines, but it doesn't sell albums. Hard work sells albums. A great product sells albums."

Elara’s hands, resting in her lap, clenched into fists. A product. The word landed with a thud. "I understand this is a business, Mr. Thorne," she said, her voice steadier than she felt. "Of course. And the first order of business is your debut album. We have a window. The public's memory is short. The momentum from the show will last three, maybe four months. We need a lead single on the radio by then, and an album ready to drop a month after that." He laid out the timeline with the detached precision of a general planning a military campaign. It was brutally fast. "Your sound—the 'Toowoomba songbird' thing, the storyteller with the acoustic guitar—it works. It's a strong brand identity. We'll lean into it. But it needs to be bigger. More polished. We need choruses that can get on the radio."

He stood up and began to pace slowly in front of the vast window, a restless energy emanating from him. "I've already reached out to a producer. Alistair Finch." Elara’s breath caught. Alistair Finch was a legend, a man who had produced some of the most iconic albums of the last twenty years, famous for his lush, layered soundscapes and his notoriously demanding personality. "He's interested. He saw your final performance. He sees potential. We'll fly you to his studio in the Southern Highlands next month to start tracking." Then came the line she had been dreading. "In the meantime, my A&R team has put together a list of co-writers for you. We can have you in Nashville in two weeks. These are the best in the business. They'll help you craft those choruses, find the hooks."

Here it was. The first, and most important, battle. The stubbornness Liam had spoken of rose up in her, a fierce, protective instinct. "Mr. Thorne," she said, her voice quiet but clear in the silent room. "I appreciate that. But I write my own songs. Alone." Marcus stopped pacing and turned to her. He didn't look angry. He looked... amused. A faint, wolfish smile played on his lips. "Elara, every major artist you can name uses co-writers. It's not a suggestion, it's how the industry works. It’s about efficiency. You can spend six months agonizing over a bridge, or you can sit in a room with a professional who can solve it in an hour. We don't have six months."

"It's not about efficiency for me," she insisted, her heart hammering against her ribs. She was arguing with the most powerful man in the Australian music industry. "It's how the songs happen. I don't know how to do it any other way. The stories are… personal. I can't build one with a committee." She expected him to shut her down, to pull out the contract and point to a clause that gave him total creative control. Instead, he walked back to his desk and stood looking down at her, his expression a complex mix of impatience and something that almost looked like respect. He was testing her, she realised. He wanted to see if there was any steel beneath the small-town fragility.

"Alright," he said, after a long, tense moment. "You want to do it your way? Prove it." The words hung in the air, a direct challenge. "The Nashville trip is cancelled. For now. You have four weeks. Thirty days. I want to see ten songs on my desk by the end of it. Not ideas, not half-finished verses. Ten complete songs. Lyrics, melodies, everything. If they're good—if they're great—we give them to Alistair Finch and you make your album. After ten solid days of self-imposed confinement, the city finally beckoned. The journey to the Southern Highlands was a gradual unwinding, a slow decompression from the frantic energy of the city. It was a landscape that felt halfway between the flat, open plains of the Darling Downs and the concrete canyons of Sydney; a liminal space, a place of transition. After two hours, they turned off the main road and onto a winding, narrow lane shaded by a canopy of towering gum trees. They drove for another ten minutes, deeper into the quiet woods, until the lane opened into a clearing. And there it was. It wasn't a modern studio complex or a sleek architectural statement. It was an old, sprawling stone homestead, its walls covered in ivy, its slate roof mellowed by age. A plume of smoke curled from a stone chimney, and a collection of mismatched, well-tended pot plants lined the wide, wooden veranda. Beside the main house stood a huge, converted barn, its timber walls a silvery grey, with large, modern glass panels installed where the hayloft doors would have been. It looked less like a recording studio and more like a secret, magical place where music was grown, not manufactured.

The driver helped her with her bags, placing both guitar cases on the veranda with a respectful nod. "This is it, miss. He's expecting you." With a quiet crunch of gravel, the car was gone, leaving Elara alone in the profound, ringing silence of the bush, broken only by the chattering of unseen birds. She stood for a long moment, simply taking it all in. This was Alistair Finch's domain. It felt a world away from Marcus Thorne's office, a place governed by nature and time, not by market trends and quarterly reports. A tall, whip-thin man with a shock of unruly white hair and a face etched with the lines of a thousand late-night mixing sessions emerged from the main house. He wore a paint-splattered t-shirt, faded jeans, and no shoes, his feet pale against the dark wood of the veranda. He looked at her with eyes that were a startlingly bright, intelligent blue, his expression neither friendly nor unwelcoming, but intensely curious, like a naturalist observing a new species. "You're Elara," he said. It wasn't a question. His voice was a low, gravelly rasp, the product of too many cigarettes and too many shouted instructions over loud monitors. "I'm Alistair. Marcus sent the demos. Let's see if they're any good in the real world. Grab your guitar. The old one." He gestured towards her battered Maton case with a long, bony finger, completely ignoring the pristine Martin beside it. "The one with the stories in it."

He didn't wait for a reply, simply turned and ambled barefoot towards the barn, his movements loose and economical. Elara's heart hammered against her ribs. This was it. No small talk, no tour, no settling in. Just the work. She grabbed her Maton, her hands feeling slick with sweat, and followed him. The interior of the barn was breathtaking. The soaring, cathedral-like roof was a web of ancient timber beams, from which hung a few simple industrial lights. The vast, open space was a controlled chaos of vintage equipment. A huge, sprawling mixing console with a forest of faders and knobs dominated one end of the room, looking like the command deck of a spaceship designed in 1975. The floors were covered in a patchwork of worn Persian rugs. Everywhere she looked, there were instruments: a Hammond organ with a Leslie speaker, a stand of vintage Fender and Gibson electric guitars, a dusty grand piano, a wall of amplifiers, and a forest of microphones on stands of every shape and size. The air smelled of old wood, warm vacuum tubes, and ozone. It was a temple dedicated to sound. Alistair walked to the center of the room, to a single, simple wooden stool placed on a large, circular rug. "Right here," he said, gesturing to the stool. "Play me the angry one. 'Glass Kingdom'." He didn't retreat to the control room behind the glass. Instead, he pulled over another stool, sat down about ten feet away from her, and simply waited, his bright blue eyes fixed on her, his expression giving nothing away. The silence was absolute, heavier and more expectant than the applause of a thousand people. This was the real audition.The silence in the barn was a physical thing, a pressure against her eardrums. It was a silence made of old wood, dust motes dancing in the shafts of light from the high windows, and the vast, patient quiet of the bush outside. In this cathedral of sound, the only noise was the frantic, frantic drumming of her own heart. Alistair Finch sat motionless, his bright, penetrating gaze fixed not on her face, but on her hands as they rested on the guitar. He wasn't looking at a person; he was looking at an instrument. The stage, with its roaring crowd and dazzling lights, had been a test of nerve. Marcus Thorne’s office had been a test of will. This felt like a test of her very soul. This man wasn't interested in her story or her brand. He was interested only in the truth, and he had the unnerving ability to see if she was lying.

She took a breath that was far too shallow, and forced her trembling fingers into the opening E minor chord. The first notes were tentative, a pale imitation of the rage she had felt in the city apartment. Alistair’s expression didn't change, but she felt his disappointment like a drop in temperature. He didn't want a performance. He wanted the thing itself. She stopped, the half-formed chord dying with a dull thud. She closed her eyes, shutting out the intimidating space and the man watching her. She summoned the memory: the cold leather of the chair in Marcus’s office, the casual dismissal of her Maton, the feeling of being a product to be polished and sold. She remembered the rage, pure and white-hot, that had fueled the song’s creation. Opening her eyes, she looked directly at Alistair, and played the chord again. This time, it was a gunshot.

The song ripped out of her, raw and unvarnished. Her strumming was a percussive assault, her palm muting the strings to create a tight, driving rhythm. Her voice, when it came, was not the clear, melancholic tone of 'Cobblestone Heart'. It was a low, snarling thing, laced with gravel and fury. *‘Got a kingdom made of glass and steel / A 34th-floor view that isn’t real / You got your platinum smile and your telephone line / But you can’t buy this ghost in this guitar of mine.’* The words, written in the lonely confines of her chic prison, echoed strangely in the warm, cavernous barn. It was a protest song shouted in a sanctuary. She poured every ounce of her defiance, her fear, and her stubborn pride into the three-and-a-half-minute onslaught. She finished on a final, crashing E minor, letting it ring out until it was swallowed by the high, timbered ceiling, the vibration travelling from the guitar through her body and into the floor.

She was left breathing heavily, her knuckles white where she gripped the guitar’s neck, the echo of the final chord still ringing in her ears. The silence that followed was absolute. Alistair didn't move. He just sat there, his blue eyes narrowed in thought. He wasn't clapping. He wasn't smiling. He was dissecting what he had just heard. Just as Elara was convinced he was about to tell her to pack her bags and go home, he spoke, his gravelly voice cutting through the tension. "You're rushing," he said, the words simple and devastating. "The pre-chorus. You’re galloping towards the hook like you’re afraid the listener will get bored. The anger is pushing you. It should be the other way around. You need to control the anger. Make it a weapon, not a runaway train."

He stood up and began to amble around her in a slow, thoughtful circle, his bare feet silent on the worn rugs. "The song isn't just the words you're singing. It's the space between them. It's the moment of quiet before the chorus hits. You're filling all the space because you're scared of it. You’re scared that if you stop shouting, I'll stop listening." He stopped directly in front of her. "The quiet is where the power is. It’s where the listener leans in. Your chorus feels like a release of tension, but you haven't built any. You're just playing loud. There's a difference between loud and heavy. I want heavy. Play it again. And this time, sit behind the beat. Let the guitar be the engine, not the passenger."

It was the beginning of the most grueling and exhilarating four hours of Elara’s life. She played the song again. And again. And again. Alistair was a relentless, obsessive conductor. He didn't offer praise, only sharp, insightful, and often frustratingly abstract commands. "No. The downstroke has to have more weight. Imagine your pick is a hammer. Not faster. Heavier." She’d play it again. "That's better, but now you're thinking about your hand, and you've lost the lyric. Mean it. Every word. You're accusing someone. Look at me and accuse me." She would sing the line directly to him, her voice shaking with a mixture of performance and genuine intimidation. "Good," he'd grunt. "Now do that without shouting. Make it a threat, not a tantrum." He made her deconstruct the song to its barest components. He had her play just the main riff for twenty minutes straight, forcing her to feel its rhythmic core, its groove. He made her sing the chorus a cappella, listening for the exact point where her breath control faltered.

She was exhausted, frustrated, and stripped raw. Everything she thought she knew about her own song was being questioned. She was used to emotion being the wellspring, the raw fuel that she just needed to channel. Alistair was teaching her that emotion was a volatile substance that needed to be handled with a craftsman’s precision. It wasn't enough to feel it; you had to know how to shape it, how to dose it out, how to make the listener feel it even more powerfully than you did. By late afternoon, the light slanting through the barn windows had turned a deep, dusty gold. Elara's fingers were sore, her voice was ragged, and her brain felt like over-stretched elastic. "One more time," Alistair said, his voice softer now. "From the top. Don't think. Just play."

Something had shifted. Exhaustion had stripped away her self-consciousness, her desire to impress. She was too tired to try. She just played. The riff began, slower than before, heavier. Her foot tapped out a steady, unhurried rhythm on the rug. The beat was a solid, menacing presence. Her voice came in, low and controlled, each word landing with deliberate weight. When she reached the pre-chorus, she didn't rush. She leaned back, letting the silence between the lines stretch, building an unnerving tension. And when the chorus finally hit, it was explosive. It wasn't just loud; it was immense, a wave of sound and fury breaking over the silence that had preceded it. It was the song she had heard in her head but had never been able to fully articulate.

She finished, her hand falling away from the strings, the final chord a perfect, resonant decay in the warm air. She looked up at Alistair. For the first time, a slow, grudging smile spread across his lined face. It transformed him, making him look less like a fearsome critic and more like a proud, eccentric mentor. "There it is," he said quietly, his voice full of satisfaction. "Now it has a heartbeat. Now it breathes." He stood up and stretched, a series of cracks echoing in the quiet room. "Alright. That's a song. We can record that. That’s enough for today." He ambled towards the door of the barn. "Dinner is at seven. There's a room for you in the house. Second door on the left upstairs. Don't be late." He walked out, leaving Elara alone on the stool in the center of the vast, quiet barn. She sat there for a long time, her aching fingers resting on the strings of her Maton, the ghost of the song she had just discovered still humming in the air around her. The silence that followed was no longer a void of pressure or a test of her nerve. It was the sound of possibility. The real work had just begun, and it was going to be harder and more wonderful than she could have ever imagined.The walk from the barn to the house was a journey through a gathering dusk. Elara’s body felt like a guitar string that had been tuned too high for too long, humming with a painful, exhausted vibration. Every muscle ached, her fingers throbbed, and her throat felt scraped raw, but beneath the physical depletion was a current of pure, crystalline energy. She had been broken down and rebuilt in the space of an afternoon, and the process had left her feeling more solid, more real, than she had ever felt before. The air had grown cool and sharp, carrying the scent of woodsmoke from the chimney and the rich, damp smell of the forest floor. Crickets had begun their insistent, nightly chorus, a sound that was so different from the distant, mournful sirens of the city. This place was alive, and for the first time in weeks, she felt truly, fully alive within it.

Her room was, as Alistair had said, the second door on the left. It was simple, unadorned, and deeply comforting. A comfortable-looking double bed was covered with a patchwork quilt, and a comfortable old armchair was tucked into a corner next to a tall, overflowing bookshelf. The wide, timber floorboards creaked under her feet, and the whole room smelled faintly of old books and beeswax. There was no television, no abstract art, no polished concrete. A single window looked out not onto a graffitied laneway, but into the deep, encroaching darkness of the bush, where the ghostly white trunks of gum trees were just visible in the fading light. It was the antithesis of the Surry Hills apartment. That had been a space designed to be looked at; this was a room designed to be lived in. It was a place to rest, to read, to think. It was a haven.

She splashed cold water on her face in the small, adjoining bathroom and changed out of her sweaty t-shirt, her movements slow and deliberate. When she came downstairs, drawn by the smell of garlic and tomatoes, she found Alistair in a large, warm, cluttered kitchen that was clearly the heart of the house. He was standing at an old, cast-iron stove, stirring a large pot of pasta sauce, a glass of red wine resting on the counter beside him. He moved with the same unhurried economy he’d shown in the studio, a man completely at home in his element. He gestured with a wooden spoon towards the heavy timber dining table. "Pour yourself a glass," he said, without looking up. "The bottle's open. Don't use the fancy glasses, they're a pain to wash."

They ate at the big wooden table, the rich, simple pasta a balm to her exhausted body. For a long time, they were quiet, the only sounds the clinking of forks against plates and the chirping of the crickets outside. Elara was content to let the silence sit, too tired and too intimidated to try and force conversation. It was Alistair who finally broke it, his voice thoughtful. "The demos Marcus sent," he began, swirling the wine in his glass. "There's one in there you called 'Horcrux'. The six-minute one about the old guitar." He took a sip of wine, his bright eyes watching her over the rim of the glass. "It's a beautiful piece of writing. The central metaphor is a bit on the nose for a literature nerd, but the emotion is pure. Marcus thinks it’s self-indulgent. Marcus has tin ears when it comes to anything that can’t be a ringtone."

The casual way he spoke, the easy dismissal of Marcus Thorne’s opinion, was a revelation. Alistair wasn’t his employee; he was a collaborator, an equal, and in this domain, a sovereign power. "He’s wrong," Alistair continued. "Its length is its strength. It’s a journey. But you’re hiding in the fingerpicking. It's intricate because you’re afraid to let the chords just ring out and let your voice carry the weight. We’re going to have to strip that one back to the studs. Find the foundation before we put the pretty decorations on." He said it so matter-of-factly, this casual deconstruction of one of her most personal songs, but there was no malice in it. It was the diagnosis of a master craftsman who saw the potential in a piece of raw timber. It was a sign of respect.

Over the course of the meal, he talked. He didn't ask her about her 'journey' or her 'brand'. He asked her what she listened to, not for influence-spotting, but out of genuine curiosity. He talked about sound, about the way a microphone placed a foot further away from a guitar could change the entire emotional tenor of a recording. He told a story about an artist he once worked with who could only sing the perfect take at three in the morning, fueled by lukewarm tea and a specific kind of biscuit. He spoke of music not as an industry, but as a living, breathing thing—something that had to be coaxed, wrestled with, and respected. He spoke of honesty in performance, of the difference between a technically perfect vocal and a vocal that carried a piece of the singer’s soul. "Any trained singer can hit the notes," he said, gesturing with his fork. "But very few can make you believe them. That’s the trick. Making the listener believe you have no choice but to sing this song, right now. That's what we're looking for. The unavoidable truth of it."

Elara listened, absorbing everything. She was a student, an apprentice, in the presence of a master. This was the part of the music world she had never dared to dream of, the part that had nothing to do with fame or celebrity and everything to do with the craft itself. This was not a business transaction; it was an act of creation. As he cleared the plates, she found her voice. "What you said before," she began, "about the quiet being where the power is. I've never thought about it like that. I always thought the silence was just… the empty part." Alistair paused by the sink, looking back at her. "The silence is the frame," he said. "It's what makes you notice the picture. Never be afraid of the empty part, Elara. That’s where the listener does their work. That's where the song gets inside them."

Later that night, back in the quiet sanctuary of her room, Elara sat in the old armchair, the comforting weight of the patchwork quilt over her lap. She was bone-tired, but her mind was electric, replaying every moment of the day, every piece of hard-won wisdom. She looked at the neat stack of notebooks on the bedside table, which held the other nine songs she had bled onto the page. Tomorrow, she would have to take another one—'Horcrux', maybe, or the quiet, lonely 'City of Strangers'—into that barn and offer it up for sacrifice. She would have to endure the same relentless process of deconstruction and rebuilding. The prospect was daunting, terrifying even. But for the first time, it wasn't fear she felt. It was a deep, thrilling, and profound sense of anticipation. They weren’t just a deadline to be met anymore. They were a curriculum. She was finally in the right school.
