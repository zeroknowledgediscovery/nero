The thought of David made him feel nauseous. He pictured the man’s confident, academic smile and the easy way he’d placed his hand on Liam’s back, and the memory was spoiled, tarnished by the knowledge of what it had cost. He had preened under that attention, basking in the ‘luminous’ glow that Malcolm had been forced to engineer. He pulled his phone from his pocket, his fingers clumsy. He found David’s number and stared at it. He was supposed to see him tomorrow. The thought was impossible. It would be like inviting a guest to admire the beautiful wallpaper in a house that was burning down around him. He pressed the call button.

David answered on the second ring, his voice warm and pleased. “Liam. I was just thinking about you.” Liam closed his eyes. He had to go. Now.

Malcolm sat in the dark of his own living room, the toolbag still by his feet like a loyal, defeated dog. He hadn't turned on a single light. He didn’t deserve light. He deserved this grey, featureless twilight, the colour of shame. The scene in the lane replayed in his head on a relentless loop, each viewing more excruciating than the last. He saw the shock on Liam’s face, the way his easy, open expression had curdled into hurt and confusion. Malcolm had wanted to be seen, and in his anger, he had forced Liam to look at the ugliest, most painful part of him. He had exposed the live wire not for connection, but as a weapon. He had wanted to hurt Liam, and the bitterest truth of all was that he had succeeded. The victory tasted like ash.

He should have just kept his mouth shut. He should have endured the dinner party with David, done the job, and retreated to nurse his wounds in private, the way he always had. He was good at that. It was a skill he had perfected over years of silent proximity. But something had snapped. The casual intimacy between Liam and David, the shared glance, the hand on the back, the appropriation of his own pain into their romantic vocabulary—it had been too much. There was only one person in the world who would come to his door like that. A wave of pure, cold dread washed over him. He had broken it, and now Liam was here to show him the pieces.

Liam stood on his doorstep, soaked to the skin, his hair plastered to his forehead, his face pale and stricken in the orange glow of the streetlamp. He looked like a survivor of a shipwreck. Please just open the door.” Malcolm’s hand went to the lock, his fingers trembling. This was it. The final, catastrophic short-circuit. He pushed himself off the counter and walked over to the kettle, flipping the switch. He needed another action, another task to anchor him. Malcolm was the first to pull away, a sharp, almost involuntary movement, stumbling back a step until the edge of the counter pressed into his spine. He stared at Liam, his face a canvas of raw, conflicting emotions: shock, disbelief, and a profound, bottomless fear. The dream he had lived with for years, a safe, contained thing he could manage, had just broken out of its cage and was now real, and breathing, and looking at him with earnest, repentant eyes. He turned abruptly, his back to Liam, and gripped the edge of the sink, his knuckles white. He felt Liam’s presence behind him, a warm, solid reality that he had no idea how to process. He was an electrician; he understood inputs and outputs, predictable currents, the laws of resistance. Liam watched Malcolm’s retreat, his heart aching with a tenderness so fierce it was almost painful. He didn't see it as a rejection. He saw it for what it was: the instinctive recoil of a man who had conditioned himself for a decade to expect nothing. He moved slowly, deliberately, and took the two mugs from the counter. He poured the boiling water, the rich, complex scents of smoke and bergamot rising to mix in the air, a strange and new combination. He brought the mugs to the small, bare kitchen table and set them down, pulling out a chair. He didn’t sit. He waited. After a long moment, Malcolm turned from the sink. He looked at the mug of Lapsang tea waiting for him, steam rising from it in the stark kitchen light. He looked at Liam, standing there, patient and waiting. Years of self-preservation had taught him that hope was a faulty component, a weak point in any system destined to fail. He took a sip of the tea. It was too strong. Liam had used too much. Malcolm stared at Liam’s face, at the raw, open vulnerability there. A willingness to learn. It wasn’t a declaration of undying love. It wasn’t a promise of a perfect future. It was a practical, humble offering. It was a starting point. It was a problem to be solved, together. For the first time, a flicker of something other than pain or fear sparked in Malcolm’s chest. It was a low-wattage, tentative glow, but it was there. He thought of the messy, unearthed wires sparking in the rain. Maybe the point wasn’t to insulate them, to bury them, to make them safe. Maybe the point was to stand in the rain, together, and figure out how to build a shelter. He picked up his mug of too-strong tea and took another slow sip, the smoky flavour coating his tongue. He gave a single, almost imperceptible nod. It wasn't forgiveness. It wasn't acceptance. It was a beginning. It was a flicker of a light in a room that had been dark for a very, very long time.The nod was a seismic event in a world of silence. For a long moment, neither of them moved, suspended in the fragile new atmosphere they had created. The kitchen, with its stark lines and unforgiving light, seemed to hold its breath. It was Liam who finally broke the spell, his movement slow and careful, as if trying not to startle a rare and timid creature. He pushed his chair back from the table, the scrape of its legs on the floor sounding unnaturally loud. He had pushed, and Malcolm had not pushed back. He had opened the door to a world of pain and complexity, and Malcolm had, astonishingly, let him in. Now, he had to prove he knew how to behave in this new, sacred space. “I should go,” Liam said, his voice quiet. He didn't want to leave. He wanted to stay in this too-clean kitchen for the rest of his life, memorising the arrangement of the spice jars and the specific way the fluorescent light caught the lines around Malcolm’s eyes. He needed time for his internal systems to recalibrate after a catastrophic, system-wide surge. “You need… to process. And I need to go home and throw out every book I own on romantic heroes, because they’re all wrong.” He attempted a small, watery smile, a flicker of their old dynamic, but it felt thin and inadequate. He stood, picking up his own mug and Malcolm’s, and carried them to the sink. He rinsed them with a careful, deliberate slowness, another small act of service, a clumsy reversal of a thousand such acts Malcolm had performed for him.

Malcolm watched him, his hands still wrapped around his own cooling mug. He watched Liam rinse the cups, a simple, domestic action that felt utterly alien and earth-shattering in its context. He hadn’t agreed to anything. He had nodded. But Liam was already changing his behaviour, moderating his presence, anticipating a need—Malcolm’s need for space—that Malcolm hadn’t even articulated. Liam turned from the sink, his hands empty, and looked at Malcolm, a question in his eyes. He was waiting for a dismissal, an order to leave. Malcolm simply gave another small, stiff nod, his consent for Liam to depart. It was the most he could manage.

If that’s okay.” He wasn’t asking for permission to text; he was asking if he was still allowed to occupy any space at all in Malcolm’s life. Malcolm looked down at the table, at the dark ring of condensation his mug had left on the polished wood. A flaw. A mark. He traced it with his finger. “Okay,” he said, the single word a monumental concession. It felt like the epicentre of an earthquake, everything still standing but fundamentally, irrevocably altered.

The following day, Malcolm went to work. It was the only thing he knew how to do. He drove the van through the Norwich lanes, the Ford Transit bucking over the cobbles, but the familiar smells and sounds offered no comfort. They were ghosts of a life that had ended last night. He was installing a new consumer unit in a draughty Victorian terrace, his hands moving with their usual deep-seated competence, stripping wires, tightening terminals, labelling circuits. Yet his mind was absent, replaying every moment from the night before on an endless, looping tape. Liam’s rain-soaked desperation. The bitter, poisonous words he himself had spat out. The kiss. He kept waiting for the shame to hit, or the regret, or the familiar, dull ache of his hopeless affection. But what he felt instead was a terrifying, high-frequency hum of pure uncertainty. When it came time to fix the first long shelf to the wall, they had to work in concert. Malcolm drove the screw home, the drill’s sharp cry tearing through the quiet. He drove in three more, his movements efficient and certain. Then he stepped back. A new rhythm established itself between them, a quiet choreography of shared tasks. Malcolm would make the cut with a handsaw, his movements economical and certain, and Liam would be there to take the finished piece, brushing the sawdust from its edge with a reverence that was both touching and slightly absurd. During the fitting of the third shelf, as Liam was holding a bracket steady against the wall, Malcolm leaned in to mark the drill holes with his pencil. Their heads were close, their arms almost touching. The scent of sawdust and sweat and the faint, clean smell of Malcolm’s skin filled the small space between them. He was so used to seeing this man in motion, a whirlwind of quiet competence, that he had rarely seen him this close, this still. He was beautiful, not in the charming, easy way of a book hero, but in a deeper, more fundamental way, like a well-made tool or a perfectly engineered bridge. He was beautiful in his integrity.

“Where did you learn all this?” Liam asked, his voice a low murmur, careful not to break the spell of their shared work. “Your dad?” The question was an intrusion, a clumsy attempt to access a part of Malcolm’s life that had always been a locked room. People whose worlds literally fall apart because they discover the life they’re living is a fake, a construct.” He stopped, a sudden, sharp awareness dawning on his face as he realised what he was saying, and to whom. The parallel was too close, too raw. “I’m sorry,” he said quickly. “That’s probably not the best topic…”

“No,” Malcolm said, his voice quiet but firm, cutting him off. “It’s okay.” He looked at the half-eaten curry on his saucer. *People whose worlds fall apart because they discover the life they’re living is a fake.* He had not been living a fake life. He had been living a real life, just a hidden one. But Liam had. He had been living in a world where Malcolm was just his quiet, dependable friend. And that world had just been proven to be a construct. And it had fallen apart. Malcolm looked up and met Liam’s worried, apologetic eyes. For the first time, he felt a flicker of empathy for Liam’s side of the cataclysm. It must have been terrifying for him, too. In that moment of shared, unspoken understanding, a tiny, fragile bridge was built across the chasm between them. Malcolm gave a small, almost imperceptible shrug. “Sounds complicated,” he said. And Liam laughed. It wasn't his big, booming laugh from before. It was a quiet, relieved, breathless sound, but it was genuine. And Malcolm found himself smiling, a small, stiff, unpracticed smile, but a real one. The sound of their shared, quiet laughter echoed for a moment in the bookshop, a strange and wonderful new sound in the dusty air.The shared laugh, a quiet and fragile sound, faded into the book-lined silence of the shop, leaving a warmth in its wake. They finished their meal, the initial awkwardness having softened into a companionable quiet. It was Malcolm who broke the spell, beginning the small, practical ritual of cleaning up. He gathered the empty foil cartons, flattening them with a deliberate, precise movement before placing them neatly into the paper bag. Liam, watching him, understood this was not a prelude to a hasty departure, but simply the next logical step in a process. He gathered the newspaper they had used as a tablecloth, folding the greasy sheets inward with a care that was entirely new to him. Together, without speaking, they restored the small patch of floor to its previous state of chaotic order.

When it was done, the reason for Malcolm staying was officially over. The curry had been eaten, the mess cleared away. The old script would dictate that Malcolm now stand, give a curt nod, and leave. Liam felt his own anxiety begin to rise, a fear that the fragile truce would dissolve and he would be left alone again in the silence. But before he could say anything, Malcolm, still sitting with his back against the wall of history books, spoke. “So,” he said, his voice a low rumble. “What do you do with all these? When they’re built.” He gestured with his chin towards the new, gleaming shelves in the back room. The question was simple, practical, and it was pointedly about the future. It was an expression of interest that went beyond the immediate task, and it was the most hopeful sound Liam had ever heard.

He felt a wave of relief so profound it almost made him dizzy. “I catalogue them,” he said, eager to answer, to draw Malcolm further into his world. “They all get a code. I have a system. It’s… a bit esoteric.” He gave a small, self-deprecating smile. “I categorise them not just by author or subject, but by… I don’t know, by the kind of question they’re asking. So all the books about doomed love affairs might end up next to books about failed polar expeditions. It’s a thematic cartography, I suppose. It drives collectors mad, but I find it makes for more interesting browsing. People find things they weren’t looking for.”

Malcolm listened, his expression thoughtful. He considered the idea, not as a piece of charming eccentricity, but as a system, a set of rules. “A thematic cartography,” he repeated quietly, testing the shape of the words. “So you’re wiring them together. Based on a shared current.” Liam stared at him. Malcolm had taken his flighty, literary concept and translated it instantly into his own language, the language of circuits and connections. He had not only listened; he had understood it on a fundamental, structural level. “Yes,” Liam breathed, a sense of wonder blooming in his chest. “That’s exactly it. A shared current.” They looked at each other across the small space, and the simple act of being so perfectly, unexpectedly understood felt more intimate than the kiss had.

It was Malcolm who eventually stood, his body unfolding with a familiar stiffness. The moment was over, and it was time for him to go. This time, Liam didn’t feel the familiar panic. He stood as well, brushing dust from his trousers. “Thank you,” he said, his voice quiet and sincere. “For staying. For the… consultation.” Malcolm just nodded, picking up his work jacket from the pile of biographies. He walked towards the door, and Liam followed, his heart beating a slow, steady rhythm. At the door, Malcolm paused, his hand on the latch. He turned, his face half in shadow. “The varnish will be ready for a second coat tomorrow afternoon,” he said. It wasn’t a question. It was a statement of intent. “Text me when you get in. I’ll come by after my job in the Golden Triangle.” He didn’t wait for a reply. He simply opened the door, and the little brass bell gave a soft, final jingle as he stepped out into the night.

The next day, Liam opened the shop with a sense of purpose he hadn’t felt in years. The chaos of the cardboard boxes was no longer a source of stress but a project waiting to be completed. The first thing he did was walk to the back room and touch the shelves. He ran his fingers along the top, then pressed a thumb into a back corner, just as Malcolm had instructed. The surface was hard, smooth, no longer tacky. It was ready. He felt a ridiculous surge of pride. He pulled out his phone, his fingers hovering over the screen. The text he sent was simple, stripped of his usual literary flair. *‘Morning. The shelves are dry. Ready when you are.’* He added no ‘x’, no unnecessary flourish. It was a report. A piece of data. He hit send and was surprised by the immediate buzz of a reply. *‘3.30.’* The brevity of it was comforting, a small island of Malcolm’s familiar, dependable certainty in a sea of new emotions.

Malcolm arrived at exactly 3:30. He let himself in, the bell a familiar announcement, and found Liam not waiting nervously, but working. He was up on a ladder, a book in his hand, trying to decide where on the existing, overstuffed shelves he could make a bit of room. He was actively trying to solve his own problem. When he saw Malcolm, he smiled, a real smile this time, open and warm and without a trace of the desperate anxiety from the day before. “Hi,” he said, climbing down. “You’re punctual.” “I’m an electrician,” Malcolm replied, the corner of his own mouth twitching. It was the ghost of their old banter, but its foundations were entirely new. They got to work on the second coat, the process familiar now, the rhythm easier to find. Liam didn’t drip the varnish this time. His strokes were more confident, more even. He asked Malcolm about his day, and Malcolm, instead of shrugging, gave him a brief, technical summary of a faulty wiring loom he’d had to replace in a café kitchen. He was sharing the details of his life, the small, unglamorous facts of his day, and Liam listened to the description of junction boxes and ring circuits as if it were the most fascinating story he had ever heard.

When the second coat was finished, the shelves had a deep, professional lustre, the grain of the wood glowing with an inner light. They stood back, a sense of finality and accomplishment settling in the quiet room. The project was complete. The tool they had used to rebuild their connection was now finished. A new, uncertain silence descended. “So,” Liam said, his voice a little too loud in the quiet. “Now for the books.” He looked at the mountains of cardboard, then at the beautiful, empty shelves. “It’ll take me a week to sort through all this.” He looked at Malcolm, his expression open and hopeful. “I don’t suppose your consultancy extends to heavy lifting and speculative literary theory?”

It was a direct request, but it was couched in humour, the now-familiar escape clause built into it. He was asking Malcolm not to fix something, but to participate in his world, in the esoteric, thematic cartography of his own mind. He was asking him to stay and help him fill the structure they had built together. Malcolm looked at the empty shelves, then at the chaotic piles of books, a physical manifestation of Liam’s sprawling, wonderful mind. He thought of his own empty, silent house. He thought of the single, controlled circuit of his life. And he thought of the new, complicated, and dangerously alive current that was humming between him and the man standing next to him. A fire you build, and then must tend. It would be a lot of work. It would be messy. It would be complicated. He looked at Liam, who was holding his breath, waiting for the verdict. “I’m not reading any of them,” Malcolm said, his voice a low grumble. “But I’ll lift the boxes.”The agreement hung in the air between them, a fragile and extraordinary thing. And so began the great sorting. The following days fell into a new, unspoken rhythm, a routine built from the rubble of their old one. Malcolm would finish his last job, his van smelling of dust and ozone and the day’s labour, and he would drive to Elm Hill. The jingle of the bell was no longer an alarm but a signal, the start of the second, stranger part of his day. He would enter the shop to find Liam already amidst the chaos, and they would work for hours in the quiet, dusty light, surrounded by the silent, waiting stories.

It was a complete inversion of their usual dynamic. Here, in the world of paper and ink, Liam was the master craftsman and Malcolm was the apprentice, the labourer. His job was simple: to lift, to carry, to open the heavy cardboard boxes and reveal the treasures within. He would slice the tape with his utility knife, the sound a sharp rip in the quiet, and then step back as Liam knelt, his face alight with a reverent excitement, like a child on Christmas morning. Malcolm would watch as Liam’s long, deft fingers would sift through the contents, a low murmur of commentary issuing from him as he did. “Ah, a first edition of ‘Canticle for Leibowitz’, look at that cover art… Oh, and the entire Vorkosigan Saga, the professor had good taste.”

At first, Liam was hesitant to direct him, his requests framed as apologetic questions. “Could you possibly move this stack over there? Sorry, I know it’s heavy.” But Malcolm, in his own gruff, silent way, made it clear that he was there to work. He would simply lift the designated stack and move it, his movements economical and powerful, and then wait, his expression patient, for the next instruction. Slowly, Liam’s confidence grew. He began to direct the flow of books with a quiet authority, a general deploying his troops. “Okay, this whole box is Golden Age pulp. That can go against the far wall for now. The box underneath it should be the New Wave stuff, Ballard, Brunner, Le Guin… that’s what we’ll start with for the new shelves.”

Malcolm worked without complaint, his muscles aching with the unfamiliar strain of lifting dead weight instead of pulling cable. He was a silent observer of Liam’s esoteric filing system. He watched as a worn paperback of Ursula K. Le Guin’s ‘The Dispossessed’ was placed on a shelf not with other science fiction, but next to a dense political treatise on anarchism and a slim volume of Taoist poetry. He saw a battered copy of ‘Frankenstein’ nestled between a medical ethics textbook and a biography of a Romantic poet. To Malcolm, a man who organised his van by function and his workshop by tool size, it was a system of pure, incomprehensible madness. He said nothing for two full days, simply lifting and placing as directed, his face a mask of stoic neutrality.

On the third day, as he was stacking a heavy pile of books Liam had handed him, he finally broke his silence. “This one,” he said, his voice a low rumble. He tapped the top book, a thick tome with a stark, graphic cover depicting a stylized whale. “‘Moby Dick’. You’ve put it with a book about automotive engineering and another one about obsession in serial killers.” The question was unadorned, a simple request for data. Liam, who had been engrossed in a box of Philip K. Dick paperbacks, looked up, a slow smile spreading across his face. He had been waiting for this. It was the first time Malcolm had questioned the system, the first sign that he was not just observing, but engaging.

“Exactly,” Liam said, his voice full of a teacher’s enthusiasm. “They’re not about what they appear to be about. ‘Moby Dick’ isn’t a book about whaling. It’s a book about a man whose relentless, fanatical pursuit of a single goal destroys him, his crew, and his ship. It’s about the catastrophic failure of a complex system due to the madness of its operator.” He gestured to the other books. “The engineering manual details the principles of catastrophic metal fatigue. And the psychopathology text is about the patterns of obsessive-compulsive behaviour. They’re all talking about the same thing, Mal. They’re all wired to the same circuit. The catastrophic consequences of a single, unchecked obsession.” He held Malcolm’s gaze, his meaning clear and unambiguous. He was talking about the books. And he was talking about them.

Malcolm stared at him, the heavy pile of books suddenly feeling weightless in his arms. He looked down at the cover of ‘Moby Dick’, then back at Liam’s earnest, intelligent face. He didn’t smile, but a profound, internal shift occurred. He was beginning to understand the language. It wasn’t madness. It was a different kind of logic, a different kind of schematic, one based on metaphor and shared currents of meaning. He gave a single, slow nod of comprehension and placed the books carefully on the shelf where Liam had indicated, the thud of them landing a soft, final sound of acceptance.

Their work became a conversation, spoken and unspoken. Malcolm would hold up a book, his eyebrow raised in a silent query, and Liam would provide the logic for its placement. “That one’s about memory and loss, so it goes with the neurology textbooks and the elegies.” They worked in close quarters, their shoulders brushing as they reached for the same shelf, their hands meeting as one passed a book to the other. Each accidental touch was a small, quiet spark, a confirmation of the current that now flowed freely and acknowledged between them. Malcolm found himself less focused on the physical labour and more on the quiet rhythm of their shared task, on the low murmur of Liam’s voice as he rediscovered old favourites, on the way the dust motes danced in the shafts of late-afternoon sun that pierced the gloom of the shop. He was tending the fire. He was learning how to keep it burning.One evening, near the end of the week, they opened the last box. It was smaller than the others, made of a sturdier, darker cardboard, and it was marked with a single, spidery word in the professor’s handwriting: ‘Ephemera’. Inside, there were no books. Instead, it was a dragon’s hoard of a different sort: bundles of letters tied with faded ribbon, loose photographs with curled edges, several leather-bound diaries, and a handful of objects that seemed to have no value but memory—a pressed flower, a ticket stub from a 1970s rock concert, a small, smooth river stone. It was the detritus of a life, the physical evidence of a subjective reality. Liam lifted the items out with a reverence that was different from the kind he showed to books. This felt more sacred, more private. He untied one of the bundles of letters and read a few lines from the topmost one, his lips moving silently.

Malcolm stood back, feeling like an intruder. This was not about shelving or logistics. This was archaeology of the soul, and he had no tools for this kind of excavation. He busied himself with sweeping up a fine layer of dust in the corner, a task that didn’t need doing, just to give his hands something to occupy them. He was aware of the silence from Liam, a silence that was not productive or companionable, but heavy with contemplation. After a long moment, Liam spoke, his voice hushed. “They’re from his wife. She died about ten years ago. They’re love letters. From when they were first courting, at university.” He carefully placed the letter back on the pile and picked up a small, black-and-white photograph. “Look.” He held it out to Malcolm.

Reluctantly, Malcolm crossed the small space and took the photograph. It showed a young, impossibly handsome version of the professor, his hair thick and dark, his expression serious. Beside him stood a young woman with a bright, intelligent face and a wide, laughing mouth. They were standing on a beach, squinting against a sun Malcolm couldn’t see, their arms linked. It was a perfect, captured moment of a life about to begin. He looked from the photograph to the bundles of letters, to the diaries filled with a lifetime of thoughts, and for the first time, he felt the true weight of what they were handling. These weren’t just objects. They were a life. He handed the photo back to Liam. “A long time ago,” he said, the observation feeling clumsy and inadequate.

“Seventy years of a life, packed into a single box,” Liam murmured, his gaze distant. He sifted through the photos, finding another one of the couple, much older this time, sitting in a garden, the professor’s arm resting on the back of his wife’s chair. Her hair was white, but her smile was the same. “He told me once,” Liam continued, his voice soft, “that he knew he loved her the first day he met her, in a lecture on metaphysical poetry. But he was too shy to say anything. He waited a whole year before he asked her for a cup of tea. A whole year of watching her from across a lecture hall, terrified.” Liam looked up from the photograph, his eyes meeting Malcolm’s in the dim, dusty light. The parallel was unspoken, a shimmering, charged field between them. A whole year. Malcolm had waited a decade.

The energy that had carried them through the physical work of the week seemed to drain out of the room, replaced by a profound, shared weariness. The job was done. The shelves were filled, the boxes were empty, save for this one last container of memories. Liam carefully placed the photographs and letters back in the box and slid the lid on, as if putting a life to rest. He didn't get up. He just sat on the floor, surrounded by the newly ordered shelves, looking small and suddenly very tired. Malcolm felt a powerful, unfamiliar urge. It was not the urge to fix, or to build, or to solve a problem. It was the urge to offer comfort. He didn’t know how. The vocabulary for it did not exist in his personal lexicon. So he did the only thing he could think of. He sat down on the floor, a few feet away from Liam, his back against a newly filled shelf of science fiction, and just stayed.

They sat in a profound, unbroken silence for a long time, the quiet of the shop settling around them like a blanket. The last of the daylight had faded from the high windows, and the only light came from a single anglepoise lamp on the counter, casting long, distorted shadows that made the room feel like a deep, underwater cavern. Liam finally stirred, drawing his knees up to his chest and wrapping his arms around them. “It’s a lot, isn’t it?” he said, his voice quiet. “All of it. A life. And then it just… ends. And all that’s left is a box of letters and someone else’s memory of the way you laughed.” Malcolm didn’t have an answer for that. He had no comforting platitudes, no grand philosophy to offer. He just nodded, acknowledging the truth of it. “Yeah,” he said.

Liam let out a long, slow sigh, a sound of pure emotional exhaustion. Then, with a movement that was so slow and tentative it was almost imperceptible, he uncurled himself and shifted on the hard wooden floor, closing the small gap between them. Malcolm’s entire body went rigid, every muscle tensing in preparation for a contact he had craved and dreaded his entire life. He braced himself, expecting a hand on his arm, a touch that would be another test, another question. But Liam didn’t touch him. He simply leaned, slowly, deliberately, until his head came to rest on Malcolm’s shoulder. The contact was shocking in its simplicity, its gentleness. It was not a passionate gesture, or a romantic one. It was an act of pure, bone-deep weariness and trust. He was not asking for a response. He was just seeking a solid place to rest. Malcolm sat perfectly still, every nerve ending screaming, the weight of Liam’s head on his shoulder a physical pressure that felt both impossibly heavy and utterly right. He could feel the soft fabric of Liam’s jumper against his neck, could smell the faint scent of tea and dust and Liam himself. He waited for the panic, for the instinct to flee, for the circuit breaker in his soul to trip. It didn’t. After a long, frozen moment, he took a slow, shuddering breath, and with an act of will that was the bravest of his life, he relaxed. He let the tension drain from his own shoulders and leaned his own head, just slightly, against Liam’s. He had found the solid part. And he was strong enough to bear the weight.
