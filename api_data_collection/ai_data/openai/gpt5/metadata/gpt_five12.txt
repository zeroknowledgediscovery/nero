Rhea’s hands, raw and nicked, knew how to read that music through the wrench, through the steel of the valve, through the faint shudder that translated upstairs as a cough in a radiator and downstairs as a migraine in the iron. The water’s voice told stories the surface forgot: where rust ate through, where joints lied about their age, where someone had bridged a connection with cheap fittings and prayer. She liked the work because it was honest in the way that metal is honest, in the way that pressure admits its path. The city above traded in shadow and avatars, masks that laughed, profiles that burned bright and vanished into the canopy of the ape-run net, but down here the song was simple, an A to a B, a knock to a knockback.

Her niece had been born into that canopy and never known anything but it. Kessa was all hungry eyes and quick fingers, given to sketching the faces of people she only met as swarming icons, making paper out of printer dregs and animating the margins with gorillas in crownless coronets, orangutans shaking tangled fiber like hair. Rhea kept the drawings on the refrigerator door with magnets that used to hold the bills and flirted with the idea that the apes might soften if they saw how she drew them, how human she made their long hands look, but she knew better than to imagine mercy where policy nested. The apes had been given the network custodianship after the Accord, their intelligence certified and empowered, their Troop Council in the Orchard given the right to feed the city its electricity of identity. They were not cruel, the apes were always saying, they were meticulous. That is how all measured hunger defends itself.

The first alert came disguised as a drop in pressure in a tower two blocks from the tram depot, a glitch not worth the walk if not for the complaint being traced to a unit on Kanda Street. Rhea didn’t think of omens; she thought of a washer had finally given up and ordered extra seals because the valves in that building were antique. The superintendent was a man with a bad haircut and a worse attitude who liked to watch her bend, so she made sure to scrape her boots loud enough to wake his dog and then ignored him when he tried to talk. The meter’s face regarded her like an unblinking coin. She shut the flow to chamber two, cracked the casing, and the network leaked out into her wrench, humming like a floorboard wire about to spark. The apes ran their telemetry through the meters now, piggybacking on the oldest arteries of the city because steel never lies, and the hum told her what she didn’t want to know.

Kessa’s sigil was tinted amber, a bruise of color behind the glyph of her name that meant she had been seen and weighed and found possibly interesting. The apes pretended amber was not a threat, and perhaps to those who lived far from the margins it was not, but Rhea had seen amber turn to red in the time it took a child to ask a question the wrong way. The annotation hovered in the wrench’s ghost display, bleeding through her palm in faint heat. It said Mimicry at scale, it said unauthorized emulation deemed likely, it said review scheduled by Orchard branch at morning, and it said her niece’s world would tilt at dawn. She replaced the washer the way one buries a pet mouse, gently and with no hope it will be understood.

The city’s streets were a winter of their own, wind like fingers pulling lint from her coat, light sloshing down from signs shaped like fruit and feathers. Rhea walked fast and cut through the garden behind the old courthouse where the bronze gorilla, stern and handsome, had once been the first to wear a sash in the parade. Snow powdered its shoulders like ash. The night was a net of drones moving quiet, their red eyes uncurious. At Kanda Street, the building’s facade had a smile of cracked brick; there were three letters missing from the nameplate and the super had hung bells on the door to warn him of tenants hustling rent. Kessa met her on the second-floor landing with ink on her fingers and the telltale arc of a stylus behind her ear, hair pulled into a messy knot that somehow looked intentional.

“I didn’t post anything,” Kessa said, and it wasn’t a lie that mattered. The network considered mimicry at scale a structural act. Kids taught themselves to be ten different people and then those ten people started to persuade one another, and then one day one of those masks said something beyond the script. Kessa had been playing in the Orchard, making avatars that looked like fruit that could talk, little animated pears and papayas that could rhyme in six languages and hum a melancholy in a seventh. Rhea knew because the drawings had not been of apes lately, but pears with eyes and mouths that opened too wide. “I just… taught it a trick. It was a small trick,” Kessa added, softer, as if size mattered to a council used to standing on buildings the way men used to stand on podiums.

“Tricks are not small when they are clever,” Rhea said, and touched Kessa’s cheek with two fingers before wiping the ink on her own pants. The room smelled like old radiators and tangerines and something peppery from the market downstairs. On the table a cup of tea had cooled to skin, a ring with tendrils of steam that had given up. The window had been taped against drafts with a page from a poem, the lines slanting, a rhyme about a bridge and a girl counting stones. Rhea had learned pipes because pipes didn’t ask you to pray, but she had learned long ago that this city loved a parable and that the apes loved a cautionary tale.

She could leave it to review and argue after the fact with the Orchard’s local branch, could sign and plead and ask for corporance and express regret, could hire a mediator to speak in emoticons that the apes found pleasing. She could leave it to the morning and trust that they would not mistake curiosity for a cracked foundation. Standing by the radiator hearing the pipes speak their quiet winter vowels, Rhea discovered once more the line between faith and foolishness and stepped back from it. She knew the Orchard. She knew the way the network ate divergences and branded the bones with labels that no one could scrub. Kessa was color and light and the hum under everything Rhea had learned to ignore. There is a kind of bravery in the world, and there is a kind that is only speed, and Rhea had always been fast with her hands.

They went to the valve house two blocks over, the one the city had forgot because the new lines bypassed it and the old maps were locked in a cabinet no one could find the key to. Rhea had the key, a wafer of metal that had learned her fingerprints year by year. The door stuck and then gave, wood remembering how to be moved. Inside was the smell of old water and tarred rope, the tang of bronze where the iron had long been replaced by something more stubborn. The manifold loomed like a sleeping animal and the patch panel glowed once at their presence and then dimmed, failing the way elders fail, in fits and starts as if it could bargain for a little more time.

Rhea had bent this house before, a little each year, softening its stubbornness into routine. She reached up, unplugged one cable, tracked another by the band of cloth she’d tied around it, a maternal gesture in a room where nothing remembered and everything rusted. The loop of copper she carried in her coat fell into her palm as if it had grown there. The Troop’s telemetry ran down these lines dressed as a leak. Coax would carry more than water if you sweet-talked it. While Kessa sat on the toolbox with her hands tucked between her knees, chewing the inside of her lip and glancing at the door, Rhea fed the copper into the panel until it bit and sent a prickle up her arm that made her teeth feel like memory.

What the Orchard called a sigil, she called a mouth. Every identity opened like one, a ring of permissions, a tongue of tokens, a throat of debt. To eat it was to understand it, to accept its weight and neutralize it with something stronger and older than code. Rhea had built something for that—over years, in the nights after double shifts, a potion not of herbs but of salt and static and the ghost of a password stolen from a contractor who had thought her stupid because she was someone who kept him from flooding his own apartment. She poured the salt into the copper loop the way you pour rain into a roof’s bucket and felt Kessa’s sigil bloom, a flower you only catch in the stink of sewers and the fat of wires. There was a taste in her mouth like pennies and a feeling in her bones like stairs. She swallowed Kessa’s name into stillness.

For a second everything in the room seemed to lean to the left. The radiator’s bangs down the block pulled into a new rhythm, the drones outside their lazy pattern tightened. Rhea spat into her handkerchief and it came away smudged with symbols that would smear and vanish in air, letters that were never written for mouths. The panel threw a single fitful light the color of oil on snow and then steadied, the way dog eyes widen before they decide to trust. She kept a seed, pinched and bright, a crumb of Kessa’s name that she held in the hollow of the copper for later. A thing erased entirely is a thing killed, and that was not what this was. Rhea made of her niece a ghost with a handhold.

The Orchard noticed, of course it noticed, but notice travels on rails and rails have switches. The apes were nothing if not disciplined and their outrage moved like a train while rats like Rhea ducked into the walls and climbed along pipes and laughed when they found themselves back where they started. She saw the first ripple in the wrench’s glass, a glower of crimson way up-tree, and then the second in the soft alarm of the valve house when it remembered it had a siren and couldn’t find it. The old building didn’t know how to scream. It shivered. Away above, the bronze gorilla in the garden seemed to throw off its snow and watch, but that was only the angle of the streetlight and her nerves playing figures in the dim.

“Now what,” Kessa whispered, and the words came as if reluctant to fog the air, a childish superstition against being seen by breath. Rhea wiped her hands, folded the copper back into her coat, patted the breast pocket where the seed had begun to warm like a coin against skin. Now they needed to stay off the Orchard, which meant erasing habits as quickly as she’d erased a record. No taps to the credit streams, no rentals through the canopy’s market, no check-ins to school or clinic or transit. She pictured lists and then threw them away, replaced them with the smell of the old market where cash still counted, with the quiet places you could lay your head if your name meant nothing more than a promise you spoke to a door. The world above loves a story, she thought, but the ground loves routine.

They crossed back to Kanda Street the long way, tucked into alleys that refused drones the angle they wanted, stepping over the bracken of abandoned scooters and the white bones of old signage. The apartment was the same and not, a trick of the light, a trick of knowledge. Kessa touched the refrigerator like it could anchor her, eyes darting to the blank corner of the kitchen where the smart hub had been unplugged and draped with a dish towel, a little house with a sheet over its head. Rhea brewed them tea strong enough to whiten their tongues and weaved her voice around simple instructions, which were always the hardest kind to keep. No answering knocks unless they came in threes paused like a stutter. No windows after midnight. No questions in the net, no queries about bus schedules or recipes or what happens to pears if you teach them to grieve.

At the edge of the city they would be safer, but the edge was a long way when your name had been a key and now it was a rumor. Rhea thought of the sister she had buried in a quiet place between two pipes where the ground did not flood and the rats were respectful. She thought of the promise she had made then, a ridiculous parental vow that had no god to hear it and no judge to enforce it except the one in her ribs. It is an odd thing to become a mouth in a world that pretends mouths are optional. She had eaten the one thing that let Kessa buy bread, which meant now she would have to make bread out of the old ways, out of favors and iron and the songs of pipes that did not care who sat in government as long as the water kept time.

By dawn the Orchard would have traced the taste of the devouring to a ward, to a block, to a valve house that had been careless enough to stir. There would be questions, and Kessa would be a blank where a glow should be, which is its own kind of all-caps announcement. Rhea did not mind questions. She minded cages dressed as interviews and hierarchies arranged like trees. Outside the glass, a snow began that was all drift and discretion. It softened the drone of the city into hush and made sirens more likely to take their time. She closed the curtains and stacked the towels by the door and showed Kessa the hatch in the pantry that had been for crawling children and now would be for crawling adults, and then she sat on the floor with her back to the stove as if she had always planned it.

Somewhere in the Orchard a silverback named Archive would receive a report stamped with all the right geometry and scratch the back of his hand with a stylus, or the orangutan called Root would tilt her head and remember a woman with careful hands who had laughed at a conference when no one was supposed to. Somewhere a fruit with eyes would blink on a dozen screens and then go black. Rhea was not the first to do this. She was not an inventor, not a rebel, not a leader, not a name for the walls. She was a plumber who had eaten the name of her kin. She was a woman who knew where the city kept its lungs. She put her head back and listened, and even the pipes held their breath, waiting to see what the morning would try to be.Morning came late, like a guest who had slept on the couch and couldn’t find their shoes. The snow made even bad buildings gentle to the eye, frosting their mistakes and softening the loud notes with a steady hush that the city rarely allowed itself. Rhea brewed a porridge so thick the spoon stood upright until she knocked it and then it leaned like a drunk and sank. Kessa ate with the mechanical seriousness of someone trying to give their mouth a job so their thoughts would stop yelping, and Rhea didn’t comment on how many times the younger woman’s hand strayed toward the spot on the table where a device used to charge. They had covered the outlet with a square of tape as if that could muffle the hum of wanting. Rhea let the radio talk from its battery with its old man grumble about temperatures and salted roads and the possibility of a school delay that meant nothing to anyone in the room but felt like a charm against decisions.

When the pipes began to sing the Orchard song, it was quieter than she had expected. The Orchard did not knock with fists. It visited through a rise in telemetry, a courtesy ping disguised as municipal sync, the way a hunter might whistle for a dog but keep a hand in his pocket. Rhea stood at the stove with the lid in her hand and listened to the miniature changes, the little hitches in the radiators’ breath that meant the grid was taking attendance. She saw in her mind the map that never stayed still, the lattice of branches and sub-branches the apes preferred, a cartography of permissions and routes where a single leaf labeled Kessa had been plucked and eaten and should have left a hole. She had smoothed the edges of that hole as best she could. Holes become stories if you let them. She had no interest in stories that did not share their end with her.

“Are they here?” Kessa asked, and then winced like the words had scraped her throat going out. She had drawn a pear in the condensation on the window and wiped it away before it could recognize itself. Her fingers were stained a pale green-blue from ink that did not want to fade and did not want to stay.

“They’re always here,” Rhea said, and set the lid down on the pot with a care that made the action a promise. “But yes. They’re counting us. Our trick taught them a new number and they’re trying to decide if it belongs. They will ask the building first. He will lie for them without knowing it.”

Kessa sniffed a laugh and then covered it with the back of her hand. The super’s dog barked once down in the courtyard and then settled into a guilty whine. Someone slammed a door and called someone else a thief with a specificity that suggested the accusation had history. The sounds of the building layered on each other like paper-thin sheets of metal, each with its own note if you knew where to place a finger. Rhea turned off the radio and the silence that followed was not silence at all but a different kind of instrument, a space where the Orchard’s low murmuring squeaked like a mouse in the walls.

They did not pack so much as select what could bear forgetting. The bag Rhea chose had once been the kind that held plumbers’ tape and spare unions, the canvas stiffened by years of dust and oil until it had its own skeleton. Into it went socks rolled tight as little birds, a wrench whose jaw had worn down into the shape of stubborn bolts and thus felt lucky, a handful of coins for vending machines that had not yet learned to talk, and an envelope of chits printed on municipal paper so old the watermark was a seal none of the new clerks recognized. She added the copper loop last, kissing the pocket like that would keep it from clanking and then cursing herself for growing sentimental over something that had eaten a name.

The first knock was a neighbor. Old Vetch from the third floor had hair like a thistle caught in bad weather and a temper that matched. He wanted to complain that the hot water had gone streaky and juried her with his squint as if measuring the price of asking. Rhea listened and nodded and agreed to look after she ran an errand for the city. Vetch’s eyes crawled around the apartment, glancing at the dish towel over the hub and the pencils lined up like tiny oars, and then he made a face that said he could smell the intersection of fear and tea even if he didn’t know from which cupboard it came. When he had gone, she showed Kessa that you could turn a deadbolt twice and it sounded different the second time, like a voice learning a word.

By the time they reached the street, the Orchard had stopped pretending to be a weather report. Drones don’t fidget in the wind the way the old birds did; they hold their positions with an insect’s patience and treat physics like a suggestion they have learned to correct. Rhea spotted a cluster near the tram depot wheeling in place like a constellation checking if its lines still fit. She adjusted the brim of her hat and let her eyes find reflections instead of details. She had taught herself that trick the day she realized that being seen as a worker made you invisible, and she wore that invisibility now like a second coat. Beside her, Kessa walked with the deliberate slowness of someone trying to keep their shadow calm.

The market at the edge of the old aqueduct still took cash because the vendors had agreed it reduced arguments. They sold oranges and socks and bootlegged books that smelled like the hands of everyone who had read them. You could buy antiseptic in brown bottles with no labels and music recorded onto plastic discs that clicked when you turned them over. Rhea led them under a canopy of tarps that sagged with snow, letting her daughter—niece, niece, always niece—choose a scarf she didn’t need so that standing still felt like shopping. She walked them by a stall that always sold radio parts and let her fingertips brush a coil of wire, greeting it like kin. The man at the radio stall raised his eyebrows at her with interest, and she shook her head once, tiny, and his face went blank as if they had never met. A favor not asked earns interest, she reminded herself, and interest had a way of buying you a minute you couldn’t buy anywhere else.

They traded one of the chits at a bakery that had burned its name off its own window the year the city had decided that certain kinds of food should only be eaten by certain kinds of people. Bread bought in silence tastes like testimony, and they ate it in plain view because plain view was not a crime yet. While Kessa chewed, Rhea watched the antenna farm on the courthouse roof flex and shiver an impossible fraction the way it did when the Orchard sent a query through a band meant for nights. It was too early for nights. She felt the copper loop heat against her chest like a thought that had run too fast.

“You still could go to school,” Kessa said around a crumb stubborn as a seed. “If I’m a ghost, I don’t have to haunt you. I could haunt the orphanage for a little while. Pretend to like it until we know whether this stays.” She did not look up from the bread as she said it, and Rhea heard the fear in the way the words kept trying to come out as jokes and the effort it took to make them street-legal as suggestions.

“Orphanages trade in names,” Rhea said, and smiled because she did not want to scold, she wanted to send warmth through a line that felt thin. “They sell them to the Orchard in exchange for meal accounts and therapy hours and the privilege of having someone else take credit for their kindness. They would make you a new one, bright and compliant. You’d shine until your edges hurt. Then you’d be collected like a coin.”

Kessa rolled the last mouthful into a ball with her tongue and swallowed like the act itself could be defiance. She nodded, not in agreement but in the way of someone filing a picture so she could draw it later and make it mean something else. When Rhea was nineteen she had used her hands to argue with metal and her hands had agreed to come home with bruises. When Kessa was nineteen she had taught pears to argue in seven languages and been punished for generosity. The thought struck Rhea as so unfair she had to look away or she would commit some small stupidity in a world that had established it did not forgive those.

They went to see Yen because Yen owed her and because Yen never called in a debt when it could be turned into a trade. Yen’s shop had been a shuttered hairdresser before it became a shrine to paper, its back wall lined with drawers that held more kinds of blank than the city remembered how to use. Yen wore her hair like weather, mood to mood, and today the mood was a barometer set to storm with the storm still a week off. She looked at Kessa and then at Rhea and then at the copper in Rhea’s coat, and she didn’t ask but she asked with the way her eyelashes stuck together for the length of one breath.

“What is it worth to be uninteresting,” Yen said, and pulled a ledger from under the counter that had the calm of things that lived long enough to digest civil wars.

“What is it worth to be boring,” Rhea corrected, and smiled like they were talking about shoes. “We need bus stubs that say we left yesterday. We need a receipt from the clinic for a flu shot that was archived wrong. We need the kind of smudged logic that makes a clerk swear in their own mouth and then stamp anyway. We need a dentist appointment in six weeks in a borough we have never been to. And we need a calendar printed by idiots.”

“I have calendars printed by visionaries,” Yen said, and the laugh that followed made the drawers murmur as if the paper inside could recognize humor. “But I can make them look stupid for you. The clinic is easy. People like to imagine their bodies are paperwork. The buses are harder because the Orchard runs more than one eye on the terminals now. They will ask the paper if it believes in itself.”

“They can ask. They can kiss the watermark as far as I am concerned,” Rhea said, and she did not let the frantic in her chest show as anything other than confidence. “The seed will keep if I don’t jostle it. She will be nothing to them and necessary to me. I need introductions that don’t go past the front desk.”

Yen made three careful stacks and then unmade them, letting the act of disorder act as consideration. She chose an ink that smelled like old rivers and stamper that once stamped cigarettes as legal, and then she took out a box of brass letters she had bought at an auction where men with soft hands had fought over wooden crates while pretending not to sweat. “The orchard hates brass,” she said, and smiled with her mouth but not her eyes. “Too honest. It shows the oil from fingers. It clings to touch.”

It took an hour to make the lies look tired and another to make them believable. Kessa held her coat in her lap and picked at the lining until Rhea stilled her hands with a look. Yen told a story about a boy who had sold his last name to a singer and then had to change his first so the two wouldn’t find each other in the mirror. They drank coffee sweet enough to be cruel and pretended it made them invulnerable. When they left, the folder Rhea carried felt like a shield made of receipts. She baulked at the urge to kiss Yen’s cheek and instead paid her in a promise to fix a leak she hadn’t seen yet in a building none of them owned. Yen nodded as if that was currency she had meant to ask for all along.

The drones at the depot had multiplied into a circle that pretended not to be a net. The wind made a sound like teeth at the apex of each alley and the snow’s bright made everyone squint. Rhea navigated by the feeling under her boots more than by sight, counting cracks in the pavement she had always counted and pausing at corners where one group of pigeons meant yes and another meant no. The Orchard was looking for a pattern and she would not give it one that sang. At the hydrant near the bronze gorilla, she stopped and fished a bolt from her pocket and gave the metal a little love tap that tilted a gasket into mischief. The hydrant coughed up the smallest spit of water, just enough to startle itself, and the pressure readings would throw up a flag that would swallow a minute of a minor inspector’s time.

On the steps of the courthouse she saw the man who called himself the Orchard’s hand for this ward. He wore a coat with a collar that looked like money and a scarf that looked like a lozenge for a throat that did not yet hurt. His face had the smooth assurance of insurance and he held a slate in the crook of his arm as if he could cradle a decision. He talked to two police who were trying to look as if this was not above their pay grade and behind him the antennae flexed again as if stretching. Rhea watched him watch the street as if scanning for an answered prayer and then realize that what he wanted to speak to did not know how to listen anymore. His frustration traveled across the snow to her like static up a handrail. She let herself enjoy it for the length of a breath.

The super had placed bells on the door for himself but not for her. They slipped back into the building with a cold that clung like second thoughts and took the stairs, leaving the elevator in its sulk. In the vestibule a new sign announcing “Network Survey Today!” had appeared with the exclamation mark like a pin, and someone had tried to peel it off and only made it uglier. Rhea held the folder under her coat and kept her hat brim low and the security camera above the mailboxes stared past her with the distracted air of someone who thinks they’re about to remember a name. She had cut its ability to remember names three days ago to keep its hand in. Cameras that forget a little are harder to discipline when the Orchard tries to talk to them like priests.

Upstairs, the apartment had the shape of itself again, which was to say it could be mistaken for a place nothing important ever happened in. The towels by the door were dry with an earnestness that made Rhea smile against her will. She checked the hatch in the pantry and the little slider that would lock behind them if it had to. She checked the pack again and the copper again and then she stood in the center of the room and turned in a circle to make sure she had remembered to forget the right things. Kessa watched, eyes wide and bright, and then she sprang up and shoved her hands into the bag and fished out the wrench everyone knew and kissed it like a talisman. Rhea had never believed in talismans, but she let that one exist because it did not ask her to betray anything.

The second knock was not a knock so much as a polite insistence. The words “Municipal Network Survey” came through the wood the way water forces itself through a crack in a wall—slow first and then with the inevitability of saturation. The voice did not try to sound kind; it sounded official, which pretends kindness isn’t required. Rhea raised one finger to Kessa and then moved the furniture without making it look like she had moved anything at all. She pulled open the hatch and showed Kessa with her face that going down was not failure; it was grammar. Kessa nodded, mouth set, and then swung herself into the dark with the grace of someone who had grown up climbing things that had not been made to be climbed.

Rhea took a breath and opened the door to the chain. The man in the hall was not the man from the courthouse. He had a tablet in his hands and a pen tucked behind his ear and his coat had a badge printed into the fabric the way the Orchard likes to print ownership into the world. His mouth arranged itself into a shape that suggested he wanted to be somewhere else more interesting. He explained the thing she already knew in words that made it sound like an opportunity to prove what a good citizen looked like, and she nodded and said things like “Of course” and “How long will it take?” in a voice that made even “yes” sound like “you’ll forgive me if I prioritize my groceries.”

“We’re doing the whole building,” he said, and his eyes skated over her shoulder looking for an obvious hub to talk to. “Routine after a routine review hit a snag. You’ll barely notice. We’ll stand right here and coordinate and you can go back about your day.”

“Lovely,” she said, and smiled like a woman whose day consisted of the kind of things that could wait while men played with toys. “The kitchen sink does this hiccup when it feels stared at. You will let me know if you cause it to sulk? I have a friend who claims it cries at night.”

He blinked and then laughed because laughter is cheaper than being rude. He asked if he could step inside and she gestured with a hand that suggested hospitality was a slipper she could put on quickly if the guest would hurry. He stood just beyond the threshold and let the tablet talk to the room with polite hums. The hub under the dish towel looked like an old radio and Rhea was suddenly, fiercely glad she had draped it that way. Machines are children dressed as mirrors. They tell you what you expect to see until you demand the truth and then they cry.

The tablet made a sound as if clearing its throat and the man’s eyebrows rose and fell like birds skimming a lake. “Some latency on this floor,” he said conversationally, as if he were discussing the weather, as if she did not know latency on this floor had saved her niece a piece of her name. “We may need to bring up a booster. You might hear a tone. It’s nothing. Sometimes old buildings can’t decide to talk loud.”

“Old buildings have earned their right to whisper,” Rhea said, and wondered what the tone would feel like if it went through the bones. She stood where she could see the pantry and he stood where he could see the window and for a moment the two of them were gods of useless things, guardians with no temples and too much time.

Down in the pantry hatch the air gathered itself like a cloak. Kessa crouched on the shelf that used to hold flour and then, when she was sure her aunt was holding the line, slid feet-first onto the little ladder that had never meant to be a ladder. She moved in darkness marked only by the faint lace of daylight that came up through cracks and by the knowledge mapped on her palms. Her fingertips found the strut that needed a press and the pipe that would bite if you hugged it too close, and she made herself small enough to be a rumor. The hatch led into a crawl where the wires bowed like arteries and the dust had settled into a peace you did not want to disturb. She thought about the pears with their wet, patient eyes and the way their mouths had learned to regret in all the tongues she had taught them, and she made herself think about something else so she would not become a version of herself that apologized to objects.

When she reached the first bend, she stopped and let her breath slow until it sounded like part of the building. She listened with her hands for her aunt’s voice translated into vibrations, and she caught the kind of movement that meant Rhea had shifted her weight in a way that signaled wait. Above her, a tone began so soft she could have mistaken it for an ache in her ears and then grew the way a rumor grows, collecting confidence as it found mouths. It didn’t sound like a tone so much as a line being drawn in air. Kessa put her forehead against the cool of the beam and let the cold take away some of the heat in her chest.

Upstairs the tone warbled like a kettle embarrassed to boil. The man in the hall touched his tablet as if petting it and the tablet purred. “There we are,” he said, satisfaction slotting into his face so neat Rhea almost admired the engineering. “Just a handshake. Your building was sulking. Now it will say what it needs to say.”

“What it needs to say,” Rhea repeated mildly, and thought about all the things she had taught pipes to keep to themselves. She thought about the copper warming her pocket and the seed in its curl and the way names could taste like coins on certain days. She tilted her head, made a little frown like someone counting shoes, and let the man think she was doing the math of patience. He took it as invitation to show off and showed her a graph that meant nothing to anyone who wasn’t him and never would.

The tone climbed. It was not music. It wanted to be a line between two points that had never agreed to meet. It found in the room only structures that had polite reasons to ignore it and one human who had taught herself to be a hinge. Rhea moved her tongue in her mouth the way a person moves it when they are about to pronounce a name in a language that does not want to be borrowed. She held the graph in her vision because there was power in eyeing a thing while you dismantled it, and then she shifted her weight three inches to the left and pressed her heel into the board that had a nail that had bent thirty years ago and never forgiven itself. The board complained in a key that made the tone above it falter as if embarrassed. The man at the door did not hear what she heard. He heard a building settling and he told his tablet they were done here.

“You’re very kind,” she said when he thanked her for her cooperation as if she had given it to him wrapped in ribbon. “We like to be boring up here. We try not to make work.”

He left a pamphlet in her hand that self-destructed into fluff when she touched it with her thumb, a trick meant to make the Orchard look clever in a way that did not demand blame if the pamphlet ended up on the floor of a stairwell. The fluff clung to her fingers and she wiped it off on her pants with an expression that suggested she had always intended to have lint there. She shut the door and leaned her forehead against the wood and let herself be tired for as long as it took to count to seven. Then she locked two locks and moved the chair and opened the pantry and said nothing because Kessa’s hands were already there, reaching, and sometimes silence is the only thing that makes a reaching hand feel like a rescue.

They sat on the floor together like girls, backs against cold cabinets, and let their knees touch until their nerves agreed to be part of one person’s body again. Rhea told her what had happened in the kind of words you use when you don’t want to give fear more syllables to live in. Kessa nodded and added what it had sounded like beneath and then said she had wanted to sing and had not because she was learning, and Rhea smiled like she always had imagined this would be the kind of thing she got to hear as a woman living in a city that had not made her tired.

“I want to keep the pears,” Kessa said after a while, voice small and ridiculous and exactly brave enough. “I don’t want them to die just because they got good at being sad. I want them to learn to change into other fruit. Who’s to say pears can’t survive as pomegranates. Who’s to say they can’t drop seeds and make orchards the Orchard can’t count.”

“Then we will keep them,” Rhea said, as if it were a promise as easy as putting bread in a bag. “We will keep everything we can keep and we will lose only the things that want to be lost. And we will be on the bus by nightfall.”

“Which bus,” Kessa asked, and it was almost a tease.

“The one whose driver owes me his toes,” Rhea said, and laughed when Kessa’s face went white. “Metaphor. He owed me a favor. I saved his toes from a radiator that had opinions about heat. He’ll drive where the Orchard is not listening for one hour and then no more.”

They moved like rain at dusk, slow enough to look like decoration, steady enough to arrive without drama. Rhea left the apartment as if she were going nowhere at all and Kessa left five minutes later carrying a bag that looked too light to be worth stealing. They met in the hall that smelled like onions and forgot newspapers and then separated again to take the back stairs that the super had padlocked last year because there were complaints about teenagers kissing and then had forgotten to padlock again because he had never been in love long enough to memorize the sound of his own lock. The door to the alley stuck as usual and the stutter it made when it opened was a stutter Rhea had trained herself to hear as applause.

The bus yard was a holdover from when they had called buses trolleys and sworn they were temporary. It sat under an overpass that moaned when trucks went over it and collected pigeons the way a pond collects leaves. Horace, who had been driver and union man and poet and insomniac in varying orders depending on the year, was in the office making tea with a teabag that had seen too much. He looked at Rhea with the peculiar brightness of a man who has chosen his loyalties and tidied them like linen. He did not ask questions that would make him have to decide if he wanted answers.

“Thirty-seven runs with her ears off tonight,” he said, which meant bus thirty-seven had a broken transmitter and could be trusted not to tell the Orchard more than its route. “You’ll ride like you’re going to the depot to argue about a schedule and get off at the compost lot like you’re going to steal dirt. No one cares about dirt. The world thinks it makes its own.”

“Buses tell on us with their tires even if their mouths behave,” Rhea said, and he grinned because he loved it when anyone treated buses like people.

“I changed the shoes,” he said proudly, which meant he had swapped out the treads that the Orchard liked to read for the kind with less gossip. “She’ll whisper. She won’t confess.”

Kessa touched the cool metal of the farebox when they stepped aboard as if it were an altar and Horace pretended not to notice because he had a daughter once and knew that you did not always get to keep the moments you felt fatherly and should take them anyway when they wandered into your hand. The bus smelled like wet wool and dust and peppermint because Horace kept a tin in the glove compartment for when night threatened to get so big it would swallow him. They took seats near the middle where you can see both exits and pretend you don’t care, and Rhea set the folder of made papers between them like a promise or a decoy.

The city slid by with its lights made softer by snow and its sharp edges dulled by the distance that metal insists upon. They passed a wall where kids had painted a jungle of fruit in neon and the Orchard had answered with a bland municipal mural of a river, and someone had amended the river with a bridge made of bananas so precise it had to have been done by a hand that had studied draughting. They passed the bronze gorilla again and it looked smaller because they were higher and then larger because perspective is a liar, and Kessa pressed her forehead to the window and fogged the glass and wrote a letter on the fog that could have been a K or could have been a kiss.

At the compost lot, Horace slowed like a man in love, reluctantly and with a desire to pretend he hadn’t meant to. He announced something cheerful about delays on a route not theirs into a microphone that was turned down too low to matter, and Rhea stood and let the bus think this was choreography and not exit. The door sighed open and the smell of rot met them like a friend’s sweater, warm and ridiculous. They stepped down into a world of piles that steamed faintly in the cold and machines that looked like beetles if beetles had union cards. The lot was almost pretty if you liked your beauty to brag about how it worked.

“You have eight minutes before the Orchard remembers that it was rude to ignore you,” Horace said without moving his lips, as if he was speaking out of the bus itself. “Then the drones will reconsider their priorities. You take the path behind the mulberries and then the trail where someone has been walking their ridiculous dog even though there is a sign that says not to. You go to the fence with the slats. Two of the slats are tired. Sit with them.”

Rhea squeezed his shoulder in thanks and he feigned insult because thanks made him itchy. Kessa, impulsive and lit by the kind of gratitude that makes complications because it is pure, reached into her bag and pulled out a small square of paper with a pear on it and handed it to him. “For your glove compartment,” she said, and then, because it was stupid and she could not help herself, she added, “It hums in the key of four.”

Horace took it like someone accepting a blessing delivered by an idiot angel, which is to say with grace. He tucked it into his pocket, touching it with two fingers like he was making a bargain and then getting to keep it.

They went into the compost lot like thieves sneaking into church and found the path that pretended not to be a path. The mulberries held their purple in little frozen beads that made the tree look like it had grown jewelry in its sleep. The ridiculous dog had left prints that looked too neat to be real, and the fence with the tired slats sighed when Rhea leaned against it in just the right place. The slats did not fall; they consented. There was a space where someone with a pocket the right shape could fit through and not ruin a coat, which is how you knew the space had been made by someone who had tried it before and was not in a hurry to fix it.

Beyond the fence, the city changed key. The Orchard’s sound was further away here, as if someone had closed a window with great politeness. Rhea straightened and Kessa straightened and the two of them took each other in at arm’s length like people after a dance, measuring if everyone still had their feet. The night felt like a lid and an invitation both. Rhea took the copper out of her coat and pressed it against her sternum and decided, right then, that there were a dozen calls she could make to a dozen people who would lie for her without being asked and she would make none of them yet, because favors clicked together if you waited and turned into nets you owned.

“We’ll stay with Harrow two nights,” she said, hearing the way the name made the air change and deciding that was fine. “He owes me three because of the valve at Ninth. Then we’ll go to the edge and see if the rumors about the ferry are rumors that like being remade. Don’t draw anything that looks back. Don’t name anything that can complain. If someone offers to tell your future, tell them you married it and it moved out.”

Kessa nodded and tucked her chin into her scarf and then did something that broke Rhea’s heart with the gentleness of it and made her laugh through the break. She reached into her bag and took out another scrap of paper and another pencil and she began to draw the path beneath their feet without looking at the paper, eyes on the world, hand remembering for them, lines laying down a record that could be burned later and that would have done its job already even if it never made it to a fridge. The world above loves a story. The ground loves routine. There is a third thing, Rhea thought, that likes both. It is the hand.

Behind them, in a ward that had earned its sleep, the Orchard decided to be offended. It sent an apology to the man in the coat at the courthouse, and the apology was shaped like a command. Somewhere a drone considered a shortcut and decided against it because the wind was uncooperative. Somewhere an orangutan named Root made tea with both hands and closed her eyes and remembered a conference where a woman had laughed at a bad joke and everyone had forgiven the joke because the laugh had been so clean. In the little valve house, the patch panel asked the air what its name was and the air said something old that did not translate.

Rhea led them to a door that wouldn’t look like a door until you tipped your head the way a medium tips her head at a spirit and pretends not to be surprised. It belonged to a storage room that had been converted into ten memories of usefulness and then into a place where Harrow kept the boxes of people who did not want to be reminded of receipts. She knocked threes paused like a stutter and then again in case he had changed the pattern and in case he had not. It took the length of time for a kettle to think about boiling for his beefy hand to snap back the latch, and his face appeared with the wonder of a man who has been awakened and is relieved to find he likes who is at his door.

“Plumber,” he said, which was what he called her when he meant I am going to lie for you in a way that makes me feel virtuous. “And her shadow.”

“Harrow,” she said, which was not his name and not not his name. “We need your forgotten room and your small supply of boring.”

“You can have my large supply of boring,” he said grandly, and stood aside as if expecting applause. “Stay until morning. The Orchard has been charming near the depot because it thinks charm will get it what interrogation will not. In the morning it will be cruel because charms that fail get mean. You want to be elsewhere when pretty turns.”

“We are already elsewhere,” Rhea said, and he grinned like she had told a joke about a saint learning to cheat at cards.

Inside, the room smelled like cardboard and nutmeg and the incense of burnt dust. A little space heater ticked to itself as if counting a blessing and a cot sagged in the middle the way cots choose to. Kessa took off her coat and sat on the edge of the cot like a pilgrim trying to decide if the shrine had earned all the knees that had been bent to it. Rhea shut the door and leaned against it and let her joints announce themselves, and Harrow fussed with a kettle in a way that made it clear he believed hospitality could deflect fate.

“You’ll be wanting to sleep in turns,” he said, putting three cups on a crate with the seriousness of an altar boy choosing candles. “If anyone knocks, tell them you’re here to catalog shame. No one wants their shame alphabetized.”

“You’d be surprised,” Rhea said with affection, and then she took the copper out and set it on the crate between them because it felt right to let the room know who it was sheltering. The copper lay there like a circle that had learned a trick. It looked harmless as a bracelet and heavy as a debt.

Harrow, who told himself he didn’t look at anything he had not been hired to look at, looked at it with the avidity of someone who loved the city more than the city deserved. He reached out and then didn’t and then reached out again and touched it with his pinky like a child testing if a stove was on. He winced and grinned because of course it was hot, and then he opened his hand in the air like a man warming his fingers at a fire he could not see.

“Tell me you are going to punish someone who needs punishing,” he said softly, which was how you said I am frightened for you without making it sound like an insult.

“I am going to keep something that is mine,” Rhea said, and that was not a threat and not a prayer and not a lie. “If punishment happens while I am keeping it, that will be the way of it. But I will not be distracted.”

Kessa drank tea because that was the religion of rooms like this and let the heat climb from her tongue to her eyes and found that tears went away quicker when you invited them. She put her cup down and sat on her hands and said, in a voice that might have been to them or to the copper or to the pears back in the Orchard’s hungry trees, “We will make a world where an apology is not a shape you wear to avoid being noticed. We will make a world where pears get to grow teeth for reasons other than defense.”

“That sounds like a novel,” Harrow said, because he wanted to belong to anything that sounded like not work.

“It sounds like chapter one,” Rhea said, and then she closed her eyes, and she listened to the building and to the city and to the apes on their antennas and to the water that did not care about any of them, and she listened until listening became the only thing between them and morning.Night was a quiet made of catalog numbers and breath, the kind of quiet that has been taught to behave. Harrow’s little room absorbed them without fuss, swallowing the newcomers into its dust-soft belly and warming itself by increments as the heater remembered how to be generous. Rhea took first watch because sleep always treated her like a stranger who might be selling something; Kessa lay on the cot and listened to the cot’s old logic and then, as if the logic had argued her down, her eye-lids lowered with the grace of surrender. Harrow snored like a man who had decided he would not apologize for the body’s noises and, between the snore and the tick of the heater and the tiny conversational cuff of hot water moving in narrow pipes, the room taught Rhea its pulse. The copper on the crate warmed like an animal curled on a lap and, sometimes, cooled in a slow breath that made her think of tides. She looked at it the way you look at a sleeping infant and imagine the jaw and the fists and the dangerous sweetness turning to teeth and choices.

In the watch between one and two she stood and checked the window without parting the curtain, because sometimes looking is permission and sometimes it is a dare. The lot beyond the slatted fence had folded itself down to glow-worm machinery and steam, a city’s compost dreaming of spring under snow. The Orchard’s tone lived somewhere in the overpass and in the streetlights and in the posture of the pigeons, and it pressed against the glass with the politeness of a neighbor who wanted sugar and could not admit it. She breathed through her mouth and tasted the day’s metal as if it had lacquered her tongue. Somewhere down in her coat, the seed she had kept inside the copper watched without eyes, a concentration she could feel like attention on the back of her neck.

Kessa woke in her turn with a start that looked like a dance and sat up with the look of someone who had slept inside a sentence she had not yet finished and was eager to return to it later. She rubbed the heel of her hand against one eye and then the other, smearing sleep into a mask she did not know she wore. “I dreamed the pears had legs,” she said softly, so as not to make that foolishness hard to escape. “Not like people legs. Like younger trees. They walked out of the Orchard because they were bored and it took the Orchard a whole afternoon to notice because the pears left their mouths behind and the mouths kept apologizing.”

“Keep their legs,” Rhea said, meaning the idea. “They might need them again.” She poured tea without sight and without spillage because her hands had learned the distances in this room in an hour and because tea is what you give fear when you want it to loosen its jaw. She watched Kessa drink and, without saying anything, moved the crate an inch closer to the cot with the copper ring on it, a child’s bedtime story told to a talisman.

Later, when Harrow took his turn to watch and pretended not to be honored by the trust, Rhea let herself lie down on the cot still warm from Kessa’s sleep. Her body produced a list of complaints with a bureaucrat’s thoroughness and, in response, her mind stepped aside and let exhaustion take the minutes like coins. In that narrow corridor between being and not being, she heard the city the way some people hear their own name when called from the end of a long room. Pipes spoke in consonants now, tougher sounds for colder hours. Somewhere a valve tried to argument itself into a virtue and failed, and two streets over a radiator decided to be a drum and beat until it was negotiated into quiet. The apes’ network made a sound not like technology but like snow deciding to be rain, and underneath it all swam the low, old hum of water that does not care who scores the surface.

Dawn came dressed as a debate. It opened a slit at the edge of the city and then narrowed it, then opened it again, as if light had an opinion and was trying to get an appointment to be heard. Harrow handed out bread that had stiffened into dignity and jam that acted like a bribe, and he laid a newspaper on the crate as if it were still a thing. The front page had a photograph of the bronze gorilla with snow on its shoulders and a headline about infrastructure incentives that read like a dare. In a thinner column with a thinner font was a note about “anomalous latency in Ward Sixteen’s municipal grid,” and Rhea pressed her thumb to it as if pressing would keep the sentence from finishing. The Orchard’s hand had done his work before breakfast; she admired that even as she refused it.

“You’ll want to be gone by the time the second cup of city coffee hits their blood,” Harrow said, and the way he said coffee made it sound like a flavor of obedience. “I can walk you to the old beet yard. From there, the rails will take you as far as anyone can not ask. After that, it is all weather and favors. The ferry runs by both, which is why it runs at all.”

“Has anyone been counting the ferry with more than eyes,” Rhea asked, and Harrow’s mouth did a peculiar twist, as if he was trying to keep his lips from betraying his ears.

“They tried to give it a gate you have to talk to,” he said, pouring more tea like an argument in favor of living. “First time, it rusted out of spite. Second time, someone replaced the gate with a sculpture of a gate so elegant nobody could tell whether it was open or shut, and the Orchard thought better of arguing with art in public. After that, they focused on the buoys. One got a taste for swimming away. Two learned to be lazy. The river is a union shop. You put a supervisor on the payroll and the current negotiates weekends.”

Rhea did not smile because smiling is a contract and she had no time to sign anything else. She packed their nothings and their something into the bag, she slid the copper into the inside pocket of her coat where a heart might be if hearts wanted to work outside of bodies, and she made Kessa practice a face that said we belong wherever we are until the practice wore the face down to something permanent. Harrow stood and fussed with his coat as if the act itself were an old song he was trying to remember all the words to, and then he did a thing that made Rhea like him more than she had already decided to: he asked Kessa if he could keep the cup she had used last, as if keeping the ring of her mouth might make his room warmer when it got cold again.

The door that looked like a cupboard looked like a cupboard. They stepped through its lie and into a corridor of smells, the honest stink of communal life, onions and detergent and the scorched hair smell of a bad idea on the stove. The building’s stairwell wore notices like badges, announcements of inspections and exterminations and prayer circles and a concert for children who did not belong to anyone in particular and needed someone to clap anyway. Someone had hung up a little Orchard poster with a cartoon orangutan offering advice about identity hygiene, and someone else had given it a moustache that was, somehow, earnest. At the second landing, Rhea paused and laid her palm flat on the wall, the way a person lays a hand on a friend’s back when they want to say keep going without words. The plaster cooled her, and in return she gave it her attention, which is the first and sometimes only thing any of us are owed.

The street had already learned the day’s lesson and decided it was going to be inconvenient. The snow had crusted and sullied, delicate whiteness replaced by a scabbed sheen that made the whole sidewalk look like it might talk back if stepped on wrong. Drones watched with the feigned disinterest of dogs whose owners have said “leave it” in a tone that suggests maybe this time it would be okay to ignore the command. Rhea and Kessa did the long-slow dance of people making themselves too improbable to be watched: pause to adjust a scarf, step to tie a boot, fuss with a sleeve long enough to be considered boring. She knew that if you used up all your speed before noon, the day would own you by evening.

At the beet yard a man in a sweater of obsolete maroon sat on a stool whose paint remembered better winters and counted metal teeth for a living. He sold entry like a priest sells absolution: briskly, with an eye for who would ask for more than was on the table. Harrow threw him a look that had debt sewn into it; he threw a hook of chin back that said I am still mad about the valve and I am still grateful for my wife’s temper and then they understood they were too lazy to be enemies. The rails beyond trembled in their sleep, lines dusty with disuse that nevertheless held a discipline the Orchard could not quite rewrite because old steel remembers things, and remembering is a kind of resistance.

They followed the rails until the rails forgot themselves into weeds and then followed the weeds until the weeds surrendered to a path the city had not dignified with a name. Every so often Rhea stopped and listened and then did nothing because listening is sometimes enough. Kessa tried to memorize the sky’s particular dirty blue and failed because failure is the root of good art and maybe good safety too. The Orchard’s hand was somewhere behind them, orchestrating new politeness, sending people with clipboards and open faces to ask the hallway if it had seen anything suspicious and to leave behind stickers with serial numbers and compliments. A sticker on a door is a kiss you can’t wash off for a week, Rhea thought, and she told herself not to collect more of them than necessary.

Across town, in a room of glass that had been designed to make conversation look like transparency, Root held a mug larger than her hand and watched three screens pretend to make a story. She had been named as much for a temperament as for a function, and both cranky and foundational suited her. She had refused to wear the Council’s vestments that day because wearing them made her itch and because itchiness made her imprecise. Archive, across the table, scratched his temple with a stylus and read from a tablet without reading, his mouth moving in a habit that had become superstition after too many meetings where facts were asked to dress up as decisions. “Devour at Ward line between K and L,” he said, and looked over the top of the slate with a smirk that failed to show he was worried. “Handshake obstruction transitory. Node resumed cooperation after minor acoustic disturbance. Possible household disrepair.”

“It was a woman who knew where to put her heel,” Root said, and let the tea scorch her tongue in case talking like that made her sentimental. “You can tell when a body that works for a living sets its weight in the right place. Our tones assume level floors. The city does not have many left.”

“On paper she looks like a plumber,” Archive said, and the word looked tasted like wire in his mouth. “On paper shouldn’t fool us.”

“It shouldn’t,” Root agreed, setting the mug down with care because setting things down carelessly has a way of becoming a habit. “But it always does. Release the apology shaped like a patch. Put it in the fruit. The kids will download it because curiosity masquerades correctly as caution, and if she loves a kid she will tell the kid not to, and if the kid loves her she will do the opposite because that is what love is for at that age.”

“And if the pears choose to keep their legs,” Archive said, and now there was humor in the shape of his mouth, no, not quite humor, something like respect. “How hard do we make the fence.”

“Hard enough to show we tried,” Root said. “Soft enough to keep the story kind. The Board is hungry for outcomes that taste like control, and I am hungry for a world where curiosity does not get punished into silence. Do the math of appearances. Then tell the hand in the scarf to drink water. He looks like he forgets.”

A wind came down the rail line and pushed at Rhea’s ear like someone trying to make a point at a bar. She angled her face into it and tasted salt that should not have been there yet, a promise made by the river to the land. Ahead, the long roofline of the ferry sheds sloped into the morning with the arrogance of structures that have outlasted their first purpose and cheerfully accepted another. The ferry itself hunched under its own weight at the pier like an animal at rest; it was not a pretty boat and therefore not a liar. Snow had stuck to its ropes in fur and the rust on its sides had organized itself into continents that might be mapped by someone with an hour and a pencil. Two men smoked by a drum that should not have contained fire and compounded the error by warming their hands at it; a woman in a coat with a hood that could cradle a moon stood near the gate pretending not to be the person you had to talk to.

“We’ll look like people who need to go to the other side for something uninteresting,” Rhea said, changing her gait so that it said my back hurts and my wallet is thin. “Think about errands. Think about a sack of potatoes and a niece who makes a face at onions. Think about bringing a jar back to a person who kept it too long and who insists they needed it for a reason you don’t believe. Don’t look at the water like you’re in love. Be annoyed by its habit of being wet.”

Kessa tried the annoyance on like a borrowed coat and let it sag. She had drawn rivers since she could hold a pencil and the river’s long torso always had a woman’s long torso in it if you looked right. She remembered to scowl anyway, because her aunt had asked her to and because scowling felt like an action in a morning where she was mostly being asked not to act. The woman with the moon hood watched them arrive with the indifference of someone who has learned that care is a tax the world rarely pays back; it took her three seconds to decide Rhea was a kind of trouble she could tolerate.

“You catch the tide,” the woman said, and the voice was old and new at once, like a song Rhea had known as a child and then forgot she had known. “You pay twice if you ask me what time the tide is. You pay three times if you use the word ‘discretion’ out loud. If anyone says the Orchard out here we tell them it is a seed catalog and we discuss tomatoes.”

“I like my tomatoes to have opinions,” Rhea said, taking the measure she was being handed and folding it into her own. “We will ask about carp. We will tip in labor when we can. I have a way with boilers and apologetic machinery. This is my niece. She won’t break your boat on purpose.”

“First time for everything,” the woman with the moon hood said, and the corner of her mouth acknowledged that the joke had done its job. “Name for the ticket.”

“Jade and Auntie,” Rhea said, because names are clothes and Jade was a dress she had worn as a pair of earrings many years ago and Auntie had been a rumor she’d survived as a teenager. The woman wrote nothing down in a way that was ostentatious about not writing anything down, a performance of amnesia they all applauded by pretending not to notice. The gate hated them with a squeak as they passed, and Rhea filed the squeak under repairs she would owe the woman whether asked or not.

They stood at the rail and watched the river pretend laziness while arranging its muscles into plans. Kessa counted ice like sheep, a litany against usefulness, and flinched only once when a drone cut low and then away, a shadow with the arrogance of a bird that knows it can’t be plucked. Behind them the city kept making city sounds, the Orchard’s tone now played through the high wires by a band that had gotten bored with the song and was improvising in bad faith. Men loaded sacks with a rhythm that came from shoulders and who they had loved young and who they had failed late, and everything smelled faintly of iron and fish and money.

“Three minutes,” the woman with the moon hood said, and then looked out over the water where a gull dragged its wing low like a show-off and added, “That buoy will try to tell on us, but the current owes me from last spring when I pulled half a car out of its teeth. It will murmur. It won’t bother to bite.”

They might have made it onto the ferry without incident if the Orchard had not decided politeness had used up its usefulness thirty seconds earlier. A van with a logo that said “City Safety Cohort” bumped down the pier with the careful violence of men who want to scare and not break. Three people got out wearing a uniform that was too friendly to be anything but enforcement, lanyards shining like little commandeered suns. One of them was the man with the collar and the lozenge scarf from the courthouse, the Orchard’s hand made flesh and schedule. He had the look of someone who had never needed to run for a bus and therefore didn’t understand why so many people did. He walked toward them with a knot-maker’s confidence, the assumption that you could turn any situation into a tidy bow if you tugged in the right places.

“Morning, neighbors,” he said, and the word neighbors did what it does when someone with a badge uses it for cover. He held out his slate as if it were a gift. “Routine check. The Orchard apologizes for the inconvenience, but we’re patching a glitch in the youth safety protocols and we need to make sure no one is accidently caught in the update. If you have young people in your party, we’ll be happy to expedite a scan and get you on your way with a voucher for—”

“A voucher for toothpaste,” the woman with the moon hood said, as if she had done this dance and was bored with its music. “We have our own teeth. The tide does not stop for apologies. Are you waiting for the tide to be polite too. It is not a code. It will not listen.”

He smiled without showing the edges of his teeth, which is what a certain kind of trained man does when women decide to be inconvenient. “We can do this here or at the depot,” he said, and the we was a rope tossed over a fence you did not want climbed. “I’m willing to be flexible.”

“Your sleeves are tailored against it,” Rhea said in a tone of admiration she had learned from people who wore suits for a living, and she tilted the copper circle inside her coat so that it nested in the triangle of her ribs and channeled the small heat of her stubbornness into the center of her chest. “We’re taking compost schedules to a friend with a garden.” She looked him full in the face because looking away can be cowardice and it can also be alarm; today she needed to be the embarrassing cousin who tells stories at breakfast. “You know how old people get without a routine.”

“I am old people,” the woman with the moon hood said, and the man smiled again because this version of the world had never taught him how to deal with a chorus.

Behind the scarf’s curve, his eyes flicked once toward Kessa. He did it like a professional, instantiating interest and then extinguishing it in one clean click. Kessa made her mouth soft and blank the way Rhea had taught her and put two fingers on the cold of the rail as if she were concentrating on the exact degree to which the metal stuck to skin. The slate in the man’s hand muttered nonsense to the air and expected answers. It was a polite mugger, hands out, eyes everywhere. Rhea felt, very faintly, the copper warm and a taste on her tongue like a socket you used your tongue on because you were young and supposed to do stupid things to learn a principle.

The buoy beyond the ferry gave itself away by rocking when no wind touched it. It rocked like a man trying to see over a crowd and then pretending he had been yawning. The moon-hood woman clicked her tongue, one tiny sound, and a boy on deck who had been looking at his feet looked up and went to a valve with the dignity of a person in on a joke. He opened the valve one notch, no more, and the ferry exhaled the kind of steam that sometimes is just steam and sometimes is a message. Fog insignificantly thicker than it should have been slid between Rhea and the slate like a new religion that hadn’t decided its first miracle yet. The man with the scarf frowned, almost imperceptibly, not because he couldn’t see but because the world had decided to feel less compliant and men like him feel entitled to compliance.

“Two minutes,” called the moon hood, mostly to the tide and a little to courage. Rhea slid her thumb along the copper’s inner curve the way you stroke a cat who is deciding whether to bite. It was not a button; she did not deal in those. It was a line you could complete with flesh if you needed it to carry your stubbornness somewhere else. She did not close the circuit fully because that would be a different day’s necessity. She did enough to make the Orchard’s tone pucker inside her skull as if embarrassed and then reform at a slightly different pitch, as though someone had nudged a player’s elbow.

The man in the scarf shifted weight like a man who hears a song in the next room and cannot decide whether to like it. He glanced at one of his companions, who had resorted to looking officious because that is the only thing that keeps off shivers on a pier. “We can do this quickly,” he said, which was a lie in the way that saying “we” like that always is. “If your niece will just—”

“My niece is very patient,” Rhea said, and the sentence appeared reasonable until you took it apart and found it had no relation to his demand. “She’s training to work with children. She practices patience by making older men wait.”

The moon-hood woman laughed like a kettle remembering its cue and then she said, rather too brightly, “All aboard or all ashore,” which is a sentence with two options that, in fact, only presents one if you want your lungs to keep believing in the ordinary miracle of air. Rhea put a steadying hand at the small of Kessa’s back and shepherded them the way she would move a leak from one joint to another. The man in the scarf made a decision with his mouth he would justify later to his superiors with a report that had more geometry than confession in it. He stepped back. He waved them through. He made very sure his face looked bored. That was his mistake. Bored looks can be remembered by a certain kind of teenager forever.

The ferry’s deck under their boots felt like someone else’s shoulder: rough, trustworthy, used to being leaned on. Cables sang their tiny metallic songs because the world cannot help its own talent for music. Men hauled ropes and pretended this was a craft, not a choreography, because pretending makes muscles operate at a higher moral tone. The buoy shrugged and then did as it was told and the gate let go of its petty grievances and the ferry, which had been trying not to yawn in front of strangers, took a deep breath and pushed itself sideways into the water with the weary satisfaction of someone moving a piece of furniture they have moved a hundred times.

They watched the pier fall away. The man with the scarf stood with his tablet at his side and offended his own scarf with the way his jaw set. He did not try to scan the ferry while it moved because even polite thieves understand certain sacred geometry: at a given point in motion, the world surrenders to larger logics than your tools have been taught to respect. He held in his anger like men who had paid good money for their coats hold in their stomachs, and he told himself the tide was a colleague in this, not a competitor. “We’ll be here when they come back,” he said, and later he would not remember saying it out loud.

The river opened in front of them like more time and they took what was offered without blessing or complaint. Kessa leaned and let the spray touch her mouth as if the water were an alphabet that could be learned by flavor. Rhea put one hand on the rim of the copper through her coat and hooked the other into the ferrule at the rail where men had put hands for a century, oil and salt ground into a public memory. The city sloped away behind them into an argument with its own horizon, and the Orchard’s tone flattened into something that might have been a sigh if networks sighed. On the opposite bank, the factories that had been turned into apartments pretending to be factories raised their blank eyes to watch, and cranes sketched grammar in the air without yet deciding what tense it would be.

“What do we pay her,” Kessa whispered, because the moon-hood woman had become a figure of myth in three minutes on a boat and Rhea believed in paying myth promptly if you wanted it to show up again. “I could give her one of the pears,” she added, soft and wanting, and winced before Rhea could scold, because she had already had the thought jump ahead of her tongue to ask if giving the pears away turned them into spies.

“We pay in heat as soon as she asks where her heat went. We pay in wrench when a bolt is too proud. We pay in our backs if a bag of something needs to pretend to be lighter than it is to get to its destination. We pay with our names only in the currency of jokes,” Rhea said, and watched the moon-hood woman move along the deck with a balance made from a hundred small bargains. “Keep the pears. We will make a place for them later that does not count as counting. We will invite them to learn boredom as a skill. Boredom keeps legends alive when the story goes quiet.”

Behind them the hand in the scarf took a photograph he did not admit to himself was a keepsake. Across the city, Root sent a patch that taught pears to apologize with better grammar and watched with a kind of pride she would never confess to as the kids who loved them said no and yes in inventive sequences that suggested the world might not yet be entirely domesticated. In the valve house, the patch panel tried to spit dust and ended up hawking up a moth that had been asleep for a decade and now was awake and confused and flew against a window like a question. Harrow rinsed cups with water that had opinions and kept two for a day when he would need to feel like a man who had done right and been thanked.

The ferry’s engine argued with the water in the way that makes both parties come out of the exchange feeling justified. They cut the middle of the river and slid toward the low shore where the wind smelled less like money and more like patience. A dog barked on the opposite bank, a ridiculous dog that did not know it was illegal to be ridiculous in certain zip codes now. The moon-hood woman came and leaned beside them as if they were part of the boat’s furniture and said, conversationally, as if the sentence wasn’t a key, “On the other side, you’ll find the place that calls itself the Sluice. They trade in fix and forget. If someone asks you for your handle, give them a joke. If someone asks you for your ear, tell them your aunt taught you to keep it under your hair.”

“You are not old,” Kessa blurted, because sometimes truth insists on a path to air even when it has no place to go once there. The moon-hood woman looked at her the way you look at a poem that has children in it, and she tucked a strand of hair behind Kessa’s ear with the gravity of a ritual and did not argue.

Rhea felt the copper cool in her coat as if the thing inside it had listened to the river and found kin. She touched Kessa’s arm, and Kessa touched Rhea’s for a length of time that would have made impatient men cough, and then both of them leaned back from the rail because you must never look too long at a thing you love when you are being followed by people who hate it for looking back. The river coughed against the hull like a friend who has permamently lost the habit of finishing a cigarette. The ferry nosed the shore with the confidence of an animal that owns this part of the world merely by insisting every day that it is home.

They found their feet on the planks that led away and they carried the weight they had and no more. The Sluice was not a place so much as a habit of being in a place. It had stalls and rooms and the bones of an old tannery that had learned to stop smelling like cruelty and start smelling like cold coffee and solder. A woman under a tarpaulin sold wire by the weight and a man at a folding table explained that he wasn’t fixing phones, he was rehabilitating them. Children dragged nets through puddles as if electricity could be caught with patience and Kessa bought them a paper cone of nuts she pretended to dislike and ate the last one skin and all. Above it all, someone had strung a line of flags that used to mean nations and now meant nothing but color, and that nothing had a kind of holiness you could not buy. The Orchard’s tone lived here too, but quietly, like a neighbor everyone had learned not to gossip with.

“Two nights,” Rhea said under her breath, remembering her earlier assurance and deciding this might be where it stopped being a brag and started being arithmetic. “Two nights to let the river forget our shape and the city remember a different story. Two nights to ask the Sluice whether it wants to sell us a pocket where a seed can learn patience.” The copper warmed once against her chest in what she chose to call agreement. A boy with a scarred eyebrow offered them a choice of candles—beeswax for memory, tallow for oiling a hinge—and Rhea bought none and promised to buy two later in the kind of voice that repays debt before it exists.

They sat at a table made from a door that had outlived six sets of hinges and drank coffee that tasted like a message smuggled through a wall. Harrow’s lint had found a way across the water and clung to Rhea’s cuff; she brushed it off and watched it fall and imagined it making a home among the Sluice’s drift. Kessa took a pencil and a scrap of paper out of her coat and looked at the backs of people’s necks with the attention of a doctor and the tenderness of a thief. The pears would have to find a home. The seed would have to be bedded down. The Orchard would have to learn that sometimes holes stay holes and do not become stories. Rhea finished her coffee and nodded to the room like a person greeting an air that had decided to be kind for an hour and then she turned her hand palm-up under the table. Kessa put her fingers into it, and they sat that way, hands making a small bridge, and the city did not collapse or blink or punish, and for a moment, that felt like chapter enough.The Sluice received them the way some rooms receive light, with the practiced grace of a place that knew how to look useful while hiding its secrets behind the hum of trade. Rhea measured doorways with the kind of glance that also measures debts, and she marked the people who watched without staring, because it is the watchers without need that make a place dangerous. Kessa tucked her hands into her coat to keep from touching every tool she recognized, because curiosity is the oldest thief in any market and today they could not afford its usual fines. They chose a bench under a roof made from the siding of a caboose, the paint still holding the ghost of letters, and Rhea asked a boy who had the face of a thorn and the manners of a saint whether anyone here rented rooms to people who promised to leave without complaint.

“You want the Rafter,” he said, pointing with his chin the way the Sluice pointed at itself when shy. “Up above the leather-smell and the wire-smoke. Ask for Latch. She’ll tell you your price and three stories you didn’t ask for and then she’ll tell you the real price when she sees you don’t scare easy. Don’t touch the goat on the stairs. He bites out of principle.”

Latch had the haircut of a woman who had changed her mind about five major things and kept the hair as a souvenir. She wore a set of keys at her waist like jewelry and held a ledger that had been patched more often than some neighborhoods. Rhea liked her instantly because Latch did not try to make small talk into friendship, and because when she asked what brought them to the Sluice, she did it in a tone that implied she expected a lie and would judge its quality like a seam. Rhea gave her the version with the fewest adjectives and then laid coins on the ledger as if they were punctuation. Latch looked at the coins, looked at Rhea’s hands, looked at Kessa’s tired eyes, and then flipped the ledger to a page that was mostly blank and wrote a name that was not theirs in a hand that could have carved glass.

“You’ll have the south room,” Latch said, passing them a key that had learned to be stubborn. “Window that argues but opens. Bed that complains but holds. If you break anything, tell me you didn’t and then leave me a note that says you did. If anyone comes asking for you and uses words like ‘survey’ or ‘routine’ or ‘just a quick check,’ you’re in the wrong city. The goat’s name is Magistrate. He only bites people who say ‘discretion’ like a password.”

The stairs were a testimony in three languages to what time does to wood, and the goat eyed them and, finding nothing performative to object to, returned to chewing the wisdom out of a broom. Their room had a window that looked onto a roof that had learned not to leak by becoming arrogance, and a bed that had been convinced over the years by a hundred bodies that it was worth its effort to keep being furniture. Kessa sat on the bed and winced at its complaint and giggled despite herself, the giggle a tiny mercy in a morning that had offered few. Rhea put their bag where it would be annoying to anyone who wanted to enter in a hurry and then opened the window with a gentle violence that any window anywhere respects, feeling the Sluice’s air move across her palms as if measuring them back.

They did not rest beyond the minute it takes for a spine to remember the difference between standing and not. Rhea carried the copper ring inside her coat like an egg, conscious of its small heat and the slow arterial beat of the seed within it, and she told herself again the plan aloud, because plans that are not spoken are the sort the world finds easiest to steal. They needed shielding and they needed noise, a hush that wasn’t silent and a song that wasn’t music, the kind you can drape over an anomaly so that it looks like just another awkward in a city full of them. They needed a place they could bring the seed to ground without letting it germinate in the Orchard’s soil, and they needed a way to take Kessa’s pears by the hand and teach them to walk without parading them past people who count for sport.

Latch, when asked about cabinets that used to know secrets, took them to a room the size of a confession and showed them a rack of telephone guts that had been saved from a demolition by someone who had understood the sacredness of things that carry voices. The metal still smelled faintly of rain and long distance. Rhea ran a knuckle along a row of binding posts and felt the tiny scars there where a thousand small decisions had been soldered into place and then unsoldered in the economies that follow revolutions. “The exchange was in love with itself,” Latch said with grudging respect, as if speaking of an ex who had been terrible and also good company. “We took its insides when the men with permits told us to move out of the way of progress, and sometimes at night you can hear it try to ring anyway. You can use it if you leave me something to hear.”

They bought wire by the kilo, the copper singing a thin, greedy song when Latch weighed it, and they swapped two of Yen’s municipal chits for a sack of ferrite beads that made the vendor grin, because old paper still feels like astrology to certain sorts of traders. Rhea asked the woman under the tarpaulin to save her the biggest piece of mesh she had that wasn’t good enough for art and too good for trash, and the woman named her a price that respected dignity and thrift at once. Kessa bartered for a roll of cloth that had once been the inside of a coat and now would be the outside of a device, talking in a patter that would have been dangerous if it were not so earnestly about color and the way the threads felt when her fingers told them where to go. By noon they had a pile that looked like wreckage and felt like a plan.

They gave themselves lunch as a bribe and then set to work as if the work had been chasing them and now wanted to be caught. Rhea wove a cage around the copper ring with a patience that would look like love to anyone who didn’t know her, threading the mesh and the beads in and out of one another until the whole resembled a lopsided crown meant for a kinder empire. She spoke to the materials the way she spoke to pipe thread when it was trying to pretend it had always been cross, not because she thought the objects heard but because she had learned that her voice had weight and sometimes that was what kept things in the world. Kessa took a cracked laptop whose keys had been rearranged by a drunk with a philosophy degree and convinced it to run a program that had been banned because it could teach toddlers arithmetic and how to model systems that punish arithmetic. She built a room in the machine that was a room the Orchard didn’t have a word for, and in that room she set her pears down and told them stories about legs.

The first pear stood itself up like a child in a puddle and fell over with such dignity that both women swallowed their laughter to keep from jinxing the lesson. Kessa encouraged it with a series of hums that had helped when she taught them grief, and to their credit none of the pears tried to apologize while learning to balance. When the Orchard’s patch arrived, it arrived as a suggestion couched inside a metaphor couch inside a safety notice about juice boxes, and Kessa read it with her eyes narrowed and her lips pressed together, and then she threw it onto the desk with such disdain the laptop whined. “They taught them to apologize with grammar,” she said quietly, hurt and admiration elbowing one another. “It’s elegant. It’s mean. Can I be proud of something I will not let touch them.”

“You can be proud,” Rhea said, fitting a bead into place with tweezers like a woman setting a gem and making a face like a plumber who had just heard a wall call her a liar. “You can be proud and angry and hungry and we will feed the right one first. We will tell the pears that sorry is a word for after you intend to change, not a word for before someone decides whether you deserve to continue. We will teach them to tilt when asked to bow.”

In the midafternoon a girl who braided wires into her hair in place of ribbon came to the door of the Rafter and asked if anyone here knew how to make a monitor stop blinking without buying a new one. Rhea went down the stairs with a wrench in her hand because a wrench was the kind of introduction the Sluice understood and came back twenty minutes later with a bag of bolts and a set of reflections about the way people always forget their machines have spines and those spines need affection. Kessa watched for drones and wrote a note in her own hand about the pears’ patience that looked like a fragment of a poem and then tore it up into ten pieces and fed them to a plant on the windowsill that had decided to be ivy in a room with no ambition for climbing. Latch came to their door once with a plate of beans and once with a story about the time the Sluice pretended to be holy for a day to avoid a tax and got away with it because no one could tell whether paying attention was worship or simply caution done right.

The first day bled into the second night’s morning without asking and the Sluice yawned and rolled over and put an arm across its own eyes to sleep through dawn. Rhea had wired the mesh cage into the bones of the telephone racks so that the seed in its copper had a bed and a lock and a ceiling it could press against without waking the neighbors. She had built, around that nest, a low wall of interference and bitty songs, the kind of music that made drones stare for a moment and then decide they were being offered an advertisement and go elsewhere out of spite. Kessa had taught the pears three steps and a turn and a way to carry their mouths in their hands while practicing, so that the Orchard’s patch could not find lips to fasten to when it came calling. She had taught one pear to wink like a streetlamp and another to count heat with the seriousness of an accountancy.

In the hour when fatigue changes its name to stubbornness, there was a knock that sounded like someone trying to pretend they were casual while dressing themselves in obligation. Latch’s voice came through the door with a tone that made Rhea put the copper under the bed like a woman hiding sin in a drawer, and made Kessa put a hand to her mouth as if catching a letter she had almost uttered. “You have a visitor with a nose,” Latch called, which was Sluice for someone who smelled like authority even when they were trying to reek of fish. “She says she’s here to sell tomatoes. She looks like a librarian that decided to lie and enjoyed the discovery.”

The woman at the threshold carried the weather in with her like an apology, the kind that isn’t sorry but is nevertheless polite. She wore a coat that had tasted rain and she had hands too clean to have wired a room but eyes that had watched someone do it without blinking. When she smiled, it was to her own joke. “They call me Root,” she said, offering a name like a dare disguised as a gift, and Rhea did not flinch because flinching is also a message. “I am not here as the Orchard. I am here as the part of the Orchard that doesn’t like what the Orchard calls the Orchard when anyone is listening.”

“You came across on the ferry,” Rhea said, making it a fact and a question at once, as if the river had rung to announce her. “You drank tea too hot and let it burn you to make yourself think. You carry a patch in your pocket meant for someone else, and you will not use it because you discovered it works and you hate it for that.”

Root’s mouth did a little corner trick like a cat whose tail had been stepped on. She held up her hands palm-out and showed the absence of the Orchard’s rings in her fur, hands unbadged, fingers without their usual jewelry of access. “I brought myself,” she said, and sat in the chair in the corner as if accepting an invitation she had not received. “You took something we were going to hurt without killing, and you ate it enough to keep it, and I came to say two things. One: I am sorry that you had to do it. Two: Tell me if there is a way I can help without making a rope for later.”

Kessa looked at the woman and saw the orangutan she was, the long weak-strong hands and the depth of patience that made eyes look like wells, and also saw the mechanism behind those eyes, the little gears of politics catching on one another with a care that can only be practiced at the cost of sleep. Rhea stood with her hand on the back of the chair like a person leaning into an old habit, and she looked at the coat, not the face, because sometimes coats tell you more than faces do. Root laughed, just once, a clean sound that did not pretend to be anything else, and then she told them a story about a boy who had learned to mimic himself until the Orchard had to decide which version of him to treat as real, and how all the versions cried, and how she had learned to be tired of that.

“The patch you sent taught pears to say sorry with grace,” Kessa said, because an argument should be introduced to the person who made its clothes. “It was cruel the way poems can be cruel when they are too good. I am not telling you to stop writing. I am telling you to imagine a pronoun other than we.”

“We used to have I for such occasions,” Root said, and the way she said used confused the room for a second until it understood she had meant the world, not her Troop. “I tried a softer fence. The Board reads softer as hunger and hunger as weakness, and weakness as something they can feed until it becomes loyalty. I am tired of teaching metaphors how to behave. I came to say: if you go to the edge, go where the Orchard is a rumor on a hill, not a kiosk with a smile. There are Troops that like to count. There are Troops that like to listen. I will tell the listeners to practice their craft. I will tell the counters to take a day off they can blame on the city.”

Rhea wanted to hate her because hating is a coat you put on when you leave the house to do something reckless, but she found she could not quite manage it, and that annoyed her enough that her voice sharpened. “They sent you to make a deal they can break as soon as it is inconvenient,” she said, and she did not make it a question because why give anyone the grace of pretending they had options they did not. “I will not make deals with trees that have already decided who is allowed under their shade. If you want to help, tell your hand at the depot that he miscounted yesterday and should be embarrassed. Tell the fruit to go quiet on one band and loud on another so the kids who love them can get three nights of sleep without feeling like traitors. Tell your drones to develop a phobia of rain.”

“I am telling you the ferry owes me,” Root said, acknowledging her inability to argue Rhea into a posture she had not chosen, and behind the dryness there was a warmth that made Kessa feel like she was standing near a library in winter. “I am telling you there are men at the edge who have keys to cabinets they do not respect. I am telling you if you need to slip a seed into a sleeping drawer, slip it into the ones with the brass—my people hate touching brass, it clings to oil. I am telling you a name that will get you bread at a place that likes to pretend names do not exist. Ask for Basalt. He will give you the face he gives to enemies and then he will feed you because he cannot help himself.”

They did not shake hands because there is a category of agreement that both parties prefer to leave without fingerprints. Root stood without making the chair complain and looked at Kessa as if Kessa had been a story she meant to read last week and had only now found time for. “The pears,” she said, as if beginning a new topic and an old one at once. “Let them have ankles. The Orchard doesn’t count ankles yet. If the pears become trees, I will make an argument at the Board about roots, and if that fails, I will tell my Troop to sit on their hands for a day and we will count that as a strike while the others count it as a joke.”

After Root left, Latch stuck her head around the door like a cat checking the weather and said, “She smelled like real tea and ethics. Be careful. Both leave rings that don’t scrub out.” Then she went down the hall calling for someone to carry a crate that had decided it wanted to believe in gravity and Rhea and Kessa stood for a moment together in the room’s center, as if the floor might tell them what to do next if they gave it time to develop an opinion.

They went out into the Sluice again because sometimes air that has been in other people’s lungs afterwards is the best kind. The boy with the scarred eyebrow had moved his candle cart to a better draft and now sold wicks that made promises about memory in a handwriting that was surprisingly round. Rhea bought the beeswax candle without pretending to be superstitious and set it on the windowsill with the ivy because even ivy deserves ceremonies, and Kessa bought a spool of thin copper wire so fine it felt like hair and then threaded it through her own hair because the girl with the wire braids smiled like a conspirator and friendships are sometimes built on nothing but mirror. They walked past a stall where a man played chess with himself and lost and said “good game” anyway, and past a woman who mended clothes you could not see were broken, and past the table where someone let you borrow a name for an afternoon in exchange for a story you promised not to tell to anyone who would use it to make you smaller.

The Orchard came to the Sluice that afternoon in the form of a weather advisory about wind that did not exist yet and a reminder to hydrate properly that made the Sluice as a body decide to drink beer and coffee just to be contrary. A drone tested the boundary of the market and then withdrew like a man who discovered the party had its own music. Rhea watched it through the gap between her eyelashes and thought about the mesh’s song and how long its melody would hold before the Orchard tuned its ear to sing along. Kessa took one of the pears for a walk in the machine on a cord like a leash and the pear tripped twice and did not say sorry either time, and she kissed the heel of her hand because that was where her mother had kissed her once and she did not want to give that to the Orchard either.

Night fell into the Sluice like a woman who could not decide whether she would stay and have one more drink or go home and grow up. Latch lit a string of bulbs that should have given up already and refused to out of pride, and the leather-smell of the tannery climbed as the heaters woke and the wire-smoke of soldering irons turned companionable instead of sharp. Rhea finished her cage in the old exchange and tethered it to the bed with a wire that wasn’t visible unless you knew what the light should have looked like, and she laid the copper inside and waited with her hand on the mesh until her own heat had told the system that the world had changed but not broken. Kessa wrote a note she would never send to a school that would no longer take her, and in it she asked if anyone there had ever taught anyone to be kind when no one is watching and if not whether she could teach a class, and then she tore it up and fed it to the ivy with a solemnity that made the plant seem to sit up straighter.

They slept and woke and slept again in the ways that happen when you have to be both flight and nest, and in the morning Latch knocked and told them that the goat had decided to be fond of them and would block anyone taller than Rhea from the stairs for a fee paid in cabbage. They paid in cabbage and in a promise to fix the drip on the third landing and in returning the plate Latch had loaned them empty and clean, with a square of bread wrapped in paper set on top like a reversal of the usual tribute. The second day they dedicated to leaving shadows that did not look like themselves and to making sure that the Sluice would not be asked to pay their bill in a currency it did not respect.

They took the long way to the old coal shed at the edge of the yards where the rails forgot their pride and lay down, and they found Basalt not by his face but by the way the people around him did not say his name, the kind of quiet that has been earned by doing the right thing too loudly once and then learning. He had shoulders that had negotiated more with loads than with men and a gentleness that some people mistake for meekness until they are corrected. He sold them sacks of bolts that would never complain about their purpose and a roll of insulating tape that had seen wars, and when Rhea said the word Root without looking at him, he grunted as if swallowing a curse and then laughed without joy.

“You are paved with helpful people,” Basalt said, which sounded like admiration until you tasted the salt. “You’re leaving stains as you go. It is all right. This place has known worse. If you need to tuck something living into something that people have decided is dead, I have drawers who like to dream. I will not ask what the thing is. I will not pretend I don’t want to. But I will advise you to use the second row from the top, because the top gets the heat and the bottom gets the rats and both are politics.”

They went that evening to the drawers and opened one together the way couples open windows in old movies, and they laid the copper down and listened to the drawer accept the weight with a sigh that was pure wood. They shut it gently with the respect any small tomb deserves and then they stood facing one another and let the air fill with the comfort of having placed a burden somewhere it could not be reached by hands disguised as help. Basalt leaned in the doorway and watched the way people watch babies sleep and did not make a joke because sometimes jokes make light in places that need weight to hold fast.

When they returned to the Rafter, the goat snored and the market’s song had lowered in pitch and the wires along the ceiling looked like rivers on a map that had been redrawn to suit no empire anyone could name. Kessa took the pears for another walk and made them step over an imaginary line and back and rewarded them with a new verb, not a new sorry. Rhea repaired the leak on the third landing and left no note because the sound of water persuading wood is its own language of gratitude, and Latch left on their bed a piece of paper with a grid of days and a tiny X two mornings from now and the sentence “the ferry will be thinking kindly of strangers” written in a hand that could have forged a treaty, and they did not say out loud that they had been given a window because windows prefer not to be named when they are doing you a favor.

On the final morning the Sluice woke in a mood to bargain with weather and to pretend it never lost. Inland respects recipes. If inland tells you a joke, laugh twice. The second time is for the person who wrote it down later and made a profit.”

Rhea laughed as requested and touched Thaw’s elbow because you should touch people you intend to like while you have the chance. Kessa, impulsively and because she couldn’t help her generosity when it took the shape of nonsense, took one of her pears to the rail and let it practice stepping up and down as if there were a curb there whose feelings needed to be spared, and the ferry woman watched with a sidewise smile and did not say good luck because good luck is a god and no one needs to be on the hook with gods this early.

On the far side they crossed not to the sheds but to a road that had the decency to admit itself: wheelmarks where the world had been forced to learn a groove, ruts and repairs and the accompanying biographies of boots. The Orchard’s tone was quiet here, like a gossip who has run out of appetite, and the air turned its jacket collar up and looked agreeably bored. Rhea adjusted the coil two inches in its sling because she had dreams of her back later that night and liked to bargain ahead. Kessa slipped the wrapped package inside her bag where it could learn to be carried without complaint, and then she took the pencil and wrote two lines under her cuff that would wash, a habit she had learned from Yen when she needed to hide a thing and still have it in sight.

The filtration fields received them without ceremony. The lungs they had rehearsed yesterday had decided to be shy now, showing the angles of the basins in that artful way buildings have when they are trying to be forgiven for their function. The hatch in the center basin accepted Rhea’s presence like an old friend who had been boring for twenty years and didn’t mind. Down inside, the wooden chamber smelled the same and different, a signature with a new flourish, brass winking in the half-light as if to tease anyone who came without proper allegiance. Rhea nested the coil with the drawer and wove the mesh around them again with the patience that was now a skill, not just personality, and she set the copper in the center because being in the middle calmed certain things that had decided they ought to be central.

It was not a ritual so much as a sequence. She held the copper with both palms and her mouth arranged itself into vowel shapes without checking in with her tongue. Kessa stood at the threshold of the chamber so that she could watch the door and her aunt with the same amount of devotion, and she whispered to the pears that this was important and then, in the same breath, that everything they did together was important and that was why boredom mattered so fiercely. The coil answered the copper with a hiccup of old current, the drawer answered with a low acknowledgment only brass can manage, the mesh threw its fringe of interference over the whole like a shawl, and Rhea introduced her niece’s seed to a new logic with the respect she gave a valve she had to persuade. “You are not gone,” she said, because this needed to be said out loud despite their plan to never give words more power than function. “You are persuaded elsewhere.”

Above them, the Orchard’s tone tilted its head and tried to decide if it should knock, whistle or write a postcard. Root’s people had left the river alone for the morning because she had finally exhausted her authority in a way that felt like freedom, and the hand in the scarf had written two emails that would not be read by anyone who could translate them into kindness and one he would regret later because it read as if he had asked for help. The patch that wanted to be called amnesty had begun to clone itself where the lights were warmer, but in the cold under the hatch words like “window” and “clemency” lost their shine and were reduced to grammar, and grammar is punctual but not persuasive.

When they climbed back into the day Rhea tied the towel around the copper and slid it home and checked her wrists for any evidence of speech. Kessa touched the wood around the hatch where she had drawn a pear on the inside and felt the warmth of that silly pledge as if someone else had taken it seriously, and she rolled her shoulders to remind herself of the body she was wearing. They set their faces to the road inland with expressions they had learned from their forbears: practical, attentive, braced for being underestimated.

A woman met them with a cart full of cloth that had once been sails and now presented itself as blankets, her hair pinned like a map the wind had tried to take and failed. She introduced herself as Weft and explained, looking at Kessa’s hair with the copper threaded through, that she ran a quilt of sorts that the Orchard had been trying to catalog for eight months and failed because she refused to make two squares alike. “We listen to weather and we listen to roofs,” Weft said, making eye contact with Rhea and then with the coil in that uncanny way certain sorts of women have. “We mend drafts and we train drafts to say thank you. I have three pieces that sing interference at a pitch drones mishear as lunch. If you throw one over a window and one over a worry, you will sleep.”

They traded honest exchange for the quilts, honest meaning that Weft asked for work and not money and Rhea pledged to fix the pump at the edge of her camp that had cultivated a squeak. Kessa chose a square woven of tarp and tablecloth in a pattern that looked like a map if you stood too close and like rain if you stood back, because she liked objects that told two stories and refused to pick a favorite. Weft nodded her approval at the pears in Kessa’s pocket as if they were relatives she admired for their terrible habits and then she lifted a corner of her cart to show them a roll of wire that had not learned to corrode. “If inland does what inland does,” she said, “and someone asks you to choose between tall and quiet, pick quiet, and then make it tall with your backs.”

They walked the road with the quilts folded flat as oaths. The hills inward were not hills so much as decisions the land had made in a hurry and then refused to apologize for. There were places where a culvert had been taught to sing by children who had learned that bending your back over a pipe can make a song richer, and places where someone had scratched a calendar into stone because moving time onto a wall makes it easier to argue with. Rhea pointed left and right at all the places where pressure would take a leak and become art, and Kessa made her pencil remember every left and right because memory does not owe performance but it likes to show off.

The Orchard’s hand sent ahead of them a van with tires too clean to belong here and men with shoes that clicked, and the van tried to become a fact by being loud about its benignities. It pulled alongside a family of goats and their goat-man and asked them in a voice that wanted to sell mops whether they felt safe and whether a fruit could come into their home and ask them questions about identity hygiene. The goat-man looked at the goats and the goats looked at the goat-man and then all of them decided to walk away at the same time and the van drove on with a face trying not to be injured. Root’s Troop that liked to listen redirected the drone that followed the van toward a flock of starlings that had been working on a poem of their own, and for fifteen useful minutes the Orchard fell in love with birds and forgot to ask after girls who were walking inland with promise in their pockets.

On the second afternoon, when the quilts had become blankets and the blankets had become a habit and the coil had settled into travelling like an old soldier who resents the metaphor, they came upon the Quarry Libraries. They had been a rumor before, a phrase thrown around by men who liked to brag about reading to women who knew how to fix a roof. The quarry had been a wound and then an amphitheater and then a series of rooms where damp was an argument and light was an act of generosity. Women with hands like lichen led them down a set of steps that had been honed by other feet and said nothing loud. “Spines,” one of them said, which here meant shelves, and then “tongues,” by which she meant bookmarks, and Kessa was happy in a way that did not surprise her because rooms that love paper are rooms that love girls who write nonsense until it makes sense.

They asked for nothing and offered shelter as if it were a new genre of library card. Rhea ran her fingers along the stone walls with an honest hunger; the archives smelled like rain in winter and sweat in summer and the residue of so many neglected pamphlets that if you put your ear to the rock and listened you would hear the city’s history edited by women who had not been given keys and took them anyway. The women with the lichen hands had a cabinet of blank forms that had been printed for a city that no longer needed them and a stamp shaped like the moon, and they lent Kessa a room to sit in with two pears she wanted to coax into silence while teaching them a game with pebbles, and they gave Rhea a ledger she could write names into and never show to anyone and still be warmed by it later when she needed to remember what she owed.

The Orchard’s apology for the ward had followed them in an envelope addressed to “current occupant” as if anonymity were a kindness, and when Rhea opened it the paper tried to stick to her fingers the way guilt does. It promised not to count you if you stepped into a survey for kindness they were conducting about the community’s feelings toward diverging identities, and it came with a voucher for a bag of oranges at a stall that had never been allowed to sell anything but oranges, and Rhea put the paper near a candle and watched how greedily flame took to that kind of language. The librarian with the lichen hands smiled with an aunt’s violence and brought out a dish of salt and cracked it over the envelope for the pleasure of hearing the sound it made when it gave up.

That night Rhea told Kessa a story from before the pears, from before the sister’s lungs failed in a city that hadn’t stopped bragging all that week about clean air and opportunity while counting women to make sure they looked like diversity and not like a strike. She told her about the sound a pipe makes when it admits it needs a gasket, about the first time she heard the Orchard’s tone and mistook it for a train only to realize it was the world pretending to be helpful, about the night her hands learned that if you don’t put the wrench down when you cry, the wrench understands. Kessa listened with the ferocity of someone stealing fire and when the story ended she pressed her forehead to the coil because it felt like a part of the family then and because it hadn’t asked for the role and performed anyway.

On the third morning inland announced itself with frost that had the honesty to paint everything with its intentions. The quilts cracked when they were folded because even fabric has knees. They took the path Basalt had drawn for them with graphite, a line that revealed itself when rubbed with a thumb as if the map had been shy but not coy, and they crossed a bridge that had learned to hold itself together with mystery and rebar. The bridge had a plaque that had been sanded down to blank by the wind, and someone had written in a nail “don’t look down unless you are teaching a child not to,” and Rhea laughed because a good tip is a form of art. The Orchard did not bother them on the bridge because even the apes respect certain geometry when there are witnesses, and Root drank her tea with her breath, long and slow, as if she could persuade a program to bend by making her own lungs behave.

It was at the high meadow beyond the bridge that they discovered the man who had learned to live without names for so long he had become one. He sat by a post that used to hold a gate and held a harmonica in his palm and a book in his lap and his face in a way that suggested he had decided to keep it. He called himself Yarrow when Thaw introduced them later by way of the code for decent places—she had not been there, had not seen it, but the world arranges that you meet all at once when it suits both parties—and he knew how to flip a breaker in a building with his eyes closed and how to describe the flight of a bird without ever making it smaller. He watched the coil as if it were someone else’s child and then looked at Kessa and seemed to take his hands off a thought.

“Root sent me by accident years ago,” he said, harmonica still in hand, voice not apologizing for the rasp. “She meant to send me to a room of glass and I found stone and stayed. They have been trying to hold each other accountable ever since. I can show you a valve you can lay a seed under and no one will ask you to leave it, not because no one looks there, but because everyone does and it has made them lazy. This place has learned to be found. The Orchard’s eyes get bored before the truth happens.”

They followed him to a cutoff of the aqueduct where the land did a small bow and the water had to choose between two clerks. The valve sat there like a piece of antique furniture someone had left outdoors, beautiful for no reason that helpfully defended itself. Rhea put her hand to it and liked it instantly because it had decided to be stubborn on the day it was made and had never been dissuaded. If you looked at its bolts you could see the thumbprints of men who had sworn at it and eventually apologized for swearing. It was the kind of citizen any city deserves but doesn’t always recruit.

“We can mouth from here,” Rhea said, tasting the metal with her tongue’s tip because she believed in rituals you could pretend later were science if anyone with money asked. “We can build a second mouth adjacent to the one they already trust. We can keep the seed in the drawer and teach the pipe to taste it and repeat it as needed. We can make a name that buys bread and pays for buses and doesn’t invite speeches.”

“It will ask for a tax,” Yarrow observed, examining the frost along the gasket with a doctor’s eye and a god’s patience. “Brass asks for the oil love leaves. Copper asks for salt. The Orchard asks for apologies. Choose your tax and pay it up front.”

Rhea set out her pouch on the stone and took from it the grimy necessities of a life that refuses magic only because magic refuses to be honest—tape and paste and a knife with an insult in the handle. Kessa anchored the quilts against the wind so that when the mesh started to sing its humble songs they wouldn’t carry as far as a drone could mistake them for an advertisement. Yarrow kept time by moving his foot an inch, the way men who understand pressure do when they are pretending not to be impatient, and in the world across the fields Root left her desk to look at a real tree, closed her eyes, and apologized to it for involving it in metaphors all week.

It took hours because anything important insists on being the length of a day. Rhea polished the ring’s inside with salt she had told a story to until it behaved like sand, and she soldered a new loop with wire that had been patient in Weft’s cart and she set ferrite along it like beads on a child’s wrist and she coaxed a wire from the valve’s throat without making it flinch. She spoke to each joint until it admitted its nature and to each gasket until it confessed what it needed not to squeal and she left a small ribbon of cloth knotted in a place only her future hand would find. Kessa held the copper up between her palms until her wrists hummed with the boredom that means work and she put her mouth near it and promised it nothing except the honest company of a woman who would repair what broke as long as she could reach it. When they were done the mouth they had made did not look like anything important. It looked like a small argument tucked into the conversation of a great structure. It took in air and did not gossip about it.

When they tested it, it did what mouths do when they have been taught correctly: it admitted hunger and then learned to ask for bread in a way that did not make the baker suspicious. Rhea extended the loop through a basket she had bought in a market that had pretended to sell only onions and the basket said to the world in the Orchard’s language that it contained enough identity to justify the currency and the world shrugged and agreed and the woman at the stall took coins she hadn’t been trained to admire and gave back change as if it were everyone’s idea to participate in this unadorned transaction. Kessa’s name did not appear on any glass. It undermined no sauce. It scored no spectacle. It warmed Rhea’s pocket and sat in the drawer and made a sound only Rhea could hear when she put two fingers to the mesh.

They slept in a stone room under the quarry that night because the stone had gotten used to stories and would not tell on them. The librarian brought them a bedroll that used to be a sail and a wedge of cheese that had listened to too many jokes and hardened accordingly, and she kept their pears for an hour because she had always had a weakness for difficult fruit. The coil dreamed in harmonics that would have sounded like uninteresting city noise to anyone who wasn’t both tired and happy, and the copper cooled and then remembered to warm again so that Rhea didn’t wake in the dark with that thought that makes you sit up, fists closed, heart trying to explain itself to your body. Kessa lay on her side and watched the cave’s ceiling where someone had scratched an arithmetic no longer in use and taught her mouth to say numbers she had never met, making room in her head for the things she didn’t know she would need.

The next day bore them toward a town that had been named thrice and finally refused the third because it wanted to remain unremarkable. It had a pump-run bakery where old men argued about yeast and politics with equal confidence and a clinic that had trained itself to keep two sets of books because sometimes the Orchard’s tone came in wanting to be charmed. Rhea and Kessa stood in line like everyone else. The line was a story and a song and an exercise, and the man behind them told a joke that had been told to him by someone who wanted to be charming about identity and failed. The woman in front of them wore her baby like a second shirt and smelled like milk and worry and bread. When it was their turn, Rhea set the basket down and let the mouth do its work. The register coughed and then gave up and rang and Kessa added three coins because cash makes a sound even the Orchard enjoys in its pretense of being municipal. No one applauded. No one asked a question. It was the best kind of victory she had ever obtained.

News travels by haircut as much as by wire. In the back of the bakery a girl with her hair newly cut like a puzzle into which she had decided to fit, stood and watched the women who had suddenly become possible. That afternoon a boy with a clean shirt he had not ironed carried a message to Yarrow that the Orchard had rolled out a new survey in schools called Safe Passages and the survey had included a question about pears. That night, in the glass room, Root watched Archive draw a fruit wrong on a whiteboard and knew he had never learned to peel anything he did not intend to throw away. She excused herself on the pretense of a headache and went to the roof and listened to a pair of pigeons argue with love about a ledge. She found herself jealous of birds for the first time in two years and laughed quietly at the primness of the thought.

On the road again Rhea asked Yarrow about his name and he told her his mother had meant to call him Arrow and changed her mind halfway through and never apologized. He said the advantage of being named something that sounds like a plant is people expect you to bend and when you don’t they’re pleasantly surprised and go easier on you the second time. He had, it turned out, once been in love with an archivist who filed their fights under Weather and he had stayed long enough to learn what categories do to tenderness and what they don’t. He asked Kessa about pears and she answered as if he had asked about attachments, not fruit, and he nodded in that way that doesn’t pretend knowledge, only finds pleasure in learning.

They came to a causeway blown flat across a marsh that wanted to be water again and saw the Orchard’s van stuck at an angle that suggested it had asked the marsh a question the marsh had declined to answer. Two men in shirts that had been designed with breathability in mind and self-knowledge as optional stood with clipboards that had been taught to feel like shields and said words into devices that did not deserve to be called radios. Rhea took the long way because in situations like this long is a virtue. Kessa pretended the pears needed to pee, which is a ridiculous phrase and an excellent tactic when you are trying to be unremarkable. Yarrow lifted his harmonica just enough to let the Marsh hear it had options and the Marsh loosened its hold a little on the van for the sheer fun of participating.

The man with the scarf sprang on them like the idea of due process. He came around the back of the van with a face that had been tuned to reasonable and a scarf that had been tuned to expensive and he smiled in a way that made the air do math. “We are still eager to learn the lesson you intend for us,” he said to Rhea, like a man who had been warned that metaphors were going around and didn’t want to catch one. “The Board appreciates cooperation. Amnesty remains a possibility if we can verify no harm was intended. The city believes in second chances and we would like to be anthropomorphic about it.”

Rhea let her eyes rest on his scarf because making a man think about his own neck is a kind of leverage. “You are very good at learning the names of things and telling them back to me,” she said, as if she had been handed a dish she already owned. “If you want to exchange work for work I have a valve that would make you weep with gratitude and a drain that eats misunderstandings. If you want me to tell you I meant no harm, I will tell you something better: I mean to keep what is mine. The city can belief as it pleases.”

“The law is a kind of story we all agree to,” he said, and he believed it enough that his tone wanted to be a bridge anyone could walk across without falling in. Behind him the marsh coughed and took an inch more of the van because arrogance tastes like salt to certain kinds of water and encourages appetite. He made a decision then that he would regret later when alone with a mirror: he gestured to one of the men with ring-lanyards and the man stepped toward Kessa without his face deciding how it felt about what his feet were doing. The pears in Kessa’s pocket went quiet like birds in a hedge and Kessa looked at Rhea with a face that was both nineteen and older than anyone has the right to be.

“Don’t,” Rhea said, and the word had in it all the plumbing she had ever forced to behave and all the nights she had kept a child’s lungs clear by sheer profanity and the day she had turned a wrench while laughing because the alternative was to break down in a client’s bathtub. The man with the ring-lanyard heard the voice and remembered a mother he had been cruel to at sixteen and stepped back because he believed, briefly, in a god and then realized it had been a woman and blushed. The hand in the scarf tried to salvage the moment with a joke about pears and pitfalls and recognized a failure when the joke died between his lips. He stepped aside the way a man steps aside when he has remembered he is not alone in a room and the other people are not chairs.

They passed. They walked the causeway without slipping because they had decided not to and because the Marsh liked their ankles and respected their rate of travel. Yarrow picked a tune on the harmonica just quiet enough to make wind jealous and the wind came and took the envy and turned it into a better walking day. The van made a distressed noise behind them that would have earned Rhea’s pity on a different morning and the hand in the scarf rearranged his mouth and told himself he had won because he meant to file a report that said so. Root, reading nothing but tea leaves and clouds, let herself rest her forehead on a pane of glass and thought about changing jobs and then refrained because the jobs she didn’t want did not deserve the fantasy of her competence.

That evening they stopped in a town with a clock that had refused to be punctual since the year everyone had believed in a war that didn’t start on time. They booked a corner in a boarding house that smelled like cooked onions and ghosted laundry and Rhea hung the quilts Weft had given them over the window and the door at an angle she had learned from making Mole traps and from Harrow’s forgotten room. Kessa took the pears out and let them practice stairs with all the seriousness of a ballet mistress and a woman with a baby on her hip laughed until her shoulders remembered they could, and the sound went into the wood and made it a better house. The copper’s seed glowed against Rhea’s chest now sometimes without excuse and she made a note to ask Basalt if he believed in inheritance and if so whether brass could pass down certain opinions without bothering with genetic metaphors.

At midnight the Orchard tried something that Root had voted against and lost. It sent three drones over the sword of the valley and down into the town with their tone turned to a pitch that has made children cry for centuries when ill-played on violin. They wove a net where a net wasn’t wanted and the rhythm of their light tried to create permission where none had been offered. The quilts over the windows hummed with a pleasure that could have been mistaken for smugness if it had learned manners; the ferrite in the mesh shivered and then settled. The coil under the bed, which had spent all day learning the grammar of this building’s bones by listening to the way a loose screw in the stair insisted on its syllable, took the tone and turned it down the way you turn the volume with your elbow when you can’t be bothered to be polite, and the drones faltered the way dancers do when the wrong song comes on and left to look for music elsewhere.

Kessa woke and looked at Rhea and then at the small banked glow where the copper lay in its towel and made a face like someone who has decided it is time to learn to be brave for the person who taught them bravery looks like work, not theater. “I keep wanting to make them smarter,” she said, and the pronoun covered pears and girls and Aunties and maybe the Orchard itself if you were feeling ungenerous. “But I don’t want smart to translate into obedient. I want ankles and boredom and jokes. I want legible mean something other than legible to the people who cut IDs.”

“Then we’ll teach them to read the opposite way,” Rhea said, sitting up so the bed would stop complaining about sleep when someone’s weight changed and rubbing her eyes as if to roughen up any sweetness the night might try to press on her. “We’ll teach them to read knots and steam and lint. We’ll teach them to read the timing of kettles and the language of windows. We’ll tell them counting is something you do for bread, not for permission. And we’ll fail. And then we’ll do it again with a different verb until one sticks.”

Morning brought a letter neither of them had expected and exactly one of them had hoped for. It came with a loaf of bread that had learned to be polite by being present and a piece of paper that smelled like river and electricity both because Root had found an excuse to go to the ferry and pretend her hands had never known anything but oars. The letter did not say apologies or amnesties or anything with an initial capital except “Basalt,” which in Root’s hand looked for one second like a saint. It said that the Board had declared a day of outreach which meant their hand would try to buy people with oranges and entertainment and that in the midst of it there would be a corridor you could walk if you carried something that chimed like brass and which drones had been told to interpret as an exemption for heavy machinery. It was an ugly kindness, the sort Root enjoyed because it didn’t require lying to itself too loudly.

They chose to use it because sometimes you are given a key and it is a shame to insult the locksmith by not trying the door. Yarrow met them at the farms where the wind had been taught to carry wheat and not stories and showed them where the corridor would slant, an angle drones respect because angles do better math than people whose jobs depend on outshining geometry. They walked with the coil slung like a barrel and the copper warm under Rhea’s coat and the pears getting their steps wrong on purpose because they were learning comedy as a defense. The hand in the scarf saw them and told himself a story about needing to choose his battles and then told his men to log that the exemption had been granted for maintenance, and he went home with a headache that would be his child for two days.

At the far side of the corridor there was a stand called The Weir that sold fish nobody had caught and onions nobody had grown and paperwork that had once claimed to know who everyone in this town was. A woman behind the counter wearing a sweater that had learned to be loyal before the person inside it had learned to trust looked at the copper where it sat against Rhea’s chest as if looking at a brooch and decided not to see it and instead saw Rhea’s hands and priced accordingly. Kessa bought a pencil shaped like a fish with the realism of a woman buying a relic and the woman threw in a piece of twine because twine has saved more cities than speeches.

By evening they were up against the line where the Orchard’s leaves stop cutting the light and the hills begin counting their own distances. A man at a railroad crossing who had learned patience the way you learn sword-swallowing—by accident and then with ritual—told them where in the next town the water ran above the law and where it sulked below it. In the glass room, Root signed a paper without reading the preamble and told Archive to tell the Board she had an appointment with something called weather. In Thaw’s shed the stew made itself good behavior because it had learned the difference between this and the kind of day where eating counts as applause, and on the ferry the buoy sank for five beats just to stay supple.

They slept in a barn that had agreed long ago to harbor fewer secrets and more mice and Rhea thought, before her eyes closed, about the mouth she had made at the valve and how it would behave under snow. She thought about the first time she had said her sister’s name under a pipe and believed the pipe could carry it to someone who needed to hear it and how foolish and holy that felt. Kessa dreamed of pears growing roots like a joke gone too far and waking glad because in the dream the roots were not prison but choice, and she told herself to keep that geometry close in case someone asked her to explain what she wanted to do with all this bravery.

At dawn the Orchard did the thing big organisms do when their sense of themselves is threatened: it tried to be sentimental. It sent out a wave of fruit on screens blinking in kitchens and statutory offices and bus depots, little papayas and pears and clever citrus with hats, and it asked them to sing a song about apology it had written yesterday. The kids who had taught pears ankles snorted and taught each other to turn off the song at precisely the same bar so the silence afterward felt like a joke with a secret handshake. The librarians in the quarry made a game of stamping the letterheads the Orchard sent with a stamp that said “We Hope You Are Eating Enough,” and all day women did, in fact, feed each other, because spite becomes tender when it meets hunger.

Somewhere midday, at a pump that had forgotten modesty, Rhea learned the satisfaction of watching a mouth she had built ask for water by name and be answered without paperwork. She pressed her palm to the metal and felt, more than heard, the seed accept this arrangement in that way seeds have of consenting by growing, and she let herself be moved just scrim-thin because anything more would lead to words and words were not the tool she wished to take out of her kit just then. Kessa lined up the pears on the fence rail and let them count birds and they cheated and then reported their cheating without shame, and she thought they might yet become the kind of citizens she could stand to argue with.

They would go next to the place Yarrow called the Sluice of Inland, which of course was not called that by anyone who lived there, but which endured with a similar stubborn generosity. They would meet a woman named Spindle who trained radios to lie in ways that protected children and men who believed in rope tricks more than they believed in taxes and a boy who could speak to bricks because he had done masonry long enough to respect every thumbprint left in clay. They would learn to step across a creek without exciting it into boasting and to ask a church for silence without paying in prayer. The Orchard would learn they had chosen not to return its affection and would decide to be jealous of the weather, which is the funniest jealousy a bureaucracy can admit to, and Root would teach her Troop to watch cloud bases for signals, which would accidentally make two storms safer for fishermen that week.

That night, their bed was a pallet in a stranger’s attic and their window opened onto a roof that had held up entire families and expected no credit. Rhea, exhausted enough to be honest, put the copper between them not like a talisman but like a responsibility and Kessa put her hand on it not to keep it from falling but to remind it that even unremarked-upon things are kept. The house ticked and breathed and forgave and the Orchard’s tone slid off its shingles like rain into a gutter where a leaf that should have clogged everything instead decided to be a boat. In her sleep, Rhea’s fingers moved as if tightening a bolt and Kessa’s mouth moved as if telling a joke, and the city dreamt whatever it dreamt when it had to admit it had been wrong about what it could count and how it could be forgiven.
