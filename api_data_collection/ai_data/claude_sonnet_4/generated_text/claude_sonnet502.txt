The world had gone to hell in ways Marcus Thorne never imagined possible when he first picked up a wrench at age twelve. Back then, engines were predictable things—pistons fired in sequence, fuel mixed with air, spark plugs ignited at precise moments. There was order to it, a mechanical poetry that made sense even when everything else in his life felt chaotic. Now, twenty-three years later, as he crouched behind the rusted hulk of what used to be a Peterbilt truck, watching shambling hordes stumble through the abandoned streets of New Haven, Marcus wondered if anything would ever make sense again.

The sound of dragging feet against broken asphalt created a symphony of decay that had become the soundtrack of existence. Somewhere in the distance, a car alarm wailed its electronic death song, probably triggered by another cluster of the undead pressing against metal and glass with their relentless, mindless hunger. Marcus adjusted his grip on the modified tire iron he carried—not much of a weapon, but it had served him well enough for the past three months since the collapse.

Their clothes hung in tatters, skin mottled with the distinctive gray-green pallor that marked their transformation. Some still retained enough coordination to walk upright, while others crawled or dragged themselves forward with whatever limbs still functioned. The fresh ones were the worst—they moved faster, retained more of their human cunning, and their hunger seemed more acute. These specimens looked older, their movements sluggish and predictable.

Marcus checked his watch, a habit that felt increasingly meaningless in a world where time had lost most of its relevance. Three forty-seven in the afternoon, though the perpetual overcast sky made it difficult to distinguish between day and night without consulting the timepiece. The weather had been strange ever since the outbreak began—not the cause of it, the scientists had insisted in their final broadcasts, but perhaps a consequence of whatever cosmic alignment or dimensional tear had allowed this plague to manifest.

His destination lay six blocks northeast: the Meridian Building, a gleaming testament to corporate excess that had once housed the regional headquarters of Blackstone Industries. Before the world ended, Marcus had never given much thought to corporate hierarchies or the machinations of men in expensive suits. His universe had revolved around the garage bay where he spent his days, the comfortable routine of diagnostics and repairs, the satisfaction of bringing dead engines back to life. The irony wasn't lost on him now—he'd devoted his career to resurrection, only to find himself in a world where the dead refused to stay buried.

The building's facade remained largely intact, though several windows on the lower floors had been boarded up with plywood and steel sheets. Someone had taken refuge there, which meant his intelligence was correct. Director Harrison Blackwell, former head of Blackstone's research division and one of the few individuals who might possess answers about the outbreak's origins, was allegedly holed up on the forty-second floor with whatever remained of his security detail.

Marcus had no particular desire to kill Director Blackwell. In fact, he found the very concept repugnant. Before the apocalypse, his most violent impulses had extended no further than occasionally cursing at particularly stubborn bolts or throwing tools when frustrated with a difficult repair. Murder was the domain of criminals and soldiers, not mechanics who preferred the company of engines to people. The more honorable the target, the greater the sword's power would become. And according to her research, Director Blackwell represented exactly the kind of individual whose death would unlock the weapon's capabilities.
