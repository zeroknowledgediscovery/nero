The morning fog clung to the cobblestone streets of Leeds like a reluctant memory, and Martha Henley pressed her face against the cool window of her third-floor flat, watching the world below move with purpose she no longer possessed. Her breath fogged the glass as she exhaled slowly, a habit she'd developed over the past six months since everything changed. The manuscript pages scattered across her desk behind her remained untouched, their edges curling slightly in the dampness that seemed to permeate everything in her life these days.

She hadn't always been afraid of heights. In fact, there was a time when Martha would climb the tallest trees in Roundhay Park just to see the city spread out beneath her like a patchwork quilt. Her childhood had been filled with adventures that took her to rooftops, climbing frames, and even the occasional church tower when the vicar wasn't looking. But that was before the accident, before the moment that split her life into a clear before and after.

It had been a Tuesday in March when she'd been researching her latest novel, a historical piece about the industrial revolution in Yorkshire. She'd climbed the stairs of an old textile mill that was being converted into luxury apartments, eager to capture the authentic atmosphere of the building's past. The developer had been kind enough to let her explore, understanding her need for authentic detail in her work. Martha had always prided herself on the meticulous research that went into her novels, the way she could transport readers to another time and place through carefully chosen words and vivid descriptions.

The staircase had been original to the building, a narrow spiral of iron and wood that creaked and groaned under her weight. She'd climbed steadily, counting the floors as she went, her notebook tucked safely in her jacket pocket and her camera hanging around her neck. The sound of construction work echoed through the empty building, a rhythmic hammering that seemed to match her heartbeat as she climbed higher and higher.

It was on the seventh floor that it happened. One moment she was reaching for the door handle, anticipating the view from the windows that would help her describe the working conditions of the mill workers, and the next moment the railing gave way beneath her hand. The sound of splintering wood and groaning metal filled her ears as she fell backwards, her arms windmilling desperately as she tried to regain her balance. Time seemed to slow as she watched the ceiling spin above her, and for a terrifying moment she was certain she would fall all the way down the stairwell.

But luck, or perhaps the intervention of some guardian angel, had been with her that day. She'd managed to grab onto a support beam just below the landing, her fingers finding purchase on the rough wood as her body swung out over the empty space. The drop below her had seemed infinite, a yawning chasm that threatened to swallow her whole. She'd hung there for what felt like hours but was probably only minutes, her arms burning with the effort of holding her weight, before the construction workers heard her cries for help and came running.

The rescue itself had been swift and professional. The workers had lowered a rope and helped her climb back to safety, their voices calm and reassuring as they guided her movements. But the damage had already been done. As Martha sat on the solid floor of the seventh landing, her whole body shaking with adrenaline and delayed shock, she realized that something fundamental had changed inside her. The confidence that had carried her through thirty-two years of life, the fearlessness that had taken her on adventures around the world in pursuit of stories, had evaporated in those terrifying moments hanging above the void.

Her writing, which had always been her refuge and her passion, began to suffer almost immediately. Her editor, a patient woman named Susan who had worked with Martha for nearly a decade, had initially been understanding.
