His younger sister Emma had fallen ill during the night, and he'd spent the early hours helping his mother tend to her fever. The McKinnon family had little money for physicians, so they relied on old remedies and prayer.

Something about her voice always made his heart skip irregularly, though he tried desperately to convince himself it was merely academic admiration. She was, after all, his instructor and several years his senior. At thirty-one, Adelaide Whitmore possessed an intelligence that sparkled in her emerald eyes and a grace that made even the most mundane discussions of literature feel like poetry itself.

She wore it pinned up in the fashion of educated women, but occasionally a rebellious strand would escape and frame her face. Today she wore a deep blue dress that complemented her complexion beautifully, though he chided himself for noticing such details.

"Mr. McKinnon," her voice cut through his reverie, "perhaps you could share your thoughts on the protagonist of 'The Corsair'?"

Lazarus's throat went dry. He hadn't been paying attention to the discussion, too lost in observing the teacher herself. "I... well, Conrad represents the romantic ideal of the outlaw hero, doesn't he? Someone who operates outside society's rules but maintains his own moral code."

Professor Whitmore's eyebrows raised slightly, and a small smile played at the corners of her mouth. "An interesting interpretation. And what do you think drives such a character to reject conventional society?"

"Perhaps," Lazarus said, finding his voice and confidence, "he's seen too much hypocrisy in the world. Maybe he believes that true honor exists outside the boundaries that others have created."

The other students chuckled softly, but Lazarus barely heard them. Professor Whitmore had called his observation excellent. She was looking directly at him with what seemed like genuine interest, and for a moment, the rest of the world faded away.
