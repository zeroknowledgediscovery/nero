Princess Lyra Windborne had always considered her gift more of a curse than a blessing. Growing up in the shadow of the Rocky Mountains in Fort Collins, she learned to keep her peculiar ability hidden from the world. The power to transform any living person into a puma with merely a focused thought was not exactly the kind of talent one discussed at royal tea parties or diplomatic gatherings.

The morning sun cast long shadows across the courtyard of Castle Windborne as Lyra practiced her swordwork alone. Each swing of her blade was precise, calculated, born from years of training under Master Aldric's watchful eye. Sweat beaded on her brow despite the cool Colorado morning air. She paused, lowering her weapon as she noticed a commotion near the castle gates.

"Your Highness!" called Sir Marcus, her most trusted knight and childhood friend. His usually composed demeanor seemed frayed at the edges as he approached with hurried steps. "We've received troubling news from the eastern provinces."

Lyra sheathed her sword and turned to face him fully. Marcus had been her confidant since they were children, one of the few people who knew about her transformation abilities. His concern always ran deeper than mere protocol.

"What kind of trouble?" she asked, accepting the leather-bound message he extended toward her.

"Princess Ravenna of Shadowmere has been making... aggressive moves. Three villages have been completely destroyed in the past fortnight. No survivors, no apparent motive." Marcus ran a hand through his dark hair, a gesture Lyra recognized as his tell when deeply worried.

Lyra broke the wax seal and unfolded the parchment. The words painted a grim picture of destruction that seemed almost senseless in its brutality. Entire communities reduced to ash and rubble, livestock slaughtered, crops burned in the fields. The reports came from multiple sources, all pointing to the same perpetrator.

"Ravenna," Lyra murmured the name like a prayer or perhaps a curse. She had heard whispers about the princess of Shadowmere over the years. Beautiful, intelligent, and reportedly completely without conscience or mercy. Where Lyra had been raised to protect and serve her people, Ravenna seemed to delight in causing suffering for its own sake.

"Father will want to discuss this immediately," Lyra said, already moving toward the castle's main entrance. Her boots clicked against the cobblestones with determination.

"Actually, His Majesty has requested that you handle this matter personally," Marcus replied, falling into step beside her. "He believes your... unique talents... might be particularly useful in dealing with someone like Princess Ravenna."

Lyra stopped walking. Her father King Edmund had always been supportive of her abilities, even when she struggled to control them in her younger years. But sending her alone to face someone capable of such destruction seemed like a significant leap of faith.

"He wants me to go alone?"

"Not alone. I'll accompany you, along with a small company of our best warriors. But yes, you would be leading this mission." Marcus placed a reassuring hand on her shoulder. "Your father believes that conventional methods won't work against someone like Ravenna. We need something unconventional."

The weight of responsibility settled on Lyra's shoulders like a familiar cloak. She had always known that someday her abilities would be called upon to serve her kingdom in a significant way. She just hadn't expected that day to come quite so soon, or under such dire circumstances.

"When do we leave?"

"Within the hour, if you're willing to accept the mission."

Lyra looked back across the courtyard toward the mountains that had sheltered and shaped her entire life. Fort Collins had been her sanctuary, the place where she learned to harness powers that could easily destroy rather than protect. Now those same powers might be the only thing standing between innocent people and a princess who seemed determined to spread chaos across the land.

"I'll need fifteen minutes to gather my things and say goodbye to Father," she said finally.

Marcus nodded approvingly. "I'll have the horses ready."

As Lyra made her way through the familiar halls of her childhood home, servants and guards bowed respectfully. Most of them had watched her grow from a troubled child who accidentally transformed the occasional palace cat into a puma, into a young woman who had learned to wield her gift with precision and purpose. Their faith in her felt both comforting and terrifying.

King Edmund was waiting in his study, surrounded by maps and reports. At fifty-three, he remained an imposing figure with steel-gray hair and eyes that missed nothing. He looked up as Lyra entered, and his expression softened from that of a ruler to that of a concerned father.

"You don't have to do this," he said without preamble. "I could send General Morrison with a full battalion instead."

"And how many soldiers would die trying to stop someone who can level entire villages?" Lyra approached the desk and studied the maps. Red marks indicated the locations of Ravenna's attacks, forming a pattern that seemed to be moving steadily westward. Toward Fort Collins.

"Perhaps none. We don't know the extent of her abilities."

"But we know the extent of mine." Lyra met her father's gaze steadily. "I can end this quickly and quietly. Ravenna may be powerful, but she's still human. Still vulnerable to transformation."

King Edmund stood and walked around the desk to embrace his daughter. "You sound so certain. But power like Ravenna's... it changes people. Makes them unpredictable. Dangerous in ways we can't anticipate."

"Then it's good that I have ways of dealing with dangerous people." Lyra returned the embrace, drawing strength from her father's presence. "I won't let her reach Fort Collins. I won't let her hurt our people."

"I know you won't." The king pulled back to look at her seriously. "But promise me you'll be careful. Promise me you'll remember that your life matters too. The kingdom needs you to come home."

"I promise, Father."

Twenty minutes later, Lyra found herself mounted on her favorite horse, a spirited mare named Tempest, surrounded by six of Fort Collins' finest knights. Marcus rode beside her, checking and rechecking his equipment with the thoroughness of a man who had seen real combat. The other knights - Sir David, Sir Thomas, Lady Catherine, Sir Robert, Sir James, and Sir William - were all veterans who had served the crown faithfully for years.As they rode through the castle gates and into the winding mountain roads that led eastward, Lyra found herself studying each of her companions. She had trained alongside most of them at various points, but leading them into actual danger felt entirely different from sparring in the practice yards. Sir David, with his graying beard and weathered face, had been protecting the royal family since before Lyra was born. His presence alone provided a sense of stability that helped calm her nerves. Lady Catherine was perhaps the finest archer in all of Fort Collins, her reputation for hitting impossible targets legendary among the castle guard. The younger knights - Thomas, Robert, James, and William - were all eager to prove themselves, though Lyra hoped their enthusiasm wouldn't override their caution when facing someone like Princess Ravenna.

The morning air grew warmer as they descended from the mountains toward the eastern plains. Lyra had always found something peaceful about traveling these roads, watching the landscape change from rocky peaks to rolling hills dotted with farms and small settlements. Today, however, the familiar journey felt heavy with purpose and potential danger. Every mile they covered brought them closer to an adversary whose capabilities remained largely unknown, save for the trail of destruction she had left in her wake.

"Tell me what else we know about Ravenna," Lyra said to Marcus as they crested a hill that offered a panoramic view of the valley below. "Beyond the obvious fact that she's dangerous."

Marcus consulted a leather journal he kept secured to his saddle. "According to our intelligence, she's approximately your age, maybe a year or two older. Raised in Shadowmere after her parents died when she was very young. The circumstances of their deaths are unclear, but rumors suggest it wasn't natural causes. She assumed control of Shadowmere at sixteen, which was unusually young even by royal standards. Her advisors initially tried to serve as regents, but three of them died under mysterious circumstances within the first year of her reign." He paused to let a merchant's wagon pass them on the narrow road before continuing. "Since then, she's ruled absolutely. No council, no advisors that we know of. Just her word as law."

"What about her abilities? Do we have any specifics about what she can actually do?" Lyra adjusted her grip on Tempest's reins as they navigated a particularly steep section of the mountain path.

"That's where the reports become... inconsistent." Marcus frowned at his notes. "Some witnesses claim she can control fire, others say she manipulates shadows. There are accounts of her appearing to be in multiple places at once, and at least two separate reports of her surviving injuries that should have been fatal. The problem is that most witnesses to her attacks don't survive to give detailed accounts."

Lady Catherine urged her horse closer to join the conversation. "Your Highness, if I may offer an observation? The pattern of her attacks suggests she's not just randomly destroying villages. Look at the locations she's targeted." She produced her own map, marked with additional notations. "Every single settlement she's hit has been strategically significant. Either they controlled important trade routes, or they were known for producing specific resources, or they served as communication hubs between larger cities."

Lyra studied Catherine's map with growing unease. The pattern was indeed too deliberate to be coincidental. "She's not just causing chaos for its own sake. She's systematically dismantling the infrastructure of the eastern provinces."

"Which raises the question of why," Marcus said grimly. "What does she gain by isolating the major cities from each other and cutting off their supply lines?"

Sir David, who had been riding silently ahead of the group, called back over his shoulder without turning around. "Makes them easier to conquer individually. Classic military strategy. Divide your enemies so they can't support each other, then pick them off one by one." His voice carried the weight of someone who had seen such tactics employed before.

The implications of this realization settled over the group like a cold mountain fog. If Ravenna was indeed planning a systematic conquest rather than simply spreading random destruction, then Fort Collins and the western provinces were not just in danger of eventual attack - they were likely the ultimate target of a carefully orchestrated campaign.

"How long do we have?" Lyra asked, though she dreaded the answer.

Marcus performed quick calculations based on the timing of previous attacks and the geographical distances involved. "If she maintains her current pace and continues westward, she could reach the outer settlements of our territory within two weeks. Fort Collins itself would be vulnerable within a month, assuming she doesn't accelerate her timeline."

"Then we stop her before she gets that far." Lyra's voice carried a determination that surprised even herself. "We track her down, and we end this threat before it reaches our people."

The road ahead stretched out before them, winding through valleys and over hills toward an uncertain confrontation. Lyra found herself unconsciously reaching out with her senses, that peculiar awareness that accompanied her transformation abilities. She could feel the life force of her companions around her, warm and steady. Further out, smaller signatures indicated wildlife in the surrounding forests - deer, rabbits, the occasional mountain lion that gave these peaks their dangerous reputation. Her power hummed quietly beneath her conscious thoughts, a constant presence that she had learned to live with over the years.

She wondered what Ravenna's power felt like. Did it burn constantly like a flame that could never be extinguished? Or did it lurk in the shadows of her mind, waiting to be called forth when destruction was needed? The reports suggested someone who wielded her abilities with the casual ease of long practice, which meant Ravenna had been honing whatever gifts she possessed for years. Lyra could only hope that her own training and the element of surprise would be enough to level the playing field when they finally met.As they made camp that evening in a sheltered grove beside a mountain stream, Lyra found herself unable to shake the growing sense of urgency that had been building throughout the day. The intelligence reports painted a picture of an adversary who was not only powerful but methodical in her approach to destruction. While her companions went about the familiar routines of setting up their temporary shelter and preparing the evening meal, Lyra walked to the edge of their campsite and stared eastward into the gathering darkness. Somewhere beyond those distant hills, Princess Ravenna was likely planning her next move, unaware that forces from Fort Collins were finally moving to intercept her. The thought should have been comforting, but instead it filled Lyra with a deep sense of foreboding.

Marcus approached quietly, carrying two bowls of the simple stew that Sir Robert had prepared over their small fire. He settled beside Lyra on a fallen log, offering her one of the bowls without speaking. The warmth of the food was welcome after the long day of riding, but Lyra found her appetite diminished by the weight of responsibility she carried. Around them, the sounds of the mountain evening began to emerge as the last traces of daylight faded from the sky. Owls called to each other from the surrounding pines, and somewhere in the distance a wolf howled, its voice carrying an almost mournful quality that seemed to echo Lyra's own troubled thoughts.

"You're worried about more than just the mission," Marcus observed after they had eaten in comfortable silence for several minutes. His ability to read her moods had always been uncanny, developed through years of friendship that had weathered the unique challenges of her unusual gifts. When they were children, he had been the first person outside her immediate family to witness one of her uncontrolled transformations, and his calm acceptance of what he had seen had helped shape her understanding of what she might become.

"I keep thinking about what Father said. About power changing people, making them unpredictable." Lyra set down her half-finished bowl and wrapped her cloak more tightly around her shoulders as the mountain air grew cold. "What if I'm not so different from Ravenna? What if the only thing that's kept me from becoming like her is the fact that I've had people around me who cared enough to guide me away from that path?" The question had been haunting her since they left Fort Collins, growing stronger with each mile they traveled toward their confrontation.

Marcus was quiet for a long moment, considering her words with the seriousness they deserved. Around their small camp, the other knights were settling in for the night, checking weapons and armor one final time before seeking what rest they could manage on the hard ground. The flickering firelight cast dancing shadows across their faces, creating an almost ethereal atmosphere that seemed appropriate for discussing the deeper mysteries of power and responsibility. Sir David was sharing quiet stories with the younger knights, his weathered voice carrying just far enough for them to hear the cadence of his words without making out the specific details.

"Do you remember the first time you successfully controlled a transformation?" Marcus asked finally. "You were maybe twelve years old, and that merchant's son had been bullying the kitchen staff. You were so angry that I could practically see the power building around you, but instead of lashing out blindly, you stopped. You took a breath, focused your intent, and when you did transform him, it was just long enough to give him a taste of helplessness. Then you changed him back and explained exactly why his behavior was unacceptable." Marcus turned to look at her directly, his expression serious in the firelight. "Ravenna might have power, but she's never learned restraint. That's what makes her dangerous, and it's also what makes you different from her."

The memory Marcus had chosen was a significant one, Lyra realized. It had been one of the first times she truly understood that her abilities could be used as tools of justice rather than weapons of destruction. The merchant's son, a boy named Frederick who had been visiting the castle with his father, had been systematically tormenting the younger servants with the casual cruelty that sometimes came with privilege. When Lyra witnessed him forcing a kitchen maid to clean up a mess he had deliberately created, something inside her had snapped. The transformation power had surged forward with an intensity that frightened her even now, years later. But instead of allowing it to consume her anger and potentially cause lasting harm, she had channeled it with surgical precision.

"I was terrified that day," Lyra admitted, surprised by her own willingness to voice the fear she had carried for so long. "Not of Frederick, but of myself. Of what I almost did to him. If you hadn't been there, if you hadn't reminded me that I had choices..." She let the sentence trail off, unwilling to complete the thought aloud. The image of what she might have become without proper guidance was too disturbing to fully contemplate, especially on the eve of confronting someone who represented exactly that kind of unchecked power.

"But I was there. Your father was there. Master Aldric, the servants who helped raise you, the people of Fort Collins who trusted you with their safety - we were all there." Marcus reached over and placed a reassuring hand on her shoulder. "Ravenna never had that kind of support system. From what we know of her history, she's been alone with her power since she was barely more than a child. That isolation, that lack of moral guidance, that's what created the monster we're going to face. You had love and structure and people who cared enough to teach you right from wrong. She had none of that."

The distinction Marcus was drawing felt important, though it also raised troubling questions about the nature of power itself. Was anyone truly immune to corruption if the circumstances were right? Could even the strongest moral foundation be eroded by enough isolation, enough fear, enough unchecked authority? Lyra found herself wondering what might have happened if she had been orphaned young like Ravenna, left to navigate the complexities of supernatural abilities without guidance or support. The thought was deeply unsettling, not least because she couldn't entirely convince herself that she would have chosen differently under those circumstances.

Lady Catherine approached their position at the edge of camp, her bow slung across her back and her expression alert despite the late hour. As the most experienced scout among their group, she had volunteered to take the first watch, patrolling the perimeter of their campsite to ensure they weren't surprised by bandits or wild animals during the night. Her movements were fluid and silent, the result of years spent hunting in the mountain forests around Fort Collins. When she spoke, her voice was pitched low to avoid disturbing the other knights who were already seeking sleep.

"Your Highness, I've been thinking about tomorrow's route," Catherine said, producing a detailed map from her leather satchel. "We have two options for crossing the Silverbrook Valley. We can take the main trade road, which is faster and more direct, or we can follow the hunter's trails through the forest, which would provide better cover but add at least half a day to our journey." She spread the map on the ground between them, weighing down the corners with small stones. "Given what we know about Ravenna's pattern of attacks, there's a reasonable chance we might encounter refugees or survivors fleeing westward along the main road."

The tactical consideration was a valid one, Lyra realized as she studied the topographical details Catherine had marked on her map. Refugees would be able to provide more current intelligence about Ravenna's location and capabilities, potentially saving them precious time in tracking down their quarry. On the other hand, any survivors they encountered would likely be traumatized and desperate, possibly slowing their progress and certainly adding emotional weight to an already difficult mission. There was also the question of whether they had sufficient supplies to assist civilians they might encounter along the way.

"What's your recommendation?" Lyra asked, though she suspected she already knew what Catherine's answer would be. The archer had always been pragmatic in her approach to military operations, but she also possessed a deep sense of compassion that made her popular among the common soldiers and servants at Castle Windborne. Her ability to balance tactical necessity with humanitarian concerns was one of the reasons Lyra had specifically requested her inclusion in this mission.

"We take the main road," Catherine said without hesitation. "The intelligence we might gain from survivors could be invaluable, and we have a responsibility to render aid to any of our people we encounter. If we're successful in stopping Ravenna, the extra half day won't matter in the long run. If we fail..." She shrugged expressively. "Then the time we saved by avoiding refugees won't have made any difference anyway."

The logic was sound, and it aligned with Lyra's own instincts about the right course of action. As future ruler of Fort Collins and the surrounding territories, she had been raised to understand that her primary obligation was always to the welfare of her people. That duty didn't disappear simply because she was pursuing a military objective, even one as critical as stopping Ravenna's advance westward. If anything, the presence of refugees would serve as a constant reminder of why this mission was so important and what the consequences of failure might look like.**Chapter 2: The First Encounter**

The morning brought with it an unseasonable chill that seemed to seep through even the thickest cloaks, and Lyra found herself pulling her traveling garments tighter as they broke camp and prepared for another day of hard riding. The decision to take the main trade road had been the right one, she reflected as they encountered their first group of refugees barely an hour after setting out from their campsite. The family of five - two parents with three young children - had been traveling through the night, their faces etched with the kind of exhaustion that came from prolonged terror rather than simple physical fatigue. Their few possessions were bundled into hastily packed sacks that spoke of a departure made in desperate haste, and the youngest child, a girl who couldn't have been more than six years old, clung to a small wooden doll with the fierce determination of someone who had already lost too much.

Sir Robert, whose gentle demeanor made him particularly effective at dealing with frightened civilians, dismounted to speak with the family while the rest of the party maintained a respectful distance. The conversation was conducted in low, urgent tones, but even from several yards away, Lyra could see the way the father's hands shook as he gestured toward the east, describing horrors that no parent should ever have to witness. The mother kept glancing around nervously, as if she expected their pursuer to materialize from the morning mist at any moment. Her protective positioning near her children reminded Lyra painfully of her own mother's behavior during the few times Castle Windborne had faced external threats, that particular combination of fierce love and helpless fear that came with trying to protect those who depended on you from dangers beyond your control.

When Robert returned to the group, his expression was grim enough to confirm their worst fears about what they might encounter as they continued eastward. He swung back into his saddle with the fluid motion of an experienced rider, but Lyra noticed that his usual easy confidence seemed diminished by whatever details he had learned from the refugee family. The other knights gathered closer to hear his report, their own faces reflecting the tension that had been building since they first received news of Ravenna's destructive campaign. Even the horses seemed to sense the change in mood, shifting restlessly and requiring firmer hands on their reins than had been necessary during the previous day's travel.

"They're from Millbrook, about two days' ride ahead of us," Robert began, his voice carrying the careful neutrality that soldiers learned to adopt when delivering particularly disturbing intelligence reports. "Princess Ravenna hit the town three nights ago, just after sunset. The family managed to escape because they were visiting relatives who lived on a farm outside the main settlement, but they saw the attack from a distance. According to the father, Ravenna didn't come with an army or even a significant escort. She arrived alone, on foot, walking down the main street like she was simply passing through on routine business."

Marcus leaned forward in his saddle, his expression sharpening with professional interest. "Alone? That seems almost impossible, given the level of destruction we've heard about. Unless her abilities are even more extensive than our intelligence suggested." He paused to consider the implications, his hand unconsciously moving to rest on the pommel of his sword. "What exactly did they see happen?"

Then she raised her hands, and shadows began pouring out of every building simultaneously. Not ordinary shadows, but moving, living things that seemed to have substance and purpose. Her own transformation abilities had always felt fundamentally connected to the life force of living beings, drawing on the essential energy that made each creature what it was. The idea of power that could drain that energy away, leaving only emptiness behind, seemed like a perverse mirror image of her own gifts. She found herself wondering whether Ravenna's abilities might represent some kind of dark counterpart to her transformation magic, a possibility that carried deeply troubling implications for their eventual confrontation.

Lady Catherine had been studying her map while Robert delivered his report, and now she looked up with a frown of concentration. "If Ravenna struck Millbrook three nights ago and was traveling on foot, she should still be relatively close to that area. Assuming she's maintaining the same pattern as her previous attacks, she'll likely spend at least another day or two in the region, making sure the destruction is complete before moving on to her next target." She traced a route with her finger, calculating distances and travel times with the precision that had made her invaluable as both a scout and a strategic planner. "We could potentially intercept her before she moves further west, if we're willing to push our pace and ride through part of the night."

The prospect of facing Ravenna sooner rather than later brought a mixture of relief and apprehension to Lyra's thoughts. On one hand, every day they delayed meant more innocent people would likely fall victim to the Shadow Princess's destructive advance. The refugee family they had just encountered represented only a tiny fraction of the suffering that Ravenna's campaign had already caused, and the knowledge that more such encounters awaited them along the road was almost unbearable. On the other hand, Lyra couldn't entirely suppress the fear that she wasn't ready for such a confrontation, that her abilities and training might prove inadequate when tested against someone whose power seemed to operate on a fundamentally different level from anything she had previously encountered.

Sir David, who had been quietly observing the exchange, finally spoke up with the measured tone that came from decades of military experience. Attempting to traverse them in darkness, with tired horses and exhausted riders, could result in injuries that would compromise our effectiveness when it matters most."

Marcus argued persuasively for maintaining their current pace, emphasizing the importance of arriving at their destination in optimal condition for combat. Catherine countered that the moral imperative to stop Ravenna's advance as quickly as possible outweighed the tactical concerns about fatigue. The younger knights offered their own perspectives, their eagerness to prove themselves in battle balanced against the sobering reality of what they had learned about their adversary's capabilities.

Ultimately, the decision fell to Lyra, and she found herself weighing not just the immediate tactical considerations but the broader implications of leadership itself. Every choice she made would affect not only the success of their mission but the lives of the people who had chosen to follow her into danger. The refugee family they had encountered served as a stark reminder of what happened to innocent people when those in power made the wrong decisions, and the weight of that responsibility felt almost overwhelming as she considered their options. Her father had often told her that true leadership meant accepting the burden of consequences, understanding that every action or inaction would ripple outward in ways that might not become apparent for years or even decades.

"We maintain our current pace," Lyra decided finally, her voice carrying the authority that came with her royal position but also the uncertainty of someone still learning to wield that authority effectively. "Sir David is right about the risks of pushing too hard too soon. We need to be at full strength when we face Ravenna, and that means accepting that we might not intercept her quite as quickly as we'd like." I want scouts ranging ahead of our main group at all times, and we gather intelligence from every refugee or survivor we encounter. The more we know about Ravenna's current location and capabilities, the better our chances of success when we do finally track her down."

The remainder of the morning passed in tense progress along the winding trade road, with Lady Catherine and Sir Thomas alternating as advance scouts while the rest of the party maintained a steady but sustainable pace through the increasingly rugged terrain. They encountered two more groups of refugees, each carrying similar stories of sudden, overwhelming destruction that had transformed thriving communities into lifeless wastelands within a matter of hours. The consistency of the accounts was both reassuring and disturbing - reassuring because it suggested their intelligence about Ravenna's capabilities was accurate, disturbing because it confirmed that they were indeed facing an adversary whose power operated on a scale that defied conventional military response.

By midday, they had reached the crest of Hawthorne Pass, a high mountain crossing that offered panoramic views of the valleys and settlements spread out below them. Under normal circumstances, Lyra would have found the vista breathtaking, a reminder of the natural beauty that made this region worth protecting. If Ravenna was indeed still in the area, a recently attacked settlement might provide clues about her current location and immediate plans. Even the wind, which had been a constant companion during their mountain crossing, seemed reluctant to disturb the unnatural stillness that had settled over the abandoned settlement. If Ravenna's associates were indeed monitoring their activities, then any preparations they made for dealing with the trap that appeared to have been set for them would be observed and potentially countered, creating a situation where conventional military planning might prove not only ineffective but actually counterproductive. At the same time, the knowledge that they were being watched provided an opportunity to potentially mislead their observers about their true intentions and capabilities, if they could develop a strategy sophisticated enough to take advantage of the surveillance rather than simply being victimized by it.

The approaching group that Thomas had identified was now close enough to be observed in more detail, and what they could see through their spyglasses confirmed that these were not ordinary refugees fleeing from destruction, but rather individuals who possessed military training and were equipped for operations that went beyond simple survival and escape from dangerous areas. The coordination of their movement, the quality of their equipment, and the disciplined way they approached the settlement all suggested professional soldiers or agents rather than civilians caught up in circumstances beyond their control. Most concerning of all was the fact that their approach route had been carefully chosen to minimize their visibility from the eastern approaches where Ravenna's trail led, suggesting that they were either trying to avoid observation by the Shadow Princess and her associates, or were coordinating their activities with those forces in ways that required careful timing and positioning.
