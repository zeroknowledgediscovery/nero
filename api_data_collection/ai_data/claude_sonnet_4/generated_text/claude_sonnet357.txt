The morning mist clung to the River Rother like a gossamer shroud, and through it came the rhythmic splash of oars cutting water. Marcus Thornfield's breath formed small clouds in the crisp October air as he powered through his final training session before the day that would change everything. His muscles, honed by years of discipline on these familiar waters, moved with the fluid precision of a master craftsman. Yet today, his mind wandered far from the gentle current beneath his scull.

Tonight wasn't about rowing. Tonight was about something entirely different, something that made his heart race faster than any sprint finish ever could. The Midlands Voice competition final awaited him in Birmingham, and after months of grueling auditions, he had somehow made it through. A rower who could sing – it seemed almost laughable, even to him.

"Marcus! You're drifting toward the reeds again," called Sarah from the towpath, her voice carrying across the water with practiced authority. As head coach of the Chesterfield Rowing Club, she had watched him develop from an awkward teenager into one of the region's most promising scullers. But lately, she'd noticed his focus wavering.

He corrected his line with a subtle adjustment of his left blade, bringing the boat back to the center of the channel. The familiar burn in his forearms felt comforting, a reminder of countless early mornings spent chasing perfection on water. This river had been his sanctuary for the better part of a decade, ever since his father had first brought him down to watch the local club's training sessions.

"Sorry, Sarah. Mind's elsewhere today," he called back, slowing his stroke rate as he approached the club's jetty. The old wooden structure groaned softly as small waves lapped against its weathered posts.

"I should think so," she replied, jogging alongside him as he glided toward the landing stage. "Big night tonight. Though I still can't quite believe you've kept this singing business secret for so long."

Marcus shipped his oars and grabbed the jetty's edge, steadying the narrow boat as he prepared to climb out. The transition from water to land always felt strange after a long training session – solid ground seemed almost unnaturally stable beneath his feet.

"Wasn't really a secret," he said, hoisting his scull from the water with practiced ease. "Just never seemed relevant to mention it. Two different worlds, aren't they?"

Sarah helped him carry the boat toward the club's boat house, their footsteps echoing hollowly on the wooden decking. The morning sun was beginning to burn through the mist now, revealing the full autumn splendor of the riverbank. Golden leaves drifted down from the overhanging willows, settling on the water's surface like scattered coins.

"Different worlds, maybe. But the same person living in both of them." She paused as they set the scull on its rack among dozens of others. "Your father would have been proud, you know. Both the rowing and the singing."

The mention of his father brought the familiar tightness to Marcus's chest. Three years had passed since the accident, but sometimes the grief still caught him off guard, arriving like an unexpected wave in calm water. His father had been the one to first notice his voice, humming along to the radio during their drives to weekend regattas.

"Dad always said I should pursue the music more seriously," Marcus admitted, running a cloth over his oars to remove the river water. "But rowing seemed more practical. Scholarships, university places, that sort of thing."

"And now you're doing both. He'd be impressed by that." Sarah's voice carried the warmth that had made her such an effective mentor to countless young rowers over the years. "What time do you need to leave for Birmingham?"

Marcus glanced at his watch – an old Omega that had belonged to his father, still keeping perfect time despite its age. "Competition starts at seven. Probably want to be there by five to get settled, so I'll need to leave around three-thirty to be safe."

"That gives you plenty of time to get nervous then," Sarah said with a grin. "Though I suspect you're already well ahead of schedule on that front."

She wasn't wrong. Marcus had been battling butterflies for days now, ever since receiving the call confirming his place in the final twelve. The competition had started with over three thousand applicants from across the Midlands, and somehow – almost impossibly – he had sung his way through three rounds of eliminations to reach the grand final.

The boat house was beginning to fill with the familiar sounds of a Saturday morning training session. Voices echoed from the changing rooms, and he could hear the clatter of riggers being adjusted and boats being lifted from their racks. This had been his world for so long that the routines felt like breathing – automatic and essential.

"Marcus!" The voice belonged to Jamie Peterson, a younger rower who had joined the club the previous spring. "Heard about tonight. That's absolutely mad, mate. You're going to be on television and everything?"

Word had spread quickly through the tight-knit rowing community once Marcus had finally revealed his secret. The responses had ranged from genuine excitement to barely concealed disbelief. Most of his teammates still struggled to reconcile the image of their methodical, disciplined training partner with someone who could command a stage with nothing but his voice.

"Hopefully," Marcus replied, hanging his kit in his locker. "Though there's still eleven other people who might have something to say about that."

"What are you going to sing?" Jamie asked, settling onto one of the wooden benches that ran down the center of the changing room. Despite being nearly twenty years old, he still possessed the eager enthusiasm of someone discovering the sport for the first time.

Marcus had been wrestling with that question for weeks. The competition rules allowed finalists to choose their own songs, within reason, and the decision felt impossibly weighty. Something too ambitious might expose his limitations; something too safe might not showcase his strengths.

"Still deciding," he said, which wasn't entirely true. He had narrowed it down to two possibilities, both songs that held deep personal meaning. One was a soaring ballad by Sam Smith that he had been working on with his vocal coach for months. The other was something much more personal – a haunting piece by Damien Rice that reminded him of quiet evenings spent with his father, learning to appreciate the subtle power of understated emotion.

The changing room gradually emptied as other rowers headed out for their training sessions. Through the small window, Marcus could see the river coming alive with activity – eights and fours moving up and down the course like water beetles, their crews calling out technical instructions that had become a second language to him.

He should probably head home soon. His mother would be waiting with breakfast and barely contained excitement. She had been his biggest supporter throughout the competition, attending every round and offering encouragement even when his own confidence faltered. The prospect of disappointing her felt almost worse than the fear of failing publicly.

His phone buzzed with a text message from his vocal coach: "Remember what we practiced about breathing. Trust your instincts. You've got this."

Helen Morrison had been working with him for eighteen months, ever since he had finally worked up the courage to seek professional guidance. A former opera singer turned teacher, she had initially been skeptical about taking on a part-time student with no formal musical training. But something in his raw, untutored voice had captured her attention, and she had agreed to give him a chance.

Those lessons had been revelatory. Helen had helped him understand that singing, like rowing, was fundamentally about technique and muscle memory. Both required countless hours of practice to achieve something that looked effortless. Both demanded the courage to push through discomfort in pursuit of improvement.

The drive home took him through the heart of Chesterfield, past the famous crooked spire of St Mary's Church that had dominated the skyline for centuries. The morning traffic was light, mostly weekend shoppers and families heading out for Saturday activities. This was his hometown, the place that had shaped him, and tonight he would be representing it on a stage that felt impossibly distant from these familiar streets.

His mother's house sat on a quiet residential street lined with modest semi-detached homes, their front gardens showing the first signs of autumn's arrival. The red Ford Focus that had been his constant companion for the past four years was parked in the driveway, looking tired but reliable. He would need it to perform flawlessly this afternoon – the drive to Birmingham was not particularly long, but Saturday evening traffic could be unpredictable.

"How was training?" his mother called from the kitchen as he let himself in through the front door. The smell of bacon and eggs filled the small hallway, accompanied by the familiar sounds of Radio 2 playing quietly in the background.

"Good. Felt strong on the water." He hung his jacket on the bannister and made his way toward the kitchen, where Margaret Thornfield was bustling around the small space with characteristic efficiency. At sixty-two, she possessed the kind of boundless energy that had sustained her through years of single parenthood and demanding work as a primary school teacher.

"I've made your favorite," she said, gesturing toward a plate piled high with food. "Figured you might need some comfort eating before this afternoon."

Marcus settled at the small kitchen table where he had eaten thousands of meals over the years. The routine felt grounding, a connection to simpler times when his biggest worry had been completing his homework or making the school rowing team.

"I keep thinking I'm going to wake up and discover this has all been some elaborate dream," he said, cutting into his bacon. "Six months ago, I was just someone who sang in the shower. Now I'm supposed to perform in front of television cameras and industry professionals."

His mother sat across from him with her own cup of tea, studying his face with the practiced eye of someone who had been reading his moods since childhood. "Your father used to say that life has a way of presenting opportunities when you're finally ready to handle them."

"What if I'm not ready though? What if I get up there and completely freeze?"Margaret reached across the table and placed her hand over his, her touch warm and reassuring. "Do you remember when you were fifteen and convinced you weren't ready for your first regatta? You spent the entire week before it worrying that you'd catch a crab in the first fifty meters and embarrass yourself in front of everyone." She smiled at the memory, her eyes crinkling at the corners. "But when race day came, you rowed one of the most technically sound races I'd ever seen you complete. Sometimes our bodies and our training know things that our minds haven't quite caught up with yet."

Marcus found himself smiling despite his nerves. He did remember that regatta – the Nottingham Head Race, where he had finished third in his category and earned his first real recognition as a promising young sculler. The weeks leading up to it had been torture, filled with exactly the same kind of anxiety that was churning in his stomach now. But his mother was right; when the moment had arrived, instinct and preparation had taken over, carrying him through the race with a smoothness that had surprised even him.

"The singing is different though," he said, taking another bite of his breakfast. "In rowing, if something goes wrong, you've got time to correct it. You can adjust your technique mid-race, find your rhythm again. But with singing, especially in a competition like this, you get one chance. Three and a half minutes to prove that you deserve to be there, and if your voice cracks or you forget the words or the nerves get to you..." He trailed off, not wanting to voice his deepest fears.

"Then you'll handle it the same way you've handled every challenge in your life," Margaret said firmly. "With grace and determination and the knowledge that you've prepared as well as anyone possibly could. Helen told me yesterday that she's never worked with a student who practiced as diligently as you do. She said your technical improvement over the past year has been remarkable."

The mention of his vocal coach's praise brought a small measure of comfort. Helen Morrison was not someone given to empty compliments – her feedback was always honest, sometimes brutally so, which had made her occasional expressions of approval all the more meaningful. During their most recent lesson, she had listened to him run through both of his potential song choices and simply nodded with satisfaction when he finished, saying only, "You're ready now. Trust what you've learned."

Marcus finished his breakfast and carried his plate to the sink, looking out through the kitchen window at the small back garden where his father had once taught him to kick a football. The memory felt both distant and immediate, the way significant moments from childhood often did. His father had possessed an easy confidence that Marcus had always envied, the ability to approach new challenges with curiosity rather than fear.

"I should probably try to get some rest before I leave," he said, though he doubted sleep would come easily. "Maybe run through the songs one more time with the piano."

The upright piano in the front room had been his father's pride and joy, a second-hand Yamaha that had been carefully restored and tuned regularly despite the family's modest budget. Marcus had learned to play basic accompaniments on it, enough to support his singing practice, though he was far more comfortable with his voice than with the keys. The instrument had sat silent for months after his father's death, gathering dust and serving as a painful reminder of loss, until Marcus had finally begun using it again as part of his musical development.

Margaret nodded approvingly. "Good idea. I'll keep the house quiet. Mrs. Patterson next door knows about tonight – she's planning to watch with her daughter. Half the street will probably be glued to their televisions, hoping to spot our local celebrity."

The thought of neighbors and friends watching added another layer of pressure that Marcus tried not to dwell on. The competition would be broadcast live on ITV, with highlights repeated later in the evening. It was exposure on a scale that still didn't feel entirely real to him, despite having signed all the necessary paperwork and releases weeks ago.

He made his way upstairs to his bedroom, intending to lie down for an hour or two, but found himself too restless to stay horizontal. Instead, he sat at the small desk by the window and pulled out the notebook where he had been jotting down thoughts and observations about his musical journey. The pages were filled with technical notes from Helen's lessons, emotional responses to different songs, and fragments of lyrics that had moved him in some way.

One entry, written several weeks earlier, caught his attention: "The difference between rowing and singing isn't as big as I thought. Both require you to find a rhythm that feels natural but looks effortless. Both demand that you stay present in the moment while trusting years of preparation. The biggest difference might be that singing asks you to be vulnerable in a way that sport doesn't – you're not just demonstrating technique, you're sharing something essential about who you are."

Reading his own words back to himself, Marcus felt a small shift in his perspective. Perhaps the vulnerability that seemed so frightening was actually the point. The judges and audience tonight wouldn't just be evaluating his vocal technique or his song choice – they would be responding to his authenticity, his willingness to stand on that stage and offer something genuine of himself.

He closed the notebook and moved to the piano downstairs, settling onto the familiar bench and running his fingers over the keys in a simple warm-up pattern. The muscle memory of the accompaniments came easily now, thousands of repetitions having embedded the chord progressions deep in his hands. He began with scales and arpeggios, letting his voice find its natural placement without pushing or forcing anything.

The sound filled the small room with a warmth that always surprised him. His voice had deepened and strengthened considerably over the past year of serious training, developing a richer timbre and more reliable control throughout his range. Helen had helped him understand that his natural baritone placement was actually quite rare among contemporary male singers, giving him a distinctive quality that could set him apart from other competitors.

After warming up thoroughly, he moved into the Sam Smith ballad that remained one of his two final options for the evening. The song demanded both technical precision and emotional honesty, building from intimate verses to a soaring chorus that showcased the full power of his voice. He had performed it dozens of times in lessons and practice sessions, but tonight it would need to tell a story that connected with complete strangers.

As he sang, Marcus found himself thinking about the journey that had brought him to this moment. The first tentative lessons with Helen, when he had barely been able to sustain a note without his voice shaking. The gradual building of confidence as technique replaced guesswork and his natural musicality began to emerge. The decision to audition for Midlands Voice, made on an impulse that still seemed uncharacteristic for someone who typically planned every aspect of his life with methodical care.

Each round of the competition had presented new challenges and unexpected discoveries. The first audition, in a church hall in Nottingham, where he had been one of hundreds of hopefuls queuing nervously in the October rain. The callbacks, held in a proper theater with professional lighting and sound equipment that made even simple songs feel epic. The semi-final round, where he had met the other contestants who would become his competition tonight and realized that the standard was higher than he had dared to imagine.

He finished the Sam Smith song and sat quietly for a moment, his hands resting on the keys. The performance had felt solid, technically secure and emotionally connected, but he found himself drawn to the other option – the Damien Rice song that seemed to reach deeper into his personal experience. It was more risky in some ways, less obviously impressive, but it possessed a haunting quality that had moved everyone who had heard him perform it.

The song was called "The Blower's Daughter," and its sparse, intimate arrangement would leave him nowhere to hide vocally. Every note mattered, every word needed to carry weight, and the overall effect depended more on emotional truthfulness than on technical fireworks. It was the kind of performance that could either create a profound connection with the audience or fall completely flat if the vulnerability felt forced or artificial.

Marcus began playing the delicate guitar-like accompaniment that he had adapted for piano, letting the melody emerge slowly and naturally. His voice entered almost conversationally, drawing listeners into the song's private world before gradually building to moments of greater intensity. This was the song his father would have appreciated, he realized – something that prioritized meaning over showmanship, that trusted the audience to listen carefully rather than demanding their attention through volume or vocal acrobatics.

As he sang, he found himself thinking about the conversation he had never been able to have with his father about pursuing music more seriously. They had talked about it in general terms, with his father encouraging him to keep singing and perhaps take some lessons, but there had always been time for deeper discussions later. The rowing scholarship to university had seemed like the practical choice, the responsible path that would lead to stable employment and financial security.

But life had intervened with its usual disregard for careful plans. His father's sudden death in a car accident had left Marcus questioning all of his assumptions about the future. The rowing had continued, providing structure and familiarity during the most difficult period of his life, but something had changed in his relationship with risk and possibility. Perhaps losing someone so unexpectedly had made him more willing to pursue dreams that might not work out, more aware that the safe choices weren't necessarily the meaningful ones.**Chapter 2**
