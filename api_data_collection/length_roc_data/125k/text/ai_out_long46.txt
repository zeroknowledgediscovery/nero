Chapter One: Echoes in the Wind

The sun bled across the cracked horizon like a wound that never quite healed. Out in the middle of the high school track field in Cherry Falls, Colorado, the wind rustled the bleachers and tugged at the worn banners that still clung stubbornly to the fences: "State Champions 2002." Faded maroon and gold, the colors of another life.

Jace Weller stood at the edge of lane three, hands on his hips, chest heaving from a two-lap shuffle that bore only a passing resemblance to the gazelle-like sprints of his past. His knees ached. His lungs felt too tight in his chest, as if they were still trying to remember how to fill the space he once owned on this oval.

"Coach would’ve called that a brisk funeral march," he muttered to himself, spitting into the grass. He didn’t smile. The joke didn’t feel like it was his. Not anymore.

Behind him, the sound of an old pickup door creaking open cut the wind. He turned.

"You really gonna try and kill yourself out here again?" The voice was familiar, half-sarcastic and half-concerned. Drew Snyder walked toward him, ball cap low over his forehead, a six-pack clinking inside a plastic bag that swung at his side.

Jace laughed once, short and breathless. "Gotta die somewhere."

Drew shook his head, passing him a bottle. "You used to fly. Now you wheeze. Why come back here, man?"

That was the question, wasn’t it?

Jace Weller was thirty-eight, jobless, divorced, and living in his late father’s basement since his company laid him off eight months ago. Once, he had been the golden boy. 400-meter state record. Scholarship to the University of Texas. Brief flirtation with Olympic trials. And then—well. Torn hamstring in ‘08. A couple of bad decisions. One too many beers. No medals. No podiums. Just years bleeding together, like ink spilled across a ledger nobody would read.

He looked out over the track. It was overgrown at the edges, the rubber cracking like a sunburnt back. Ghosts ran here—versions of himself at seventeen, at twenty, at twenty-five. Their legs pumping, their eyes full of fire. He was always chasing them now. Never catching.

“I’m not here to relive it,” Jace lied. “Just trying to remember what it felt like.”

Drew gave a snort and sat down on the lowest bench of the bleachers. “Bullshit. You’re trying to bring it back.”

“Is that so wrong?”

“Only if you’re expecting a different ending.”

Back in the house—no, the basement, let’s not pretend—Jace stood shirtless in front of the mirror, fingers grazing the ragged white scar across his upper thigh. The result of his final competitive race. Nationals. 2008. It tore during the curve on the third leg. He fell face first, watched the rest of the heat blur past him from the cinder.

He used to dream about that exact moment. Still did, sometimes. The sound of his own grunt as he hit the ground. The silence afterward.

In the kitchen upstairs, his mother’s footsteps creaked the ceiling. The smell of boiled eggs and coffee drifted down like a judgment.

“Jace? You going to the store today? We need bread.”

“I’ll go,” he called back. He didn’t move.

The town hadn’t changed much. Cherry Falls was the kind of place people left, not the kind they came back to. Except Jace. He’d circled back like a lost dog, tail between legs. The people at the grocery store either didn’t recognize him or pretended not to.

All except Lacey.

“Weller.” Her voice cracked through the dusty silence of aisle three. He turned. She still wore her hair in a messy bun, but there were lines now around her mouth and eyes that hadn’t been there when she cheered from the sidelines, pompoms raised. “Didn’t expect to see you haunting these aisles.”

“Didn’t expect to be haunted,” he replied.

She raised an eyebrow. “Still dramatic. You coaching or just jogging around the old track for the hell of it?”

“Just… getting back into shape.”

“Shape of what?” she grinned. Then her eyes softened. “You okay, Jace?”

“No one asks that question if they really want the truth.”

“I do.”

He didn’t answer. She let the silence rest between them like a folded letter.

“Hey,” she said suddenly. “You know about the alumni invitational next month, right?”

He blinked. “The what?”

“They’re doing this big anniversary thing. Twenty-five years since the championship team. Little exhibition race. You should come. Hell, you should run. People would lose their minds.”

He laughed, genuinely this time. It came out dry and coughing. “Run?”

“Just a hundred meters. For fun. Nostalgia, y’know?”

“I haven’t sprinted a hundred in over a decade.”

“Then maybe it’s time.”

She walked off with a wink, the rustle of her grocery bags echoing behind her.

Back at home, Jace dug through the musty bins in the attic. His old spikes, long since rusted and yellowed with time, stared up at him like bones. His Texas track hoodie still smelled faintly of sweat and Gatorade and defeat.

He found his journal buried under a stack of high school trophies. It opened to a page from 2007:

"Keep pushing. There’s no glory in easy."

He slammed it shut. His hand trembled slightly.

Training was slow. Painful. His body resisted like a rusted engine coughing to life. Each morning he ran the straights, walked the curves. Pushups in the basement. Crunches. Ice baths that left his skin purple and his breath jagged.

Drew came by sometimes, watching from the bleachers, tossing sarcastic encouragement like pebbles.

“You really think you’re gonna win this thing?”

“I’m not trying to win.”

“Uh-huh.”

By week three, his form was coming back. Not like it was. Never like it was. But the rhythm returned. Knees lifting. Arms driving. The sound of feet on rubber was its own kind of heartbeat.

The night before the race, Jace stood in front of the mirror again, dressed in a replica of his old kit. It didn’t quite fit the same. His stomach wasn’t what it was, his shoulders broader with age and wear. But in the eyes—there was still something. A flicker.

His mother knocked gently on the doorframe.

“I washed your shoes,” she said.

“Thanks.”

She hesitated. “Don’t hurt yourself.”

“I won’t.”

“I just mean—don’t go chasing something that’s already gone.”

He nodded. But in his heart, he wasn’t sure she was right.

Race day. The stands were packed. Not like a college meet, but more than he expected. Faces from his past, aged and blurred. Coaches, old rivals, kids in jerseys too new to know his name. Lacey waved from the snack tent. Drew brought a folding chair and a cooler.

The sun was harsh and merciless. Lane four. His lane.

The other runners were former teammates, beer-bellied and laughing, but as the gun was raised, the air grew still. Something sacred, almost.

Crack.

He drove out of the blocks like memory. His legs screamed. His lungs tore. But for ten, maybe fifteen seconds, the world fell away. There was no past, no future—only the rush of wind in his ears and the taste of something sharp and clean.

He didn’t win. He came in third.

But as he crossed the line, Jace raised his arms anyway, laughing like someone who had remembered something vital at last.

And the crowd cheered—not for a comeback, not for a champion—but for a man who had run, not from something, but toward it.

Jace sat on the grass after the race, legs stretched out, sweat drenching the back of his shirt. His chest still heaved from the effort, though the pain had settled into something dull and oddly comforting. The sun was starting its slow descent over Cherry Falls, casting golden light across the field. His legs were jelly, but there was an ember burning in his chest—small, defiant, something that hadn’t stirred in years.

He stared at his hands, fingers trembling slightly as he traced a line of callus at the base of his palm. They used to wrap starting blocks with these hands, tie his laces with purpose, hold batons with the trust of teammates who believed in him without question. Back then, every ache was proof of progress, every drop of sweat an investment. Now the pain simply reminded him that he still had skin in the game, even if he didn’t quite know which game he was playing anymore.

Lacey wandered over and dropped beside him with two paper cups. “Gatorade, though I think it’s the powdered kind. Budget cuts,” she said, offering one.

He accepted it with a grunt of thanks, the drink lukewarm and overly sweet, but it tasted like high school and lost afternoons. She took a sip from her own cup and leaned back on her elbows, squinting toward the field.

“You looked... almost like you used to. There was this second, maybe halfway through, where I swear I forgot it was an alumni thing.”

Jace chuckled, the sound low and scratchy. “You flatter me.”

“No, I’m serious. For a heartbeat, I saw seventeen again. You running like hell, the crowd losing it. Just like old times.”

“Old times,” he echoed, looking toward the goalposts at the far end. “Feels like a movie I barely remember watching.”

Lacey didn’t say anything for a moment. The quiet between them was thicker now, tinged with something unsaid, something both heavy and familiar. She picked at the grass with her free hand, her fingers twitching.

“You know,” she said, finally, “you could stick around. Help coach the team. Kids could use someone who’s been through it.”

He shook his head. “I don’t think I’m what they need.”

“Don’t be so sure.”

A small group of kids was still hanging by the track, some of them replaying videos on their phones, laughing, pointing, mimicking exaggerated sprint poses. One boy, probably fifteen or sixteen, noticed Jace and lifted a hand.

“Hey, man! That was sick out there!”

Jace raised a hand in return, nodding.

Lacey smirked. “Seems like at least one of them disagrees.”

He finished the Gatorade, crumpling the cup in his hand. “I’m not sure what the hell I’m doing here, Lacey.”

“You ran. That’s something.”

She stood and brushed the grass off her jeans. Her eyes softened as she looked down at him. “You don’t have to know the destination to start walking. Or running.”

He watched her go, the sway of her gait slow and steady, like she’d figured out some balance he’d never quite mastered.

Later that night, in the basement, Jace lay on the old pull-out mattress surrounded by trophies he hadn’t bothered to box back up yet. He stared at the ceiling, listening to the quiet thump of the furnace kicking on. Somewhere upstairs, his mother was watching TV, the faint sound of a news anchor’s voice filtering through the floorboards.

He grabbed the journal again. Opened to the last entry.

“Keep pushing. There’s no glory in easy.”

He flipped to a new page. The pen scratched across the paper, unevenly at first, then steadier.

June 2nd. I ran today. Not like I used to. But I ran.

He paused.

It felt like a beginning. Or an ending. Or maybe just a moment. Either way, I think I needed it.

He capped the pen and let the journal fall to his chest.

The next morning, he didn’t need the alarm. He was awake before the sun had breached the horizon, his body sore but oddly electric. The kind of soreness that reminded him he was still in the game, even if he didn’t know the score.

He pulled on his old hoodie, the fabric thinned from age but soft from wear. Outside, the streets were still bathed in early light, a quiet gold that settled on the pavement like a promise. He ran—just two miles, slow and steady, his breath fogging the air in soft puffs.

At the end of his route, he paused on the bridge over Little Creek. Water rushed below, fast from spring melt. He leaned against the rail, breathing hard.

What was he chasing? He didn’t know.

He only knew that for the first time in years, he wasn’t standing still.

At Cherry Falls High, Coach McKenna greeted him with a smirk and a clap on the back hard enough to jolt his shoulder.

“Well I’ll be damned. Weller. Thought you were a ghost.”

“Close enough,” Jace replied, cracking a half-smile.

They walked along the track together, the younger athletes watching them curiously. McKenna, grey-haired and thick around the middle now, waved a clipboard like a preacher with a Bible.

“I saw you run. Still got something in those legs, huh?”

“Something. Not sure what.”

“Doesn’t matter. These kids? They need someone who knows what it’s like to push through failure. Not just the kids who win everything. The ones who fall and get up again. You want to help out? There’s a spot.”

Jace didn’t answer right away. He watched a girl in lane two trip during a hurdle drill, fall, then scramble back up before anyone could say a word. He remembered that feeling—the sting of failure, the desperate need to prove you could shake it off.

“Yeah,” he said quietly. “Maybe I do.”

That night, he drove past the old gas station, the one where he used to meet friends before Friday night games, where he once kissed Lacey for the first time while her cherry slush dripped down her hand. It was boarded up now, faded graffiti scrawled on the side.

He turned left on Main, past the coffee shop that still had the same barista working weekends, and pulled into the parking lot of the rec center. A flyer was posted near the door: Community 5K – Fundraiser for the Youth Track Club. Volunteers Needed. Runners Welcome.

He tore off one of the tabs at the bottom and stuffed it into his pocket.

The future didn’t look like gold medals or Olympic trials anymore. It looked like this—cracked tracks, second chances, kids trying to run away from their own shadows.

Jace had run from a lot of things. But maybe now, at last, he could start running toward something.

A week after the alumni race, the soreness had faded into something almost nostalgic. The kind of ache that followed real effort, not the dull fatigue of desk jobs and disappointment. Jace had returned to the track each morning like a ritual. No spectators, no stopwatch, just the sound of his breath and the rhythm of his shoes on the lane. He liked the solitude. He liked knowing no one expected anything of him but motion.

It had started to rain the third morning in a row. Thin, chilly drops that dampened the rubber underfoot and clung to his eyelashes. He didn't stop. Something inside him had shifted, subtle but irreversible. He wasn't chasing trophies anymore, and he wasn’t interested in living in the golden years that had become a kind of prison. He wanted to feel the ground under him, to feel the wind in his face—not for the sake of proving anything, but simply to feel alive again.

That same morning, as he jogged the final curve, he spotted a lone figure sitting under the bleachers, knees hugged tight to her chest. It was the same girl who had tripped during hurdle practice the week before. Her name was Mariela, a freshman with long legs and uncertain eyes. Coach McKenna had mentioned her during a post-practice chat, saying she had raw speed but no confidence to match.

She didn’t notice Jace at first, and when he approached, she startled slightly.

“Didn’t mean to sneak up on you,” he said, trying to sound casual. “Rain makes good cover, I guess.”

She shrugged, wiping at her face in a way that could’ve been rain or something more.

“You alright?”

“Yeah,” she said too quickly. Then, “I’m just tired.”

Jace sat a few feet away on the lowest bleacher step, careful not to invade her space. “Tired’s fair. Track takes it out of you. Especially when you care.”

She hesitated. “It’s not that. I just... I don’t think I’m any good.”

“Why’s that?”

She looked down at her muddy sneakers, the laces frayed. “I fell. Again. Everyone saw.”

“You think I never fell?”

“You ran at state. People talk about you like you were a legend.”

He chuckled softly. “Legends fall too. They just fall louder.”

Her eyes flicked toward him, uncertain.

“I fell hard,” he continued. “Broke down in front of thousands of people. Tore a muscle, lost my scholarship, drank too much, burned out. And for years, I thought that fall defined me.”

“It didn’t?”

“No. What did was standing up. Not right away. Took me a long time. But I’m here, aren’t I?”

She sat in silence, processing that.

“You’ve got good form,” he said. “You run angry. That’s a good start. You want to work on your starts sometime?”

She looked up. “Would you?”

He nodded. “Be here tomorrow. 7 a.m. Rain or shine.”

She gave a small smile, the kind that almost doesn’t make it to the lips. “Okay.”

By the end of the second week, word had spread that Jace Weller was helping with the team. He never made it official, and neither did Coach McKenna. He wasn’t paid. He didn’t even have a whistle. But the kids came to him anyway—awkward, shy, half-curious. He didn’t give speeches or try to sound wise. He just showed up. He ran the warmups with them. Timed their intervals. Gave quiet pointers that hit harder than any pep talk.

Mariela improved first. Her starts became sharper. Her strides less panicked. She began finishing practice with a grin rather than an apology. Then others followed. Carter, the wiry junior with a lazy gait but explosive speed. Darnell, the shot-put kid who had hidden sprints under a layer of sarcasm and sweatpants. Even Nia, the captain, started to seek him out after practice, asking about techniques and nutrition.

It was strange, this feeling. Being useful again. It scared him a little, how much he wanted to protect them from the mistakes he’d made. How badly he wanted them to find the finish line he never did.

On a Friday evening, after practice had ended and the sun dipped low behind the bleachers, Drew showed up again. Same baseball cap, same cooler, same crooked grin.

“You’re becoming a regular Mister Miyagi out here,” he joked, tossing Jace a bottle of water.

“They’re good kids.”

“They always are. Until life gets at ‘em.”

Jace didn’t argue. Instead, he glanced across the track where Mariela was still doing stretches alone. She held each pose with careful concentration, as if afraid to move too quickly and mess it up.

“You remember when we used to run in the dark?” Jace asked.

Drew snorted. “You mean when you ran and I huffed behind you cursing your name?”

“Yeah. Those nights were better than I knew.”

“You only know what things mean when you’ve already left ‘em.”

Jace sighed, tipping the water bottle back. “Maybe I’m not done running.”

Drew tilted his head. “You thinking about that 5K?”

He hadn’t said it aloud yet. Not even to himself. But the flyer was still in his pocket. Crumpled, sure. But still there.

“Maybe.”

“Think you could do it?”

“I think I need to find out.”

The next morning, he didn’t go to the track. He drove twenty minutes east to a trail that wound through the foothills, one he hadn’t visited since college. It was uneven, rocky, and brutally inclined in parts, but the kind of place where each mile felt like a revelation.

He parked by the trailhead, stretched in silence, and began to run.

The uphill burned immediately. His thighs screamed in protest, and sweat stung his eyes within minutes. But he kept going. One mile. Then two. Each footfall was a drumbeat. His breathing was ragged, but it had rhythm.

By the fourth mile, something cracked open inside him—not physically, but deeper. A memory: his father cheering from the sidelines, a time before the pressure had turned joy into performance. Another: the first time he kissed Lacey beneath the bleachers, the cold aluminum against his back. And another: sitting in a hotel hallway after Nationals, a bottle of whiskey in one hand, an icepack in the other, wondering if he had wasted it all.

He reached the top of the trail just as the sun crested the ridgeline. The light hit him square in the face, sharp and clean and unflinching.

Jace dropped to his knees in the dirt, heart thundering, breath caught somewhere between a sob and a laugh.

He had nothing to prove. Not anymore.

But he still had something left to give.

Later, back at home, he stood in front of the bathroom mirror again. Same scar. Same tired eyes. But the set of his shoulders had changed.

He pinned the 5K flyer to the corkboard above his desk. Then he picked up the phone.

“Coach McKenna,” he said when the voice answered. “You still got that stopwatch?”

“Always.”

“Think I’m ready to get timed again.”

A pause. Then, “You serious?”

“Dead serious.”

“Well, damn. Let’s make it count.”

Chapter Two: The Long Run

The morning of the trial run, the air tasted like cold metal—clean, crisp, and full of warning. Jace stood on the edge of the high school track once more, this time not as a nostalgic visitor, but as a man with a goal. Not a medal. Not a headline. Just a number on a clock. Coach McKenna leaned on the rail with the stopwatch in hand, the old-school kind that still clicked when the button pressed.

“Alright,” McKenna said, chewing on the corner of a toothpick. “Three miles. Let’s see what kind of rust we’re dealing with.”

Jace nodded and took his place in lane one, bouncing slightly on the balls of his feet. His breath fogged in the morning air, muscles tense but ready. He’d warmed up carefully. There was no use in pulling something before even starting. He reminded himself, again and again, that he wasn’t here to win. He was here to run.

McKenna’s voice broke the silence. “Ready?”

Jace exhaled. “Yeah.”

“Go!”

The first lap felt good. He kept his strides even, his arms tucked in close, resisting the urge to push too hard too soon. His body was older now, wiser in its own battered way. It didn’t respond to adrenaline the way it once did—it required pacing, patience, and a quiet kind of respect.

By the second lap, his legs began to protest. Not the burning ache of exhaustion yet, but a familiar tension in the tendons and joints that warned of limits. He adjusted his gait, focused on breathing through his nose, drawing in long, slow pulls of air. McKenna called out the splits as he passed each marker, his voice steady, neutral, giving nothing away.

By mile two, his lungs felt tight, the cold air slicing into him like small blades. But he didn’t slow down. He couldn’t. Something old had awakened inside him again—something feral and relentless. It wasn’t pride. It wasn’t even redemption. It was the memory of motion itself, the sacred geometry of running. One foot in front of the other, over and over again, as if each step was a vow to not stop.

When he crossed the final line, he collapsed to one knee, coughing into the dirt, chest heaving. McKenna didn’t say anything for a moment, just clicked the stopwatch and whistled.

“Twenty-five thirty-six,” he finally said. “Not bad for an old man with busted knees.”

Jace laughed breathlessly. “Could’ve been worse.”

“Could’ve been a lot worse.”

Jace looked up. “Think I can cut it under twenty-two in three weeks?”

McKenna squinted at him. “With enough work? Maybe. But that’s not really why you’re doing it, is it?”

Jace stood slowly, wiping sweat from his brow. “No,” he said. “But it’d be nice.”

That afternoon, he returned home sore but buzzing. He soaked in the tub for nearly an hour, the water tinged with Epsom salt and heat. His body hummed with the dull symphony of effort and repair. Every joint, every muscle had played its part. He had tested the limits. Found them. And discovered they were just far enough away to make the next goal possible.

The phone rang somewhere in the other room. He ignored it. His mother would answer if it was important.

Instead, he lay back in the bath and closed his eyes, remembering a night years ago, when he’d first broken fifty seconds in the 400-meter dash. The roar of the crowd. The feel of his coach’s hands on his shoulders. The unbearable brightness of the stadium lights. He could still feel it in his chest—the burn that felt more like triumph than pain.

He hadn’t thought about that night in years. Had buried it under failures and invoices and lost years. But now, that moment glimmered in his memory like a lighthouse. A place he could steer toward, even if he never reached it again.

The following week was a blur of training, planning, and unexpected connection. Mariela met him on the track every other morning. She rarely spoke unless asked, but she listened carefully. Her starts grew sharper. Her breathing more controlled. She had a runner’s stubbornness and a beginner’s hunger, and watching her improve lit something in Jace that he hadn’t felt since college.

Other students trickled in too. Sometimes it was just questions—what to eat before a meet, how to shake off the nerves, whether shin splints ever went away. Sometimes they stayed to run drills. More often, they just wanted to talk. To feel like they mattered to someone who had worn their shoes.

One morning, while leading warmups, Carter asked the question everyone had been tiptoeing around.

“So, Coach—uh, Jace—are you, like, trying to compete again? Like... for real?”

Jace paused mid-stretch, considering the question. “Not exactly.”

“But you’re doing the 5K, right?”

“Yeah.”

“Why?”

It was a fair question. The group was quiet, waiting for the answer.

Jace stood, brushing dirt from his knees. “Because I spent too long sitting still,” he said. “Because I used to run from things. Now I want to run toward something. Even if I don’t know what it is yet.”

They didn’t say anything for a beat. Then Carter nodded. “That’s kind of cool.”

“Yeah,” Mariela added, surprising everyone. “It is.”

He spent more time in the evenings now with Lacey, usually after practice. Sometimes they sat in her small backyard drinking beer and reminiscing. Sometimes they didn’t talk at all. There was a comfort in the silence between them, in the easy rhythm of two people who had once been close enough to hurt each other and far enough now to let it heal.

One evening, as the sky turned to velvet, Lacey turned to him. “Do you think you’re going to be okay?”

He took a long sip of his drink. “I don’t know.”

“But you’re trying.”

“Trying feels like a start.”

She leaned her head on his shoulder, her voice soft. “You’ve changed.”

“People do.”

“No,” she said. “People get older. You’ve changed.”

He wasn’t sure how to reply. But her hand found his in the grass, and they sat like that until the mosquitoes drove them inside.

With each passing day, his training grew more disciplined. He logged each mile in a notebook. Not for metrics—he wasn’t trying to impress anyone—but to track the rhythm of his body. He paid attention to food again. To sleep. He stopped drinking beer, started eating real breakfasts. Every morning, before the sun came up, he ran the quiet streets of Cherry Falls while the world still slept.

And in that silence, the past stopped chasing him.

He no longer saw himself falling in slow motion at Nationals. He didn’t replay the phone call from Texas, the one where they pulled his scholarship. He didn’t think about the divorce, the shouting matches, the silences that came afterward. He didn’t hear the voice of his younger self asking what the hell went wrong.

He just ran.

The day of the community 5K arrived with no fanfare. Overcast skies. Damp grass. A smattering of spectators with coffee cups and folding chairs. It wasn’t a grand event, just a local tradition. But it felt monumental.

He arrived early, warming up quietly along the outskirts of the park. Mariela and a few of the others had come to watch. McKenna gave him a nod from the finish line. Even Drew was there, eating a doughnut and pretending not to be invested.

The gun cracked, and he launched forward.

The pace was fast at first. Young guys surged ahead, high on adrenaline. He let them go. He knew better than to sprint the first half-mile. He stayed steady. He listened to the ground. He watched the runners ahead of him—how their gaits shifted, how their arms tightened with fatigue.

One mile down. Two. His breath came ragged but controlled. He passed a few of the early leaders, their youthful energy burned out too soon. By mile three, only a handful remained ahead of him.

But he didn’t chase.

He just ran.

He crossed the finish line at twenty-one forty-two. Better than expected. Enough to make McKenna raise his eyebrows.

He doubled over, gasping, the world spinning slightly. But when he stood upright, hands on hips, he smiled.

Because this time, he wasn’t running from anything.

And it felt like freedom.

After the race, a quiet settled over Jace that he hadn’t expected. It wasn’t pride—at least not in the way he’d remembered it. There was no crowd roaring, no podium, no medal. Just a folded paper number pinned to his shirt, soaked with sweat, and a time scribbled in pencil on a whiteboard behind the community center. But as he walked through the group of milling runners, slapping hands and nodding to strangers who offered congratulations, he felt something unfamiliar: peace.

Lacey was waiting by the snack table, holding two cups of orange slices in one hand and a bottle of water in the other. She didn’t cheer, didn’t shout or clap. She just smiled when she saw him approach, a soft, knowing expression that said more than a thousand cheers would have.

“You finished,” she said, handing him the water.

“I did,” he replied, gulping it down between breaths.

“And?”

He thought about it. “It wasn’t about the clock.”

She nodded as if that’s exactly what she expected him to say. “You looked good out there. Like yourself.”

“I don’t know if I know who that is anymore.”

“You’re figuring it out.”

They found a bench near the edge of the park, away from the buzz of conversation and cleanup crews beginning to take down folding chairs and pack away leftover muffins. He sat heavily, legs aching, knees stiff. Lacey didn’t press him to talk, just sat beside him, quiet, their elbows brushing.

“I was scared,” Jace admitted after a few minutes. “Not of losing. Not of hurting myself. I was scared I’d feel nothing. That I’d cross the line and just… still be empty.”

“But?”

“But I didn’t.”

He turned to her. “It didn’t fix anything. My life’s still a mess. Still jobless, still living in a basement, still figuring out what I even want. But out there, I didn’t feel like a failure. I didn’t feel like a has-been.”

“You felt like a runner.”

He nodded. “Yeah. Like that was still mine. Even after all this time.”

Lacey rested her head on his shoulder. “That’s something.”

It was.

In the weeks following the race, things didn’t magically fall into place. There were no sudden job offers, no dramatic turns of fortune. The house still creaked in the cold mornings, his mom still asked when he planned to “get back out there,” and his bank account still frowned with each rent-free month. But something had shifted beneath the surface.

He started coaching officially—McKenna filled out the paperwork, cleared it with the school, and handed him a clipboard that smelled faintly of old sweat and permanent marker. The paycheck wasn’t much, but it didn’t matter. He showed up every morning before school and again in the afternoons. The kids called him “Coach Jace,” and the name stuck.

He poured himself into it. Not because he was trying to relive anything anymore, but because he knew what it meant to have someone believe in you. He saw himself in all of them—the eager, the frightened, the cocky, the quiet. Each one had a version of the same question behind their eyes: Am I good enough? And each day, he tried to help them find the answer for themselves.

Mariela started placing in the top three at local meets. Carter shaved half a second off his hundred-meter time. Even Darnell, now half-jokingly dubbed “The Freight Train,” started leading the warm-ups. The team had changed. And so had he.

There were bad days too. Days when the knee wouldn’t bend right, when his body reminded him of years spent ignoring doctor’s advice. Days when he woke up in a fog and couldn’t shake the weight on his chest. He still saw the scars—some visible, some buried deep—but he no longer let them steer.

One afternoon in late fall, Lacey met him after practice with two steaming cups of cider and a worn manila envelope.

“What’s this?” he asked, taking a sip.

“Mail came to my place,” she said. “Old habit. You still use me as your second address on some things.”

He opened the envelope. Inside was a letter from a small private college in northern Colorado. One of the assistant coaches had heard from McKenna about Jace’s work with the team and wanted to talk about an open assistant coaching position.

Jace read the letter twice, blinking slowly.

“They want an interview,” he said.

“I know.”

He looked at her, heart thudding.

“I didn’t send anything,” Lacey said quickly. “But Coach McKenna might’ve made a few calls.”

He held the paper like it might turn to ash. “It’s part-time. But it’s something.”

“It’s more than something. It’s a door.”

He folded the letter and slid it back into the envelope. “What if it doesn’t work out?”

“Then you try something else. But at least you’ll know.”

He let out a long breath, staring out at the empty track.

“I think I’m scared of wanting things again,” he admitted.

“You wouldn’t be human if you weren’t.”

They sat in silence again, watching the wind blow old leaves across the lanes.

The interview was set for the following Tuesday. Jace drove the two hours north in his beat-up sedan, fingers gripping the steering wheel tighter than necessary. The school was modest, nestled among pine trees and stone buildings that looked like they belonged in a novel. The head coach, a wiry man named Leon Patel, welcomed him with a handshake and a quiet intensity that Jace recognized instantly.

They didn’t talk much about his past. A few polite nods to his college record, a brief acknowledgment of his injury. The real focus was on now. On how he worked with young athletes. On his philosophy of development and failure and growth.

“What do you want to bring to our program?” Patel asked.

Jace thought for a moment. “I want to help kids understand that their worth isn’t tied to their time on the clock,” he said. “That running can be more than performance. That it can be identity. Healing. A way forward.”

Patel smiled. “We could use that.”

That night, on the drive home, the mountains were quiet silhouettes beneath a moonlit sky. Jace rolled down the window and let the cold air whip through the cabin. He didn’t know yet if he’d get the job. He didn’t know if Cherry Falls would always be home, or just the place he’d rebuilt himself.

But for the first time in a long while, he didn’t feel stuck in a chapter he couldn’t turn the page on.

He was moving forward.

Not fast. Not for glory.

But forward.

And sometimes, that was enough.

Chapter Three: Shadows and Stride

By mid-November, Cherry Falls began to freeze around the edges. Morning runs left Jace’s eyelashes frosted and his breath billowing in thick clouds. The track, often covered with a thin glaze of ice before sunrise, shimmered like a sheet of glass under the weak winter sun. The runners hated it, but they showed up anyway. He showed up too, sometimes before dawn, sometimes before even the maintenance staff had unlocked the gates. The solitude didn’t bother him. He liked the quiet discipline of cold mornings. It reminded him that improvement didn’t require comfort—it required consistency.

The coaching job at Northridge College was still in the air. Coach Patel had emailed to say the interview had gone well, but they were reviewing a couple of other candidates. Jace told himself not to hope too much, but the truth was that he checked his email twice as often now. He told no one about it. Not even Lacey. If disappointment came again, he wanted to meet it in private.

In the meantime, the high school team kept growing stronger. Mariela had become a quiet leader among the younger runners, her once-timid presence now marked with confidence and precision. Carter, now deep in winter conditioning, asked to train with Jace on Saturdays. Even Nia, the captain, began to defer to his input without the defensiveness she’d shown early on. What had begun as a reluctant return to the track was evolving into something deeper—something that resembled purpose.

Still, old habits returned in whispers. There were nights Jace couldn’t sleep, his mind replaying moments he hadn’t asked to remember. The injury. The hotel room. The call from Texas. The silence after the divorce papers had been signed. They drifted back like ghosts, not with malice but with melancholy. Even healing carried shadows. He’d learned not to run from them. Instead, he ran with them—each mile layered with memories, each stride an echo of who he used to be and who he was still becoming.

One Thursday afternoon, just as practice was winding down, Coach McKenna called Jace over to the edge of the bleachers, waving a sheet of paper with that familiar glint in his eye.

“Guess who’s been invited to speak at the state coaching symposium?” he said.

Jace blinked. “Me?”

McKenna smirked. “Don’t act so surprised. People are starting to notice. Your girl Mariela’s times dropped seven seconds in six weeks. That’s not normal.”

“That’s her work, not mine.”

“It’s both. And don’t pretend otherwise.”

The symposium was in Denver, a two-day event packed with clinics, panels, and coaches from around the state—some from major universities, others from tiny rural schools. McKenna had been invited too, and it didn’t take much arm-twisting for Jace to agree.

Still, as he drove toward the city that weekend, he couldn’t help but feel like an imposter. He’d spent the last decade away from this world. Who was he to stand among professionals who had never left it?

The event center was a converted hotel ballroom, the kind with thick carpet and lukewarm coffee. The energy was brisk, serious. People wore polo shirts embroidered with team logos, khakis, and name badges. Jace felt underdressed in his jeans and plain black hoodie, but no one seemed to care. His panel was a small one—“Rebuilding Confidence After Injury and Burnout.” Just a half hour.

When his name was introduced, a few heads turned. One woman in the second row leaned over to her colleague and whispered, “Is that the Cherry Falls guy? Weller?”

He stood at the podium, cleared his throat, and began.

“I’m not going to talk to you about technique. I’m not here to sell drills or conditioning programs. I’m here to talk about something most of us avoid—loss. Loss of performance. Loss of direction. Loss of self.”

A quiet fell over the room.

“I used to think success was permanent. That once you hit a mark—once you hit your PR or got the scholarship—you’d made it. But success is fragile. It evaporates when you stop feeding it. And I stopped feeding it. Not just the training, but the belief.”

He talked about Nationals. About the injury. About losing the scholarship and spending six years pretending he hadn’t once been someone worth watching. He talked about coming home. About failure. And how the most important race he ever ran was a local 5K with no prize and no crowd.

“I realized I didn’t need to reclaim the glory,” he said. “I needed to reclaim the why.”

When he finished, the applause was hesitant at first, then steady. He shook a few hands, took a few business cards. One coach from a school in Wyoming asked if he’d consider consulting on a new team. Another invited him to speak to their athletes in the spring.

As he left the ballroom, Jace felt the strangest thing: belonging. Not to the past. Not to a memory. But to a community he hadn’t realized was still waiting for him.

Back in Cherry Falls, the cold deepened. Snow began falling more regularly, blanketing the track in silence. Practices moved indoors—into the narrow gymnasium hallways, where runners did laps under flickering lights and dodged volleyball nets.

During one of these practices, while demonstrating form drills in the hallway, Jace felt a sharp tug in his hamstring. Not a tear—thank God—but enough of a warning shot that he limped for two days afterward.

Lacey drove him to urgent care despite his protests. The doctor gave him a soft wrap, some ibuprofen, and the predictable lecture about “knowing your age.”

“You think I don’t already know?” Jace muttered under his breath.

Lacey heard him. She smiled. “You’re not old. You’re just not bulletproof anymore.”

“That’s worse.”

But she helped him back into the car anyway, and when they pulled into his driveway, she didn’t leave. She made soup while he iced his leg. She curled up next to him on the couch and fell asleep against his shoulder during a rerun of some terrible detective show.

That night, for the first time in years, Jace didn’t dream about falling behind. He dreamt of a quiet track, of warm sunlight, and of someone waiting at the finish line with open arms.

Two days later, the email arrived.

The job was his.

Assistant Track Coach, Northridge College.

He sat at the kitchen table, phone in one hand, the letter open on his laptop. His mother watched from the stove, hands on her hips.

“You going to take it?” she asked, as if the answer wasn’t obvious.

He didn’t respond right away. He stared at the offer like it might vanish. There would be changes. He’d have to leave town. Find a place to live. Start over again—again.

Lacey walked in through the back door, holding two coffees.

“You saw it?” she said.

He nodded.

She handed him one of the cups. “So?”

“I don’t know.”

She smiled. “You do.”

He laughed softly, shaking his head. “I’m scared.”

“So was I,” she said, “when I stayed here after everyone left. When I opened the bookstore. When I decided I didn’t need to be someone else’s dream anymore.”

He looked at her. “Will you be okay if I go?”

She didn’t answer right away. She took a sip of her coffee and then sat down beside him.

“I’ll be okay. But I’ll miss you.”

He reached for her hand. “I’ll miss you too.”

They didn’t talk for a while. The winter sun filtered through the kitchen window, casting soft shadows across the table. He looked back at the screen.

And this time, he didn’t hesitate.

He clicked “Accept.”

The weeks that followed felt like a blur pressed between long drives and cardboard boxes. Once the decision was made, the reality came fast. Paperwork. Emails. Housing searches. Farewell dinners. There was something almost comical in how mundane the transition felt, considering how significant it truly was. After all the soul-searching, pain, and slow rebuilding, Jace was finally leaving Cherry Falls—not in defeat this time, but with intention.

Northridge College wasn’t big. The campus was tucked into a quiet corner of the pine-backed hills just outside Silver Creek. Snow dusted the roofs and walkways, the air sharp with the smell of evergreens and woodsmoke. The track itself was modest, a six-lane oval behind the recreation center, partly buried under winter frost. It didn’t matter. Jace didn’t need the grandeur anymore. He needed work. He needed to show up. And, slowly, he was learning how to do that.

The head coach, Leon Patel, greeted him with the same calm professionalism he’d shown at the symposium. He introduced Jace to the team over lunch in the student union—a collection of faces that ranged from bright-eyed freshmen to fifth-year seniors who looked at every new coach with quiet skepticism.

“He’s a little older than most of you,” Patel said, cracking a smile as he stood beside Jace, “which means he probably knows twice as much and has pulled muscles you didn’t even know you had. So listen up.”

There were a few chuckles, a few raised eyebrows, but overall, the introduction went smoothly. Jace took it all in without ceremony. He wasn’t here to impress them. He was here to work. And, in time, maybe even belong.

His apartment was a small unit on the edge of campus—one bedroom, thin walls, and a view of the pine forest that dipped into the valley. He furnished it sparingly: a mattress on a metal frame, a desk near the window, a used couch from a thrift shop, and the same corkboard from his old basement in Cherry Falls. He pinned the 5K flyer to it again, even though the race was long past. Not as a trophy, but as a reminder. A mile marker. Proof that he'd taken a step.

The work at Northridge was more structured, but not less rewarding. The athletes here were hungry, driven. Some had been recruited, some walked on with something to prove. Jace found himself drawn to the ones who didn’t quite know how to believe in themselves yet. The ones who, like Mariela, still had the raw pieces but didn’t know how to put them together.

There was a girl named Jasmine who reminded him of his younger self—not in her speed, but in her relentless self-criticism. She ran middle distance and could never quite meet her own expectations. She’d finish a race with a solid time and still mutter under her breath about how she should’ve kicked earlier, or how she let the second corner eat her form.

“You know,” Jace told her one afternoon after she’d thrown her spikes against the fence post-practice, “there’s a difference between wanting to be better and beating the hell out of yourself.”

She frowned. “I don’t want to make excuses.”

“That’s not an excuse. That’s understanding your own limits. You’re not a machine.”

She looked away. “I just don’t want to waste it. My scholarship. This chance.”

He nodded. “Then run like you love it. Not like you owe it.”

She didn’t answer, but she picked up her spikes and carried them home instead of leaving them behind.

Back in Cherry Falls, life moved on without him, the way all towns do. Lacey sent him texts every couple of days—mostly short updates or blurry photos of snowfall clinging to the high school sign. She told him Mariela had set a new school record for the 800 meters. Carter had started talking about applying to college track programs. Coach McKenna still kept a bottle of antacids in his desk drawer and blamed it on “too many damn teenagers with attitude.”

They spoke on the phone sometimes too. Sometimes just for five minutes. Other times for an hour. It was different now—no longer the vague almost-romance that hovered just out of reach, but something softer. Something deeper.

One night, after a long session reviewing training footage with Coach Patel, Jace returned to his apartment to find a package waiting for him. Inside was a framed photo of him crossing the finish line at the 5K, a wide, weathered grin on his face. Behind him, the crowd—modest, scattered—blurred into trees and benches. Tucked behind the frame was a note in Lacey’s handwriting:

“This is who you are. Don’t forget.”

He set it on his desk beneath the corkboard.

By mid-February, the indoor season was in full swing. Jace traveled with the team to a meet two hours away, where he found himself pacing nervously before heats, checking runners’ laces and stretching bands like a man on a mission. It reminded him of his early days at Texas, but this time, he wasn’t the one about to hear the gun. He was the voice in their ear, the calm before the storm.

Jasmine qualified for finals. She ran with a fire he hadn’t seen in her before, keeping her form crisp even as fatigue pulled at her limbs. When she crossed the line, she looked over at him immediately. And though she didn’t smile, there was something in her eyes. A kind of clarity.

“That’s what it’s supposed to feel like,” she told him after.

He didn’t need to say anything. He just nodded.

Later that night, alone in his apartment, Jace opened a notebook and began to write again. Not training notes, not race strategies. Just thoughts.

"There’s no perfect race. Only the one you run. I’m still learning how to finish what I start. But I think, for the first time, I’m not afraid of what comes next."

He closed the notebook and leaned back, listening to the wind outside, the pines brushing against each other like slow applause. There was still more ahead. There would always be more—more miles, more setbacks, more ghosts. But for the first time, he was ready to face them.

Not with regret.

But with stride.

Chapter Four: Breaking the Tape

Spring arrived slowly in Silver Creek. The snow retreated inch by inch, uncovering browned grass and the skeletal remains of last year’s leaves. The track thawed in patches, until finally one morning, the entire oval was clear—slick in places, but usable. The first outdoor practice of the season brought with it a new kind of electricity, and Jace felt it before he even reached the field.

Runners spilled out from the locker rooms in layers of mismatched sweats and thermal shirts, laughing as they jogged the perimeter. The sound of their shoes on rubber, the call-and-response of stretching drills, even the tinny beat of someone’s portable speaker—it all made Jace feel like he had stepped into a waking memory. Not one from the past, but from a dream of what his life could’ve looked like if things had broken differently. And now, somehow, it had broken differently. Not through some miracle, but through work. Through persistence.

Coach Patel tossed him the clipboard before Jace even reached the infield. “You’re taking lead on starts today. Everyone’s rusty.”

Jace grinned. “Aren’t they always?”

The team had grown into him, or he into them. There was trust now. Familiarity. Even the seniors, initially cautious, now deferred to his advice without the wary glances of a few months ago. He wasn’t just the guy with the second chance anymore—he was Coach Weller. It still sounded strange in his head. But not unwelcome.

That morning, while coaching block starts, Jace caught himself demonstrating posture in a way that felt eerily automatic—like his body still knew the geometry of speed, even if the muscles weren’t quite built for it anymore. Jasmine watched him closely, her arms crossed, brow furrowed.

“You ever miss it?” she asked when the others were out of earshot.

He looked at her. “Miss what?”

“Running. Competing. The burn. All of it.”

“Sometimes,” he admitted. “But I don’t miss who I was when I thought that’s all I was good for.”

She nodded slowly. “That’s what I’m scared of. Like, what if I get good? Like really good? And then that’s the only thing people see when they look at me?”

“That’ll happen,” he said. “But you don’t have to believe them. You get to decide what part of yourself gets the final word.”

Jasmine looked down at the blocks. “I don’t know who I am without it.”

“You don’t have to yet. You’ve still got time to run.”

She didn’t say anything more, but later that day, she set a personal record in the 400-meter trial heat. When she crossed the finish line, she didn’t look at the clock first. She looked at Jace.

In late March, Jace drove back to Cherry Falls for a weekend meet where the Northridge team would compete alongside high schools, junior colleges, and a few D-II squads. When he stepped onto the track at his old alma mater, it felt like the world had shrunk and expanded all at once.

The field looked smaller now, the bleachers shorter. But the memories hit hard—teenage Jace running wild, the ghost of a kid with everything ahead of him and no idea how fragile it all was.

He found Lacey in the stands, bundled in a grey scarf, waving as he approached. She stood to hug him, and the embrace lingered in a way that made the past and present blur.

“You look lighter,” she said, stepping back to study him. “Like something fell off your shoulders.”

“Probably all the baggage,” he joked.

They sat together as the events kicked off. Cherry Falls High was still scrappy—short on funding, long on heart. Mariela anchored the 4x800 relay and blew past her final lap like a girl possessed. She’d grown leaner, smoother in her stride, no longer the uncertain freshman hiding behind oversized warmups.

“God,” Jace muttered. “Look at her go.”

“She’s chasing something,” Lacey said. “But not ghosts. She’s chasing herself.”

When the relay team won by nearly ten seconds, Mariela caught his eye across the field. She didn’t wave. She just nodded, a small, firm acknowledgment. They both knew what it meant.

That night, over dinner in the back room of the town’s only Italian restaurant, Jace and Lacey sat across from each other in a booth with worn leather seats and a table carved with initials from decades of local dates and teenage bravado. They talked easily—about the team, about Lacey’s bookstore, about a dog she was thinking of adopting. Jace told her about Patel’s offer to extend his contract another year and add more responsibility to his role.

“That sounds permanent,” Lacey said.

“It might be.”

“You okay with that?”

He took a sip of water and nodded. “I think I am. I thought I’d always feel like I was stuck between what I had and what I lost. But now I’m starting to think... maybe I was never meant to go back. Maybe I just needed to find a new way forward.”

She reached across the table, her hand brushing his. “You found one.”

He smiled. “I had help.”

The next morning, Jace went for a run through the old neighborhood. He didn’t time himself. He didn’t stretch or warm up or wear proper racing shoes. He just pulled on an old hoodie and started moving. The streets hadn’t changed much. Same cracked sidewalks. Same leaning mailboxes. Same trees with half-carved hearts on their trunks from kids who thought love was something you could anchor with initials and bark.

He passed the gas station. The library. The cemetery. All places that had marked time for him once. But now they felt like pages in a finished chapter.

When he reached the track again, he walked to the center lane and stood still. The wind stirred gently. There were no races today, no whistles, no crowd.

Jace took a deep breath, closed his eyes, and began to run.

Not to reclaim.

Not to prove.

Just because he could.

Jace stayed in Cherry Falls for two more days after the meet. He told Coach Patel he needed to check on family, which wasn’t untrue, but the real reason was harder to admit. Being back stirred something in him—something deeper than nostalgia. It wasn’t longing, exactly. More like a reckoning. He needed to walk the streets, visit the old haunts, and prove to himself that he could be here without losing his footing. That he could return to the scene of his collapse and not collapse again.

On Sunday afternoon, he stopped by the cemetery. His father’s grave was near the back, shaded by a row of pine trees that dripped sap in slow beads across the worn stones. The marker was modest, engraved with his father’s full name and a quote Jace’s mother had chosen: “The race is long, but worth running.” Jace had never liked it at the time. He thought it sounded like a line from a church pamphlet. But now, standing over the grave with his hands in his jacket pockets, the words made a little more sense.

“I took the job,” he said aloud, though the trees were the only ones listening. “And I’m not screwing it up this time. At least not the same way.”

There was no breeze, no stirring of branches to mark a response. Just the quiet breath of windless air and the faint sound of birds in the distance. But something shifted inside him—some knot loosening just enough to let him breathe deeper.

He knelt briefly, resting one hand against the stone. “Thanks for putting the track shoes on me when I was five. Even if I didn’t get it then.”

He didn’t stay long. Just enough.

Later that night, over coffee in Lacey’s living room, he found himself flipping through one of her photo albums—real ones, with plastic sleeves and smudged corners. She laughed softly every time he lingered on a particularly embarrassing shot: him in a letterman jacket two sizes too big, or his prom photo with hair too gelled and a smile too forced.

“I kept these because I thought you might disappear one day,” she said, curling her legs beneath her on the couch.

He looked up. “Disappear?”

“Yeah. You were always faster than the rest of us. Not just on the track. You were always running toward something. We just didn’t know what.”

He considered that. “Neither did I.”

She flipped the page to a team picture—senior year. Jace stood in the center, tall and grinning, his arms around two teammates. Behind them, the old scoreboard read CHAMPIONS.

“Do you miss it?” she asked.

“Sometimes.”

“I meant the glory.”

He thought about that longer. “No. I miss the feeling of knowing what came next. I miss the clarity. You train, you run, you win—or you lose. Either way, it’s simple. Life’s messier than that.”

“It is,” she agreed. “But maybe it’s better, too.”

When he returned to Northridge, spring had begun to settle in earnest. The snowmelt ran thick through the gutters, and small yellow buds broke through the ground like promises trying not to be broken. The team was gearing up for their biggest meet yet—one that would bring scouts, local press, and alumni. Coach Patel was pushing harder now, sensing potential not just for fast times, but for the kind of momentum that builds a program’s future.

Jace threw himself into it. He led extra sessions. He hosted film reviews in the study hall. He met one-on-one with runners who didn’t even know how badly they needed to be heard. And all the while, his own legs itched to move—not in regret, but in understanding. He was no longer the runner at the center of the lane. He was the voice at the edge, steadying, guiding, helping others find their rhythm.

One Friday, while watching Jasmine run intervals on the back stretch, Coach Patel stepped up beside him, arms folded, watching silently for a beat.

“She’s got grit,” he said.

“She’s got demons too,” Jace replied.

“Don’t we all.”

They stood in silence for a moment, watching Jasmine finish her final rep, bent over in exhaustion but refusing to collapse.

“She reminds me of someone I used to coach,” Patel added.

Jace smirked. “Let me guess—young, angry, too proud to ask for help?”

“Something like that.”

“She’s going to burn herself out if she doesn’t learn how to recover.”

“Then teach her.”

Jace glanced sideways. “You really think that’s my job now?”

“I think you’re the only one she listens to.”

It wasn’t a compliment, not really. More like a challenge.

A week before the meet, Jace sat in his apartment, sifting through a small box of belongings he hadn’t opened since the move. Inside were old journals, receipts, race bibs, and tucked at the bottom, a crumpled letter he barely remembered keeping: the rejection letter from the U.S. Olympic Trials committee.

He unfolded it. The wording was formal. Cold. “We regret to inform you…” followed by dates and language meant to soften the blow but only sharpened the failure. He held it for a while, not with anger or sorrow, but with something quieter. Almost pity.

He had thought, once, that this letter ended something. That it had closed a door and left him out in the cold. But now, sitting here, he realized it hadn’t closed anything. It had simply sent him down another path—a longer one, maybe, with more bends and stumbles, but a path that had finally brought him back to his feet.

He tore the letter in half, then into quarters. He dropped the pieces into the small trash can by his desk and exhaled.

It didn’t matter anymore.

The night before the big meet, Jace stood on the track under the lights, alone. The rest of the campus was quiet. The field hummed with a kind of potential—like something was waiting, just beneath the surface. He walked the lane slowly, hand brushing the rough rubber, counting steps.

He thought about all the versions of himself that had stood on tracks just like this—some sure, some terrified, some already broken. He thought about the first time he’d broken fifty seconds. The time he missed a race because of a hangover. The last sprint he ever ran before the tear.

But he didn’t feel haunted.

He felt whole.

He stood at the start line, bent slightly at the waist, eyes on the invisible finish.

Then he whispered, not to himself but to whatever part of him had waited for this moment:

“I’m still here.”

And that was enough.

Chapter Five: Wind at the Back

The day of the Northridge Spring Invitational dawned bright and windless—a runner’s dream. The sky was a high, uninterrupted blue, the air just cool enough to keep muscles warm but hearts steady. By mid-morning, the campus buzzed with energy. Vans rolled into the lot behind the track complex. Students handed out programs. Athletes stretched in quiet corners of the field, headphones in, minds locked into race mode. There was tension, sure, but it wasn’t fear. It was focus. Everyone understood this meet mattered more than the stopwatch could show.

Jace stood at the edge of the infield wearing a navy windbreaker with the Northridge logo stitched across the chest. His whistle hung loose around his neck, the clipboard tucked under his arm filled with handwritten notes. He’d gone over splits the night before, memorized athlete lanes, even double-checked the weather. There were still nerves—there always would be—but they were different now. Not the nerves of self-doubt. These were the nerves of someone who cared. Of someone invested.

Coach Patel appeared beside him with a cup of black coffee and a calm expression.

“You know it’s weird,” he said, glancing out at the warming athletes. “Some years it’s just about surviving the season. This year? Feels like we’re building something.”

Jace nodded, eyes scanning the track. “They’ve earned it.”

“You’ve earned it,” Patel added.

Jace didn’t respond. Not because he disagreed, but because he didn’t quite know how to hold praise yet. It felt like a currency he hadn’t earned. Or maybe he just wasn’t sure how to spend it.

As the first heats began, Jace slipped into rhythm. Timing races. Checking on form. Offering subtle cues—lift your knees, breathe through the corner, don’t muscle the start. His voice was steady, not loud. He didn’t bark orders or shout for attention. He gave just enough, like a compass more than a motor.

By the time Jasmine stepped onto the track for the 400-meter final, the crowd had swelled. Students lined the fences. Parents clutched phones in one hand and bottled water in the other. A small cluster of regional scouts sat near the finish, clipboards in hand, eyes sharp.

Jasmine wore her game face—a blank, unreadable look that Jace had come to recognize. Not nerves. Not arrogance. Just presence.

He walked up beside her as she adjusted her starting blocks.

“You know the splits,” he said. “You’ve done the work.”

She nodded, eyes forward.

“But remember one thing.”

She turned slightly.

“Don’t run scared. Run free.”

She gave a single nod. “I got it, Coach.”

Then the gun sounded, and she exploded from the blocks.

Her start was clean—aggressive but balanced. Jace tracked her stride: eleven paces to the break, good posture, arms tight. She surged down the back stretch with precision, holding position through the curve. At 300 meters, she made her move. She pushed off the turn with everything she had, form tightening slightly under fatigue but not breaking.

The final straight was a test of will.

Jace didn’t shout. He didn’t need to.

She crossed the line in first.

Fifty-eight point three.

A school record.

The crowd erupted. Her teammates mobbed her before she could even catch her breath. But through the noise, Jasmine found Jace on the sideline. She didn’t grin, didn’t celebrate—just raised a fist and pointed once to the track. We did this.

He nodded back. No grand gesture. Just a shared understanding.

That night, after the field cleared and the sun dipped behind the hilltops, Jace found himself alone again on the track. Not for ceremony. Just for reflection. He walked the curve where Jasmine had made her move, replaying the moment over and over. There was a strange ache in his chest—familiar but not unwelcome. Not sadness. Not even longing.

It was satisfaction.

The kind of quiet fulfillment he never found in trophies or articles or headlines. The kind that came from giving something away and watching someone else carry it forward.

Behind him, footsteps crunched on gravel.

He turned to see Coach Patel walking up with a duffel slung over his shoulder.

“Thought you’d be gone by now,” Patel said.

“Hard to walk away sometimes.”

Patel stopped beside him. “You ever think about the long game?”

Jace raised an eyebrow.

“This program. Where it goes. What it becomes. I don’t just want to build a season. I want to build a legacy. Culture. Identity.”

“I figured you already had one.”

“I’m not talking about me,” Patel said. “I’m talking about you.”

Jace looked at him, surprised.

“You want me to stay on full-time?”

“I want you to help me lead it.”

The idea landed like a weight and a lift all at once. He hadn’t thought beyond the season. Hadn’t dared to. And yet… the thought didn’t scare him. Not the way it used to.

“I’ll need time to think about it,” he said honestly.

“Take all the time you want,” Patel replied. “But not too much. They need you. And not just for sprints.”

Later, in the quiet of his apartment, Jace sat by the window, looking out over the pines. The trees swayed in the wind like they were keeping time with something old and true. He picked up the notebook again, flipped to a clean page, and began to write—not about training, or athletes, or numbers.

He wrote about stillness. About endurance. About what it meant to teach someone to run without fear.

He wrote about the gift of motion.

The courage of starting again.

And the quiet triumph of staying.

The following week was quieter, a kind of soft exhale after the high of the meet. Practice resumed with less urgency but no less effort. The team still came early, still stretched in silence, still hit their intervals with focus—but now there was a calm underneath it all. The pressure to prove something had lifted. What remained was the work itself, and strangely, that was enough for everyone.

Jace noticed the shift most clearly in Jasmine. She didn’t ask for her times at the end of every rep. She didn’t slam her shoes against the fence when her pace fell behind. She had found something on that track during the meet—some confidence rooted not in performance but in process. It radiated from her posture, from the way she encouraged her teammates, from the fact that she now stayed after practice to stretch properly instead of rushing off in silence.

One afternoon, as the sun dropped low and cast long shadows across the infield, she lingered near the bleachers where Jace was packing up cones.

“You think I’ve got a shot at the conference title?” she asked, wrapping her jacket tighter.

“I think that’s the wrong question,” he said without looking up.

She waited.

“You’ve got the shot. That’s not in doubt. The question is whether you’re going to keep running scared, or start running like you don’t owe anyone anything.”

“I thought I already was.”

He looked up at her, not with critique but care. “You’re close. But there’s still something tight in your stride when you’re ahead. Like you’re bracing for someone to catch you.”

She considered this. “Maybe I am.”

“You don’t have to be.”

She nodded slowly. “I’ll think about that.”

Then, after a pause, “Thanks for not giving up on me.”

Jace slung the last cone into the bag. “You didn’t give up on yourself. I just made sure you knew someone saw the effort.”

She walked off with her hands in her pockets, shoulders relaxed. Jace stood for a moment longer, watching her cross the field, and thought of all the kids he’d known who never heard that someone was proud of them. It wasn’t just about performance. It was about permission—to try, to fail, to get back up again.

A few days later, Jace received a letter in the mail from Cherry Falls High. The envelope was addressed in Coach McKenna’s uneven scrawl. Inside was a short note and a photograph: Mariela standing on a podium, a gold medal around her neck, her face half-sunburned but beaming.

State champion, 800m – 2:10.14.

Underneath, McKenna had written, “You were the first leg in this relay.”

Jace read it twice. Then a third time. The words weren’t flowery. They weren’t poetic. But they landed with a kind of finality that left his chest full.

He pinned the photo to the corkboard in his apartment, just beside the 5K flyer and the picture Lacey had sent. Beneath it, he scribbled a date and a single sentence:

“Proof that leaving something doesn’t mean you’ve stopped carrying it.”

In early April, Lacey came to visit.

She’d been meaning to for weeks, and now that the bookstore had a weekend lull, she made good on it. She arrived late on a Friday night, her hair still wind-tangled from the drive, two overnight bags in tow and a half-warm pie on the passenger seat.

“Is it weird,” she said as he helped her unload, “that this place smells like pine and ambition?”

“It smells like muscle liniment and forgotten laundry,” Jace said, laughing.

They stayed up late that night, sitting on the small couch with their knees touching, trading stories about the kids they’d watched grow, the people they’d watched fade. She asked about Jasmine. He asked about Carter. They both talked about Mariela like a shared little sister who’d found her voice.

At one point, Lacey grew quiet, her hand tracing the rim of her mug.

“You’re different,” she said finally.

“Is that a good thing?”

“It’s a grounded thing. You used to bounce between wanting the world and wanting to hide from it. Now you’re just… here.”

He leaned back, the couch groaning beneath them. “I think this is the first time I’ve ever stayed somewhere not because I had nowhere else to go, but because I wanted to.”

They sat in silence for a while. Outside, the wind moved through the trees like slow applause. Inside, something fragile but steady wrapped itself around the moment.

On Sunday morning, they walked the trail that wound behind the campus—past the stream, through the patch of tall grass where kids sometimes went to sneak cigarettes. It was cool, the kind of cool that reminded you spring was only a visitor, not yet a tenant.

“I got another offer,” Jace said, watching the path ahead.

Lacey looked over. “From who?”

“Another school. Bigger program. Better funding. Full-time. Head coaching job.”

She didn’t speak right away.

“You thinking about it?”

“I was,” he said. “I’m not anymore.”

She stopped walking.

“Why?”

He turned to her, hands in his jacket pockets. “Because I don’t want to start over again just to chase something that looks better on paper. This place, these kids—they’ve taught me that sometimes the right thing isn’t the biggest. It’s just the most honest.”

Lacey smiled. “So what now?”

He shrugged. “I keep showing up. I keep coaching. I stay.”

She stepped closer. “And me?”

He looked at her then, really looked. “I’d like you to stay, too.”

And for once, neither of them needed to fill the silence that followed. It was enough to stand there, two people who’d circled each other for years finally stepping into the same lane.

By mid-month, the Northridge team had shattered three school records, earned a place at the regional qualifiers, and developed something Jace hadn’t seen in a long time: camaraderie without ego. They worked like a relay team, even when running separate events. They cheered for each other without irony, competed without spite. There were still mistakes, still setbacks—but now they knew how to carry them together.

One afternoon, Coach Patel pulled Jace aside after practice.

“I put your name in for Coach of the Year,” he said matter-of-factly.

Jace blinked. “That’s a joke, right?”

“Nope. I don’t joke about committee politics. Doesn’t mean anything really. But it’s good for the program. And you’ve earned it.”

Jace shook his head. “It’s not about me.”

“Then you’re the right person to get it.”

He didn’t win. Another coach from a southern university with a string of national qualifiers took the honor. But it didn’t sting. Not even a little. Because Jace hadn’t needed the title. He already had the reward.

They called him Coach.

And they meant it.

The regional meet loomed large on the calendar, circled in red ink, underlined twice. The team felt it in their shoulders, in the way they double-checked their splits, the way even laughter around the water coolers had taken on a sharper edge. Everything seemed to orbit that date—classes, diets, sleep schedules. Jace watched them all closely, making quiet mental notes: who was overtraining, who was mentally teetering, who was pretending to be fine but running slower every week.

It reminded him, painfully, of himself during the months before Nationals. How he'd ignored the signs, powered through discomfort until it became damage. How no one had said the one thing he needed to hear—slow down before it breaks. He wasn't going to make that mistake again.

During a midweek practice, Jasmine came up short in a 300-meter repeat, clutching her side with a frustrated grimace.

“Talk to me,” Jace said, crouching beside her as she sat in the grass.

“Cramp,” she said quickly. Too quickly. “Didn’t hydrate enough.”

He watched her for a beat, then offered a bottle of water. “You’re lying.”

She looked at him, defensive.

He didn’t press. Just waited.

After a long silence, she sighed. “It’s not the cramp. It’s everything. I can’t sleep. I feel like I’m supposed to PR every time I run. Like if I don’t, I’m wasting everyone’s time.”

“You know that’s not true.”

“Do I?”

Jace sat down next to her. “Let me tell you something. I trained for years thinking if I didn’t beat my last time, I was a failure. And when I couldn’t beat it anymore, I stopped running altogether. You know how many races I missed out on, just because I was afraid I wouldn’t be better than I used to be?”

She didn’t speak.

“You don’t owe this team a personal best. You don’t owe me anything. You run because it’s yours. That’s the only reason that matters.”

“Then why does it still feel like I’m going to disappoint someone?”

He looked at her, and the answer came honestly. “Because you care. But there’s a difference between pressure and punishment. Don’t confuse the two.”

She didn’t say much after that. But the next day, she showed up early. Ran her reps at a steady pace. And smiled—just once—when she finished the last one.

The night before the meet, Jace gathered the team in the rec center lobby. They sprawled on couches and folding chairs, some with ice packs strapped to legs, others tangled in their headphones, zoning out.

He stood at the front, holding nothing. No clipboard. No stats. Just his voice.

“Tomorrow’s not going to be perfect,” he said. “Someone’s going to false start. Someone’s going to slip. Someone’s going to miss their mark. That’s not defeat. That’s reality. We don’t control perfection. We control presence. We show up. We compete. And we carry each other.”

He looked around the room, meeting each pair of eyes in turn.

“If someone falls behind, we don’t look away. If someone wins, we celebrate them like we won too. Because this isn’t just about who crosses first. It’s about the people you’ve become since you started this season.”

No one clapped. No one needed to. The silence was full enough.

Race day broke under overcast skies, the kind of grey that flattened shadows but kept the temperature cool and perfect for fast times. Buses pulled into the stadium lot from all over the state. The infield buzzed with warm-ups, uniform checks, and the quiet electricity of athletes preparing to toe their respective lines.

Jace moved like a conductor without a baton—circulating between athletes, checking spikes, tightening straps, offering short, pointed cues. His voice never rose. His heart did.

He watched the long jumpers and distance runners. Timed intervals with a stopwatch worn smooth from years of use. Then it was Jasmine’s turn.

She lined up for the 400-meter heat, eyes forward, jaw tight. Jace leaned in just before the starter called them to the line.

“You don’t need to prove anything today,” he said.

She gave a small nod, just enough to show she heard him.

When the gun fired, she didn’t burst out too early. She ran smart. Her first hundred was smooth, the second tight and efficient. At the curve, she kicked—not with panic, but with clarity. And in the final stretch, when another runner crept up in lane three, Jasmine leaned—not to win, but to finish well.

Fifty-seven point eight. A new PR.

She didn’t look surprised. Just satisfied.

Later that day, Jace sat on the edge of the bleachers, watching the sunset drag long stripes of color over the mountains. The meet was over. Northridge had taken second overall—higher than anyone predicted. The kids were back on the bus, high on endorphins and sugary drinks, half-asleep, half-invincible.

Patel came to sit beside him, unwrapping a granola bar with one hand.

“You keep doing this, and I’m going to have to promote you,” he said.

Jace smiled. “And then who would run practices while you give interviews?”

They sat for a minute, the quiet between them easy now.

“You still thinking about that bigger program?” Patel asked.

Jace shook his head. “I’m already where I want to be.”

Patel nodded. “Good.”

Jace looked out at the track one more time. It didn’t feel like a battlefield anymore. Not like something he’d barely survived. It felt like home. Like purpose. And for once, that was more than enough.

That night, he called Lacey.

She answered on the second ring.

“How’d it go?” she asked.

He paused, then grinned. “We didn’t win.”

“That bad?”

“No,” he said. “We didn’t win. But we built something. Something good.”

On the other end, he heard her smiling.

“That’s what I wanted to hear,” she said.

“Yeah,” he replied, stretching back against the couch, his legs sore but his spirit steady. “Me too.”

Chapter Six: The Second Lap

By the time summer settled over the valley, everything had slowed. Classes were over, the track team’s official season was complete, and most students had scattered—back home, to internships, to camps or jobs that barely paid but promised the illusion of experience. The campus grew quiet, humming only with maintenance crews and the occasional early-morning jogger.

But Jace stayed.

He didn’t have to. Coach Patel had told him to take a break, to step away for a few weeks and come back rested. But Jace wasn’t tired. Not in the way he used to be. He kept coming in every morning, checking equipment, logging notes, and drafting schedules for fall conditioning. He liked the quiet repetition. There was peace in preparation, in maintenance. Not every phase of life needed a finish line. Some parts just needed tending.

One Thursday, he drove out to a local middle school track to watch a summer youth clinic that one of his runners, Darnell—now coaching part-time—had helped organize. There were dozens of kids, most under fifteen, all wearing oversized T-shirts and mismatched shoes. Their strides were awkward, their timing inconsistent, but their joy was real.

Jace watched from the fence as they stumbled over mini hurdles and grinned through mock races. One girl, no taller than his hip, ran a full 400 meters with her arms flailing wildly, then collapsed in the grass and shouted, “I WON!” even though she’d come in last.

He laughed out loud. Not at her, but with her. That was it. The thing he’d lost for so long. Not the ability to win. But the joy of moving forward, even if the path was uneven.

“Coach Weller?” a voice said behind him.

He turned to find Mariela standing there, slightly taller now, her posture even more confident than the last time he saw her. She wore a team shirt from the University of Denver, her backpack slung casually over one shoulder.

“Didn’t expect to see you here,” she said.

“I could say the same.”

“I’m volunteering,” she added, glancing toward the field. “They needed help with drills.”

“Seems like you're doing alright.”

She smiled. “Freshman year was brutal. But I’m getting there.”

They stood together, watching the next group line up for a relay. Jace noticed how naturally she gave instructions, how the kids listened to her without hesitation.

“You ever think you’d be the one coaching?” he asked.

“Not until I realized I didn’t want other kids to feel like they were running alone.”

He looked at her, the words hitting him in the chest.

“That’s exactly why I stayed,” he said.

Later that month, Jace and Lacey took a weekend trip to the coast. It wasn’t elaborate. Just a rented cabin, a cooler full of fruit and cheap wine, and a beach that was more rock than sand. They walked the shoreline at dusk, the wind sharp and salt-laced, the horizon blurring into a soft, endless blue.

He told her, quietly, that he was thinking of staying at Northridge for the long haul.

“You mean like… another season?” she asked.

“No. I mean, like… roots. A home. Not a pit stop.”

She didn’t answer right away, just leaned her head against his arm.

“You’re allowed to have that, you know.”

“I didn’t always believe I was.”

“You do now?”

“I’m learning.”

They sat on the rocks until the stars emerged, until their hands were cold and the silence was comfortable. It felt like something sacred—not an ending, not a beginning, but a continuation. Like hitting the bell lap in a race that mattered not for where it ended, but for how you ran it.

In early August, Coach Patel held a meeting in the athletic department’s conference room. He had news: two of Northridge’s sprinters had received regional honors. One of the relay teams had qualified for a fall invitational. A sponsor was offering new equipment grants. There was talk—just talk—of an expanded coaching staff.

And then, he turned to Jace.

“Starting next semester, you’re no longer assistant anything,” he said, his voice dry but warm. “You’re Co-Head Coach.”

A quiet clap spread around the table. Jace blinked. It hadn’t been expected—not yet. But in his gut, it felt earned.

Afterward, as he packed his notes, Patel stepped beside him.

“I didn’t do it as a favor.”

“I didn’t think you did.”

“You built something here. Not just with the kids. With this whole program. It needed a heart. You gave it one.”

Jace didn’t know how to respond. So he just said, “Thanks.”

But the real thanks came the following week, when the athletes started returning—more of them than ever before. New faces. Old ones. Kids who’d heard about the team not from flyers or recruiters, but from other runners. From word-of-mouth. From results that came not just in seconds shaved, but in stories shared.

The track felt different now. Not because of new paint or better equipment. But because of the people who ran on it. They ran for each other. They ran like they were part of something bigger.

And Jace, finally, knew exactly where he fit.

The morning before the new season began, Jace returned to the track alone. It was early. The sun had barely cracked the mountains, and the air was still cool from the night. He stood in the middle lane, eyes closed, listening to the distant hum of sprinklers and the rustle of pine trees. No ghosts came this time. No regret. No pain.

He began to jog. Slowly at first. Then faster. His legs remembered what to do. His heart stayed steady. He made a full lap, then another. Not timed. Not measured. Just moving. The way he used to. The way he always wanted to again.

And when he stopped, he looked down the straightaway and smiled.

He wasn’t chasing glory.

He wasn’t chasing youth.

He wasn’t chasing anything at all.

He was exactly where he was supposed to be.

Still running.

Still whole.

Still here.

Jace’s first day as Co-Head Coach began like any other—a cup of black coffee, a walk to the fieldhouse, and the low hum of summer crickets retreating as the sun crested over the tree line. But beneath the familiar rhythms, something had shifted. His name was now etched on the office doorplate beside Coach Patel’s. He had a key to the equipment room, a new whistle still in its packaging, and his own login for the athletic department portal. Small things. Details. But together, they made it real.

The morning practice roster was full. Too full, almost. Tryouts had yielded a larger incoming class than expected, and the returning athletes were energized by the previous season’s momentum. More than once during warm-ups, he caught snippets of conversations about goals—conference finals, transfer scouts, personal bests. These weren’t just kids trying out a sport anymore. These were athletes with conviction. That knowledge thrilled and humbled him at once.

Coach Patel joined him near the edge of the field, sipping from a thermos that probably hadn’t been washed in weeks.

“You’re not going to be able to remember all their names in one week,” he said.

“I’ve got a system,” Jace replied, holding up a notepad thick with scribbled bios and notes from individual meetings.

Patel peered at the paper. “You still doing this by hand?”

“Helps me remember. Muscle memory.”

Patel grinned. “God help us if we ever digitize.”

Jace returned his focus to the track, watching as Jasmine led a set of incoming sprinters through warm-up drills. She had stepped naturally into a captain’s role, coaching in the way that Jace recognized from a younger version of himself—by doing, not declaring.

After the first set of 200-meter repeats, Jace gathered the runners at the bleachers for a talk. Not a speech—he didn’t like speeches—but a tone-setting conversation.

“You don’t have to be the fastest to belong here,” he began. “You don’t have to be the strongest. But if you show up, if you do the work, and if you run for more than just the clock, then this team will meet you where you are.”

He scanned their faces. Some nodded. Others simply stared, listening, weighing him.

“I don’t want heroes,” he said. “I want teammates.”

That landed. He could see it in their postures. It wasn’t applause, but absorption.

That evening, he called Lacey. She was back in Cherry Falls after spending a week helping her sister move. The call wasn’t about anything urgent—just a check-in. But they talked for nearly an hour, the way people do when silence feels like a waste of something rare.

“Do you miss it?” she asked at one point. “Cherry Falls?”

“I miss the smell of rain on the football field,” he said. “I miss Coach McKenna’s grumbling. I miss the sound of cleats on the hallway floors. But no—I don’t miss living there. That chapter ended. I think I’m okay with that now.”

“I’m proud of you,” she said, and he believed her.

“Would it be crazy,” he asked slowly, “if I said I want you to move here?”

A pause. He could hear the tension in the breath she held.

“It wouldn’t be crazy,” she said softly.

“I’m not asking now. Not today. But I’m not going anywhere. Not for a long time. And I’d like… I’d like to build something here. With you. If it’s right.”

She didn’t answer with words. Just a long, quiet sigh that felt like relief and promise bundled into one.

“I’ll think about it,” she said. “But it doesn’t feel crazy. That’s something.”

Over the next few weeks, Jace settled deeper into the role. He coordinated with academic advisors, helped arrange training schedules that fit around work-study jobs, and even sat in on budget meetings with the athletic director. Every once in a while, the old fear crept in—What if I’m not enough?—but he let it rise, then pass. The fear wasn’t failure anymore. The fear was abandoning something just as it was starting to bloom.

One late afternoon, a thunderstorm rolled in unexpectedly, cutting practice short. The athletes scattered under the pavilion, laughing, cursing the sky, wrapping towels around their shoulders. Jace stood at the edge of the field, watching the rain pound the empty track.

Patel approached beside him. “You ever miss running?”

Jace nodded. “Every day.”

“But not like before.”

“No. Now it’s not regret. It’s… memory. Fuel.”

“Good,” Patel said, turning away. “That means you’re where you’re supposed to be.”

Fall crept in slowly. The leaves on the ridge behind the stadium turned from sharp green to soft gold. The mornings grew brisk. Practices moved earlier. The team was in sync now, and the results showed—top-three placements, personal bests dropping across the board, whispers of Northridge becoming a program to watch.

But the highlight, for Jace, came not from a stopwatch, but a letter.

It arrived in a plain envelope, addressed in a teenage scrawl.

Inside was a note from a freshman named Eric, a quiet kid who’d walked on in the summer but dropped out before the second week, citing “schedule conflicts.” Jace had spoken with him once—just once—after the first practice, when Eric struggled to finish the cooldown lap and apologized for “being slow.”

The note was short.

Coach Weller—
I wanted to say thank you for telling me I didn’t have to be the fastest. That stuck with me. I’m training again. Not for a team. Just for me.
Maybe I’ll try out again next year.
Eric

Jace folded the note carefully, slipped it into his notebook between the training plans and roster printouts, and sat in his office for a long time, just breathing.

It wasn’t about medals anymore.

It was about moments like this—quiet, unseen, deeply real.

The ones that reminded him that what he had once lost was never the end.

Only the beginning of another kind of race.

Chapter Seven: The Finish and the Flame

The year wound down as autumn hardened into early winter, and the edges of the days grew sharper. The sun no longer lingered in the sky—it dipped early, often unnoticed between the last laps and the last weight room sessions. Frost clung to the bleachers in the mornings. Runners layered up. Practice became less about loud intensity and more about the quiet effort of discipline—form, focus, rest, repair. It was a season of patience. Of waiting for results that wouldn’t show for months.

But Jace found he loved this time of year most.

There was something honest about it. You couldn’t fake it through cold workouts. The weather didn’t flatter effort. It demanded something deeper—consistency without recognition. Motion without reward. It reminded him of what it took to come back from nothing, to run without spectators, to believe in a finish line you couldn’t yet see.

He started hosting evening office hours in the small coach’s suite near the locker rooms. He never advertised them, never posted a sign. Just left the door cracked open and sat behind his desk, grading split sheets or jotting notes. Slowly, one by one, runners started stopping in—not to talk track, always, but to talk life.

Jasmine came in first, half a week before finals. She slumped into the chair across from him without ceremony.

“You ever feel like none of it’s enough?” she asked.

Jace didn’t flinch. “What part?”

“All of it. School. Training. Like you’re doing everything right and it’s still not going to mean anything in the end.”

He considered her carefully before answering.

“Yes,” he said. “I used to feel like that every day. And sometimes I still do.”

She looked at him, clearly expecting a different response.

“But,” he added, “that feeling passes. It’s not truth. It’s fatigue dressed as doubt. Your body and mind whispering nonsense because they’re overwhelmed. And when you rest, when you breathe, when you give it a day—sometimes the meaning comes back.”

She didn’t say anything for a long time. Then she stood. “Thanks.”

“For what?”

“For not trying to fix it.”

He nodded. “That’s not my job.”

“Then what is?”

“To remind you that you’re not alone.”

Later that week, after the team’s final group run of the season, Jace stood in front of them at the edge of the fieldhouse. The light was fading fast, turning everything blue and gold in equal measure. They stood quietly, huddled in jackets, steam rising from their shoulders.

“You don’t measure a season by your PRs,” he told them. “Or the podiums. Or who got written up in the paper. You measure it by how many days you showed up when it was easier not to. By how many teammates you lifted when you could’ve focused on yourself. You measure it by how much you gave even when no one asked.”

They listened without shifting. No glances at phones. No glib remarks.

“You’ve made this team something real,” he said. “Don’t forget that.”

When they walked away, some in small clusters, some alone, he watched their backs. Not looking for posture or form or fatigue. Just presence. They were there. Still walking. Still together.

That was everything.

On the last official day before winter break, Coach Patel brought in donuts and bad jokes and told the athletes to go home and forget about running for at least a week. Jace stayed behind to lock up the weight room, which had become his sanctuary in recent months. As he turned out the lights and set the alarm, he stood for a moment in the doorway, watching the shadows stretch along the floor mats and barbells.

He didn’t need to be here. He could’ve gone home. But he wasn’t lingering out of habit.

He was honoring something.

The second chance.

The decision to return to the sport—not as a star, not as a savior—but as a builder.

That weekend, he went back to Cherry Falls for a few days. It wasn’t an obligation anymore. It was a visit. He stopped by the high school track early Saturday morning. The gate was unlocked. The air was still. A light snow covered the rubber, soft enough to leave prints with every step.

He walked a lap. Then another.

At the far end, by the curve, he knelt to clear snow from a small section of lane three.

Etched in faded chalk, still faintly visible beneath the frost, were the numbers 49.38—his high school record.

He smiled. Not with bitterness. With fondness.

“Thanks for getting me started,” he whispered to the cold wind. “But I’ve got other records to chase now.”

On his last day in town, Lacey joined him for breakfast at a tiny café just off Main Street. They sat by the window, watching bundled townspeople move past with paper cups and holiday bags.

“You’re not staying this time,” she said, more observation than question.

“No. I’m not.”

“I thought that might scare me.”

“Does it?”

She shook her head. “No. Because I know where to find you.”

He reached across the table, took her hand. “Come visit more. Or more than visit.”

“I will,” she said. “I already started looking at jobs up there.”

They didn’t toast to it. Didn’t make plans with too much weight. Just let the words hang, shared and understood.

Back in Silver Creek, as the first deep snow of winter blanketed campus, Jace stood at the window of his apartment watching the field below. The track was gone now, covered completely, like it had never existed.

But he didn’t need to see it.

He knew it was there. Waiting. Ready.

He picked up his notebook, flipped to the last blank page, and wrote:

“You can run out of breath. You can run out of luck. But if you run with heart, you never really stop. You become the road. You become the fire. You become the finish, even when you start over.”

He closed the book.

The season was done.

But he was still running.

And now, he was running home.
