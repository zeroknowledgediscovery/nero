It was 4:32 in the morning when Jonathan Markham stepped out onto the dewed front porch, keys in hand, suitcase in the other. The fog still hung low over the lawn, curling around the tires of his old Ford Explorer like it wanted him to stay. He didn’t. Not anymore. The air felt heavier than it should’ve, a strange mixture of betrayal and finality that stuck to the roof of his mouth like bitter coffee grounds.

He didn’t slam the door behind him. That would’ve been theatrical. And he hated theatrics. Instead, he shut it with a soft pull, the way you’d close a book you’d rather forget ever reading. Inside, his wife—soon to be ex-wife—still slept, tangled in bedsheets that once held meaning. But now? Now they were just cotton and deception.

Her name was Rachel. Sixteen years, one dog, no kids. Not by choice. That fact had carved a canyon between them over the past decade. A canyon that she’d apparently tried to fill with someone else.

He’d found the emails two weeks ago. Not even hidden. A thread called “Wednesday Thoughts,” from a guy named Nick. Something about the title had been innocuous, laughable even. But the contents—they burned.

Jonathan didn’t scream. Didn’t throw a lamp or start an argument. He didn’t even cry. He just read them, every last message, all the way back to their origin: July 13th. Their anniversary.

He booked the motel that same night.

Now, two weeks later, he was leaving. Not for the motel—he’d only lasted there a night. No, he was heading west. There was a town in Utah—Pine Wells. Population just under a thousand. He didn’t know anyone there. And that was the point.

Chapter Two: Pine Wells

The first thing he noticed about Pine Wells was that it didn’t smell like anywhere he’d ever lived. Not salt, not smog, not freshly cut lawns or burning garbage. It smelled like distance. The kind of distance you can’t measure in miles.

Main Street was half pavement, half dust. An old man in a Broncos hat waved to him outside a shop called Maureen’s. A faded sign underneath read “books + pie.” He nodded back, unsure if it was politeness or protocol.

Three days in, Jonathan started walking to Maureen’s every morning. The coffee was awful, the pie acceptable, but the quiet—that was golden. Maureen herself was dead, but her granddaughter Elise ran the place now. Thirty-something, loud laugh, hair like wildfire. She called him “Suitcase,” and never once asked what he was running from.

He didn’t deny it.

Chapter Three: Elise

Chapter Five: The Horse in the Yard

Chapter Six: Letters Never Sent

He began writing again. Not books—he hadn’t done that in years—but letters. None mailed. Just stacks of yellow legal pads tucked under the floorboard.

One to Rachel. One to Nick. Several to the version of himself that used to believe marriage was permanent and people didn’t lie.

He wrote one to Elise, too. Then burned it.

The words were weight. And weight had to go somewhere.

Chapter Seven: The Barn Fire

It happened during the county fair. A sudden blaze at the north edge of town. Someone said lightning. Someone else whispered arson. Jonathan didn’t care about the why—only the flicker of danger, the pull of chaos.

They kissed. It wasn’t romantic. It wasn’t even right. But it was real.
