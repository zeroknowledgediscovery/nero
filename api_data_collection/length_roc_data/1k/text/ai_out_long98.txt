And yet, I never once felt alone.

It was nothing overt. No voices. No laughter echoing from the walls. But I would find things out of place. A chair shifted two inches. A drawer left ajar. My boots set neatly beside the door, even when I remembered leaving them in the barn. I began setting traps—not cruel ones, just little arrangements: coins balanced on the edge of a shelf, thread pulled across doorframes, a piece of charcoal resting on a windowsill. Every morning, one would be disturbed.

I told myself it was the house settling. Or rodents. Or habit. But the truth I couldn’t say aloud was this: I’d never left the orchard. Not really. It had grown through me now. Its shape was in my spine. Its silence lived behind my eyes. The seed was buried, yes. Forgotten, no.

You don’t survive something like that. You carry it.

One night, the phone rang.

It startled me so badly I nearly dropped the kettle I was carrying.

No one had called the house phone in months. Eve
