The ground pulsed.

Somewhere, a photograph slid off a dusty shelf.

He traveled less after that.

Not because he couldn’t.

Because he began to understand that place had never been the point.

He was no longer a messenger between spaces.

He was the space.

People began to find him instead. Not physically, but in dreams, in half-remembered lullabies, in the moment between waking and sleep where truth loses its costume. Some wrote him letters without knowing the address. Some left drawings on park benches and chalked symbols onto sidewalks. In every case, the act itself became a door.

He opened each one gently.

Never with a key anymore.

Now, he just was the opening.

And still, he carried.

But differently.

More rhythm, less strain.

More weaving, less rescue.

He wasn’t saving anything now.

He was listening.

Letting people stitch their own names back into the fabric of waking.
