“I’m not afraid of the silence anymore.”

She took a long breath and kissed him, slow and unhurried, like a period at the end of a long, complicated sentence. When she stepped back, she smiled again, this time with more weight behind it.

“I’ll come back when the leaves are gone and the frost is in the ground.”

“I’ll keep the coffee warm.”

As the train pulled into the station, Eliot helped her with her bag and watched her board. She didn’t look back through the window. But he stood on the platform until the train disappeared into the trees, until all that remained was wind and the fading vibration of steel on rail.

The following weeks moved slowly, but not without shape. Eliot kept his routine. The pharmacy still opened at nine sharp. Marta still made jokes at his expense. Customers still asked for the same medications with the same quiet desperation. But everything carried a different undercurrent now. Not absence. Expectation.
