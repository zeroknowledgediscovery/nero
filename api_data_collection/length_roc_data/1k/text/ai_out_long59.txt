In Georgia, outside a town called Morrow’s Reach, he passed a house that wasn’t there the day before. He knew this because every local diner had the same explanation: “That stretch of road’s been empty since the storm in ’82.” But the house stood nonetheless—three stories, red shutters, Victorian in style, with paint too fresh and a picket fence too white.

He walked the perimeter at dusk. Every window reflected a different hour of day. One showed sunrise. Another showed night. He didn’t go inside. He didn’t need to. Whatever had built itself there was still arranging the timeline. Stepping into it could lock him into a loop he might not escape. He marked it in the journal Elion had given him—coordinates, observations, the emotional pressure he felt in his chest like altitude sickness.

That became routine.
