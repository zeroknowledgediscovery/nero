But the world no longer cared what he wanted.

Later that day, he returned to Keller-Morne. His desk sat just where he left it, untouched. The projects on his screen—concept sketches, structural overlays, digital maquettes—now looked hollow, as if they had been designed by someone else. He recognized the technical lines, the proportions, the logic of the cantilevers. But there was no soul in them.

He opened a new file.

This time, he didn’t design a tower.

He designed a memory.

He didn’t know what form it would take, only that it responded to shape. A sphere hovering above a triangular base. Seven arms extending outward, each leading to a different architectural form—an arch, a spiral staircase, a descending tunnel, a bridge, a door, a mirror, and a blank wall.

Each arm bore a name.

Not names he remembered writing.

But ones that came to him like rain:

Ilyra. Cord. Emrich. Kit. Seraphine. The Architect. Himself.
