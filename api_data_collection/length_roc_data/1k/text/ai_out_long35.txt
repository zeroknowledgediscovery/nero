They returned to the glade just before the rains, the boys weary but alive, their clothes soaked with sweat and dust, their limbs heavy with the weight of having done something that mattered. The storm that followed came in sheets, drenching the camp, flooding the narrow stream that ran beneath the ridge, and forcing them to huddle in the half-built shelter Cem had constructed weeks earlier. Rain drummed against the wooden beams like the tapping of fingers on a table, restless and steady.

For two days, they did not train. They sat beneath the leaking roof, repaired gear, told quiet stories, and ate the last of the preserved goat meat Halim had bartered for in the town. Cem used the time to listen, not to teach. He had begun to understand that instruction could not always be given by form and footwork alone. There was knowledge in silence. Discipline in listening. Growth in the long, hard stillness between one task and the next.

It was during one of those rain-bound evenings
