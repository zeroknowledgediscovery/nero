She was remembering through her body now, letting the muscles lead where logic couldn’t. Inside, the air was stale, heavy with mold and mouse droppings. The floorboards groaned but didn’t give. The chalkboard still bore faint white lines, and a broken abacus lay strewn beneath the teacher’s desk.

Maeve moved to the front of the room and stared at the wall behind the blackboard.

“He drew here. Liam. I remember. The teacher let us draw on the back wall.”

Jonah raised a brow. “They let you kids behind the blackboard?”

Maeve didn’t answer. She walked to the edge, ran her fingers along the seam where the board met the plaster. Then she stopped.

“There.”

Jonah helped her pry it loose. The panel gave with a long creak, revealing a shallow recess. Inside was an old tin lunchbox, the kind kids carried in the ‘80s, Spider-Man faded to blue and rust. Maeve lifted it out gently, breath held.

When she opened it, the hinges squealed.
