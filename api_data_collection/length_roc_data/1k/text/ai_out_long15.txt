Bernard didn’t try to find it. Instead, he sat on a mossy rock and pulled out the small notebook he had brought with him. The first few pages were filled with odd lines and fragments, half-formed thoughts about forgotten names and places with no maps. He turned to a blank sheet and began to write, slowly, without intention. The words weren’t about Merrin or Aveline. They weren’t even about himself.

They were about a woman who collected dreams in glass bottles, about a train that never stopped but whose passengers aged backwards, about a small room hidden behind every mirror. Ideas came like seeds on the wind, drifting, settling. He didn’t try to arrange them. He simply let them land.

Later, walking back into town, he felt lighter. Not relieved, not finished, but open.
