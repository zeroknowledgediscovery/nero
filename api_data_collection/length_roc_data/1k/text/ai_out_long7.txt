Caleb stayed close, a steady shadow beside her as she moved from glass case to velvet rope, tracing the outline of things once housed in the estate: a silver tea set with the Langston crest, a painting she remembered from childhood, a worn ledger her grandmother had kept locked in her writing desk.

They bid on seven items. They won four. The others went to strangers with no knowledge of the Langston name. Cecily kept her composure, but when they exited the building into the bracing autumn air, she stopped on the steps and let the loss settle over her like ash.

“I know they’re just objects,” she said. “But it feels like pieces of her I’ll never get back.”

“They’re more than objects,” Caleb said. “They’re continuity. They’re evidence that something real existed.”

She turned to him. “That’s what this whole thing has been, hasn’t it? Holding on to proof. That we were here. That we didn’t just survive—we mattered.”
