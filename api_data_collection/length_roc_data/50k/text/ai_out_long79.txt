“There’s more,” Sarah said, her fingers trembling. “He said I could stay with him forever if I just let go. He said the water would take me, but it wouldn’t hurt. He said it’s like falling asleep. That’s what he said.”

She closed the notebook. Her knuckles were white against the worn cardboard. “He wanted to go, Tom. I think... I think he chose it.”

Tom didn’t answer. He turned and stepped out of the shed, staring at the lake with a mixture of dread and resolution. The wind picked up suddenly, scattering dry leaves across the grass, and from the woods came the sound of a voice. Faint, melodic, familiar.

They both turned.

“Did you hear that?” Sarah asked.

Tom nodded. “It was him.”

They followed the sound, pushing through the underbrush. It didn’t sound quite like their son, not anymore. There was an echo to it, as if it had passed through a long tunnel before reaching their ears. But there was something in the tone, a lift at the end of each sentence, that was unmistakably his. And it was singing.

They emerged into a small clearing neither of them remembered. In the center stood a ring of stones, flat and pale, arranged deliberately. At the heart of the circle was a depression in the earth, filled with dark, still water. A natural spring, maybe. Or a sinkhole. Or something else entirely.

Tom stepped toward it, but Sarah grabbed his arm.

“Don’t,” she said. “Not yet.”

“Why not?”

“Because I think this is where it started.”

That night, Sarah couldn’t sleep. She lay awake beside Tom, who had finally drifted off into a shallow, restless doze. The sound of the lake filtered in through the thin windows, a gentle lapping that had begun to feel like breathing. Or whispering. Or both.

She got up quietly and wrapped herself in her coat, stepping out into the darkness with nothing but a flashlight and the locket in her pocket. The moon was low, casting long shadows across the yard. She walked back to the stone circle, flashlight beam trembling slightly as it passed over trees and brush. The night was thick with silence, broken only by the distant hoot of an owl and the occasional snap of a twig beneath her boots.

When she reached the circle, she knelt down at the edge and looked into the water. Her reflection stared back, dim and distorted. Behind her image, another face hovered—pale, boyish, half-smiling. She turned quickly, but the woods were empty.

Then a voice came from the trees. Calm. Curious. Young.

“You brought it with you.”

Sarah’s heart thundered. “Who are you?” she asked, though the answer had begun to solidify in her mind like ice forming across a lake’s surface.

“I’ve been here a long time,” the voice said. “Longer than you think. I didn’t mean for him to stay. Not really. But he wanted to. He wanted to forget.”

“Forget what?” she whispered.

There was a long pause. “You.”

Sarah’s knees gave out. She sat hard in the dirt, tears sliding down her face unnoticed. “You took my son.”

“I didn’t take him. I showed him the door.”

She opened the locket and held it over the water. “Then take this too,” she said. “If he’s down there—if he really wanted to go—let him speak. Just once.”

The surface of the water rippled. Not from wind. Not from rain. From something below.

And then, clear and unmistakable, she heard it.

“Mom.”

Her breath caught. “Jamie?”

“Why did you stop looking for me?”

She sobbed. “We never did. We never stopped. We didn’t know where to look.”

“I was just under the dock.”

The words hit like a blow. She remembered that day—how they searched everywhere but the dock itself, assuming the riptide had carried him out farther. How they’d called his name from boats, from the shoreline, from helicopters.

She leaned closer. “Are you still there?”

No answer came. Only stillness.

Then the water stilled, as if nothing had ever disturbed it.

And Sarah was alone again.

When she returned to the house, Tom was awake, pacing. He rushed to her when she entered.

“Where were you?”

“I saw him,” she said. “I heard his voice.”

Tom stared at her, expression unreadable. “So did I.”

They stood in the kitchen in silence for a long moment. Then, without another word, Tom reached for the notebook again. He opened it to the last page.

There, in a handwriting that hadn’t been there before, were four words:

“Let go. Follow me.”

The ink was still wet.

Tom stared at the words, his thumb trembling where it rested against the page. The ink had begun to bleed into the paper, softening the edges of each letter like they were dissolving. The room was so still he could hear the slow tick of the old wall clock upstairs, broken but ticking anyway—an impossible noise they’d never explained. Sarah leaned over the notebook, her breath catching.

“It’s him,” she said.

Tom didn’t respond right away. He closed the notebook and placed it gently on the kitchen table, like setting down a body. “I don’t know if it is,” he said quietly. “Or if it’s something that knows what to say to sound like him.”

Sarah blinked rapidly and gripped the back of a kitchen chair. “You heard him too. Last night. You said so.”

“I did. But that doesn’t mean it was him. These things—whatever they are—they know our grief. They know how to wear it.”

She turned on him suddenly. “And what if it is him? What if we walk away again, and he’s down there still waiting for us?”

Tom looked down at his hands. “Then he’s already gone. If we follow him, there might be no coming back.”

The silence between them felt more like a verdict than a pause. They’d lived so long with the weight of absence, with questions and fragmented memories, that the idea of release—no matter how terrifying—had started to resemble mercy.

Sarah left the kitchen without another word. Tom heard her footsteps climb the stairs and then, a moment later, the bedroom door closing gently. He stayed where he was for a long time, watching the last of the morning fog curl against the windowpanes.

That afternoon, the weather shifted again. The sky turned the color of rusted metal, and the wind carried with it a faint, sour scent that made Tom’s stomach turn. He stepped outside with the old binoculars from the hallway closet and scanned the lake. It remained still, too still, with none of the small ripples or occasional splashes that usually marked the movement of fish or birds. The surface seemed sealed, unbroken. A lid, not a mirror.

He turned the binoculars toward the far shore, and for a moment—barely a second—he saw movement. A figure. Small. Standing perfectly still at the treeline.

Tom lowered the binoculars and stared. Nothing. When he looked again, the figure was gone.

He didn’t tell Sarah. Not yet.

Instead, he spent the afternoon searching the property again. The woods, the shed, even the old cellar that still smelled of damp rot and mold. It had once held canned goods and tools. Now it held silence, and something deeper—a sense of eyes that watched without blinking.

Under a pile of rotted wood and mouse-nibbled insulation, he found a scrap of cloth. Faded blue. A piece of a shirt.

His son had been wearing a blue shirt that day.

He held it like it might disintegrate, the edges crumbling between his fingers. And then he saw the blood.

Not much. Just a smear. But unmistakably there.

He didn’t bring it back to the house. He buried it beneath the tree where they used to hang a tire swing. The earth was soft, and the roots gave way too easily. When he covered it again, he whispered something—he didn’t even know what—and pressed his palm flat to the soil like an apology.

That night, the rain came.

It wasn’t gentle. It slammed against the roof in sheets, relentless, and the gutters overflowed, turning the yard into a darkened soup. Lightning forked in the distance, pale and sudden, and the thunder that followed cracked like something splitting in two.

Tom and Sarah sat in the living room, lights off, watching the fireplace cast flickering shadows against the walls. Bruno paced, whining, tail between his legs. The dog had grown increasingly restless with each passing day, as if some old animal instinct told him what his humans refused to accept—that the house was no longer just a house.

“I think we need to go back to the spring,” Sarah said quietly.

Tom didn’t respond.

She turned toward him. “Whatever’s down there... it’s not going to stop. It’s coming closer. It’s reaching.”

“And what do you expect we’ll find?” he asked. “A door? A grave? A second chance?”

“I don’t know,” she said. “But it keeps calling.”

Tom rubbed his hands over his face. “And if it’s a trap?”

“Then we go down together.”

They waited until the storm passed. By morning, the sky had cleared to a brittle blue, and the air smelled sharp, metallic. The grass was heavy with water, and every branch held the echo of the night’s violence. They packed light—just a flashlight, rope, and the notebook. Sarah wore the locket again. Tom carried a small knife in his pocket, more out of old habits than belief in its usefulness.

The forest was eerily quiet as they made their way to the spring. The stone ring was untouched, though now the ground around it was soaked and muddied. The pool at the center had grown wider, its surface bubbling gently, as though stirred from beneath.

Sarah knelt again, her fingers brushing the water’s edge. “It’s warm,” she whispered.

Tom stepped to the edge and looked down. For a moment, he saw his own reflection. Then it shifted—eyes darkened, mouth curled into a faint smile. Not his smile. Something else’s.

He stepped back. “It’s waiting for us.”

They secured the rope to a tree and tied it around Sarah’s waist. She took the flashlight, hesitated, then lowered herself into the water.

The surface resisted at first, like pushing through thick plastic, but then gave way. She sank slowly, the beam of her flashlight illuminating cloudy depths. Tom fed the rope as she descended, heart pounding with each foot. The seconds stretched.

Then the rope stopped moving.

“Sarah?” he called. “Sarah!”

No answer.

He waited a minute longer, then cursed under his breath and tied the end of the rope to himself. Without hesitating, he plunged in after her.

The water closed around him like a mouth. It was warmer than he expected, thick and heavy. He opened his eyes briefly, saw shapes—not solid, not quite human—drifting at the edges of his vision. He descended blindly, reaching for the rope.

Then he felt her hand.

She pulled him down further, and suddenly they broke through.

They weren’t in water anymore.

They stood in a vast chamber—stone walls slick with moisture, dim light filtering in from somewhere unseen. The pool they had emerged from sat behind them, still as glass. Ahead, a corridor led into darkness.

Sarah turned to him, eyes wide but clear.

“He’s here,” she said. “I can feel him.”

Tom didn’t speak. He just took her hand.

And together, they walked into the dark.

The corridor stretched forward in a way that defied logic—too long for the space they’d descended into, too silent for something that pulsed with the feeling of breath. The stone underfoot was smooth and cold, as if shaped not by hands but by time itself, worn down by something moving back and forth over centuries. No sounds came from their footsteps, no echoes bounced from the walls. The silence here was total, not an absence of sound but a suffocation of it.

Sarah held the flashlight tight, but the beam reached only a few feet ahead before the darkness swallowed it. Tom walked beside her, eyes scanning the edges of the hall, the seams where walls met ceiling, as though expecting the stone to shift and reveal a doorway, a figure, a memory. The deeper they went, the heavier the air became, thick with moisture and something less tangible—sorrow, maybe, or guilt.

Then the walls began to change.

Carvings emerged, shallow and worn, but unmistakably human in their intent. Rows of faces, not quite realistic but not abstract either. Each one different, with eyes open and mouths shut, as if caught mid-listen. Some faces were distorted—long jaws, extra eyes, expressions of rapture or pain—but each was turned inward, facing the path they now walked. Guardians, perhaps. Or warnings.

Sarah reached out and traced one with her fingertips. “They’re watching,” she whispered.

Tom didn’t reply. He was focused on something up ahead. The corridor opened into a wide chamber, its ceiling high and vaulted. At the center, rising from the stone like a pillar, was a large circular platform. And on it, sitting cross-legged, was a boy.

He looked no older than fifteen. Pale skin, dark hair, head tilted downward as if asleep or in prayer. His clothes were dry, though the air around him shimmered with dampness. As they stepped into the room, his eyes opened.

“Eli,” Sarah breathed.

The boy looked up. He smiled faintly, and it was the same smile they remembered from all those years ago, but it didn’t reach his eyes. Those were older. Timeless.

“You found the way,” he said. His voice was clear, smooth, and quiet. “You followed the sound.”

Tom stepped forward, but not too close. “What is this place?”

Eli looked around slowly. “A place beneath remembering. Where the things we bury keep breathing.”

Sarah shook her head. “You brought him here. You brought our son.”

“I offered him a truth,” Eli said, “and he chose to follow.”

“Follow what?” Tom demanded. “You? Or something else?”

Eli stood, slow and fluid. “He was looking for something. You both were too busy grieving the boy you wanted him to be to see the one he was becoming.”

Sarah’s voice cracked. “We loved him. We still love him.”

Eli nodded. “That’s why you came.”

Behind him, the stone wall rippled like water. Slowly, the surface gave way, and an archway opened. A soft light glowed within. And from inside came a voice.

“Mom?”

It was him.

Jamie’s voice.

Sarah dropped the flashlight. It clattered on the floor, its beam flickering against the ceiling.

Tom stepped forward instinctively, but Eli raised a hand.

“You can enter,” he said. “But only one of you may leave.”

They froze.

“What do you mean?” Tom asked.

“There is balance,” Eli said. “There must always be someone here to hold the weight. If you take him back, something must stay.”

Sarah’s breath shuddered. “This isn’t a deal. This is a punishment.”

Eli tilted his head. “It’s a truth.”

Tom turned to her, voice low. “We don’t know what he’s become. If he’s even... him.”

“I heard him,” Sarah said. “It’s him.”

Eli stepped aside. The archway remained open.

“You can see him,” he said. “But decide before you speak. Once the door closes, the choice is sealed.”

Sarah looked at Tom. Her face was pale, eyes wide, glassy.

“I need to see him,” she said. “I need to be sure.”

Tom nodded, jaw clenched. “We go together.”

They stepped through the arch.

The space inside was not a room.

It was a memory.

They were standing on the dock again, the sun setting over the lake, the air warm with summer. The boards beneath their feet were dry. Birds called in the distance. And at the edge of the dock stood Jamie.

He turned and smiled.

He was older now—his face longer, his frame taller—but his eyes were still his. Full of light and questions. He held the locket in his hand.

“I missed you,” he said.

Sarah ran to him, wrapped her arms around him, sobbing. Tom followed more slowly, unsure, afraid. He reached out and touched his son’s shoulder. Solid. Warm.

“I don’t understand,” Tom said.

Jamie looked up at him. “I followed him because I was tired of feeling alone.”

Sarah pulled back, cupped his face in both hands. “You weren’t alone. We were right there. We never stopped looking.”

“You weren’t there,” Jamie said softly. “Not really. After everything happened. You stopped talking. You stopped smiling. You buried me before the lake ever did.”

Tom stepped back. “We were trying to survive.”

Jamie nodded. “So was I.”

The air shifted. The dock darkened. The water lapped higher against the posts.

“Time’s almost gone,” Jamie said. “You have to decide.”

Sarah turned to Tom. “I can’t leave him.”

“I know,” Tom said. His voice was quiet, calm. “I knew before we came down here.”

She began to shake her head, but he stepped closer and kissed her forehead.

“You always heard him before I did,” he said. “And now you’ll get to hold him again. That’s more than either of us ever hoped for.”

Sarah wept, shaking.

“I’m not afraid,” he said, then turned to Jamie. “Just take care of her. Let her remember the sound of her laughter.”

Jamie stepped forward and hugged his father. It wasn’t a boy’s hug—it was full, adult, human. And it carried a weight that went beyond grief.

When they stepped back, the dock was fading.

The water rose.

And then Sarah and Jamie were gone.

Tom awoke on the porch of the lake house, alone.

The rain had stopped. The sky above was clear.

In his hand was the locket.

Inside it were two pictures now.

Sarah. And Jamie.

Smiling.

Chapter Four: A Body That Remains

The lake was silent that morning—not empty, not abandoned, but quiet in the way a house is after the last guest has left and all that remains is the furniture, the lingering breath of conversation, and the weight of absence. Tom stood at the edge of the dock, hands in his coat pockets, his shoulders hunched slightly forward like the cold had burrowed into him during the night and made its home there. The locket rested against his chest, warm despite the chill. He hadn’t opened it again since he found himself back here. He didn’t need to. The images burned behind his eyelids every time he blinked.

The birds had returned to the trees, warbling uncertain notes, and the water now shimmered faintly beneath the morning light. It looked normal again. Deceptively so. But Tom knew better. He knew what slept beneath it.
