Chapter One: The Last of the Embers

The fire was nearly out.

Mira sat hunched in the old rocking chair beside the stone hearth, a threadbare quilt wrapped around her shoulders, her knees drawn up like a child’s. The logs had burned down to a scatter of red ash and a single coughing ember that winked like an eye. She stared at it. It looked as though it would blink out any moment.

The wind howled outside the cabin walls, a thin scream that came through the cracks in the wood. Snow had piled against the door, which moaned softly on its hinges every time the gale shifted. She’d used the last of the firewood two nights ago—her mistake, truly. She had overestimated how long it would last. Now she sat wrapped in wool and regret, wishing she’d gone to fetch more from the trees that bowed in the valley below.

But Mira hadn’t gone back down the hill in four days.

The last time she had stepped out beyond the porch, she’d found tracks. Bare feet in the snow. Not animal. Human. Too large for a child, too narrow for a man. And they had stopped right at her doorstep.

No one had knocked.

No one had left.

She hadn’t opened the door since.

The kettle, hanging above the fading coals, was empty. No point in keeping it there. The water in the barrel had frozen into a hard brick of ice. She would need to chip some out later. Or sooner. If she didn’t get something warm in her, she might not last the night. Still, she stayed seated, as if rising would break the fragile shell that protected her from something nameless.

She’d come up to the cabin for solitude. For quiet. For the kind of isolation only the northern ridges of Evron could offer, where the only neighbors were foxes and frost. After what happened in the city—after the fire and the trial and the woman on the bridge—she had no right to be among people.

But now, it seemed, people—or something pretending to be them—had come looking for her.

The wind faltered. Then silence.

Mira stood. Her legs were stiff, her feet numb even inside thick wool socks. She moved toward the hearth, crouched, and prodded the coals with a bit of iron. They hissed faintly, defiant but dying.

With one hand she reached into the shadowed basket beside the hearth and withdrew the last bit of kindling—a crooked, half-rotten branch she had meant to toss out. She broke it in two, laid the pieces across the ember, and blew gently.

Once.

Twice.

The ember blinked again.

Then, miraculously, caught.

A slow flame licked the wood, curling pale yellow and then orange. Her breath fogged the air in front of her as she fed the fire scrap after scrap—old paper from the journal she’d stopped writing in, a sliver of pine bark, a bit of towel too frayed to mend. She watched the flames grow with a strange hunger in her chest. Not just for heat. But for life. For motion.

When the cabin was quiet again, filled with the soft crackling of fire, Mira sat back and stared at the door.

There had been no more prints in the snow after that first set. Nothing since.

But she had heard the knock.

Three slow taps.

And then the sound of something breathing just outside.

Now, three days later, she still hadn’t dared to open it.

But the fire had changed something. Warmth crept back into her fingers. Her thoughts were no longer dull and fogged with cold. She stood again and walked to the door. Laid her hand against the wood. It was colder than death.

She waited.

Nothing.

Mira unlatched it.

The wind blasted her back.

She staggered, shielding her face as snow whipped into the cabin like a fury. The fire behind her hissed in protest. She gritted her teeth and pulled the door shut with effort, forcing it against the piled snow until the latch caught again.

Outside, just before she closed it, she had seen something in the snow.

A shape, half-covered by the storm. A bundle of cloth. A dark smear.

She went still. Then moved.

Her coat was under the cot. Boots still damp from days ago. She dressed quickly, moving without thought, just muscle memory and dread guiding her. She grabbed the lantern from the hook, lit it with a finger of kindling, and stepped out.

The cold slapped her like a curse.

Snow crunched beneath her boots as she stepped off the porch. The wind clawed at her face, tearing her breath away. She stumbled toward the shape.

It was a person.

No. A body.

Mira dropped to her knees.

It was a man, curled against the side of the cabin, arms wrapped around himself. His face was pale blue, beard crusted with frost. His eyes were closed.

But his chest moved.

He was alive.

“Oh gods,” she muttered, already grabbing his shoulder. “Hey—hey!”

He didn’t respond.

She looked around. There were no footprints leading to him. No trail. Just... him.

She dragged him inside.

It took all her strength. He was heavy, half-frozen. His clothes were strange—rough-spun, but stitched with symbols she didn’t recognize. His boots were laced with red cord. Something in his coat jangled when she pulled him through the door.

She got him to the hearth, propped him up against a stack of wool. The fire had grown stronger now, crackling with a golden heat. She rubbed his hands, then peeled off his outer layers, trying to find skin, warmth, anything to tell her he was still real.

Then his eyes opened.

Dark. Almost black. Unfocused.

“Where am I?” he whispered.

Mira stared. “You’re at a cabin. On Greyfen Ridge.”

He blinked slowly. “The door. You opened it.”

“Yes.”

“I was... waiting.”

Her heart slowed.

“You knocked?”

He didn’t answer.

Instead, he looked around the cabin, eyes tracing every line of the walls, every nail. As if he recognized it. As if he were remembering.

“I need your name,” she said. “Are you hurt?”

“I’m not supposed to be here,” he muttered, as if to himself. “Not again.”

“What’s your name?”

He looked at her then.

“I think... I was Elias,” he said. “Before the crossing.”

She stepped back. “What crossing?”

But Elias had slumped forward, unconscious.

And outside, beneath the storm, something howled—not wind. Something older.

Something hungry.

Chapter Two: The Things We Forget

Mira didn’t sleep that night. She sat beside the fire, watching the man—Elias—murmur in his sleep. His lips moved without sound. His hands twitched. Once, he cried out.

“Don’t open it,” he whispered. “Not again.”

She stayed beside him until dawn, feeding the fire with whatever she could find—pages from old books, broken furniture, a coil of birch bark hidden in the eaves. Snow rattled against the windows like a fist. The storm was worse than ever. But inside, it was warm.

Warm enough to think.

Warm enough to remember.

Mira hadn’t told Elias her full name. Not yet. He didn’t need to know she was Mira Veln, once Captain of the City Guard. Not when she had failed so many. Not when the woman on the bridge had fallen screaming because of her mistake.

That was months ago. A lifetime. But sometimes, in dreams, she still heard the splash.

And now here was Elias, appearing from nowhere, wearing symbols she couldn’t read, speaking in riddles and warnings.

She should have left him outside.

But she didn’t.

In the morning, he woke. Pale, sweating, eyes sharp now.

“You kept the fire going,” he said.

She nodded.

He stared into it. “It’s important.”

“Why?”

He looked at her with something like fear.

“Because it sees through the flames.”

She frowned. “What does?”

But he didn’t answer. He just looked back at the fire and said: “If it goes out again, neither of us will wake.”

She didn't know if she trusted him—not yet. But she couldn't deny the chill his words left behind, as if he’d peeled back something invisible and let the cold inside. Mira kept the fire alive with trembling hands. She moved slowly, deliberately, her body still recovering from nights spent freezing and still. Elias said little after that, only sat cross-legged near the flames, head bowed, his hands extended to soak in the warmth. She noticed the faint burns on his palms, old scars in the shape of circles, like coins pressed into flesh and never healed.

"Those marks," she said after a while. "What are they?"

He didn’t look up. "They’re not important anymore."

"Don’t lie to me."

His eyes lifted then, and something passed between them—a flicker of recognition, or maybe it was just shared exhaustion. He reached into the inner pocket of his coat and pulled out a small leather pouch. It was cracked at the seams, its drawstring frayed. He tossed it gently into her lap.

"Open it."

Mira hesitated. Then, slowly, she unfastened the string and peeked inside.

Ash.

At first she thought it was just dirt, maybe burnt leaves. But the scent hit her—sharp, pungent, like scorched cedar and iron. And something else. Something faintly sweet.

"What is this?"

"Memories," Elias said. "Or what’s left of them."

She blinked. "You’re mad."

"Maybe. But I know what’s coming."

"Why were you at my door?"

"I wasn’t. Not really. Not entirely. It doesn’t work that way."

"Then explain it to me. Gods, I haven’t spoken to anyone in weeks, and the first voice I hear belongs to a lunatic."

Elias gave a thin smile. It didn’t touch his eyes.

"You remember the old tales? The ones they told children when the nights stretched too long? The stories of the Grey King and the mirror that devoured sound?"

Mira laughed dryly. "Those are campfire lies. Things meant to keep kids from wandering too far into the forest."

"And yet," he said, "here you are. In a cabin built on old bones. With snow that never melts, and wind that talks in dreams."

The wind did speak, sometimes. She’d thought she imagined it. Whispers just beneath sleep, luring her toward the trees. A woman’s voice, sometimes a child’s. Mira had stopped walking too close to the pines after she heard her own name on the breeze.

"You think this is some old curse?" she asked.

"I think something’s waking up," Elias said. "And it remembers you."

"Me?"

"You and the others. You were all there. Once."

She rose, furious, confused. "I’ve never met you in my life."

"Not in this one," he said quietly.

That stopped her. She wanted to throw him out, to scream or laugh or cry. But she just stood still, one hand clenched by her side, the other pressed to her ribs like she could keep her heart from breaking open.

He watched her carefully. "You don’t dream, do you?"

"I try not to."

"Because you’re afraid of what you’ll remember."

"No," she said too quickly. "Because the dreams aren't mine."

Elias nodded slowly. As if that confirmed something for him.

"Then it’s already begun."

She sat again, suddenly cold. The fire popped loudly, and a piece of wood split down the center, curling open like a wound. Sparks fluttered into the air, vanishing before they reached the ceiling.

"Tell me what you know," she said.

Elias leaned forward, elbows on his knees, his voice low and even.

"There’s something beneath this ridge. A door, of sorts. But not one we built. It’s older than the city, older than the mountains. People used to come here to keep it sealed. Rituals. Sacrifices. The usual rot. Over time, they forgot what it really was. They built stories around it to dull the truth. You’ve heard them—the Black Watchers, the Fire That Walks. All fragments. But the core remained the same. Something sleeps beneath the snow. And something else guards it."

"And you?"

"I came to stop it from waking. But I was too late. It’s already seen you."

"You keep saying that—'it.' What is it?"

Elias hesitated, then whispered, "I don’t know its name. Only the feeling it leaves behind."

Outside, the wind roared again. A deep, rising pitch like laughter stretched thin. Mira stood and moved to the window, brushing frost from the pane with her sleeve.

The forest swayed violently. Branches clawed at each other in frenzy. But there, between two old firs, stood a shape.

Still.

Watching.

"Someone’s out there," she breathed.

Elias joined her. He didn't need to look twice. "Not someone."

She turned to him. "What do we do?"

"We keep the fire going. Always. And we don’t let it cross the threshold."

"Is it the same thing that left the footprints?"

Elias frowned. "That wasn’t it. That was a scout. A shadow, maybe. This—what you see now—that’s a fragment. A piece it can send ahead."

"Then it’s not here completely?"

"Not yet."

Mira grabbed the axe from beside the door. Her hands were steady. She hadn’t held a weapon in weeks, but the grip felt natural, comforting. She turned back to Elias.

"If it comes through that door, we fight."

He nodded. "But not just with steel."

He knelt and opened his pouch again, drawing a line of ash along the doorframe, muttering in a language Mira didn’t recognize. It sounded wet and strange, like words formed underwater.

"Salt would help," he said. "Silver, too. Do you have either?"

"No," she replied. "But I have iron nails. And this cabin was built by my grandfather. He knew things."

"Then maybe," Elias said, "we have a chance."

The wind dropped suddenly. No fade. Just silence.

Mira stepped back from the window. The shape in the trees was gone.

And on the glass where she had wiped away the frost, a handprint remained.

But it was on the inside.

Chapter Two: Ash and Echoes

The handprint remained on the window well into the morning, though the warmth of the cabin should’ve melted it away. Mira avoided looking at it. She tried once to wipe it off, but it refused to smear, as though the glass itself had been branded. Elias said nothing, simply watched the hearth and stirred the coals, feeding it in slow, rhythmic intervals with a focus that made her uneasy.

She busied herself with small things. Water. Food. Or what passed for it. The last of the dried venison, some brittle crackers she’d rationed poorly, and bitterroot tea that curled the tongue like sour milk. Elias ate little. Sipped the tea without complaint, but Mira noticed how his hands trembled when he held the cup. As if his nerves frayed further each hour the fire burned. When she asked what was wrong, he only replied, “The boundary’s thin today.”

Later, when she stepped outside for more wood—finding a few half-frozen logs buried near the edge of the porch under brittle tarps—she noticed something else.

The snow had changed.

Where once it had fallen in natural, wind-scattered drifts, now it lay in oddly geometric patterns, soft ridges carved with straight lines and angled curves. One of them resembled a circle, perfectly formed, no wider than her shoulders, sunken just slightly into the crust of snow. Another looked like a sigil she couldn’t decipher, but stirred something in her—a memory that wasn’t hers, a whisper half-heard during a fever dream.

She stepped away quickly, dragging the logs behind her, forcing herself not to look over her shoulder until she reached the cabin. Even then, she saw no tracks. No indentations but her own.

When she returned, Elias was drawing again—this time not in ash, but with the charcoal stub from the fire’s edge. He sketched onto the back of an old sheet of parchment she’d used for lists, his fingers quick and deliberate. The shape looked familiar.

“You saw it too?” she asked, setting the wood near the hearth.

“I see them in sleep,” he replied without glancing up. “But now they’re here. So yes.”

“What does it mean?”

He set the charcoal down and ran a hand over his jaw. “That the gate is waking. That its roots are stretching through the snow and into the world again.”

Mira folded her arms tightly. “You speak like someone who’s read too many fables.”

“I lived in one,” he said, and his voice cracked at the edges.

She sat across from him, leaning forward. “You’re not just some traveler who got caught in a storm, are you?”

“No.”

“Then what are you?”

He hesitated for a long moment. Then he lifted his gaze, and in the flickering light of the fire, something flickered behind his eyes—a shimmer, faint but unmistakable, like candlelight behind gauze.

“I was part of the Veilguard,” he said finally. “We weren’t soldiers. Not priests. We were... sentinels. Chosen to watch the sealed places. This one was mine.”

“Why you?”

“Because I made the mistake of opening it once.”

The room seemed to inhale with him as he said it. Mira blinked. “You... opened it?”

“Not fully,” he said quickly. “A sliver. Just a breath of it. I thought—I was told—it had to be done to stop something worse.”

“What happened?”

“Everything changed. The people in the valley below—gone. The trees withered. The lake turned black. And those who remained...” His voice trailed off. He looked at the fire. “They changed.”

Mira stood and began to pace, the motion helping her think. “Then why are you here again? Why come back?”

“Because it’s still hungry. Because when it tastes warmth—life—it remembers the shape of what it once was.”

“And me?” she asked, pausing. “Why does it know me?”

Elias looked at her with an expression of sorrow that felt too old for his face.

“Because you were there before,” he said. “You were part of the closing.”

Mira’s laugh was sharp, sudden. “I’ve never seen this place before this year. I came to get away. I was born in Harrowfen. Raised in the city. I had a job. A unit. A normal life.”

“No,” Elias said quietly. “That life came after.”

She stared at him.

“There are lives we live,” he continued, “and ones we forget. Some are taken. Some we give up. And some are stolen.”

Mira shook her head and turned away. She opened the door to the root cellar and descended quickly, more to escape the weight of his words than to retrieve anything. Below, the cold was sharper, purer, untouched by the hearth’s warmth. Shelves lined the walls—canned beans, pickled turnip, dried herbs in glass. She stared at them and realized her hands were shaking. She wasn’t afraid of Elias. Not quite. But she didn’t trust him, either.

Not yet.

From above, she heard footsteps.

Then a voice—not Elias.

“Mira.”

The voice was hers.

Faint. Echoing.

“Mira, come home.”

She dropped the jar she was holding. It shattered on the stone floor.

Above, silence.

She rushed up the stairs two at a time. Elias was standing near the window, eyes narrowed. He turned as she entered.

“You heard it,” he said.

“It sounded like me.”

“They use familiarity. Echoes. They want you to open the door. Let them inside.”

“But they’re already inside, aren’t they?” she asked. “The print on the glass. The voice in the cellar. They don’t need the door.”

“They do,” Elias said grimly. “There are rules. Old ones. But they’re weakening.”

The fire popped again. She turned to look. The flames had shifted color—now tinged with green at the edges, the smoke curling upward with a foul smell.

“Something’s wrong,” she said.

Elias darted to the hearth and threw in another log, then another. The flame sputtered. He muttered under his breath again, that same strange tongue that tasted of water and iron in the air.

The green faded.

But it didn’t go out.

They sat in silence after that, listening to the wind, to the groaning timbers of the cabin, to the hum of something pressing closer with each hour.

That night, neither of them slept.

And in the morning, a second handprint joined the first.

Smaller.

Childlike.

They watched the second handprint slowly frost over during the day, obscured by the spreading ice that feathered across the glass in patterns too intricate to be natural. Mira found herself pacing again, as if movement could keep her sane. Elias sat near the hearth, occasionally murmuring his incantations and feeding the fire, which never seemed to dim, though they were nearly out of wood.

“Did you ever go down there?” she asked abruptly.

Elias looked up from the coals. “Down where?”

“Wherever it is—the gate, the seal, the place beneath us. If you were watching it... did you ever see it with your own eyes?”

He hesitated. “Once.”

“What was it like?”

“Wrong,” he said. “Not in the way a dead animal is wrong. It was... unstructured. Like a place in between forms. A wound in the world. It breathed, but not like lungs. It pulsed. It—”

He stopped. His mouth was dry. Mira waited, arms crossed.

“There was a sound. Not loud, but constant. Like water dripping, but with a pattern. I thought I could make sense of it if I listened long enough. I think that’s how it gets in.”

She shivered despite the fire’s warmth. “You never thought to seal it again?”

“I did,” he replied. “I tried. But it requires more than stone or salt. It requires memory.”

She didn’t understand. Not fully. But something about the way he said it made her stomach turn. Elias leaned back, voice distant.

“There was a woman,” he said, “when I first arrived here. Decades ago. Her name was Sariel. She kept the watch before me. Said the cold was the only thing that kept it asleep. So she made this cabin a place of fire, so the cold could never forget what it was missing.”

“What happened to her?”

“She stayed too long. Gave too much of herself. Toward the end, she said things that didn’t make sense. That the sky had teeth. That her reflection whispered secrets. One night, I woke and found her outside, barefoot in the snow. Smiling.”

Mira swallowed. “Did she survive?”

“No. But I don’t think she died, either. Not in the usual way.”

They fell into silence again. The wind outside had changed—it now rose and fell in steady intervals, as though it were breathing. Sometimes, Mira swore she could hear footsteps on the roof, light and deliberate. She checked three times that day. Nothing. No tracks. No signs of tampering.

That night, the fire nearly went out.

She woke to darkness. Not the usual, flickering kind, but thick, suffocating blackness that swallowed the edges of the cabin. She sat up, heart pounding, and reached instinctively for her knife, though she wasn’t sure what she expected to fight off with a blade.

The fire was no more than a dull ring of ember. Elias was nowhere in sight.

“Elias?” she whispered.

No answer.

She stood, wrapped herself quickly in her coat, and crept toward the hearth. The coals were cold. Not even a glow. That shouldn’t be possible.

“Elias!” she said louder now, stepping toward the door.

That’s when she saw the footprints.

They led from the hearth to the door. Bare feet. Wet. Leaving dark impressions on the old wooden floorboards. She followed them with her eyes until they disappeared at the threshold.

The latch was unbroken.

She opened the door slowly, dreading what she might see.

The snow outside was undisturbed.

But something lay at the base of the steps.

A piece of wood. A totem. Carved roughly, barely more than a stick-figure, with arms outstretched and a head shaped like a triangle. Someone—or something—had left it there.

She closed the door and barred it.

When she turned back toward the hearth, Elias was standing there.

“You weren’t in the cabin,” she said.

“I was,” he replied. “But not entirely.”

“What does that mean?”

“The fire went out. That opened a seam. Just enough for me to see through it.”

“See what?”

He looked old then, impossibly old, like the firelight brought out the cracks in his skin and the weight in his eyes.

“A future where none of this remains.”

Mira shook her head, turning away. “You speak in riddles, always. Just once, tell me what you really mean.”

He stepped closer. “I mean that this cabin, this hill, this forest—all of it—is built on a fault. Not a geological one. A temporal one. The world thins here. We’re standing on a seam stitched together by memory. If that stitch unravels, it won’t just be us who pay.”

“And you think you can stop it?” she asked. “You, with your ash and mutterings and your half-truths?”

“No,” he said. “Not me.”

“Then who?”

“You.”

Mira scoffed, but he didn’t flinch.

“You were part of the last binding,” Elias said. “You knew the name. You carried it. You buried it. And you paid.”

“You keep saying that. That I was there. That I did something. But I have no memory of it.”

“Because memory was the price.”

She wanted to deny it again. To laugh. To tell him he was insane and that she wasn’t some mythical guardian or forgotten priestess or any of the nonsense he hinted at. She was a soldier. A failure. A woman with blood on her hands and a silence she couldn’t ever quite fill.

But something inside her had begun to shift. A pressure behind her eyes. An itch in her bones. Each night she dreamed, and in those dreams, she stood at a shore that didn’t exist, holding a torch that never went out, and something vast and heavy called her name from beneath black water.

She just hadn’t wanted to believe it.

“What do I need to do?” she asked finally.

Elias exhaled.

“Tomorrow, we go to the hollow,” he said. “To the place beneath the pines.”

“The gate?”

“Yes.”

“Is it wise to approach it?”

“No. But neither is waiting.”

That night, the fire burned longer than usual. Mira sat awake, oiling the blade of her axe, watching the green flicker still hiding deep in the heart of the flames. Elias slept fitfully, muttering words from a dead tongue, words that curled in her ears like smoke.

In the morning, they dressed in silence. The wind had eased. The air held a kind of stillness that made her teeth hurt, like the forest was holding its breath.

They left just after dawn.

As they crossed the edge of the clearing, Mira paused and looked back.

On the cabin’s window, where the frost had melted, three handprints now marked the glass.

The third was larger than the first.

And it was still wet.

The forest welcomed them with silence. No birds, no wind, no distant crack of branches. Just snow muffling their steps and the occasional creak of a tree settling under the weight of its own frostbitten limbs. Mira felt the pressure in her skull deepen with every yard they walked. It was like descending into a place where time no longer moved forward. The deeper they went, the heavier everything became—not physically, but mentally, emotionally, as though thought itself had to be pried from some thick, invisible mire.

Elias moved ahead of her, his gait steady, boots leaving perfect prints in the snow. He held a thin staff now, a crooked length of pine polished by use, the end of it etched with sigils Mira didn’t recognize. Occasionally, he would pause to mutter beneath his breath and touch the staff to the ground, leaving behind faint blackened patches in the snow that smoked for several seconds before fading.

“You do that every ten paces,” she observed. “Why?”

“It’s not just a path,” he replied. “It’s a permission.”

“To walk?”

“To return.”

That answer disturbed her more than she wanted to admit. She walked faster to keep up. The trees grew denser. These weren’t the same pale birches that ringed her cabin. These were older, rough-barked giants with roots like claws and trunks gnarled with knots that resembled faces, though she told herself it was her imagination. One of the trees they passed bore what looked like a mouth—split open and dark, gaping at shoulder height. Mira stared at it too long, and Elias gently took her arm.

“Don’t look at them directly,” he said. “They’re not trees, not fully.”

“What are they?”

“Vessels. Vain ones. They tried to contain the name, and the name outgrew them.”

Her skin crawled. The deeper they walked, the more Mira began to feel the presence—less a figure than a weight. Something watching, but without form. Her thoughts scattered easily now. She had to keep reminding herself who she was, where she came from, why she had come here. Her mind wanted to wander, wanted to forget.

“Is this it?” she asked as they came to a clearing.

Elias didn’t answer immediately. He stepped forward, dragging the staff in a wide circle around them. They were in a bowl-shaped depression between the ridgelines. The snow here was shallow, and the earth beneath it was not frozen, but soft. Black mud peeked through, steaming faintly in places. The trees around the edge of the hollow leaned inward, as if drawn by gravity not their own.

In the center was a stone.

It was smooth, black, and unmarked, save for a single line etched across the top like a seam. It looked simple, but her stomach churned when she saw it.

“That’s it?” she said. “A rock?”

“It’s not a rock,” Elias replied. “It’s the lock.”

She stepped forward slowly, almost against her will. The closer she got, the more she felt her memories fray like old thread. She forgot what year it was. She forgot the names of people she’d known all her life. She forgot why her left hand trembled whenever she tried to hold a cup of tea.

“It’s calling,” she murmured.

“No,” Elias said behind her. “It’s remembering.”

She turned to him. “You said I was part of the sealing. That I gave something. That I paid.”

“Yes.”

“Then why don’t I remember it?”

He held up the pouch of ash again. “Because it’s here. Inside this.”

“You have my memory? My life?”

“Not all of it. Only what was needed to close the door. There were four of you. Each gave something. Yours was memory. Another gave blood. One gave voice. The last... gave future.”

She didn’t know what that meant. She didn’t want to ask.

“And now it’s waking?”

“Yes. The bindings are failing. The others are dead or gone. Only you remain near enough to answer it. And if it rises fully, if the name escapes the lock, it won’t stop at these hills. It will call to every lost soul who ever felt the weight of forgetting.”

Mira clenched her fists. “So what do we do?”

“We rebind it.”

“With what?”

“With you.”

She stepped back. “No. No, I didn’t come here for this. I came to forget, to be left alone. Not to be dragged into some ancient war.”

“You were always in it,” Elias said. “Even when you slept. You were always its witness. And its warden.”

Her hands trembled. “I don’t remember. I don’t know how.”

“You will. I brought your memory. But once you take it back, there’s no returning to before.”

He held out the pouch. She stared at it.

“What happens if I refuse?”

“Then it opens. And the cold will follow.”

Mira reached out slowly, but before her fingers touched the pouch, the ground shuddered. A low groan echoed through the hollow, like the exhale of something immense. The stone in the center began to pulse faintly with a sick, amber light. The seam across its top widened, not by much—just a sliver—but enough.

From within came a whisper.

She couldn’t make out the words. But she knew the voice.

It was her own.

Her legs buckled. She fell to her knees, clutching her head.

“It remembers me,” she gasped.

“Because you were the last to leave it,” Elias said.

Her.
