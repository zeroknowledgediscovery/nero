Title: Ink Beneath the Skin
Chapter One: Black Lines, Bright Eyes

The sharp buzz of the tattoo machine pierced the silence of the studio like a wasp's warning. Rain pattered against the blacked-out windows of the shop, streaking the glass like nervous tears. Inside, the air smelled of antiseptic, old leather, and the sweet metallic tang of ink. Flickering fluorescents above cast a sickly yellow over the walls, half-covered with flash art and faded Polaroids.

Micah Delaney hunched over the inner arm of a college kid who smelled of too much Axe and nervous sweat. His hand, calloused and steady, moved with near surgical precision, guiding the vibrating needle over pale flesh. He was adding the final shadow to a crude koi fish swimming upstream, the young man's idea of perseverance immortalized in grayscale.

"Almost done," Micah muttered without looking up.

The kid flinched as the needle kissed bone. "It—it hurts more than I thought it would."

"That’s the point."

Micah finished the line and leaned back, wiping away the excess ink and blood. He studied the piece with a practiced eye, then gave a small nod. "You’ll live."

The kid tried a grin, but his lips were tight. "Thanks, man. This, uh, means a lot."

Micah offered a grunt and motioned toward the front desk. "Pay up front. Tip if you got morals."

He cleaned his tools in silence after the kid left, the studio once again swallowed by quiet. The buzz of the machine still rang in his ears, even with it off. Some said you never forget the sound—like a ghost brushing your eardrums.

The shop, Skin Scripture, had been his for five years, but it felt like a relic older than the bones it branded. The walls held pieces of stories: the snake coiled around a dagger inked on a widow who couldn't cry anymore, the matching runes etched into the shoulders of two brothers who stopped speaking the day after they got them. Ink told truths, even if people lied.

Micah turned off the lights and stepped into the rain, the city soaked in the bruised twilight of another Seattle evening.

Chapter Two: Ghosts in the Needle

The next morning, the bell over the door rang at exactly 10:04 a.m.—four minutes after opening. Micah, nursing coffee black as motor oil, glanced up to see her.

She walked in with a stiff posture, the kind learned from ballet or trauma. She had a folded sketchpad under one arm and a streak of red in her otherwise black hair. Her boots were wet. She looked like she hadn’t slept well in weeks.

“I need a cover-up,” she said without preamble.

Micah raised an eyebrow. "What kind?"

“The kind that erases mistakes.”

He gestured to the worn leather couch by the desk. “Sit. Let’s see it.”

She hesitated, then peeled back the sleeve of her hoodie. On her forearm was a name, clumsily scrawled in cursive: Tomás. The ink had bled with age, the letters smudged and twisted like a regret whispered too many times.

“I was eighteen,” she said.

Micah nodded. “A lot of bad tattoos start that way.”

She opened her sketchpad and handed it to him. A rough drawing of a fox curled among tangled vines, its eye open and watchful. The lines were shaky but sincere.

“You draw this?” he asked.

She nodded. “I want it to go here. Over it. Replace him.”

Micah flipped the pad shut and set it on the counter. “Come back Thursday. Noon.”

“Just like that?”

“I do cover-ups. Not therapy.”

She almost smiled. “Fair.”

Chapter Three: Beneath the Surface

Her name was Ren. Short for Serenity, which she said with a roll of her eyes. “My mom was into yoga. Real into it.” She returned Thursday, right on time, a thermos of tea in one hand and a look that dared him to judge her in the other.

As he worked the design over her arm, she told him bits and pieces. Not the whole story—never the whole story—but enough for him to hear the outline of something cracked. A musician, she said, used to play cello until her wrist shattered in a motorcycle accident.

“Tomás?” he asked.

“Bassist. Left me in a Motel 6 after a gig in Tucson. Took my wallet and my backup strings.”

Micah didn’t respond. The needle did the talking, stitching lines of fur and vine over old regrets.

“I read somewhere,” Ren said, wincing, “that tattoo ink never really leaves your body. Like, even if you laser it, traces stay in your lymph nodes.”

“That’s true.”

“So... you never really get rid of it.”

“You just bury it.”

She stared at the wall, eyes glazed. “That’s the most honest thing I’ve heard all year.”

Chapter Four: Red Ink

The fox was finished in three sessions. Each time, Ren returned with more stories, more tea, fewer defenses.

Micah found himself looking forward to her visits. She spoke with hands as much as with words. Her laughter, rare but real, cracked the silence of the shop like sunshine through a boarded window.

One night, after her final appointment, they shared a cigarette out back under the flickering alley light. She smoked like someone who had quit five times and still hoped the sixth would stick.

“Ever get inked yourself?” she asked.

Micah lifted the sleeve of his shirt. On his shoulder was a complex geometric design, inked in obsidian black. “First one I did. Sixteen. Homemade gun.”

“Dare?”

“Dare to survive.”

She looked at it a long time. “Looks like a maze.”

“It is.”

She flicked ash. “Do you ever regret it?”

Micah didn’t answer.

She stayed another minute, then left without saying goodbye.

Chapter Five: The Hollow Needle

Business picked up. Word of Micah’s cover-up skills spread quietly. People came with names, scars, symbols they no longer believed in. He didn’t ask for backstories anymore. Not unless they offered.

A man in his sixties wanted a Nazi symbol covered with a phoenix. “I was a different man once,” he said, voice shaking. “This isn’t who I am anymore.”

Micah inked the phoenix with slow, deliberate lines. The man cried when it was finished.

Then Ren didn’t come back. Weeks passed. Her number rang once and went straight to voicemail.

Micah didn’t know why it bothered him. People vanished all the time. But Ren had become a fixture in the quiet corner of his world, and now she was gone, leaving behind a fox with watchful eyes.

He started sketching again at night. Designs that didn’t belong to anyone yet. Strange patterns. Symbols that meant something only to him.

The rain returned. Seattle’s version of consistency.

Then, one Thursday, she came back.

Her right hand was wrapped in gauze. Her eyes were glassy. She didn’t bring tea.

“I need another cover-up,” she said.

Micah stared at the bandage. “What happened?”

“I made a mistake. Again.”

He let her sit before asking.

“This one’s on my side,” she said. “It’s fresh. I did it myself.”

She unzipped her jacket, lifted her shirt just enough to reveal angry, jagged letters near her ribs: STAY GONE.

Micah stared at the damage. “You want that gone?”

“No,” she said quietly. “I want it hidden. Not gone.”

He nodded once. “Come back Monday.”

Micah didn’t drive. He had a truck, sure—an old Chevy with more rust than metal—but most days, he walked. The city streets offered a kind of muted static that helped him think. As he stepped through puddles that reflected streetlamps like bruised halos, he lit a cigarette and let the cold drizzle soak into the seams of his hoodie.

Beneath the ash and smoke, his thoughts wandered. Tattoos weren’t just ink. They were stories etched into skin, warnings and wishes, prayers wrapped in muscle and memory. He remembered the first one he’d ever seen: a spider crawling out of the webbed elbow of a man who used to crash on his mother’s couch. He was six then, and the man had let him trace the lines with a finger.

“Spiders trap liars,” the man had said. “That’s why I keep one close.”

Micah hadn’t believed him, but he never forgot it.

The streets around Pioneer Square were quiet, the usual clusters of nightlife scattered thin by the rain. He passed shuttered cafes, graffiti-tagged dumpsters, and an old man who slept under a canopy of cardboard. His name was Arlo, a Vietnam vet with a sharp wit and a phantom leg.

“Evenin’, Ink Man,” Arlo rasped through the dark.

Micah pulled a twenty from his wallet and handed it over. No words. Just a nod. Arlo pocketed it with a grin full of gaps.

Micah made it to his apartment above a Thai bakery just as the wind picked up. The smell of lemongrass and fried oil clung to the stairwell. His place was a second-floor walk-up, walls bare save for one cracked mirror and a shelf with a dozen sketchbooks arranged like scripture.

He sat at the desk by the window, turned on a low lamp, and opened a pad at random. Page after page of designs stared back at him—wings, thorns, knives wrapped in roses, eyes with ink-dripping lashes, sacred geometry patterns spiraling into nothing. Some were good. Some weren’t. He didn’t care. He drew because it kept the silence from swallowing him.

An hour passed. Maybe two. He wasn’t sure when the knocking started.

It was soft at first. Then sharper. Persistent.

Micah went to the door, one hand instinctively brushing the pocketknife clipped to his jeans. He opened it to find a kid—no more than seventeen—soaked to the bone, hoodie plastered to his ribs. His lip was split and his eye swollen.

“You open?” the kid asked. His voice trembled, whether from cold or fear, Micah couldn’t tell.

“No appointments now,” Micah said. “Come back tomorrow.”

“I got cash.”

Micah looked at him again. There was blood on the collar of the kid’s shirt, and not all of it looked fresh. His hands were shaking, fingers clenched into fists like he was trying to hold something in.

“What do you want?”

“A name. Just a name. On my back.”

Micah exhaled, long and slow. He stepped aside.

The kid sat stiff in the chair while Micah prepped the tools.

“What’s the name?”

“Delia.”

“Who is she?”

The kid’s face twisted. “My sister.”

Micah didn’t press. He laid the stencil, adjusted the lighting, and began the linework across the kid’s upper back. The rain kept whispering against the glass, the machine buzzing in low, angry notes.

The skin accepted the ink well. As he worked, Micah could see the tension bleed out of the boy, little by little. He wouldn’t cry, though. Not the kind of kid who cried in front of strangers. Probably learned that lesson young.

When it was done, Micah wiped the excess away and leaned back. The name Delia arced across the boy’s back in clean, Gothic lettering.

“She dead?” Micah asked before he could stop himself.

The kid nodded. “OD’d. Three days ago.”

Micah nodded back. No words. Just that gesture, quiet and certain, like a pact between survivors.

The kid left without looking up.

Micah locked the door behind him.

Back at the desk, he flipped to a new page and started sketching. Not a name this time. Not a symbol. Just a pair of eyes staring out from behind a curtain of thorns.

Chapter One continued: The Ink That Stays

Two days later, a woman in her forties came in with a trembling hand and a wedding band she couldn’t quite take off. She had an elegant way of speaking, the kind that came from too many years of saying the right things.

“I want something... simple,” she said.

Micah raised an eyebrow. “Define simple.”

“A compass. On my wrist.”

He tilted his head. “Getting your bearings?”

A tight smile. “Losing them.”

She didn’t elaborate. Micah didn’t ask. He worked in silence, ink sinking into skin like rain into dirt. When it was done, she stared at it for a long time.

“My husband said tattoos were trashy,” she said finally.

“Was?”

She stood. “Is. But I don’t listen to him anymore.”

She left a hundred-dollar tip.

People thought tattoos were about identity, rebellion, artistry. They weren’t. They were about time. A record of where you’d been, who you were, what you wanted to forget but never could. A person came in one way and left another, carrying their past like a ghost sewn into the flesh.

Late one night, Micah found himself walking toward the waterfront, wind carving against his skin like cold teeth. He passed the ferry terminal, turned down an alley, and stopped outside an old iron door. The sign above it read: Vesper’s – Tattoo & Oddities, letters faded like old bone.

He hadn’t been there in years.

Inside, the air smelled of burnt sage and machine oil. Behind the counter was Vesper herself, age indeterminate, hair white and coiled like smoke. Her arms were a mosaic of color, symbols no one could decipher but her.

“Micah,” she said, voice like sandpaper and silk. “Thought you left the trade.”

“I opened my own place.”

She leaned forward, eyes sharp. “So why come back now?”

“I need ink,” he said.

“You do your own.”

“Not this one.”

Vesper studied him, then nodded slowly. “What’s it for?”

Micah’s jaw tightened. “Someone I lost.”

She said nothing, just pointed to the chair.

He sat.

And for the first time in years, someone else held the needle.

The buzz of Vesper’s coil machine hummed like a lullaby from an old world. It was slower than Micah’s rotary, rougher, the kind of tool you didn’t use unless you knew how to read skin like scripture. He sat still, eyes unfocused on the peeling tin ceiling above him, as her hands moved with ritual precision. Her touch hadn’t changed—firm, unhurried, nearly reverent.

“You still carry the tension in your shoulder,” she said, her voice low, like she was speaking to the ink itself.

Micah didn’t respond. The truth was, he didn’t know what he was doing there. Not really. He’d walked away from this place nearly a decade ago after everything with Isaac fell apart. After the fire. After he swore he’d never let someone else leave a mark on him again.

But something had been shifting. Not just with Ren. With the kid, with the widow, with every person who walked through his shop and left with a little less weight. He’d told himself he was just providing a service. That he didn’t care why they came. But that wasn’t true, and he knew it. Each of their stories etched a groove in him. He carried them all.

Vesper worked in silence, except for the occasional dip of her needle into ink, the rhythmic wiping of the rag against his skin. The design was one he had drawn himself—a single feather, tattered and curling in on itself, wrapped in a strand of barbed wire that looked halfway like ivy. It wasn’t beautiful. It wasn’t meant to be.

When she finished, she stood back, wiping her hands on a towel. “There,” she said, not asking if he liked it. “Now it’s real.”

Micah exhaled slowly and rose, the familiar ache settling under his shoulder blade. The pain was sharp, but beneath it was something else. A dull warmth. A tether.

He left without saying much more. The air outside was colder now, the night pressing in close. He pulled his jacket tight and walked without direction, boots hitting pavement with mechanical rhythm. Around him, the city had shed its coat of rain and shimmered under neon signs and flickering lamplight.

He ended up at a bar he hadn’t been to in years, a hole-in-the-wall called The Vessel. Inside, the air was thick with old jukebox tunes and the stink of spilled beer. He ordered a whiskey, neat, and sat at the far end of the counter.

As the glass warmed his fingers, he caught a glimpse of himself in the mirror behind the bar. The new ink was hidden under his shirt, but he could still feel it, like it was whispering something he hadn’t heard in a long time. He studied his own face—tired, lined, older than he used to admit. But not dead. Not yet.

A voice pulled him from his reflection.

“You’re Delaney, right?”

Micah turned to find a woman standing beside his stool. She was tall, maybe late thirties, with short-cropped hair and eyes that didn’t blink enough. She wore a military surplus jacket and a camera slung around her neck.

“Who’s asking?” he said.

“My name’s Cass,” she said, offering a hand he didn’t take. “I’m working on a photo project. Portraits of tattoo artists. The ones with stories behind the stories. Someone mentioned you.”

Micah raised an eyebrow. “I don’t do press.”

“It’s not press,” she said. “It’s memory.”

He took a slow sip of whiskey. “I’ve got enough of that.”

Cass didn’t sit, but she didn’t leave either. “Look, I’m not trying to put your face on a magazine cover. I just want to capture something real. You’ve got that look—like someone who’s seen too much and still hasn’t looked away.”

Micah smirked. “Sounds like a curse.”

“It is,” she said, then pulled a card from her pocket. “Think about it. I’ll be around all week.”

She left it on the bar and walked out without another word.

Micah stared at the card for a long while before finally pocketing it.

The next morning, Ren came back. She looked worse than before—like she hadn’t slept at all, eyes bloodshot, lips chapped from biting. She didn’t speak when she entered, just handed him a folded piece of paper and sat down hard on the couch.

He opened it. A sketch. Crude. A human heart bound in string, pierced by a sewing needle. Not stylized. Raw. Messy.

“I want that,” she said hoarsely.

Micah nodded slowly. “You draw this?”

She shook her head. “A friend did. The one I lost.”

He didn’t ask how. Or why. Or when. He just looked at her for a moment, then the design, then her.

“Okay,” he said. “Same spot?”

Ren pulled up the side of her shirt to reveal the scarred, still-healing mess of the “STAY GONE” tattoo. Her skin was inflamed, stitches still fresh in places.

“It’s gonna hurt,” Micah warned.

“Good,” she said.

They waited three days to let her skin settle before he could work on it. In the meantime, she came by and sat on the couch like a stray cat who’d claimed a corner of his world. She brought books she didn’t read, stared at walls she didn’t see.

He didn’t tell her to leave.

Instead, he set up a second chair across from the sketch desk. Just in case.

By the time he finally inked the heart onto her ribs, there was something different in the air between them. Not warmth, exactly. Something closer to familiarity. The kind you earned, not chose.

The session took nearly four hours. They worked in silence except for the hum of the machine and the occasional hiss of pain she couldn’t hold back.

When it was done, she stood and looked at herself in the mirror, her reflection shadowed and lean, the fresh tattoo dark and brutal against pale skin.

“It’s ugly,” she said.

“Yeah,” Micah replied. “But it’s honest.”

She nodded once, then sat back down, wrapping her arms around her knees.

“I don’t know what I’m doing,” she said after a while.

“Neither do I,” Micah said.

Outside, the rain began again, tapping softly against the glass like a familiar voice at the door.

Micah looked around the shop—the flash on the walls, the machines on the counter, the silence that no longer felt quite so empty—and realized that something had shifted. Not just in Ren. In him.

The past, the ghosts, the ink—it was all still there. But for the first time in a long while, he wasn’t drowning in it.

He was learning how to carry it.

And maybe, just maybe, he wasn’t carrying it alone.

Chapter Two: Lines That Don’t Heal Right

The sun never really rose in Seattle—it seeped. A weak, gray dilution of morning filtered through low clouds and settled like dust on the city’s worn bones. Micah opened the shop late. He didn’t care. Ren had left before dawn, muttering something about needing to be somewhere without saying where. He didn’t ask. He rarely did.

Skin Scripture smelled of coffee grounds and ink, and he’d barely turned on the sign before the bell above the door jangled. The woman who stepped inside looked out of place—mid-fifties, business casual, hair pinned back so tightly it looked painful. Her coat was pressed, and she smelled faintly of lavender and printer toner.

“I need something fixed,” she said, stepping forward with a kind of defiant tension that belonged more in a courtroom than a tattoo shop.

Micah nodded toward the chair. “Let’s see it.”

She hesitated, then peeled up her blouse to reveal a half-finished tattoo spanning her upper back—a cluster of irises fading into what might’ve been a name, now covered by a thick, clumsy blackout. The lines were shaky, like someone had been crying during the inking. Or drunk. Probably both.

“My daughter did it,” she said. “In the garage. She wanted to bond.”

Micah frowned. “Looks like she used a sewing needle.”

“She said she watched a video.”

He turned to his station, already prepping gloves and solution. “You want it cleaned up or gone?”

“Not gone,” the woman said, and her voice caught. “Just… reimagined.”

Micah spent two hours reworking the mess, turning the botched lettering into a dark feather, burying the wounds under something soft and organic. When it was finished, she looked at it in the mirror and whispered, “She hasn’t spoken to me in three years.”

“Maybe this helps,” Micah said, but he didn’t believe it. Some breaks can’t be mended with ink.

When she left, he didn’t watch her go.

Instead, he opened his drawer and pulled out one of Ren’s sketches. She’d left it behind days ago—a wolf, half-decayed, half-living, snarling through tangled vines. There was something wild in it, something unfinished. Micah had been studying it every night, trying to figure out why it clung to him like smoke.

By midafternoon, the clouds broke just enough for a shaft of light to spill through the window. It hit the corner of the shop where old Polaroids had curled in their frames. One showed Micah and Isaac standing outside Vesper’s studio, both grinning like idiots, arms slung over each other’s shoulders. Isaac’s smile had been wide, reckless. Full of teeth.

Micah hadn’t seen him in six years.

He was still staring at the photo when Ren walked in.

She wore sunglasses even though it was barely bright out, and her mouth was drawn into a line so tight it made her look older. She carried a duffel bag, slung heavy over one shoulder, and her voice was flat when she said, “I’m staying with a friend. But it’s temporary.”

Micah nodded once. “You need a place?”

She sat down on the couch. “I need to not be where I was.”

He didn’t offer the apartment above the shop. Not yet. But he left the door unlocked that night, and she was still on the couch when he came back from the store with two bags of groceries and a bottle of cheap bourbon.

They didn’t talk much that evening. She sat at the sketch desk, flipping through designs, tracing the edge of each one with her fingers like they were runes. Micah cooked eggs and rice in the tiny back kitchen, and they ate on mismatched plates while the rain whispered against the awning.

By midnight, she’d fallen asleep on the couch again, one hand tucked under her cheek, the other still clutching a pencil. He covered her with a blanket and turned off the lights.

It was easier than it should’ve been.

Three days later, Cass returned.

This time, she brought a portfolio—large, battered, crammed with black-and-white photos clipped to handmade matte board. She laid them out on the desk like offerings: a man with burn scars across his chest, cradling his dog; a woman with a teardrop tattoo covering a bullet wound; an old couple holding hands, matching tree roots curling down their forearms.

“I’m not just documenting art,” she said. “I’m documenting survival.”

Micah looked at the photos, then at her. “So you think I’m a survivor?”

She met his gaze, steady and unapologetic. “Aren’t you?”

He didn’t answer, but after a long pause, he pointed to a photo of a girl with broken teeth and a delicate rose tattooed along her jawline.

“She used to come here,” he said. “Her name was Tasha. She got that after she got out.”

“Out?”

“Group home. Bad one. She said she needed something to prove she still had a face.”

Cass nodded and carefully returned the photo to its sleeve. “That’s exactly why I’m doing this.”

He agreed to a session, eventually. Said he’d sit for her photo, but only if she waited a week. She didn’t ask why.

Ren had taken over the back corner of the shop by then. She brought her own pencils, her own markers, even a rusted-out lamp with a fringe shade that barely worked. She didn’t talk much while she drew, but she muttered to herself under her breath—things like “no symmetry” and “don’t trust straight lines.”

Micah caught himself watching her more than he should’ve. Not in a way he understood. It wasn’t attraction, not exactly. More like gravity. Like they were two things orbiting the same dark center and didn’t know how to pull apart.

One evening, she asked, “Did you ever lose someone in a stupid way?”

Micah was wiping down his station. He didn’t look up. “Define stupid.”

“I mean… not a tragedy. Not an overdose or an accident or a fire. Just—someone who walked out of your life and forgot to close the door behind them.”

Micah paused, rag clenched in one hand. “Yeah,” he said. “I had a brother.”

Ren blinked. “You never mentioned him.”

“I don’t.”

“What happened?”

“He left.”

“That’s it?”

Micah nodded. “That’s it.”

She went quiet for a long time, chewing on her lip.

Finally, she whispered, “Mine was my mother.”

The next morning, someone spray-painted the front of the shop.

Micah arrived to find the words FAKE HEALER scrawled in red across the glass. The paint was fresh, dripping down in thin streaks like blood from a shallow wound.

He stood there for a long moment, then walked inside without saying a word.

Ren showed up half an hour later, saw the mess, and clenched her jaw.

“Who the hell—?”

Micah held up a hand. “Doesn’t matter.”

“Yes, it does.”

“No,” he said. “It really doesn’t.”

He cleaned it off with acetone and steel wool, the red leaving faint shadows on the glass like bruises under pale skin.

That night, after locking up, he went back to his sketchbook and started a new design. A face—his own, maybe—with a needle driven through the cheek. The ink bled like tears.

He didn’t stop until the sun began to rise.

Micah’s phone rang just after noon the next day—an unfamiliar number, local. He almost ignored it, the way he did with most unknown calls, but something in the timing made him answer.

A voice on the other end was thin and rasping, but not old. Worn down, not aged. “You still working out of Skin Scripture?”

Micah stiffened. He hadn’t heard that voice in six years, but it clung to his memory like cigarette smoke in old fabric.

“Isaac,” he said. It wasn’t a question.

There was a pause on the other end, then a dry chuckle. “Thought you’d hang up.”

“I considered it.”

Another pause. Longer. “I’m in the city,” Isaac said. “Not for long. I wanted to see you.”

“Why?”

“I got something to show you. Something you should see.”

Micah glanced toward the front of the shop, where Ren sat cross-legged on the floor, sorting through a stack of sketchbooks she had rescued from a thrift store.

“What is it?”

“You’ll understand when you see it. Meet me at Vesper’s. Midnight. She’ll let you in.”

The line went dead.

Micah stood with the phone still pressed to his ear, not moving for a long time. Ren noticed. She didn’t say anything right away, but she watched him the way wild animals watch the sky before a storm.

“You okay?” she finally asked.

He didn’t look at her. “Old ghost.”

Ren didn’t push, just nodded and returned to her sketching. She had started adapting Micah’s older designs, reshaping them, twisting his clean, mathematical geometry into something messier and more alive. Her style was rough, sometimes even chaotic, but it breathed in a way his work hadn’t in years.

That night, Micah walked to Vesper’s.

The city had turned colder. Not in temperature, but in presence—like it was watching him from its dark windows and rusted rooftops. He lit a cigarette but didn’t inhale, just let it burn between his fingers until the wind stole it away.

When he reached the door, it was already cracked open.

Inside, Vesper stood behind the front counter, arms folded, expression unreadable.

“He’s waiting in the back,” she said.

Micah walked through the narrow hallway, past the rooms that still smelled of smoke and sweat and citrus cleaner. The walls were lined with ancient flash art—sailor swallows, daggered hearts, old-school skulls that grinned with too many teeth.

In the last room, Isaac was sitting on the old barber’s chair, his arms bare, covered in tattoos Micah didn’t recognize. His hair was shorter than it used to be, but his eyes were the same: restless, cunning, hungry.

“You look tired,” he said.

Micah didn’t answer.

Isaac rolled up his sleeve and turned his arm over. There, on the inside of his bicep, was a name: M. Delaney.

Micah’s stomach twisted.

“I got this the night after the fire,” Isaac said. “After you disappeared.”

“That wasn’t disappearing,” Micah said. “That was surviving.”

Isaac leaned back in the chair. “You think I didn’t pay? I did. Just in a different way.”

“I don’t care.”

“You should. Because I have something else.”

He reached into his jacket and pulled out a photograph. It was old and crumpled, the edges stained. Micah took it without thinking.

It was a picture of them—young, laughing, covered in soot and ink, in front of a wall they’d once painted together: a mural of broken wings.

“I kept this,” Isaac said. “Even after everything.”

Micah didn’t want it. He folded it, put it in his pocket anyway.

“Why now?” he asked.

Isaac’s jaw tightened. “Because I’m dying. I got maybe two months. Liver’s done.”

Silence filled the room, thick and acrid.

“You came to confess?”

“I came to ask,” Isaac said. “I want you to finish what we started. That mural. It’s still there. Half-covered in grime. I want it back before I go.”

Micah stared at him. He could feel the old pain rising, hot and raw, like a burn beneath fresh skin.

“I’ll think about it,” he said, though he already knew he would.

He left without another word.

The next morning, Ren found the photograph on the counter. She didn’t ask, just studied it for a long time, running her fingers over the faded ink.

“You look different,” she said when Micah returned from his errand run.

“People change.”

“Not in photos.”

She waited. He didn’t elaborate.

Instead, he handed her a key.

“To the upstairs apartment,” he said. “It’s yours if you want it.”

She blinked. “Are you sure?”

“I don’t use it.”

She didn’t thank him. Not aloud. But she took the key and clutched it like a breath she wasn’t ready to exhale.

Three days later, they went to see the mural. It was on the side of an abandoned dance studio near the shipyards, hidden behind a collapsed chain-link fence and three dumpsters. The building stank of mildew and old memories.

The mural was worse than he remembered. Time had eroded the wings into something more abstract, jagged feathers and cracks bleeding into flaked paint.

Ren stood beside him, arms crossed. “This was yours?”

“Mine and Isaac’s.”

She didn’t ask who Isaac was. She already knew.

“It looks like it’s molting,” she said.

Micah exhaled. “It needs to be something new.”

Ren looked at him, something sharp in her expression. “So change it.”

And so they did.

They returned the next day with supplies—paint, ladders, floodlights, brushes thick and worn. Ren brought a can of bright red, the kind that bled like ink in sunlight. Micah brought stencils he hadn’t touched in years.

They worked in silence for the first hour, then in bursts of conversation. Ren sketched freehand vines up the lower edges, while Micah filled in geometric forms, feathered arcs that clashed against the old lines.

By midnight, the wall began to breathe again.

He wasn’t sure how long they stayed there, only that at some point Ren stood back, paint in her hair, hands stained to the wrist, and said, “It looks like it wants to fly again.”

Micah looked at the mural, half-finished but alive, and realized she was right.

The next night, Isaac showed up. He stood in the alley, gaunt and coughing, leaning on a cane like he was holding himself up with old bones and spite.

“You really did it,” he said.

Micah didn’t reply.

Isaac walked to the mural and touched one of the wings. His eyes were wet, though he tried to hide it.

“I thought it was gone forever,” he whispered.

Micah stared at the painting. “Some things don’t die. They just wait.”

They stood there a while longer, three ghosts in the shadow of something broken and beautiful.

When Isaac left, he didn’t say goodbye.

Micah watched until he turned the corner and vanished into the dark.

That weekend, Cass returned for the portrait.

She set up her camera in the back of the shop, near the supply shelves and the high stool where Micah usually sat to draw. Ren hovered nearby, pretending not to watch.

Micah sat in the chair, arms crossed, shoulders squared. Cass didn’t direct him. She just clicked the shutter. Once. Twice. Then again.

“This isn’t for a gallery,” she said. “It’s for them.”

Micah didn’t ask who them was.

He didn’t need to.

Chapter Three: The Skin Remembers

The mural took four days to complete. By the time the last line dried, Micah’s hands were raw from paint thinner and bristle-burn. He and Ren had built something neither of them expected—a hybrid of past and present, chaos and order, grief and survival.

The wings stretched farther than they had in the original piece, less symmetrical, more fractured. The feathers were jagged, some torn, others woven into thick vines of black and red. What once had been a monument to flight now looked like something that had crashed, burned, then pulled itself upright.

People began to notice. A few passersby stopped to stare, phones lifted for photos. A local artist tagged it in a blog post, and within a week, the mural had its own hashtag. Micah hated that, but he said nothing. He wasn’t doing it for clicks or shares.

He was doing it for the boy he used to be, the one who believed in permanence. And he was doing it for Isaac, who faded faster than the mural ever would.

The call came one gray Wednesday morning. Vesper.

“He’s gone,” she said.

Micah didn’t say anything. Didn’t ask where, or how, or if there had been anyone at the end. He just set the phone down on the counter, leaned forward on his elbows, and stared at the floor until the bell above the door jingled and Ren walked in, carrying two coffees and a bag of day-old pastries from the bakery on 4th.

She paused when she saw his face, the tension in her shoulders rising.

“What happened?”

He took the coffee and sipped before answering.

“Isaac’s dead.”

She didn’t offer condolences. She just pulled up a stool beside him and sat in silence. It was enough.

That night, Micah went back to the mural alone. The rain had started again—thin, persistent, the kind that soaked through denim and into the bones. He brought a single can of white paint. On the wall, beneath the wings, he scrawled one word in block letters:

REMEMBERED

The next day, someone tagged over it.

By afternoon, he’d painted it back.

Ren’s art began to change. It was subtle at first—her lines became more fluid, her subjects darker, more human. Gone were the wolves and foxes, replaced by figures with too many eyes, hands cupped over invisible mouths, spines that coiled like vines.

One sketch caught Micah’s eye more than the rest. A woman standing in a pool of ink, her skin stitched with tattoos that resembled scars. He found it late one night, left open on her desk.

“She’s haunted,” he said when she walked in behind him.

“She’s trying not to be,” Ren replied.

He didn’t ask if it was her. He didn’t need to.

That week, Ren inked her first piece.

It was on herself, of course—she didn’t trust anyone else yet. A small mark behind her ear, just a triangle intersected with a crooked line.

“What’s it mean?” Micah asked.

“It means I made it through something.”

She didn’t say what.

Cass brought in a new subject the next day. A man named Porter, skin like dried clay, who had spent twenty years in prison and now wore his regret like a badge on his chest—literally. A crude self-done tattoo of tally marks over his heart, one for every year inside.

He wanted it covered with a window. Just a window.

“I need to feel like I’m still looking out,” he told Micah.

The design took days. A narrow vertical frame, old wood around the edges, panes cracked just enough to suggest something had hit it from the inside. Outside the window, Micah added storm clouds, a single branch of a tree.

Porter didn’t flinch once during the session. When it was over, he stared into the mirror for almost ten minutes, eyes glassy, jaw trembling.

“I thought I’d hate myself for doing this,” he said. “But I don’t.”

Micah said nothing. Just nodded.

After Porter left, Ren asked, “Do you ever think about what people carry under the ink?”

“All the time.”

“And you?”

Micah considered that. “Mine’s still sinking in.”

She didn’t push. She never did. That was one of the things he liked about her.

The shop grew busier. Word of Micah’s cover-up work kept spreading—quietly, mostly word-of-mouth. Survivors brought other survivors. Wounds brought more wounds.

He inked over an engagement ring tattoo for a woman whose fiancé had disappeared into a desert rave and never came back. Turned it into a black orchid. She cried, not for him, but for the part of herself she finally let go.

He reworked a set of initials across the ribs of a trans man who whispered, “This used to be my name. Now it’s just noise.” They covered it with the diagram of a heart—not the cartoon kind, but the anatomical kind, all valves and vessels and honest blood.

Every piece left something behind. Micah felt it in his fingers, in his spine. There was a cost to the work, a kind of spiritual erosion. But for the first time in years, he didn’t mind paying it.

He wasn’t erasing people. He was helping them become something new.

One night, Ren asked, “Do you think we’re allowed to be happy?”

They were sitting on the roof, watching the mist curl around the rooftops.

Micah lit a cigarette and passed it to her. She took one drag and handed it back.

“I don’t think happiness is a place,” he said. “I think it’s something that visits. Briefly. Like a stray cat.”

“And if it stays?”

“Then it probably wants something.”

She laughed, a dry, surprised sound. “You’re such a goddamn pessimist.”

“Realist.”

She bumped her shoulder into his. “Still. You let it in, didn’t you?”

He didn’t answer.

But he didn’t say no.

The next morning, a letter arrived.

Not an email. Not a message. A real letter. Handwritten.

It was from Tasha—the girl with the rose jawline, the one Cass had photographed.

She wrote to say thank you. That the tattoo Micah gave her saved her life, that every morning she looked in the mirror and saw something beautiful where the pain used to be. She’d gotten clean. Enrolled in a class. Started drawing again.

At the end, she added, You probably don’t remember me. But I remember what you said: “It’s not about forgetting. It’s about turning the scar into a story.”

Micah read it three times. Then folded it up and tucked it into the drawer beneath the register.

He didn’t show it to Ren.

Not yet.

That evening, after locking up, Ren surprised him with a gift: a design of her own, tattooed just under her collarbone.

It was a fox. Not a pretty one. It looked like it had crawled through fire. Its tail was ragged, its eyes wild. But it stood upright, defiant.

Micah touched the edge of the ink, not quite making contact.

“You did that yourself?”

She nodded.

“Why?”

“Because I survived,” she said. “And I’m done hiding it.”

He didn’t say anything. Just turned out the lights, one by one, until the only thing left in the room was the faint echo of her voice.

The city shifted subtly as spring crept in through cracks in the sidewalks and along the rusted railings of fire escapes. For once, the rain slowed, and sunlight, tentative and watery, cast longer shadows through Skin Scripture’s front windows. Micah didn’t trust it. Bright weather in Seattle was like a promise made by someone who had already broken too many—it might feel good in the moment, but it wouldn’t last.

Still, the shift brought something else: a change in tempo. The shop’s rhythm settled into something close to steady. Ren worked most mornings now, setting up flash pieces, drawing obsessively. Her designs were still rooted in chaos, but there was something more deliberate in them, as if she had begun talking back to whatever voice inside her once insisted she was only capable of destruction.

Micah watched her from the periphery. Not in a possessive way—he didn’t even know what to call it—but there was a quiet tether forming between them. It didn’t ask for much. Just presence. Breathing in the same space. Surviving near one another without needing to fill every silence.

One day, while reorganizing old portfolios, Ren stumbled across one of Micah’s earliest design books. The binding had cracked and the pages were smudged with old fingerprints and splashes of ink. She brought it over to him, flipping it open to a page with a large, angular tree split down the center.

“This one,” she said, tapping the image with a charcoal-smudged finger. “It looks like a spine.”

Micah glanced at it and shrugged. “It was supposed to be a family tree. Back before I knew what that word meant.”

Ren didn’t laugh. She didn’t make a joke. She just sat across from him and said, “You should ink it.”

He looked up. “On who?”

“On yourself.”

Micah shook his head. “I don’t put that stuff on me.”

“You don’t put anything on you anymore. Except other people’s pain.”

There was no edge in her voice. No accusation. Just an observation, too true to ignore.

That night, after she went upstairs, Micah stared at the tree in the design book. It wasn’t symmetrical. One side leaned heavier than the other, the roots curling like veins. He could see the flaw in it, the imbalance. And for the first time, he wondered if maybe that was the point.

He didn’t ink it, not then. But he traced it with his finger, and that was a start.

—

Two weeks passed. The shop continued to fill with stories: a man who wanted the names of three women erased from his skin, each name a different ex, each done in a different language. A woman who had tattooed a quote on her chest in Sanskrit only to find out it translated to something completely different. A couple who had lost a child and wanted matching images of a swallow carrying a tiny locket.

Micah gave each of them time, care, silence when needed. People often talked the most just before the needle hit skin, as if the machine could hear confession and offer absolution. He didn’t believe in that, but he understood the need to pretend.

Cass returned with a new idea—a gallery show of Micah’s work, portraits of his clients with their cover-ups, alongside photos of the ink that came before. “Healing through documentation,” she called it. “An exhibit of before and afters.”

Micah was skeptical. “Most people don’t want to remember the before.”

Cass shrugged. “They don’t have to come. But others might need to see it.”

He told her he’d think about it. She didn’t push.

Ren, however, was all in. “You should do it,” she said later. “Let people see what they become.”

“You really think anyone wants to look at scars?” he asked.

“Yes,” she said. “Because if they don’t look at them, they keep pretending they’re not there.”

—

One evening, just before closing, a girl walked into the shop who couldn’t have been more than sixteen. She had bruises on her arms and a hollow kind of defiance in her eyes. Said her name was Sera, and she wanted a tattoo—something on her wrist. Just a line. A single black line.

Micah refused. “Come back when you’re eighteen.”

“I’ll find someone else,” she said, teeth clenched.

“Maybe. But it won’t be me.”

She didn’t argue. Didn’t cry. She just left.

Later that night, Ren asked, “What would you have done if she’d lied about her age?”

“I would’ve known.”

“Because you’ve seen it before?”

“Because I was her once.”

They sat in silence for a while after that, the shop dark except for the low light from Ren’s desk lamp. She was drawing again—this time a figure with no mouth, a scream trapped behind a stitched mask.

“I wanted my first tattoo when I was fifteen,” she said, not looking up. “Did it myself. Sewing needle and pen ink.”

“Did it get infected?”

“Yeah,” she said. “Got a fever. Thought I was gonna die.”

Micah leaned back in his chair, arms folded. “You didn’t.”

“No,” she said. “But something else did.”

—

The gallery show began to take shape. Cass rented a space downtown, a small industrial loft with exposed beams and white walls stained with history. She began collecting stories from the people Micah had worked on, photographing the new ink, encouraging them to write a few sentences about what their tattoos used to be and what they had become.

Some refused. Some cried. A few asked for their stories to be anonymous.

Porter wrote a full page about the window over his heart. “It’s not that I want to forget,” he wrote. “I just want to stop carrying it in the shape of chains.”

Tasha sent a voice recording. In it, she said, “I used to think my skin was a curse. But now it’s a canvas. It still hurts, but now I choose what the pain becomes.”

Micah didn’t contribute his own story. Cass asked once. He shook his head. She didn’t bring it up again.

Ren, however, submitted a photo of the fox on her collarbone. Beneath it, she wrote: I was made of fire. I thought that meant I had to burn. But I don’t. I can just be warm.

—

The night before the opening, Micah stood alone in the gallery. The lights were soft, the photos framed in matte black, each paired with a caption or a sketch or a fragment of memory. He walked the room slowly, pausing at each one, letting himself feel the weight of every story. He saw what he had helped others become, and it scared him—how much power he wielded, and how little he had ever claimed.

When he reached the far wall, he found something he hadn’t expected.

A framed copy of his own tree design. The one Ren had taken from the sketchbook. Beneath it, a note handwritten in her neat, awkward lettering:

Even if the roots are broken, something still grows.

Micah stared at it for a long time.

Then he left, walked back to the shop, and pulled out his tattoo machine.

He inked the tree over his heart. Just above the scar no one had ever noticed.

Chapter Four: Ink and Ashes

The gallery opened on a Thursday, a night the city seemed unusually awake. Traffic pulsed louder than usual on 1st Avenue, and the air, for once, was clear of mist, as if even the weather had paused to take in what was happening. Inside the loft space, light spilled from every track lamp, gleaming off polished concrete floors and bouncing off the framed photographs like reflections on water.

Micah stood near the back wall, dressed in his usual black hoodie and worn jeans, his boots scuffed and his hands in his pockets. He hated being seen—loathed it—but Cass had asked him to show up, at least for the first hour. And Ren had insisted with that quiet, absolute tone she only used when something mattered.

“Don’t disappear,” she’d said as she brushed graphite from her fingers earlier that afternoon. “Not this time.”

Now, she moved through the space in a dark denim jacket, black shirt, and a silver chain that caught the light every time she turned. She looked different under gallery lighting—sharper, but softer too, like someone rediscovering how to move without armor.

People filtered in slowly. First a trickle, then a wave. Some came in pairs, murmuring their way from frame to frame. Others came alone, pausing in front of each photograph with quiet intensity. A woman stood for nearly twenty minutes in front of the image of the swallow tattoos—the couple who had lost a child—her hand pressed lightly to her chest.

Micah stayed out of the way. He didn’t mingle. He watched.

Cass floated through the crowd like a conductor, guiding small clusters from one story to the next. She kept her tone low, never performative, never selling. She didn’t need to. The stories spoke louder than anyone in the room.

And every now and then, someone came up to Micah. A nod. A hand on his shoulder. Quiet thanks. Not everyone knew him, but enough did. Enough recognized the name on the wall, etched beneath a title Cass had chosen without asking:

“The Ink Beneath the Skin: Stories Etched in Survival”

Halfway through the evening, Porter arrived. He wore a clean flannel, dark jeans, and a nervous expression that made him look younger. When he saw his photo—chest bare, window tattoo gleaming—he let out a breath that came from somewhere deep in his gut.

Ren stepped up beside Micah then. She didn’t say anything for a while. Just stood beside him, arms crossed, watching the people pass through the space they had accidentally helped create.

“Did you ever think it’d come to this?” she asked.

Micah shook his head. “I didn’t think it would come to anything.”

She bumped her shoulder against his, just once. “Well, it did.”

He turned to her. “You okay?”

“Not even close,” she said, but smiled. “But I’m not where I was.”

Later that night, when most of the guests had left and Cass was packing up her notes, Ren disappeared briefly into the back hallway. Micah found her sitting on a short bench, staring at her own portrait—the fox on her collarbone lit from above, her eyes caught in that fierce, quiet burn Cass had somehow captured.

“I don’t recognize her,” she said, voice barely above a whisper.

Micah crouched beside her. “I do.”

She looked over at him, something raw flickering behind her eyes. “I don’t know if I like her yet.”

“You don’t have to. Just keep her alive.”

They didn’t speak again for several minutes. The hum of fluorescent bulbs filled the silence.

Then Ren said, “I think I want to do more. Real pieces. Clients. Not just sketching in the background.”
