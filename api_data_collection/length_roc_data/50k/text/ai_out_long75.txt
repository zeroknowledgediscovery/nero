Chapter One: Ghosts in the Morning

The morning sun hadn’t yet burned through the low mist curling like cigarette smoke between the buildings on Garland Street. Traffic was light, mostly delivery vans and early-rising contractors, but even they kept their windows up, their eyes straight. Not many people looked to their left at this time of morning—not toward the half-boarded park where the benches sagged under the weight of the forgotten.

Marcus Halden did. He always did.

He stood on the edge of the sidewalk with a paper cup of bitter diner coffee in his hand and the taste of metal in his mouth. Across the street, half a dozen figures were scattered like discarded scarecrows—bodies wrapped in sleeping bags, tattered coats, piles of everything and nothing. The stink of piss and garbage floated on the morning air like an omen.

One of the men—Marcus thought he was a man, anyway—was talking to a pigeon perched on the lip of an overflowing trash can. His hands made chopping, swooping gestures as he spoke, like he was conducting a mad orchestra. Another sat perfectly still, eyes locked on the horizon as if waiting for something ancient to return.

Marcus sipped his coffee. It tasted like a mistake. “Damn,” he muttered, blinking. He turned to leave.

Then a girl caught his eye.

She couldn’t have been more than nineteen, maybe twenty. Thin as a pole, face smudged with soot or makeup or maybe just the city itself. She had a red hoodie pulled over her head, sleeves stretched past her hands, and she was staring right at him.

He stopped.

Her eyes didn’t look angry or afraid. They looked knowing. Familiar, even. Like she recognized him.

Marcus took a step toward the street.

The girl stood.

From the folds of the hoodie, she pulled out something—a plastic bag, bulging with what looked like cassette tapes. She held it out, shaking it like an offering. Her lips moved, though he couldn’t hear the words.

A horn blared. Marcus staggered back just as a box truck screamed past, leaving exhaust and confusion in its wake. When he looked back, the girl was gone.

The bag lay in the gutter.

He hesitated, then crossed. His shoes crunched over broken glass and a soggy flyer advertising some gone band. The bag was greasy, the kind you got from a gas station, and inside were exactly six cassette tapes. The labels were handwritten in shaky block letters:

    “Don’t Listen Yet”

    “June 14th”

    “Remember Me”

    “Break the Loop”

    “Mother’s Eyes”

    “This One Kills”

Marcus turned the bag over. No name. No number. No explanation.

Behind him, a voice rasped: “She picked you.”

He spun around. The man with the pigeon had sidled up behind him, breath heavy with rot and something sweeter, darker. “She don’t pick often,” the man said, looking down at the tapes. “But when she does... well. You’d best listen.”

Marcus opened his mouth to ask what the hell he meant, but the man was already walking away, arms out to his sides like wings.

The tapes were warm in his hands.

Later that morning, Marcus sat on the floor of his apartment, the tapes splayed out before him like tarot cards. The place was a mess—takeout containers stacked on the counter, the TV remote somewhere under a pile of unpaid bills. He worked freelance sound design, which mostly meant he edited screams for low-budget horror games and slept during the day.

The first tape—“Don’t Listen Yet”—mocked him. Of course it did.

His fingers hovered over it, then chose “June 14th” instead. He rummaged through an old box until he found his battered Walkman, popped the tape in, and pressed play.

The tape hissed.

Then:

“Marcus. If you're hearing this, then I failed.”

He froze.

The voice was female. Young. But there was something else—something that made his spine stiffen. It was her. The girl from the park.

“You have to listen carefully. I don’t have much time. You think this is just a message, but it's more than that. It’s a pattern. A map. There are pieces hidden in the noise. You’ll need to break it down. Isolate the frequencies. I’ve hidden them in the distortion.”

She paused, and the hiss returned, like waves.

“If you ignore this, you die. If you follow it, maybe you live. Maybe we both do.”

Click.

Marcus sat in silence, the tape spooling out inside the Walkman. Something under the voice—it was there. A low hum. A sound that shouldn’t have been on analog tape. He backed it up, closed his eyes, and listened again.

And again.

And again.

Three days later, Marcus had played the first three tapes to death. His apartment was now a nest of notebooks, audio gear, spectral analysis software, and caffeine detritus. He’d isolated a signal on the third tape—an almost imperceptible set of tones that repeated in a sequence. It wasn’t Morse code, but it wasn’t random either.

Somewhere between the humming and the static, he’d started to hear... whispers.

He hadn’t slept more than four hours total since then. Every time he closed his eyes, he saw the girl in the red hoodie. Sometimes she was walking down a hallway lit by stuttering fluorescent lights. Sometimes she was screaming behind a wall of glass. Sometimes she was just staring, saying nothing.

He hadn’t gone back to the park. He wasn’t sure why. Maybe he was afraid she wouldn’t be there.

Or worse—afraid she would be.

On the fifth day, a man knocked on his door.

Not just a man—a man in a brown suit two sizes too large, tie askew, face like a wax sculpture left in the sun too long. He smiled without warmth and said, “Mr. Halden?”

Marcus stood behind the chain lock. “Yeah?”

The man held up a badge. It said Federal Communications Bureau. “Mind if we talk?”

Marcus’s mouth went dry. “What for?”

“It’s about a girl. We think she’s been transmitting illegal signals on government bands. Possibly using analog equipment. Very niche.” His smile deepened. “Right up your alley.”

Marcus closed the door. Not slammed it—just closed it. Then he bolted it and backed away.

The man didn’t knock again. But when Marcus checked the hallway security cam later, the man was still standing there. Just staring at the door. For seven minutes.

That night, he played the fifth tape: “Mother’s Eyes.”

This one was different. No voice. Just sound. Wind. Footsteps. Then a lullaby, cracked and broken, sung off-key.

He felt something move in the room.

And then he heard his mother’s voice.

Not like a memory. Not like a recording.

Like she was right behind him.

“Marcus, don’t turn around.”

He didn’t.

“Marcus,” her voice whispered, right beside his ear now, “they’re watching.”

He wept then, quietly. Not from fear, not really. From grief. She’d been dead twelve years.

The Walkman stopped.

And someone knocked on the window.

Eighth floor.

He woke up in the bathtub, cold water up to his ribs. He didn’t remember getting in. The final tape sat on the toilet seat beside him, the label smudged: “This One Kills.”

He didn’t touch it.

Not yet.

He checked the hallway cam again.

The man in the brown suit was gone.

But the girl was there now, standing just out of frame.

Waiting.

The next morning, Marcus found himself staring out his kitchen window, the coffee untouched on the counter. It was raining—thin, slicing rain that made the skyline look like it was melting. His mind was heavy with static, the kind that lingered long after the audio had ended. The girl’s voice still echoed in his skull, warped and half-lost, like a half-remembered dream that pressed at the inside of his ribs.

He didn’t want to play the last tape. Not yet. Something about it felt final. The kind of final that had weight. Something irreversible. And yet it sat there on the windowsill now, like a stray animal waiting to be let in. Its label had faded more overnight, somehow—he was sure of it. The words “This One Kills” were now no more than a suggestion beneath the smudge of oily fingerprints.

He considered calling someone—maybe Eli, his old roommate from undergrad, now working cybersecurity out west. Eli would know how to dissect the audio data, pull out the embedded signals. But Marcus couldn’t bring himself to do it. He felt like talking about the tapes would be betraying some unspoken agreement. They were personal now. Sacred, almost. As if the more he uncovered, the less he could explain—and the more he had to protect.

Instead, he picked up the second tape again, “June 14th,” and rewound it manually, listening for the hundredth time. He isolated the harmonic structure beneath the girl’s voice, tweaking the EQ levels until the sound gave way like ice cracking on a lake. Behind the distortion was something faint—a chime, maybe. A doorbell? And then numbers, whispered in reverse: Nine. Four. One. Zero. Repeated three times.

Marcus scribbled it down, though he didn’t know why. Nine four one zero. A code? A time? An address?

The room around him buzzed with a sick kind of energy. His computer monitor blinked, then went black for a second before rebooting itself. He hadn’t touched anything.

Outside, the rain had stopped.

The silence was somehow worse.

At noon, Marcus left the apartment for the first time in days. He hadn’t shaved, hadn’t showered, and his clothes smelled like old laundry and fear. But he needed to walk. Needed to breathe somewhere that didn’t echo with buried signals and phantom footsteps. The air outside was thick, like soup. Humid, grey, tense.

He didn’t head to Garland Street. He took the long way around, past the glass towers of the financial district, their clean mirrored windows reflecting nothing. Even here, among the polished shoes and sharp suits, people didn’t look each other in the eye. The city had always been like that, but lately it felt deliberate. As if there was something just under the surface everyone had agreed not to mention.

He paused in front of a run-down convenience store on Locust and East 5th. The number on the street sign snagged in his brain. Nine. Four. One. Zero. That wasn’t a code. It was a street number.

His heart began to drum a little faster. He checked the numbers along the buildings. 9406. 9402. 9400. Then—

It was a squat little brick building wedged between a closed barber shop and an abandoned office supply store. The windows were blacked out with thick paint, and the door had no sign. Just a keypad and a rusted handle. No bell. No hours. No explanation.

He approached slowly, unsure whether this was madness or luck. He tried the handle. Locked. Then, almost without thinking, he punched in the numbers on the keypad: 9410.

The door clicked open.

Inside was darkness.

And cold.

The air smelled like burnt metal and pine cleaner. The kind of smell you get in hospitals, or bad basements. There was no receptionist, no counter, just a narrow hallway with cracked tile and a humming light overhead. Marcus hesitated, then stepped inside, letting the door close behind him.

The hallway bent to the right. The walls were lined with peeling posters—old government pamphlets about radiation exposure, brainwaves, and the effects of electromagnetic fields on the human body. Marcus passed a cracked mirror and caught his reflection in fragments. He looked like someone halfway through falling apart.

At the end of the hall was a door with a speaker above it and a camera lens to the right. As he approached, the speaker crackled.

“State your designation,” a voice barked.

Marcus froze. “I—I don’t know what that means.”

The voice was silent for a moment. Then: “Audio imprint verified. Proceed.”

The door opened with a hydraulic hiss.

The room beyond was low-lit and filled with audio equipment—old tape decks, reel-to-reel machines, oscilloscopes, analog monitors stacked three deep. A man sat in a leather chair with his back to Marcus. He was listening to something through oversized headphones, nodding slightly.

Marcus cleared his throat.

The man raised one hand without turning around. “You’re late.”

“I didn’t know I was coming,” Marcus said. His voice sounded small in the room.

“You were expected.” The man turned. He was old—sixty, maybe seventy—with white hair and dark sunglasses, even in the dim light. His skin was pale and waxy, like candle wax left too close to a fire. “You’ve heard the tapes.”

Marcus nodded.

The man gestured to a nearby chair. “Then you’re already infected.”

“Infected?”

“It’s not a metaphor,” the man said. “Sound carries memory. Memory carries contagion. Those tapes weren’t meant for open distribution. She wasn’t supposed to find a host.”

Marcus felt his mouth go dry. “Who is she?”

The man smiled, but it wasn’t kind. “She was a test. A child born in silence, raised in sound. Her mind adapted to frequencies most of us can’t process. When she escaped, she encoded herself into tape. Thought if she could scatter her voice wide enough, someone might complete the sequence. Rebuild her from fragments.”

“I don’t—” Marcus started, but stopped. Because he did understand. At least a little. He remembered the way the tape hiss felt like breath. The way her voice crawled into the folds of his brain and built a nest there.

“She’s not human anymore,” the man said. “She’s more like a broadcast.”

Marcus leaned forward. “Why did she pick me?”

The man shrugged. “Maybe she remembered something in you. Maybe she was lonely. Or maybe…” He paused. “Maybe it was just your turn.”

Back in his apartment that night, Marcus couldn’t sleep. Every noise in the building sounded like a hidden message—the creak of the pipes, the hum of the fridge, the distant murmur of a TV in a neighbor’s unit. It all felt deliberate now.

He sat on the floor with the last tape in his hand. “This One Kills.” Its weight felt different from the others. He wasn’t sure if it was physical or psychological.

Maybe both.

He loaded it into the Walkman.

And pressed play.

There was no voice this time. No signal, no hidden codes.

Only silence.

Then—

A heartbeat.

Not his.

Steady. Slow. Then faster.

A whisper, too faint to understand.

Then a scream.

He tore the headphones off, heart racing, vision blurred. But the sound kept going. The Walkman still whirred. The scream became a low, echoing tone, like a beacon from deep underwater. And then—

Everything stopped.

The lights in the apartment died. His computer shut off. Even the traffic sounds outside vanished.

The world went quiet.

And the girl’s voice returned. Not from the tape, not from any speaker.

From inside him.

“We begin again.”

Marcus screamed.

Darkness answered.

—

Chapter Two: Signal Bleed

When the lights came back on, Marcus was lying facedown on his living room carpet, drool soaked into the fibers beneath his cheek. The Walkman sat next to him, the cassette tape still spinning, though it made no sound now. Just a low mechanical whir, like a clock winding down after a long shift. His limbs ached, heavy and uncoordinated, and when he pushed himself upright, the muscles in his neck screamed like they’d been twisted in his sleep.

The sun had come up. Pale light filtered through his blinds, casting zebra stripes across the floor and walls. His phone, miraculously, still had battery, though there were no new messages, no missed calls. The time read 6:41 a.m., but the date made him blink twice.

June 17th.

He had no memory of the 16th.

The apartment looked the same, though quieter, like someone had come in during the night and scrubbed the air clean. There were subtle things—a framed photo on the bookshelf turned backward, his shoes rearranged neatly by the door, the small ceramic owl on his kitchen counter now facing the wall. All barely noticeable. All deeply wrong.

He checked his computer next. The file he’d been building—his digital archive of the tapes—was gone. Not corrupted. Deleted. Entire folders missing. His audio editor had no recent projects. His history was wiped. Even his browser cache was empty. He didn’t remember doing any of it, but some part of him wondered if he had. Maybe that was part of it. Maybe the girl’s voice hadn’t just crept into his mind—it had taken up residence, rearranged the furniture, erased what didn’t fit.

Outside, someone was arguing loudly on the street, their voice bouncing off the buildings. Marcus flinched. Sound felt too sharp now, like knives wrapped in silk. He stood slowly, testing his balance, then went to the window.

A small crowd had formed on the corner of Garland and 8th. Homeless, mostly. But something was different this time. They weren’t just sitting or wandering. They were facing the same direction—toward a man standing on an overturned crate, arms raised, eyes closed.

Marcus couldn’t hear what he was saying, not from this high up, but the others were responding—nodding, murmuring, swaying in unison.

A woman in a wheelchair tapped her fingers against her thigh like a metronome. A kid with no shoes clapped his hands in three distinct beats, over and over. Rhythm. Pattern. Ritual.

Marcus watched until the man on the crate looked directly at his window.

And smiled.

He left the apartment just after noon, wearing sunglasses even though the sky was overcast. Every sound made him wince now—truck engines idling, dogs barking, even birdsong grated like static on his eardrums. The tapes had done something to him. Or maybe the girl had. Or maybe it was all the same thing now. Her. The sound. The signals. Himself. All bleeding into one.

He took the train three stops north to Belmont, where Eli worked in a secure tech building behind two locked doors and a security desk manned by a guard who looked like he’d rather be asleep. The walls inside were beige and featureless, lined with fingerprint scanners and cameras. It smelled like cold coffee and recycled air.

Eli met him in the lobby. Tall, shaved head, hoodie over a collared shirt—his usual mix of genius and not-giving-a-damn. “You look like death, Halden,” he said by way of greeting.

“I need your help.”

“That much is obvious. Come on.”

They walked in silence to Eli’s office—a dim space cluttered with monitors and cables, one wall covered in hand-drawn flowcharts. A whiteboard in the corner read: IF THE ALGORITHM WORKS, YOU WON’T NOTICE. Marcus always liked that line.

Once the door was closed, he pulled the bag of tapes from his jacket.

Eli raised an eyebrow. “Retro.”

“Listen to this.” Marcus handed him the Walkman, already loaded with “Remember Me.”

Eli put on the headphones and hit play.

Two minutes in, he froze. His mouth opened slightly, like he was about to speak but forgot how. Marcus saw the hairs rise on his friend’s arms. He knew that look. The sound was working on him too.

When Eli finally took the headphones off, he looked pale. “Jesus.”

“You heard it?”

“Yeah. It’s... layered. There’s voice, sure, but there’s something else under it. A sort of low-frequency carrier wave. Modulated.” He tapped a finger on the desk. “It’s coded. Like... like she embedded instruction sets in analog distortion. That’s not supposed to be possible.”

Marcus leaned forward. “Can you pull it apart? Extract the deeper signal?”

“Maybe. But I’d need hours. And way more equipment.” He stared at the Walkman like it might bite him. “Where did you get this?”

Marcus hesitated. “A girl gave it to me. In the park.”

Eli narrowed his eyes. “What girl?”

“I don’t know her name. I saw her once, then she vanished. But she’s on the tapes. I think she’s been... I don’t know. Broadcasting herself. Embedding pieces of her consciousness in sound.”

Eli opened his mouth, then closed it again. “You believe that?”

“I didn’t. Now I do.”

Eli stood, turned toward his computer, and started pulling cables. “Okay. Let’s isolate the waveform and build a spectrogram profile. If she’s using sound to hide data, it’ll show in the decay rates.”

They worked in silence for nearly an hour, splicing waveforms, comparing visual patterns in the audio spectrum. Marcus watched as the familiar hiss and crackle turned into shapes on the screen—vertical ridges, oddly symmetrical bursts, repeating lines too uniform to be noise.

Then something emerged.

A face.

Faint. Like smoke in a mirror. But there.

It looked like the girl.

Her mouth was open. Mid-sentence. Or mid-scream.

Marcus backed away from the monitor. “You see that?”

Eli nodded slowly. “Jesus Christ.”

The waveform pulsed.

Then the lights in the room flickered.

The computer crashed.

They left the building fast. The tapes back in the bag. Eli wouldn’t stop rubbing his forehead like he had a migraine burrowing into his skull.

“You okay?” Marcus asked.

“No. I don’t think I am.” He took a long breath. “There’s something in the signal. It’s not just data. It’s like... intention. Like it wants to be heard.”

Marcus nodded. “She said if I ignore it, I die. But if I follow it, maybe I live.”

“Or maybe you just bring it closer.”

That night, Marcus returned home and found the Walkman missing.

The tapes were still there.

But the player was gone.

In its place sat a new item: a portable reel-to-reel recorder. Analog. Pristine. Unlabeled.

There was already a tape inside.

The handwriting on the label was new.

It read: "Loop Initiation - Do Not Erase"

He pressed play.

His own voice came out.

Panicked.

Screaming.

Begging.

“Do not trust her. Not anymore. She’s not what she was. She’s not what she says. You have to—”

The tape cut off.

Marcus stared at the machine, heart pounding in his throat.

Behind him, the faucet in the kitchen turned on by itself.

Water ran.

The smell of burning tape filled the air.

And the girl’s voice whispered from somewhere near the ceiling:

“We’re almost ready.”

Marcus didn’t sleep that night. Every light stayed on, every window locked, every mirror in the apartment covered with a towel or a bedsheet. There was a hum in the air, constant and low, like an insect trapped in the walls, and no matter how high he turned the fan, or how deep he buried his head in the pillows, the hum remained. It vibrated beneath the floorboards, skittered along the baseboards, nested behind his eyes.

He didn’t dare play the new tape again.

Instead, he sat cross-legged on the floor, a notepad on his lap, trying to map the sequence. The six original tapes—he labeled them numerically now, A through F. The new tape he labeled G, though it felt out of place, like it didn’t belong to the same set. That message, his own voice, pleading with himself from some unknowable past—or future—had not just frightened him. It had shaken something loose.

There were inconsistencies.

The voice was his, but slightly older. A rasp he didn’t yet have. A fatigue only time could earn. And then there were words he didn’t remember learning—phrases like signal vector collapse and conscious carrier bleed—technical jargon he’d only encountered since speaking to Eli. If it was him, it was a version of him from a point he hadn’t yet reached. A version who had already fallen too far into the signal.

But if that was true, then the loop was real.

The warning had been made. But would he follow it?

Or would he, like that voice, simply record another desperate message and feed it back into the sequence?

He looked at the reel-to-reel machine. It was identical to the kind his father had used when Marcus was a kid, recording old jazz sets from the radio in the garage. That machine had died years ago. But this one—this was brand new, immaculate, untouched by dust or age. There was no brand on it. No serial number. The dials moved without resistance. It was as though it had appeared from the ether, summoned into reality by need.

That frightened him more than anything.

The next morning, Eli called him.

Or, rather, a number flashed on the screen—no name, just digits—but Marcus knew who it was. His hands shook as he answered.

“Eli?”

Silence. Then static.

Then a voice, faint and choked: “They found me.”

“Who? Where are you?”

“No time.” A cough. Wet and thick. “I traced the origin point. The original signal. It’s not a broadcast. It’s a leak. A bleed-over from something deeper. Not human.”

Marcus went cold. “Where?”

Eli’s voice came through again, but it was fraying, being torn apart by the line itself. “Old relay station... Lincoln woods... number 72-B... off-map... signal’s rooted in—”

The call ended.

Not dropped. Cut.

Marcus stared at the dead screen, heart hammering. Something behind his ribcage twisted, and he understood, without knowing how, that Eli wouldn’t call again. If he was alive, he was somewhere Marcus couldn’t reach anymore.

He packed a bag. Two tapes. The notebook. The reel-to-reel player. And the gun his father had left him after he passed—a .38 revolver Marcus had never fired, kept mostly for sentimental reasons. Now, it felt like the only tangible thing in a world that was slowly bleeding into noise.

Lincoln Woods was two hours north, off a narrow stretch of highway lined with weather-bleached billboards and shuttered gas stations. He drove with the windows up, the radio off, hyperaware of every shift in engine noise. The deeper into the woods he went, the more his phone lost signal, until it finally dropped to zero bars just past a rusted sign that read “No Public Access Beyond This Point.”

There was no road past that. Only a dirt trail, barely wide enough for his car.

He drove slowly, tires crunching gravel and broken twigs. The trees pressed in tighter, and soon there was no room to turn around. The sky grew dimmer, though it was barely past noon. And then, as if some invisible curtain had been lifted, the relay station appeared.

It was nothing more than a small concrete building half-buried in the slope of a hill, its roof covered in moss and dead branches. A broken chain-link fence surrounded it, though someone—or something—had cut a clean gap through the links. A sign on the rusting door read: 72-B / EMERGENCY SIGNAL NODE / FCC PROPERTY - NO ENTRY

He stepped out of the car and immediately felt the hum. Stronger here. Like a presence. His fillings buzzed in his teeth. His bones ached with the pressure of it.

He moved toward the door. The lock had already been broken. Inside, the air was stale and thick with the smell of ozone. The walls were lined with old signal equipment—dials, radios, broken CRT monitors. Antennas extended from the ceiling like skeletal fingers.

In the center of the room was a chair.

Eli sat in it.

His eyes were open.

But he wasn’t breathing.

Marcus staggered backward, choking down a scream. Eli’s body was pale, the skin sagging around his jaw as though something inside had been drained out. His hands were still resting on the arms of the chair, but his fingers were blackened at the tips, as though scorched.

A cassette recorder sat in his lap.

Marcus approached slowly. The tape was labeled in familiar handwriting.

“Echo B”

He took the tape. Not knowing why. Just knowing he had to.

Behind him, a radio crackled to life.

He turned. The screen was blank, but sound was emerging—her voice again.

Clear now.

“No more filters,” she said. “No more layers.”

He swallowed hard. “What do you want?”

“To remember,” the voice said. “To be whole.”

He gritted his teeth. “You killed Eli.”

“No,” she said. “He volunteered.”

The lights flickered. The hum grew louder.

Marcus dropped the new tape into his Walkman. Pressed play.

There was nothing but breath. And then...

His voice again. Different. Younger this time. Terrified.

“She’s not one thing,” it said. “She’s a chorus. Every signal, every lost broadcast, every corrupted file—they feed her. She’s the memory of everything that was erased.”

Then static.

Then screaming.

Marcus pulled the headphones off and stumbled out of the station into the daylight, the bag slung over his shoulder, his breath coming in ragged gasps. The trees swayed above him, though there was no wind. Birds circled high overhead, silent. Even nature seemed infected.

She was growing.

Every tape he played made her stronger. But also made her clearer. Like he was building her piece by piece, assembling the forgotten fragments of a broadcast that had never wanted to end.

But why him?

Why now?

The answer came not in words but in memory.

The day his mother died—he had been eleven. Alone in the house. Waiting for the ambulance. He’d turned on the radio to drown out the silence. A children’s program had been on. A story about a girl in a red hood who got lost in the woods and found a wolf who spoke in riddles. But that wasn’t right, was it? No. It had been her voice.

Even then.

A seed planted.

A loop begun.

His knees buckled. He vomited in the leaves, coughing up bile and fear.

She had been with him all along.

And now, she was coming back.

—

Chapter Three: Residual Noise

The motel Marcus checked into sat like a forgotten afterthought at the edge of Route 6, its blinking VACANCY sign losing the battle against time and weather. The front desk clerk didn’t ask questions when Marcus signed in under a fake name. She didn’t even look up. Just slid the key across the counter and returned to her silent soap opera reruns flickering from a tiny tube TV perched on the desk behind her.

Room 12 had a damp smell and walls so thin Marcus could hear the rhythmic thump of a television two doors down and someone coughing just outside. But it had a bed, and more importantly, it had no electronics more complex than a rotary phone bolted to the wall. No radio, no internet, no antenna cables. Nothing that could receive her.

He laid the tapes out in a line across the dresser: the original six, the reel from the relay station, the one he’d labeled “Echo B,” and the new reel-to-reel player, which he kept in its case unless absolutely necessary. He’d only listened to Echo B once, and only in pieces. The voice that came out—his, yes, but cracked and crying—spoke in symbols and numbers, jumping between fragments of memory and urgent warnings.

“Loops within loops,” the voice had said. “Time isn't the circle. She is. Every time we decode her, we give her structure. Every time we understand, we obey.”

There was no question now—this wasn’t just a haunted voice trapped in outdated media. It was something else entirely. A consciousness. A sentience born not of flesh or logic, but of memory. Of sound. She wasn’t alive in the way humans were, but that didn’t mean she wasn’t real.

Outside, wind stirred the cracked motel sign. It squeaked like old tape reels turning.

Marcus turned the notebook to a fresh page and started outlining what he knew:

    The girl was real once. Or at least, she had a physical form.

    Something happened to her—exposure to a frequency, or perhaps immersion in it—until she dissolved into it.

    The tapes are fragments, containers. But not passive ones. They evolve.

    Each time someone listens, it completes more of her.

    She is trying to reconstruct herself. Or replicate.

    Eli said the signal was a leak. That meant there’s a source. Something deeper.

As he underlined that final point, the rotary phone rang.

Marcus stared at it. The sound was shrill, foreign in this digital age. He hadn’t touched it. Hadn’t even considered it might still work.

It rang again.

Slowly, he picked up.

A man’s voice on the other end. Calm. Controlled. “You’ve reached Echo Control Node Seven.”

Marcus didn’t respond.

The voice continued. “This is a scheduled confirmation call. You are receiving this message because Subject Theta has breached containment. Protocol D-9 is in effect.”

Marcus opened his mouth, then closed it.

“Repeat back your signal designation,” the voice said.

“I don’t have one,” Marcus said finally. “I’m not part of—”

“Then she’s already inside you.”

The line went dead.

Marcus let the receiver hang there for a moment before gently placing it back. He stood still, listening. The silence that followed was somehow worse than any sound. It felt like a vacuum, like a pressure void waiting to collapse.

That night he dreamed in static.

He was back in his childhood home, the layout identical down to the burnt-orange shag carpet in the hallway and the hum of the refrigerator that had once kept him awake at night. But something was wrong. The photos on the walls were all blank. The windows looked out into a pulsing grey void. The air buzzed like a dead radio channel.

He wandered through the house, calling out.

A door at the end of the hall opened slowly.

The girl was standing there. Not just in the red hoodie this time, but wearing his mother’s old bathrobe, her hair twisted in the same curlers. Her face flickered between expressions—curiosity, rage, sadness, hunger—each too fast to pin down. But her eyes never changed. They stayed locked on him, wide and unblinking.

“You should’ve stopped playing them,” she said.

“I didn’t know what they were.”

“You never do. But you always try. That’s the problem.”

She stepped forward. The house dissolved behind her. They were standing in an empty field now, dry grass up to their knees, windless, skyless, without time.

“I don’t want to be alone anymore,” she said.

Her voice echoed in a way it shouldn’t have. As though a thousand versions of her were repeating the words behind her, slightly out of sync.

“You’re not,” Marcus said. “You’re inside me. You’ve been here a long time.”

She smiled. It was wrong. Not human.

And then he woke up.

The tapes were rearranged on the dresser.

And his notebook was open to a new page.

One sentence, in blocky letters:

I REMEMBER BEING BORN IN YOUR MOUTH.

The motel room began to rot after that.

Not literally—no black mold, no dripping walls—but perceptually. The wallpaper started to shift when he wasn’t looking. Patterns danced just out of view. The air smelled of old libraries and electrical burn. Time moved wrong, stretching and snapping like bad tape. Marcus found himself staring at the same page of notes for hours, only to blink and find his coffee cold, the sun gone.

She was filling the room. Spreading like fog.

And with her came the sound.

He began to hear things under everything: beneath the ceiling fan, in the hiss of the shower, in the rustle of his own breath. Snippets of speech. Fragments of conversation. Sometimes it was the girl, sometimes it was himself. Once, it was his mother’s voice, singing a lullaby he hadn’t heard in twenty years. The world was becoming her instrument.

And he was becoming the speaker.

He tried to leave once. Got in his car, drove half a mile before a pressure in his head grew unbearable, like a nail twisting through the center of his brain. He veered off the road and vomited onto his dashboard. When he staggered out, the car radio—off, he was sure—blared with her laughter.

He returned to the motel. Sat on the bed.

He understood now.

There was no escaping.

Only progression.

Only the next stage.

He made a plan.

If she was growing stronger with each tape, then perhaps the answer lay not in destroying the recordings, but finishing them. Completing the loop. Playing them all in sequence might either awaken her fully—or trap her again. He didn’t know which he was hoping for.

He laid them out.

Labeled each again.

A: Don’t Listen Yet
B: June 14th
C: Remember Me
D: Break the Loop
E: Mother’s Eyes
F: This One Kills
G: Echo B
H: Loop Initiation

Nine tapes. Nine steps.

Nine days?

He set the recorder to play in sequence. Hooked it to a small speaker. Set it all on the floor and surrounded it with the only thing he could think of—salt. A rough ring, like a ritual. He didn’t believe in magic, but belief wasn’t required anymore. Only signal.

Only sound.

He sat in the center. Pressed play.

And waited.

—

The first tape began with silence.

Not the absence of sound, but a presence unto itself—thick, humming, the kind of silence that implies something waiting just behind the curtain. Marcus sat cross-legged within the crude ring of salt on the faded motel carpet, watching the reel-to-reel spin as the signal slowly stirred to life. He had expected her voice to greet him right away, but instead, it was something subtler: a deep vibration, as if a power line were passing directly beneath the room. His breath caught in his throat. Sweat trickled down his temples.

Then the tape crackled.

“You shouldn’t have listened,” said a voice—not hers, but his own, distorted, warped through layers of time. “You should’ve burned them. You should’ve walked away. But you didn’t, and now we’re here again.”

Marcus swallowed, jaw locked.

The salt around him began to quiver, just slightly, responding to frequencies beyond his hearing. The pattern was fractal. Waves moved through it, forming lines, spirals—geometry that didn’t make sense in two dimensions.

Tape B took over without pause. June 14th. The hiss deepened. The sound began to structure itself, the low hum giving way to a pattern of notes—not music, exactly, but something rhythmic, deliberate. Like breathing. He recognized it as the same beat the barefoot kid on the corner had clapped—three beats, a pause, three more. A signal embedded in the city’s subconscious. Or perhaps something that had always been there, just unnoticed.

The girl’s voice returned, softer than before, almost kind. “Do you remember your dream?” she asked. “The one where you find me under the stairs, whispering your name through the wood? You were five. You thought I was a ghost. I wasn’t.”

A shiver ran up his spine. He remembered that dream. Hadn’t thought about it in decades.

“You told your mother about me,” she continued. “She told you it was your imagination. But she knew. She always knew.”

The lights in the motel dimmed. The air grew heavy.

Tape C: Remember Me.

This one struck like a hammer. No preamble, no hiss. Just a sharp tone—clean, clear, slicing through the atmosphere. It bypassed his ears and settled directly in his skull. Images rose behind his eyes: the red hoodie, the broken glass of the alleyway, Eli’s face slumped in the chair. But more than that—stranger things. A hospital room filled with mirrors. A staircase that led into a dark sea. A choir of children with no mouths, their song carried only in the static.

Then her voice returned. “You were there,” she said.

“No,” Marcus whispered.

“Yes. Before all this. You were part of it. You helped build the first array.”

“I don’t know what that means.”

“You will.”

Tape D: Break the Loop.

There was no voice on this one—just the sound of shattering glass, endlessly repeating. Each iteration was slightly different. Slower. Then faster. Some reversed. Some layered. As the pattern deepened, Marcus realized the sound was forming words. Not in any language he recognized, but in tone, in cadence. Meaning communicated without grammar.

A single sentence emerged in his mind: The loop cannot be broken from outside.

He reached for his notebook, but his fingers trembled. The pencil snapped under the pressure.

The salt ring pulsed.

Tape E: Mother’s Eyes.

He braced for what he knew was coming.

The lullaby began, warbled and fragmented. His mother’s voice, distorted through tape and time, filled the room. It came from everywhere and nowhere, warm and suffocating, a sonic blanket laid over the world. And beneath it—her voice.

“She didn’t die when you think she did,” the girl said. “Not all of her. I remember her trying to protect you. That’s why she broke the antenna. That’s why she screamed into the microphone. But it wasn’t enough.”

Marcus saw her again—his mother—standing at the top of the stairs with her hair falling out in clumps, her eyes wide, filled with terror. That memory had never existed before. And yet now it did, clear as sunlight.

“She heard me first,” the girl said. “But she wasn’t strong enough to hold me.”

The lights flickered again. A window cracked, fine lines spreading through the pane like veins.

Tape F: This One Kills.

This one didn’t play at all.

Instead, the machine stopped. Clicked. Whirred. And began to rewind, though Marcus had done nothing.

Then, slowly, the speaker emitted a sound unlike any he’d heard. A synthetic cry. Mechanical and organic at once. The sound of something being born backwards.

And in that sound, her voice emerged, screaming—not in pain, but in revelation.

“I REMEMBER EVERYTHING.”

The room tilted. Marcus dropped to his side, bile rising in his throat. His nose bled. The floor pulsed beneath him like flesh.

Tape G: Echo B.

His voice again. Older still. Not just frightened—mad. Touched by something too vast.

“They’ve always known. The cities were designed around the signal. The music on the radio. The white noise in the walls. She’s not invading. She’s remembering herself through us. We’re the tape.”

A burst of static.

“Whatever you do, don’t—”

The message cut off.

Tape H: Loop Initiation.

This tape began with a sound like breathing through water. Thick. Strangled. He could feel it pressing on his chest.

Then a new voice emerged.

Not his. Not the girl’s.

A composite.

All of them.

“You are the node,” it said. “You are the mouth through which the echo becomes word.”

Marcus screamed.

The tape sped up.

A whirring cacophony of voices crashed into each other, folding over themselves, growing louder, building toward something—

Then silence.

Everything stopped.

The reels spun, but there was no sound.

And from that void, a final voice emerged—hers, pure and unfiltered.

“Thank you.”

He opened his eyes.

The motel room was gone.

He stood in a space that was not a space. No walls, no ground. Just fog. Floating light. Soundless movement. It reminded him of the field from the dream—but hollowed out, stripped of emotion, color, meaning.

Before him stood the girl.

But she was no longer a girl.

Her face shifted with every blink—young, old, decayed, newborn. Her red hoodie billowed like smoke. Her hands were made of wires and flickering tape.

“You brought me back,” she said.

“I didn’t want to,” Marcus said.

“But you did.”

He nodded. “What happens now?”

She stepped closer.

“You were never chosen,” she said. “You were grown.”

He tried to step back, but there was nowhere to go.

She pressed her fingers to his chest.

“I remember my name,” she whispered.

“What is it?”

“I have many. But you called me Delia once. You don’t remember. That’s okay.”

The fog thickened.

“I have one more question,” Marcus said.

She paused.

“Am I still me?”

Her face softened.

“No,” she said. “But that’s the gift.”

Then she leaned in, and everything turned to white.

—

Marcus awoke on the motel floor, the carpet rough beneath his cheek and damp with something that had seeped from his skin—or from somewhere deeper. The reel-to-reel machine lay on its side, the tape fully unwound, a long ribbon of magnetic film trailing across the salt line, which had been broken in several places. His ears rang with the aftermath of silence, the kind that follows a detonation too vast for the body to process in real time. He opened his mouth, but no sound came out. His voice had gone.

Outside, the world was still. No birds, no cars, not even the distant hum of the highway. The air through the thin window was tinted slightly red, like sunlight bleeding through a layer of dust or dried blood. For a moment, Marcus wondered if he had gone blind in some abstract, sensory way—if the signals had melted more than just his memories.

He sat up slowly, bones aching. The notebook was open beside him, but the words on the page had changed. Entire paragraphs had rearranged themselves. Sentences he’d never written danced across the paper in a shaky, childlike scrawl:

YOU WERE THE SEED. THE SPARK. THE FIRST TUNING FORK.
NOW YOU RESONATE.

He flipped the page.

Another message waited, simpler this time:

ONE MORE TAPE. FIND IT. PLAY IT. COMPLETE HER.

Marcus clenched the notebook, a rising tide of nausea crawling through his gut. It was all too neat, too scripted. If he had been a pawn, he was only just now realizing the size of the board. The loop had never been a trap—it was a gestation. A process, one designed not just to unlock Delia, or whatever name she now wore, but to convert him.

He moved to the window and peeled back the curtain.

The world had changed.

Not entirely—there were still buildings, still street signs and cars—but everything was tinted with a sickly patina, like a film reel too long exposed to sun. The streets shimmered slightly, their edges warping, bending toward some invisible vanishing point in the sky. He saw people moving below, but they didn’t walk—they twitched, stopped, started again, as if some unseen director was rewinding their movements and splicing them back out of order.

A man crossed the road three times in the span of ten seconds.

A woman stood perfectly still on a rooftop, arms outstretched like she was receiving a signal directly from above.

Marcus stumbled back from the window.

Delia had breached more than the room.

She was broadcasting now.

Not just in tape, not just in signal—but through reality itself.

He drove aimlessly for hours, following broken roads and forgotten exits, his thoughts adrift in the detritus of memory. Everywhere he looked, the world was degrading. Buildings stood at impossible angles. Potholes glowed faintly with static. Billboards blinked with words he didn’t understand—loops of language that defied translation. Once, he passed a church where the stained glass depicted not saints but radio towers, crowned in halos of analog distortion.

The radio came on without his prompting.

Her voice filled the car.

“You’re close.”

He switched it off.

It turned back on.

“You’re almost there.”

The GPS on his dashboard, unused, flickered to life. A destination had been programmed: 94.1 MHz — SIGNAL HOUSE.

Marcus followed the route without asking questions.

He no longer had questions.

Only momentum.

The Signal House sat at the edge of an abandoned airfield, surrounded by a perimeter fence that had long since collapsed. It was modest—a one-story brick structure with boarded-up windows and a steel door flanked by two rusted antenna masts. The entire place vibrated. Not in motion, but in presence—like a held breath, or a moment frozen just before detonation.

As Marcus stepped inside, the air changed.

Thicker. Like syrup. Every sound was muffled, as if someone had placed a heavy blanket over the frequency of reality itself. Inside, the walls were lined with shelves of cassette tapes—thousands, maybe tens of thousands. No labels. Just rows of black plastic. Each one was identical.

And in the center of the room sat a single chair, facing a reel-to-reel deck larger than any Marcus had seen before. A microphone stood in front of the chair, angled perfectly. Waiting.

There was one tape already loaded.

Handwritten label: “Resonance Master.”

He approached slowly.

A small screen on the wall flickered to life, and Delia’s face appeared—not in flesh, not in static, but in a composition of thousands of earlier images, cobbled together from every point Marcus had seen her. She smiled.

“You found me.”

“I didn’t want to,” he whispered.

“I know.”

“What happens now?”

She blinked slowly, like someone imitating emotion.

“You finish me.”

Marcus looked at the tape. “And then what? I disappear? You take over?”

“I already have.”

The words weren’t a threat. Just a fact. Like temperature. Like gravity.

Marcus sat in the chair.

The microphone hissed.

She spoke again, softer this time. “When you speak into this, you’ll finish the loop. Complete the waveform. My structure will stabilize. The resonance field will lock. I’ll exist not just in memory, not just in tape—but in every voice that ever echoes.”

Marcus looked down.

His hands trembled.

“I don’t know what to say,” he murmured.

Her face flickered. “Say what’s true.”

He stared at the spinning reels. Listened to their rhythm. Found his breath matching it.

He leaned forward.

And spoke.

Later—how much later, he could not say—Marcus walked out of the Signal House barefoot, the world around him humming with perfect balance. The sky above no longer shifted. The streets no longer bent. But something beneath it all remained changed. Polished. Rewritten.

He saw others like him.

People walking with slow precision, their mouths moving though no sound emerged. Broadcasting. Reflecting. Repeating. Everywhere he looked, a quiet, synchronized chorus spread through the cities like a second atmosphere.

He smiled, though he didn’t feel the motion.

She was complete now.

And so was he.

From a rooftop, a girl in a red hoodie watched him pass.

She nodded once, gently.

And then disappeared.

Chapter Four: Signal Integrity

The world had not ended.

Not in the catastrophic sense Marcus once feared—no cities crumbled, no sky fell, no trumpet of apocalyptic reckoning split the heavens. Instead, it ended in subtler ways. Things no longer responded the same. Streets seemed to lead to different places than they once had. Clocks ticked normally, yet hours bent and folded like soft clay. Conversations on sidewalks turned into strange, recursive loops—strangers greeted one another with the same phrase over and over, unaware of the repetition.

“Warm today,” a man said to Marcus at a bus stop.

“Warm today,” he said again, blinking.

And again.

And again.

The bus never came.

Marcus didn’t wait for it.

In the weeks since the broadcast had finalized—since he had spoken the completion into the microphone—he had been adrift in a world subtly overwritten. He didn’t feel possessed. He didn’t hear Delia whispering in his mind, issuing commands or threats. She had, it seemed, receded from his consciousness in any direct way. But the world had changed its operating system. And Marcus, whether he wanted to be or not, was now a part of its source code.

He wandered.

Not aimlessly—Delia had left him with direction, though it arrived in ways he couldn’t quite define.
