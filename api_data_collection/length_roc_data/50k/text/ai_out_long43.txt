Title: "The Light Giver"

Chapter One: A Smile for the Camera

There was no city like Halberton. Gleaming towers reached toward the heavens like fingers grasping for divinity, and at their base, the streets writhed with people clawing for purpose. Somewhere between the penthouses and pavement, Eliot Voss built his kingdom—block by block, deed by deed, grant by grant. A savior, they called him. A man who gave without asking, who lifted neighborhoods and washed them in gold.

That morning, Eliot Voss was on the 37th floor of the Lucent Foundation building, standing before the cameras, smile practiced to perfection.

“…and with this pledge of two hundred million dollars,” he said, voice smooth as satin, “we’re not just building a hospital—we're ensuring Halberton’s children will never have to travel for treatment again.”

Applause thundered through the room. Rows of journalists snapped photos. Behind them, donors and officials nodded, sipping champagne. The mayor clapped too hard, clearly aware of the power of proximity.

Then came the handshake—the long, firm clasp with the hospital board’s chair. Flashes burst like fireworks. The photo would be in every major outlet by morning.

But Eliot’s eyes, calm and pale like lake ice, flickered past the lens. Beyond the applause. Past the veneer.

He saw someone.

A man in the back.

Thin. Sallow. Watching too intently.

The smile faltered for a fraction of a second, so brief no one noticed.

Except the man.

He raised a single hand and tapped two fingers to his temple, like a salute. Then he was gone.

The rest of the day passed in measured excellence. Meetings, speeches, signatures on crisp linen paper. Eliot’s secretary, Lena, kept everything to the minute. No wasted motion, no deviation.

Until the drive home.

The rain began the moment he stepped outside the building—Halberton’s skies often timed themselves for cinematic effect. His town car purred at the curb, doors unlocked as always.

As he slid into the leather seat, Eliot removed the smile from his face like a mask. The driver didn’t speak. They never did.

It was nearly ten by the time they reached the Northpoint District. Trees lined the avenues here, centuries old, gnarled like arthritic hands. The gates of the Voss estate opened slowly, metal teeth pulling apart.

Inside, everything was in its place. Polished wood floors. Brushed steel fixtures. Art too expensive to comment on. Still-life silence. Home.

But Eliot did not rest.

He went underground.

Behind the wine cellar, a biometric scanner read his retina. The hidden door slid open with a hiss. Fluorescent lights flickered to life in the corridor beyond.

He descended.

Below, the room was white, windowless, surgical. A single computer sat in the center, surrounded by shelves lined with files—not digital, but real. Paper. Folders. Ink.

He pulled one down.

A name: “Branick, Joseph. Age 32. Status: Released. Condition: Unstable.”

He flipped through photos. A hospital ID. A grainy image of the man in a crowd—taken hours ago.

Him.

The man from the press conference.

Eliot sat down at the terminal. Entered a code. A screen glowed.

“Command: TRACE SUBJECT.”

He waited.

Outside, thunder cracked.

Chapter Two: The First Echo

Joseph Branick had been presumed dead two years ago. That was no accident.

It had been part of a "containment protocol," a phrase Eliot had long ago stopped finding unsettling. But Branick had resurfaced. And he remembered.

That could not be allowed.

Yet Eliot hesitated.

He stared at the flickering monitor, the red pulse tracking the last known signal. Branick's burner phone had pinged in the Croft District—one of the few places in Halberton Eliot’s reach barely grazed.

He would have to go there himself.

Lena found him the next morning sipping coffee in the rooftop garden, back in his public skin.

“You’re scheduled with the Education Trust at nine,” she said. “Then a lunch with the ambassador. Do you want to skip the governor’s gala tonight?”

He smiled faintly. “No. I rather enjoy pretending I like the governor.”

Lena didn’t laugh, but the corner of her mouth twitched. “I’ll prepare your notes.”

As she turned, Eliot said, “Lena. Have I ever asked you to look into someone’s disappearance?”

She froze.

“You mean outside of Foundation business?”

“Yes.”

She glanced toward the garden’s edge, where a gust of wind rattled the ivy. “A few times. Discreetly. You always had reasons.”

“I still do.”

She nodded slowly. “Name?”

“Joseph Branick.”

The name hit like a dropped plate.

“I’ll start digging,” she said softly.

He watched her go. She had never asked questions. Eliot didn’t know whether that made her loyal or complicit. Or both.

Later, as he stood at the podium of a youth outreach event, shaking hands and posing with check recipients, he thought about the first time he'd met Branick.

It had been in the Orpheum Project.

Ten years ago, the Orpheum had been Halberton’s ugliest secret—a state-run institution for children with nowhere else to go. Voss had bought it, “reformed” it, turned it into a state-of-the-art educational facility.

That was the headline.

The truth had been... messier.

Branick had been a counselor there. Young, brilliant, empathetic. He had believed in real change. Until he found out what was happening in the lower levels—what Voss had authorized.

Experimentation.

Surveillance.

Correctional procedures.

Nothing officially criminal, of course—there never was—but enough to break Branick’s soul.

Eliot had seen it happen in real time.

He hadn’t flinched then.

So why now?

Why did Branick’s return gnaw at the edges of his sleep?

That night, Eliot skipped the gala.

He went to Croft.

Croft smelled like rust and secrets. The streets were narrow, hemmed in by buildings that had stopped pretending to be anything but forgotten.

He walked alone. No bodyguard. No escort. Just a black coat and a steady pace.

He found the tenement easily—Branick’s old hideout. The door was locked, but Eliot knew the code. He stepped inside.

The apartment was bare, save for a mattress and a single corkboard on the wall. Scribbled notes. Newspaper clippings. A photo of Eliot, circled in red.

Then a sound.

Behind him.

Eliot turned, just as a figure burst from the closet.

Branick.

Older. Gaunter. Eyes wild.

He had a knife.

It came at Eliot in a blur. He ducked, grabbed the wrist, twisted hard. The knife clattered to the floor. They grappled.

Eliot pinned him.

"Joseph," he said, breathing hard. "You're making a mistake."

Branick spat. "You made it first. You made all of this."

“I saved you.”

“You ruined me.”

Their faces inches apart, Eliot saw it—the despair beneath the fury. The haunted, human cost.

“I should kill you,” Branick hissed.

“But you won’t,” Eliot said softly.

They stared.

Finally, Branick sagged.

"Why did you come?" he asked.

"Because I need to know why you’re still alive.”

Branick laughed bitterly. “Then you don’t understand a thing about what you built.”

He passed out.

Eliot caught him before he fell.

Back at the estate, Branick was locked in the underground suite. Not a prison—technically. But he wouldn’t leave without a conversation.

Eliot stood outside the glass wall.

“You’re safe,” he said.

Branick looked at him through one bruised eye.

“You think that matters now?”

“I think it’s all that ever has.”

Outside, dawn began to claw its way into the sky.

Halberton gleamed.

But the cracks had started to show.

Branick hadn’t moved from the narrow cot in the glass room for hours. The lights were kept dim to soothe his frayed nerves, but Eliot knew from the twitch of his fingers, from the hard angles of his shoulder against the white bedding, that he wasn’t sleeping. A man who’s tasted erasure doesn’t sleep lightly. Eliot sat beyond the glass, a quiet observer, his hands steepled under his chin. It was the third hour of silence. He preferred it that way—silence wasn’t absence, it was space. In space, people gave themselves away.

“Why me?” Branick finally asked, his voice ragged, as though unused for years. “Why not just finish it?”

Eliot leaned forward slightly, elbows on his knees, watching his former subordinate through the haze of reflection. “Because I thought you were already dead. And because someone—somewhere—has helped you crawl back out. I want to know who.”

Branick turned his head slowly, his face a ruin of old injuries and new fears. “So you can erase them too?”

Eliot didn’t answer at first. The question sat between them, heavy and dense, like lead. Then he said, “If they’re a threat, yes. I will.”

“You’re a goddamn machine,” Branick whispered, sitting up and swinging his legs over the edge of the cot. His eyes were sunken, feverish. “Do you even remember why you started all of this?”

Eliot’s silence was colder this time.

“Do you remember Orpheum?” Branick asked, rising to his feet. “Not the facility, not the program. The smell. The kids. The files. Do you remember what it felt like?”

Eliot stood as well, though he did not move closer. “I remember.”

“I used to believe in you.” Branick’s voice cracked. “You said we could fix it all. Not just the city, but the sickness inside it. You said—”

“I know what I said.” Eliot cut him off, not angrily, but firmly. “And I meant it. But there is a line between fixing a system and being devoured by it. You crossed it the day you took those files and ran.”

“They were children,” Branick shouted, hands curling into fists. “You were experimenting on children!”

“I was researching behavior under stress,” Eliot said. “I was tracking trauma, resilience. You think corporations don’t do worse? You think governments don’t?” He took a step closer. “I did it to learn. To stop the patterns from repeating.”

“They were screaming.”

Eliot stopped moving.

“You can coat it in any language you want, Eliot,” Branick said, his voice low now, trembling. “You lost the thread a long time ago. Maybe you think the hospitals, the scholarships, the monuments—they buy redemption. But I saw the basement floors of your sanctuaries. I saw what no one else did.”

“Then why are you still alive?”

Branick laughed, a short, bitter sound. “Because somewhere, I think you want someone to stop you.”

They stood facing each other, not as predator and prey, but as two men whose shared history had been rewritten in blood and ambition.

The silence stretched again.

Eventually, Eliot turned away.

“I’ll have Lena bring you food,” he said. “You’re not a prisoner. But if you leave, I won’t protect you.”

Branick didn’t respond.

As Eliot ascended the steps from the underground chamber, the walls seemed narrower than before. He pressed a palm against one, feeling the cool steel through the skin of his hand. For a moment, he felt the weight of every decision. But then the wall passed, and so did the doubt.

Lena was waiting in his office with a file folder and two glasses of neat whiskey. She didn’t drink often, and never before dark, but Eliot knew it was a signal. Something was wrong.

“Talk,” he said, accepting the drink.

She placed the folder on the desk and opened it to the first photo. It was grainy, likely captured from a traffic cam, but the figure was clear. A woman—tall, olive skin, dark braid.

“Her name is Delia Stone,” Lena said. “Used to be with the Foundation’s wellness division. Psychiatrist. Low profile, but effective. Branick worked with her briefly at Orpheum. She resigned a few weeks after he disappeared.”

Eliot nodded. “Connections?”

“Too many. She’s been quietly funding a network of shelters across the outskirts. They’re off-grid. No permits, no press. But very well supplied.”

Eliot flipped to the next photo. It was clearer—Delia in a clinic, tending to a child’s bruised arm.

“She’s building something,” Lena continued. “Something outside your reach.”

“No one’s outside my reach,” Eliot said, but the words felt heavier than they should.

“She’s smart. And cautious. She’s also running a program out of one of the old metro tunnels.”

“That’s a death trap.”

“She has protection. Ex-military types. People who vanished from the public sphere but never left the city.”

Eliot leaned back. “Branick didn’t crawl out alone.”

“No. He had help. And he’s not the only one.”

The implication sank in.

There were others.

Others who knew.

“I want everything on her,” he said. “And on those tunnels. If she’s hiding something down there, I’ll rip the concrete apart to find it.”

Lena hesitated. “If I may… Delia wasn’t the enemy, back then. She questioned things, yes, but she believed in the work.”

“So did Joseph.”

“And now?”

Eliot’s jaw tensed. “Now I don’t care what they believe.”

Later that night, Eliot stood alone on the balcony overlooking the city. Halberton shimmered under the sodium glow of streetlamps and ambition. In the distance, sirens sang their mechanical lullaby. Below, the courtyard’s marble statues cast long shadows across the flagstones—monuments to generosity carved with hidden guilt.

He sipped whiskey, thinking of the first grant he ever gave.

It was to a school. A failing, flooded public school with no roof and too many rats. He gave them a quarter million. Renovations. Books. Uniforms.

The school had burned down the following year.

Some said it was arson. Others said it was a message. Eliot never found out which, because he never looked. He moved on. He gave again. Built again. Replaced again. Like a machine.

Philanthropy was its own kind of laundering.

Not just for money.

But for conscience.

He finished the drink and returned inside, where the lights dimmed automatically in his wake.

In the depths below, Branick dreamed of screams and fluorescent lights. Of the hum of machines that listened even when no one else did.

And Delia Stone, in a clinic without permits, wrote a letter that would never be mailed:

“You built a kingdom from suffering. Now we’ve built one beneath it. And it remembers what you forgot.”

Above them all, the city held its breath, as though it too had secrets.

Chapter Two: Beneath the Vein

The tunnel stank of rust and groundwater. Long-forgotten maintenance tracks veined through the earth like exposed capillaries, brittle and trembling underfoot. Fluorescent strips—battery-fed and jury-rigged—lit the narrow corridor in fits, flickering like nervous memories. Delia moved carefully, ducking beneath a low-hanging beam as she led the three newcomers deeper into the guts of Halberton.

They were kids, relatively speaking. Two women, one man. Late twenties, nervous hands. Their backpacks were too light. They hadn’t brought enough to stay long.

"You can turn back," Delia said without looking. Her voice echoed oddly off the wet concrete. "No one will think less of you. Not everyone is meant to see what’s buried."

The taller woman, auburn hair tied tight at the nape, answered first. “We’ve seen the videos. The files. We know what Voss did.”

Delia paused and turned, her eyes hard and dark in the artificial light. “No. You know what he let be seen. That’s not the same thing.”

They walked in silence after that, the only sounds their footsteps, the hum of electricity, and the occasional groan from the city’s ancient bones above.

They reached the Hub twenty minutes later. A converted junction chamber, it had once been part of the city’s power infrastructure—a distribution node for a system that had long since gone digital. Now, it was something else entirely. Warmth pulsed from thick copper coils retrofitted into the walls. Cables wound across the ceiling like creeping vines. Beds lined the far side of the chamber, and workstations hummed softly on the other. Children moved among them, half-curious, half-shy, glancing at the newcomers but never speaking.

One boy tugged at Delia’s sleeve.

“Marcus fell again,” he whispered, eyes wide.

Delia crouched. “Where is he now?”

“In the lower drain. He was trying to fix the vent.”

She touched his shoulder. “Take Janine and go sit by the heater. I’ll handle it.”

The boy nodded and scampered off. The three newcomers watched this silently.

“We run triage here,” Delia said, standing again. “Physical, psychological. But the real work is above us—reversing the narrative. We’re writing truth, finally. With names. Faces.”

The man—dark-eyed and rail thin—finally spoke. “You said you used to work for him.”

“I did. Before I understood what was happening beneath the surface. Before I knew what Orpheum really was.”

“And now?”

“Now I pull the roots from his garden,” she replied.

She left them to settle, disappearing into the southern corridor.

Marcus was fifteen, maybe. Wiry and fast, but uncoordinated when it counted. He’d fallen into the lower spillway chasing a wrench. Delia found him sitting on a broken grate, his ankle at a strange angle and his mouth trying not to cry.

“It’s bad,” he said through clenched teeth.

She nodded and crouched beside him. “Don’t be brave. Be still.”

He gritted his jaw. She examined the ankle carefully, pressing and listening for sharp breaths. It wasn’t broken, but the ligament was angry.

“You’ll live. But you’re off vent duty for a while.”

Marcus gave a shaky laugh. “Sorry.”

“You’re not the first to fall in this city,” she said, helping him up. “Just don’t be the one who stays down.”

Back in the estate, Eliot was on the phone with a man named Claremont—a senator whose campaign had been purchased, refined, and gift-wrapped by Lucent Foundation funding three years earlier. Claremont was nervous now, murmuring about whispers in committee rooms, about new oversight proposals and open-data legislation.

“They’re calling it the Transparency Mandate,” he said. “Aimed at nonprofits. You’d have to disclose all financial flows, staffing, affiliations. Even offshore assets. It’s a direct shot.”

Eliot didn’t blink. “Then shoot first.”

Claremont coughed, uncomfortable. “You want dirt?”

“I want leverage. Find out who sponsored it. Then find out who paid them. Everyone has a shadow.”

After he hung up, Eliot stood in front of the tall windows of his private study. The skyline glittered with silent vanity. Somewhere beneath it, Delia Stone was building a myth of her own. And she had help.

He walked to the shelf behind his desk, pressed his thumb to a hidden groove beneath a bronze sculpture. A panel slid aside, revealing a wall of drives—dark, silent, patient. He pulled one marked “ORPHEUM-LEVEL4” and slid it into the port at his desk.

The screen came alive.

He navigated through index files, skipping past the sanitized records—the education data, the aptitude studies, the official transcripts. Those were public.

He entered the clearance key and dropped into the deeper archive.

Video files.

Uncut logs.

Coded assessments.

And names.

Joseph Branick. Delia Stone. Marcus N. Abel. Lydia Kent. Santiago Hall.

Some marked as “REDACTED.” Others as “DECEASED.”

The rest marked “UNRESOLVED.”

Eliot stared at the list and saw it not as a record of failure, but as a to-do list. A catalog of ends that had never properly been tied.

He was methodical.

He believed in closure.

His phone buzzed.

A message from Lena: Surveillance at Croft has a match. Stone seen near Station 22. Estimated headcount underground: 40-60. Includes minors.

He typed back: Prepare a recon team. Quiet entry. No engagement. Full visual confirmation.

A pause. Then: And if they resist?

Eliot hesitated for the first time that day.

Then we remind them who built the city.

In the Hub, Delia sat cross-legged near one of the long battery tables, watching a new child draw shapes with charcoal on a flattened cardboard box. The girl—Carla—had arrived two nights ago from the East Edge, smuggled in by a rail technician who knew one of Delia’s scouts. She hadn’t spoken since. But the drawings were intricate. Not childish. They showed buildings. Floors. Corridors. One image looked exactly like the Orpheum observation hallway—down to the vent pattern on the walls.

Delia’s throat tightened.

“Did you live there?” she asked gently.

The girl looked up with eyes far too old for her age. She didn’t nod. Didn’t speak.

She just drew the next image: a man standing behind glass, watching.

Delia recognized him.

Not by face.

By posture.

By distance.

She folded the cardboard slowly, placed it into her satchel. “You’re safe now,” she said, though she didn’t quite believe it anymore.

She rose and walked toward the far chamber, where a dozen data nodes hummed against the cavern wall. There, she found Alis—mid-forties, compact, scar across one cheek—running analysis on satellite-pulled routing maps.

“They’re scanning the drainage lines again,” Alis said, not looking up. “Two probes in the last hour. Controlled sweep. Quiet, but deliberate. This is Voss.”

Delia sighed. “We need to shift. Split the pods. Move families to the Ravine shelters.”

“Won’t hold.”

“No, but it’ll scatter the profiles.”

Alis finally looked up. “They’ll still come. Even if you scatter. They’ll still come.”

Delia nodded. “Then we take something when they do.”

Alis tilted her head. “Like what?”

“Truth. We take something real. Expose it while they’re watching.”

“That’s a dangerous game.”

“No.” Delia’s voice sharpened. “It’s the game.”

By midnight, Eliot’s recon team was in motion. Five operatives, unmarked gear, no lights. They entered through a maintenance duct five blocks from the Hub, guided by real-time mapping from Eliot’s terminal. Their orders were precise: observe, tag infrastructure, identify primary actors.

They moved silently through the dark, past crumbling walls and broken signage from another era.

Then they saw it: the light. A field of it, warm and golden. And the people—eating, reading, talking, laughing in soft voices. A subterranean sanctuary, unbroken by the violence above.

The lead recon agent—Callen—lifted his camera.

Snapped three photos.

And paused.

One of the children looked up, straight at him. No fear. Just awareness. As if he’d expected this moment for days.

Callen lowered the camera slowly.

Behind him, the smallest sound—a cough.

Not from the Hub.

From the tunnel behind.

Callen turned.

Too late.

The gas hissed from the canister with quiet efficiency.

One by one, the recon team collapsed where they stood.

Delia watched the monitors as the recon team's heart rates flatlined one by one. The biometric readings came from tiny transponders they'd been allowed to keep—intentionally—planted during the sweep Delia’s people had intercepted three days prior. A simple chemical patch on the team’s boots had triggered a low-dose sedative the moment they crossed a specific junction threshold. Nothing fatal, just enough to shut them down for three hours.

“Five down,” Alis confirmed, turning from the terminal. “They’ll wake up groggy and confused, with no memory past the last clean checkpoint.”

Delia leaned against the edge of the console, arms crossed. Her expression didn’t change. “That gives us time to decide what to do next. What they’ve seen is dangerous, but what we let them see is more important.”

“You still want to leak something?”

“We have to. We’re not going to win this war underground.” She straightened, her voice firmer. “We win it with eyes. Cameras. Testimony. Story.”

Alis raised a skeptical brow. “And what happens when Voss comes down himself? He won’t just send teams next time. He’ll send demolition charges and call it a gas line rupture. Or he’ll buy the land and pave over the tunnels with a train station.”

Delia nodded. “Which is why we’ll be gone before that happens.”

“You’re going to scatter the Hub?”

“No. We’re going to migrate it.”

Alis blinked. “To where?”

“Level Six.”

Even Alis, seasoned and skeptical, paused at that.

“You’re not serious.”

Delia’s smile was grim. “I’m completely serious.”

Level Six wasn’t a place. It was a myth. Whispers among former Foundation employees, deep-stored data scraps, the remains of a project Eliot had buried so thoroughly it took a stolen hard drive and a network of underground data-rats to even prove it existed. It was said to be beneath the Orpheum site—lower than legal construction allowed, deeper than city records admitted. Delia had spent years chasing it.

“It's unstable,” Alis said. “Unmapped. Possibly irradiated. If it exists at all.”

Delia moved to the map wall and peeled back the main layer, revealing a crude paper diagram behind it—a sketch of tunnels, fault lines, pressure vents. Hand drawn. Stained. Marked in different colors of ink.

“It exists,” she said. “I’ve seen it.”

Meanwhile, in the Voss estate, Eliot stood in front of the recon agent Callen, who was upright but visibly shaken. He sat rigidly in the steel chair, eyes slightly unfocused, jaw tight with the strain of trying to remember.

“What do you recall?” Eliot asked.

“Approach. Entry vector. We crossed the first checkpoint, initiated silent sweep. Then...” Callen shook his head. “It’s gone. No audio. No visual past the second corridor.”

“You were out for two hours.”

Callen’s eyes flinched. “I’m sorry, sir.”

“This isn’t your fault,” Eliot said smoothly, though inside his stomach turned. “It means they’re more sophisticated than I thought.”

He dismissed the man and turned to Lena, who had been standing silently behind the console the entire time.

“They’re not hiding,” he said. “They’re provoking.”

Lena nodded. “They know we’re watching. And they’re preparing for a move.”

“Where?”

“We don’t know yet. But I pulled all archival data from the Orpheum project. Including the decommissioning logs.”

Eliot raised an eyebrow. “And?”

“Something strange. The final report—dated June 11th, six years ago—notes the closure of the upper five sublevels. But there’s a sixth level mentioned only in an engineering memo two months before that. No formal record. No blueprint. Just a line item under something called Project Palinode.”

Eliot’s eyes narrowed. “I remember that name.”

“You should. You signed off on it.”

Eliot turned to the archive again, typing quickly. He opened a terminal window, accessed the encrypted Foundation internal memory, and searched for Palinode. A single file blinked into view, marked with red tags and a clearance level so high even Lena couldn’t view it without permission.

He opened it.

The screen filled with grainy black-and-white footage: a circular room, concrete walls curved inward like a pit. At the center stood a child—ten, maybe eleven—hooked to sensors and speaking into a microphone.

“Day twelve,” a voice said off-camera. “Subject still reports hearing the signal. No decay in cognitive pattern. Emotional volatility increasing.”

The child began to cry.

Eliot slammed the window shut.

“That file stays here,” he said. “No backups. Not even to the Foundation network.”

Lena hesitated. “What was Project Palinode?”

He turned away. “A mistake.”

In the tunnels, Delia stood before the sealed steel hatch that marked the entrance to Level Six. The air was cold, drier here. Decades of silence seemed to hum behind the door. She held the access card in her hand—lifted from a discarded badge, reprogrammed, reactivated.

Around her, the advance team waited. Alis. Two medics. One coder.

“This is the point of no return,” Delia said. “Once we open this, we’re committed. There may not be another way back up.”

“Then we go down,” Alis said.

Delia nodded and slid the card.

The hatch clicked, screamed once, and then groaned open with the reluctant breath of forgotten machinery. Beyond was a tunnel—not like the others. This was clean. Preserved. Like it had been sealed just yesterday.

Lights blinked on down the corridor in a slow progression. Motion-sensitive. Still powered. That meant the generators were still functioning, somewhere in the deep.

They stepped through.

Every footfall sounded like betrayal.

Back at Lucent headquarters, Lena received an unmarked envelope from a courier with no badge. Inside was a single photo—grainy, taken from a high angle. It showed Eliot, young, standing beside a metal crib in a sterile room. The child in the crib was faceless, only the outline visible under tubes and gauze. On the back of the photo was written:

“You said it was for the future. What if the future is watching?”

No name. No date. Just ink and implication.

She didn’t bring it to Eliot.

Not yet.

Instead, she slipped it into her desk drawer and locked it, then deleted the security footage of the courier’s arrival.

Something had shifted.

Something was watching.

In Level Six, Delia found what she’d been searching for: records. Preserved, uncorrupted, unencrypted. Room after room of analog archives, as though someone had intentionally designed the level to be shielded from modern erasure.

She opened the first cabinet. It was filled with tapes—reel-to-reel. Labeled with names. Case numbers.

She pulled one: Branick, Joseph. 0092-A.

Another: Subject 002. Vocal Resonance Study. Palinode Cross-Sample.

“God,” Alis whispered, staring at a wall covered in photos. “They catalogued everything.”

Delia stepped into the next chamber, where a small, dust-coated projector stood in the center of the floor. She loaded a tape.

A humming noise began. The image flickered onto the wall.

A girl, perhaps nine, stood in a white dress, speaking into a fixed microphone.

“I don’t want to talk anymore,” she said. “Please.”

A voice responded: “Continue the phonetic cycle.”

“No.”

The film ended abruptly with static.

Delia stood frozen.

This wasn’t science. It wasn’t research.

It was ritual.

She turned to Alis.

“We release this. All of it. Tonight.”

And somewhere far above, Eliot Voss felt an unease he hadn’t known in decades—because for the first time since Orpheum fell, someone else held the story.

Chapter Three: The First Leak

Morning in Halberton was always loud. Sirens and streetcars, angry commuters and paid advertisements screaming from wall-screens. But on this morning, the noise had a different edge—one formed by confusion and the first trembling ripple of scandal.

Eliot Voss awoke at 5:42, as always. By 5:48, his face was washed, his tie knotted, and the first espresso poured by a silent machine. At 5:51, Lena entered his private study, tablet in hand, lips pressed into a thin line.

“You need to see this,” she said, no preamble.

She tapped the screen and a video bloomed into life. Grainy. Faint hum in the background. But unmistakable.

A girl’s voice. “I don’t want to talk anymore.”

Then the voice off-camera. Flat. Male. “Continue the phonetic cycle.”

“No.”

The footage ended. Static hissed.

Beneath the player window, the caption read:

EXPERIMENTS AT ORPHEUM: THE CHILDREN WHO WERE NEVER FOUND. BROUGHT TO YOU BY THE PEOPLE WHO REMEMBER.

Below that, a timestamp.

Published: 5:43 a.m.

The video was already at half a million views.

“Who has this?” Eliot asked quietly.

The door clicked shut behind her.

Eliot returned to his desk. He opened a drawer beneath the main terminal and removed a black, matte folder. Inside were printouts—reams of scanned pages from Project Palinode, most of which had never been digitized. He had preserved them because some things were too volatile to entrust to wires and code.

He stared at the page for several seconds before closing the folder.

Delia watched the chaos from a dusty monitor deep in Level Six. Her fingers drummed against the makeshift desk—a slab of bolted wood balanced on ancient filing cabinets. The video had traveled farther, faster, than she’d expected.

Just a second.

The same footage.

The girl in white.

“I don’t want to talk anymore.”

People noticed.

The illusion cracked.

The screen went black.

Eliot exhaled.

If Delia wanted a war of memory, he would give her one of silence.

The old man’s name was Halrick.

There were few people left in Halberton who remembered that name, and even fewer who dared speak it. Once, long ago, he had been a clinical psychologist with an uncanny understanding of behavioral manipulation—until the ethical boundaries of his work grew too porous to be traced. Halrick had not merely studied compliance, he had authored its architecture, shaping the mind not with coercion but with opportunity. To those in Eliot’s circle, he was a myth: the man who helped design the emotional scaffolding of the Orpheum Project, then vanished before the first audit.

Eliot said nothing for a long while.

Santiago Hall.

A test.

A threat.

Eliot Voss.

And they would show the world what he made.

Chapter Four: Faces Behind the Glass

The studio was makeshift, but it had weight.

It was buried in one of the auxiliary chambers adjacent to Level Six, repurposed from what once had been an observation lab. The walls were padded now, muffling the low hum of generators, and a single softbox light bathed the interview space in warm, clean light. The camera was old but sturdy—analog by choice, with no wireless transmission. Anything broadcast would be by physical media, hand-delivered. There would be no backdoors.

Carla sat on the plain stool, hands folded in her lap. Her hair was pulled back tightly, and her clothes were simple. Behind her, the wall was draped in black cloth. No logos. No signals. No distractions. Only her.

Delia stood behind the camera, headset dangling around her neck, not speaking. She had already explained everything: what this would mean, the risk, the process. Carla had simply nodded, eyes hard as flint.

The red light blinked on.

The camera rolled.

“My name is Carla Hall,” she began.

There was no tremor in her voice.

“I’m sixteen years old. I was born in Halberton. When I was nine, I was taken to a facility under the Orpheum Complex. They told me I had a unique kind of mind. That I could ‘hear patterns’ others couldn’t. They said I could help.”

Delia watched from behind the lens, breath shallow.

“At first, it was tests. Word games. Shapes. Songs. Then… it became something else.”

Carla paused, not to collect herself, but to ensure each word landed.

“They put me in a room—circular, metal walls, no doors except one. There was a microphone hanging from the ceiling. I had to say things into it. Over and over. Until I stopped crying. Until I couldn’t remember my own name.”

She turned slightly, as if addressing someone just out of frame.

“Sometimes there was a man behind the glass. He never spoke. Just stood there. Watching. He wore a suit. His hands were always still. I never knew his name… until now.”

A long silence stretched.

Her eyes met the lens again.

“His name is Eliot Voss.”

Delia nearly exhaled in relief, but held herself still.

Carla continued.

“He runs the Lucent Foundation. People call him a savior. They show pictures of him opening hospitals. Smiling with children. Giving checks. But I remember him before that. I remember the rooms. The wires. The cold. I remember my father.”

Delia’s fingers tightened on the tripod handle.

Carla swallowed once.

“They said he died in a fire. But he was one of the engineers on Level Four. He tried to stop what they were doing. And then he was gone.”

Her eyes glistened but held.

“They called it research. They said it was for the future. But they broke us. One by one. And when we stopped being useful, they made us disappear.”

The camera blinked red again.

The tape stopped.

Delia stepped forward. “You did perfectly.”

Carla stood. “Now let them see me.”

It hit the digital bloodstream like acid.

This was a child.

A living, breathing witness.
