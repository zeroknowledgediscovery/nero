Chapter Three: The Echo Pattern

Jon did not dream that night, or perhaps he did but could not remember how. Sleep in the new forest came without sequence or sensation, like falling through still water that didn’t ripple. When he opened his eyes, the sun was hanging too low in the sky for morning. Its light fell at an oblique angle, casting long shadows that pointed the wrong way. Mari sat cross-legged across the glade, her back to a hollow tree, her hands pressed to the soil as if feeling for something underneath.

He stood slowly, muscles stiff, neck aching. The tower was gone—not merely out of view, but absent in a way that suggested it had never existed. The patch of ground where it had stood was now overgrown with a carpet of violet moss that pulsed faintly in time with his heartbeat.

He joined Mari, noticing how pale she looked in the strange half-light.

“How long was I asleep?” he asked.

“I don’t know,” she said without looking at him. “Time’s stopped telling the truth.”

Jon squatted beside her. “Have you heard anything? Felt anything?”

“I thought I felt Reyna. For a moment. Like a draft from a door left cracked open.” She looked up, her eyes shadowed. “But I think she’s gone for good this time.”

“You mean dead?”

Mari shook her head. “No. More like... written out. Unremembered. Like she slipped behind the last page.”

Jon frowned. “So then, what are we supposed to do now? If the chain is broken, are we just stranded here?”

Mari ran her fingers over the moss, tracing a slow spiral. “Not stranded. Released.”

“That’s not an answer.”

She sighed and looked at him fully. “This place—this forest—it’s changing again. And we’re still inside the pattern. The difference is, now it’s reacting to us, not guiding us.”

Jon stood and paced the edge of the glade, watching the trees. They did not look threatening, but he remembered how quickly they could become something else. “Do you mean we’re in control now?”

“No,” Mari said. “But we’re visible.”

He stopped walking. “To what?”

Mari hesitated, then answered. “To the other loops. The broken ones.”

Jon was about to speak when the ground shifted beneath his feet. Not violently, but subtly—as if the entire forest had inhaled. The moss under his boots darkened for a moment, then faded back to violet.

A sound followed. It was not a hum this time, but a rhythm. Three soft pulses, then a pause. Three more, then silence. Like a code being tapped out by roots.

Mari stood. “They’re knocking.”

“Who?”

“The echoes.”

The word settled heavily between them.

From the east, where no path had existed before, a corridor of trees had opened. The bark on their trunks had turned pale, nearly white, and the leaves above them rustled in silence. Beyond them, a narrow trail wound between thornless brambles.

Jon looked to Mari. “You knew this was coming.”

“I suspected,” she admitted. “The moment you walked away from the pillar, you cracked the pattern. That doesn’t destroy it. It multiplies it.”

“Are you saying there are now multiple versions of this world? Of us?”

Mari nodded. “Somewhere out there, there’s a version of you who stepped into the seventh pillar. There’s one who never found the letter. One who never asked me to come.”

Jon’s stomach tightened. “And they can reach us?”

“Not all,” she said. “But some. The strongest patterns persist. Especially the angry ones.”

He looked toward the path. “And we’re following it?”

“We have to,” she said. “The echoes don’t just watch. They search. The longer we stay in one place, the louder we become.”

They packed quickly—though “packed” was generous. Jon’s notebook was gone, swallowed by the tower or left behind in some version of the forest that no longer touched this one. Mari carried only the stone marked Fifth, now faded and warm, and a bundle of letters that had appeared by her feet sometime after Jon had fallen asleep.

They didn’t read them. Not yet.

**

The new path wound downward through gently sloping terrain. The deeper they walked, the warmer the air grew, as if the forest were tilting toward some hidden fire. The foliage changed subtly—leaves turned serrated, bark grew translucent, revealing slow-moving veins beneath the surface. The forest no longer resembled any place Jon had ever seen, yet it still whispered with familiarity, like a dream pulled from someone else’s childhood.

Midway down the trail, they encountered the first echo.

It stood motionless beside the path: a man, almost exactly like Jon in height and build, but with eyes too wide and a mouth pulled tight in a false smile. He wore a coat that Jon remembered owning in university, though he’d lost it years ago. The echo did not move or speak. It simply watched them pass.

Jon stared as they walked by. “That was me,” he whispered.

Mari nodded. “One of you. One who waited instead of followed.”

“He looked wrong.”

“He’s incomplete,” she said. “The ones who don’t reach the pillar tend to fragment.”

Farther down the trail, more echoes appeared. One crouched in the crook of a tree branch, legs dangling, whispering to itself in a rhythm Jon couldn’t quite understand. Another hung suspended between vines, unmoving, her skin covered in painted spirals. Some bore only fragments of recognition—a shared gesture, a familiar turn of the head—while others looked like strangers wearing the cast-off memories of their hosts.

By the time they reached the base of the slope, the echoes had stopped watching. They had begun to follow.

Mari noticed first. She slowed, adjusted the strap of her satchel, and whispered, “Don’t turn around.”

Jon tensed. “How many?”

“Three at least. They haven’t spoken yet.”

Jon kept walking. “What happens if they do?”

“They ask questions. Don’t answer.”

They passed a narrow arch made of bone-white trees woven together, their bark pulsing with slow light. As they stepped beneath it, Jon felt a presence brush past him—no body, no sound, just pressure, as if a memory had jostled his mind on its way to someone else.

Behind them, a voice spoke.

“What did the letter say?”

Jon flinched. Mari reached out and gripped his hand.

“Keep walking,” she murmured.

Another voice followed.

“Do you remember the humming? Or did she tell you to forget?”

The trail narrowed, forcing them closer. Mari picked up her pace. Jon matched her.

A third voice, closer now. “You didn’t close the chain, Jon. You broke it open. You let us out.”

Mari let go of his hand and spun suddenly, her voice sharp and louder than Jon had ever heard it.

“You are not us. You are residue.”

Silence fell.

The echoes stopped walking.

For a long moment, neither she nor Jon moved. The forest grew still, the leaves stiffened. The air cooled.

Then, quietly, the echoes turned and walked backward into the woods, fading into the trees like smoke pulled through cracks in the world.

They reached a clearing just before nightfall. The trees parted into a rough circle, where the ground had been scorched at some distant time. At its center stood a single object: a mirror, tall and narrow, its frame made from twisted roots and feathers that did not come from any bird Jon knew.

Mari approached it cautiously. “It’s a threshold.”

Jon eyed the glass, but no reflection came. Instead, the surface shimmered with static, like water stirred by invisible fingers.

“To where?” he asked.

She studied the surface. “Another version. Not the same loop, not quite. Something parallel.”

“Why would we go there?”

Mari turned to him. “Because we need to find the ones who remember the beginning. The first chain. Before it broke.”

Jon frowned. “There’s a first?”

“There always is,” she said. “Even in a spiral.”

He stepped toward the glass, close enough that he could feel its cold breath.

“What’s on the other side?”

Mari didn’t answer. Instead, she unwrapped one of the unread letters from her satchel and slipped it into the roots at the base of the mirror.

The glass flared.

And then the world bent again.

The world on the other side of the mirror opened with a soundless gasp, like lungs drawing breath after a long submersion. Jon stepped through first, but he didn’t remember the moment of transition—only that his feet landed not on soil, but on smooth tile, etched with patterns that danced away from his gaze the longer he stared. The air was colder here, sterile, and heavy with something like ozone. Above him, the sky was a pale, uninterrupted grey, and the trees—if they were trees—stood motionless in rigid, symmetrical rows, each trunk perfectly the same width, each leaf shaped like a flat coin suspended in stillness.

Mari stepped through behind him, and the mirror vanished. It didn’t close, didn’t fade; it simply wasn’t there anymore, like it had never been.

“This place is wrong,” Jon said quietly, and it wasn’t an observation. It was instinct.

Mari was already moving forward, her eyes scanning the horizon. “It’s not wrong,” she said. “It’s preserved.”

“Preserved how?”

“Like a memory in amber. A version that never decayed.”

They walked between the perfect rows of trees, their footsteps echoing slightly off the tiled path beneath them. The landscape had a frozen quality, not in temperature but in time. Nothing moved. Nothing shifted. Even the air felt bound in place, as if the wind itself had been told to hold its breath.

The path led them to a structure in the distance—angular, symmetrical, with no windows or doors visible from this side. As they approached, Jon began to recognize the proportions, though he couldn’t quite name them. It was shaped like a monolith, but fractured across its center was an imprint. A symbol. A spiral.

“It’s another version of the tower,” he said.

“No,” Mari corrected. “This is the first.”

The entrance revealed itself only when they stepped directly in front of it. A seam slid open down the center, not with the mechanical precision of a machine, but like skin reluctantly parting around a blade. Inside, the walls were made of dark stone, smoothed flat and polished to a sheen that reflected no image. Lights blinked overhead—pale orbs floating just out of reach, like eyes watching through half-lidded curiosity.

The chamber they entered was circular, massive, and empty—save for one object: a book.

It sat on a pedestal in the center of the room, thick and bound in some material neither of them recognized. The pages looked yellowed, but whole. There were no chains, no locks, no guards.

Jon stepped toward it slowly.

Mari hung back, her face unreadable. “It’s called the Root Ledger.”

He looked at her. “How do you know that?”

She lifted one of the other letters—now aged and frayed at the edges—and held it up.

“I wrote it down,” she said. “In a loop I barely remember. When I saw this place once before, in a dream or a trace-memory, I wrote everything I could. Most of it didn’t survive the jump. But that name did.”

Jon placed his hand on the cover. It was cool to the touch. He opened it, expecting symbols or shifting ink, but the pages were plain and crisp. Each one bore a name at the top, followed by a sequence of events written in tight, uniform script.

The first name was Mari Aven.

He skimmed downward. The script listed her age, the moment she received the letter, the way she reacted, how long she waited before entering the forest. Every detail. Every deviation.

He turned the page.

Jonathan Vex.

His name. His path. His doubts. Even the exact wording he’d spoken—“How do I know you’re not lying?”—was written clearly, in ink that still glistened.

Jon turned another page, but this one was blank.

Mari joined him. “That’s the breach.”

“What breach?”

“The broken version. The one you made when you refused the seventh pillar.”

He stepped back. “So this is a record of every loop?”

“Every echo. Every possible variation of the chain.”

He paged forward again, flipping quickly now, searching for a pattern—but the entries became erratic. Some were written in languages he didn’t recognize. Some were blank. Others were crossed out in violent black strokes that tore the paper beneath.

Mari stopped him. “Too much will blur you.”

“Blur me?”

She nodded. “The more echoes you touch, the less anchored you are to your own. That’s how the watchers find you. They track the ones who lose their origin.”

Jon let go of the book, stepping back. “So why are we here?”

Mari turned toward a second pedestal he hadn’t seen until now. On it rested a sphere—glassy, but dense, like a crystal grown in the dark. “To pull one of the originals.”

Jon frowned. “You mean...?”

“We can’t fix the breach,” she said. “But we might be able to reach across it. Find the version of Reyna who never crossed the moss ring. The one who stayed.”

He looked at the sphere. “Why her?”

“Because she’s the only one who didn’t fracture. She stayed out of the chain. She was the anchor.”

Jon approached it. The sphere didn’t react. No glow, no pulse.

“How do we use it?”

Mari handed him the last letter—the one she hadn’t opened.

“This is her echo,” she said. “We anchor her through memory.”

Jon took it carefully, unfolded it.

The writing was delicate. Sharp. Familiar.

    “If you find this version of me, tell her I remember the blackberry vines. Tell her I still have the penny she gave me in third grade. Tell her I left the window open on purpose.”

Jon closed his eyes.

He remembered the penny. The vines. The window and the long summer when he thought maybe he loved her but never said so.

He pressed his palm to the sphere.

The room vanished.

He stood in a field that smelled of dry grass and rain. The sky above was a pale spring blue, scattered with clouds that moved like spilled milk. He wore different clothes now—old jeans, a school jacket, his hair shorter. The world around him had softened, blurred at the edges like a photograph remembered after years of forgetting.

A small house stood at the edge of the hill, white paint peeling, a yellow curtain flapping from the open upstairs window. And standing beneath the lone oak tree in the yard was Reyna.

She turned before he could speak.

“Jon,” she said, her voice gentle but unsurprised. “You finally got here.”

He swallowed. “Do you know why I came?”

“I know you didn’t come to bring me back,” she said.

“I thought I did,” he replied. “But maybe you’re right.”

They sat beneath the tree. A breeze moved through the grass like water across silk. He didn’t ask where they were—this version of the world was so whole, so untouched, that it felt wrong to question it.

“Do you remember the letter?” he asked.

She nodded. “I found it once. But I didn’t open it. Something in me knew I wasn’t supposed to.”

Jon smiled faintly. “You were the only one who said no.”

Reyna plucked a blade of grass and twisted it around her finger. “That’s not always a good thing.”

“It is this time.”

He hesitated, then pulled the letter from his jacket. “She wrote this. Mari. From one of the versions where everything almost worked.”

Reyna took it, read it silently. Her eyes softened.

“She remembered the penny,” she said.

“She never forgot.”

Reyna stood and looked to the sky.

“Will it change anything?” she asked. “Me remembering?”

“I don’t know,” Jon said. “But I think it gives us a way out.”

“A way out of what?”

“The spiral,” he said. “The pattern. The watchers. All of it.”

She didn’t reply right away. Then she walked toward the house.

“I’ll come with you,” she said. “But only as far as the edge.”

“That’s all I need,” Jon said.

When he awoke, he was back in the tower, Mari beside him, the sphere now cracked and dark. Her eyes widened when she saw him.

“You found her?”

He nodded.

Mari stepped back. “Then the root’s memory is whole again.”

Jon looked toward the book. The blank page now held something new.

It didn’t list names. It listed choices.

Chapter Four: The Shape of Unwritten Things

The tower shifted as the choices began to write themselves. Not physically—no bricks moved, no walls fell—but in sensation. The air grew heavier, and the light, which had always been flat and cold like winter moonlight, started to fracture. Thin lines of warmth pulsed in the stones beneath Jon’s feet, spreading outward in spiderwebs of golden glow. The sphere on the pedestal had split in two, but instead of crumbling, it held its form, now translucent and filled with slow-turning smoke.

Jon watched as the Root Ledger responded to the change. The blank page that had held the echo of his conversation with Reyna was now full, and the script was unlike anything on the previous pages. It was jagged, raw, written with a rush that suggested the ink hadn’t had time to dry before history began folding around it.

He leaned in. Mari joined him, her breath catching as the first line revealed itself:

“This version begins with refusal, continues with memory, and ends without the watchers.”

Jon touched the page lightly. “It’s writing what hasn’t happened yet.”

Mari nodded slowly. “Because it’s being made now. We’re not following anymore. We’re authoring.”

The room darkened slightly, though no shadows fell. A low tremor passed beneath their feet—gentler than before, more like the exhale of something immense finally resting. The floating lights overhead, which had never flickered or dimmed, now pulsed slowly, like the heartbeat of the tower itself. The path forward was no longer one of uncovering; it was one of choosing.

Jon stepped away from the book and faced Mari. “Then what comes next?”

She looked toward the far wall. A seam appeared there—different from the others they had encountered. This one was not straight or symmetrical. It curved, forming a spiral that widened as it turned, until finally a passage cracked open, and warm light spilled through.

“We walk into the place that doesn’t remember us,” she said. “And we teach it our names.”

Jon hesitated. “That sounds like it’ll hurt.”

“It will,” she said, then smiled faintly. “But less than forgetting.”

**

The passage did not lead into darkness. It led into a world mid-formation.

At first there was only light—too bright to be natural, but not painful. Then came color: deep ochres and soft purples, the palette of a world not yet defined. The air smelled like parchment and rain. As they walked, the path beneath them built itself in layers of soft ground and stone, coalescing only a few feet ahead of their steps.

“What is this place?” Jon asked, shielding his eyes as the light shifted again.

Mari ran her fingers along the edges of a tree that appeared beside them, its trunk unfinished—half bark, half silhouette. “This is the interstice,” she murmured. “The seam between pattern and void. A place where the unfinished waits.”

“For what?”

“For authors.”

He turned toward her. “You really believe that’s what we are now?”

Mari nodded. “We aren’t part of the chain anymore. We’re the pen. The story doesn’t have rules here. It only has memory.”

As if summoned by the word, shapes began forming ahead of them—not full people, not yet, but outlines. Gestures. Impressions. A woman laughing with her hand pressed to her mouth. A child dragging a stick along the ground. A figure kneeling in prayer or exhaustion. They moved like echoes, flickering in and out, made of light and dust.

“They’re possibilities,” Mari said. “Versions of us. Of everyone caught in the chain before. This is what it looks like when the pattern breaks and tries to remember itself.”

They stepped through the shapes, each one dispersing into wind as they passed. Jon saw himself among them—at least, a version of himself—sitting at a desk, writing furiously, his eyes wild with panic. Another stood in a tower, alone, holding a sealed letter he refused to open.

“Is it dangerous to see them?” Jon asked.

“Only if you believe them more than you believe yourself.”

Eventually the unfinished landscape opened into a wide field of white flowers, glowing softly beneath a sky that had never hosted stars. The petals turned gently as if drawn toward something unseen, and a soft wind began to move, carrying with it the sound of low voices. Not words, just sound—tonal, rhythmic, like a language no longer spoken but still understood.

In the center of the field stood a tree—immense, ancient, but somehow still growing. Its bark was etched with the same spirals that had haunted them since the beginning. Its branches reached out not in symmetry, but in a slow, sweeping arc that made the whole thing feel as if it were mid-movement.

Mari approached it and placed her hand on the trunk.

“This is it,” she said.

Jon came to her side. “The beginning?”

“No. The seed.”

Mari knelt and reached into her satchel. She pulled out the remaining letters—the fragments, the ones they hadn’t dared to open—and began to drop them, one by one, into the hollow.
