Title: The Weight of What’s Owed
Chapter One: A Crack in the Glass

They laughed when he tripped—flat on his face, palms scuffed raw against the rust-gritted pavement. Laughed not because it was funny, but because it was him. Carter Vale. The kid who always seemed out of place, like a stuttering glitch in their clean suburban simulation.

Carter stood slowly, brushing flecks of broken beer bottle from his jacket sleeve. It was secondhand—too long in the arms, tight across the chest—and the patch where the school crest had been was unevenly sewn with a clumsy red thread. Behind him, the gang of boys in the varsity jackets turned back to their pickup truck, their laughter already fading like a dying engine. One of them tossed an empty soda can at Carter’s feet without looking.

"Shouldn't've been standing there, man," muttered one as he climbed into the passenger seat.

The truck roared away. Carter didn’t move until the taillights dipped below the hill. Then he knelt down, picked up the can, crushed it hard between both hands, and tucked it in his backpack. Metal was worth five cents a pound at Wick’s Scrap. And Carter knew the price of everything.

He walked home beneath the dim hum of cracked streetlights, eyes on the ground, a million thoughts crawling behind his ribs. They’d taken his sketchbook last week. The week before, they’d locked him in the gym supply room after hours. But this—this wasn’t just teasing anymore. This was erosion. A steady sandblasting of who he was supposed to be.

His mom was asleep on the couch when he slipped through the front door. The old CRT TV flickered over the room in pale hues. Some crime show rerun, volume just a whisper. He stepped lightly, careful not to wake her. Her job at the hospital was wrecking her spine. She had no time for another report from the school counselor.

He pulled the door to his room shut behind him. His sanctuary. Or at least it used to be. The walls were papered in sketches, mostly done on the backs of junk mail or the insides of cereal boxes. Detailed drawings—mechanical limbs, drones with dragonfly wings, cities sculpted inside glass spheres. All the things he couldn’t build. Yet.

But the sketchbook was gone. Taken. Probably burned. That one had been real leather. His dad gave it to him before—

He didn’t think about his dad. Not now.

Instead, he reached under his bed, pulling out the crate he’d kept hidden since July. Inside were pieces. Wires stripped from old electronics. Bits of brass fittings. A length of copper tubing. Discarded smartwatch cores from the tech recycling bin behind McClaren’s. And—most importantly—a cracked lens from the VR headset stolen out of a bin outside Birchfield Academy.

He wasn’t proud of that one. But pride hadn’t paid rent in years.

Carter stared at the pieces, his fingers already itching to start connecting them, aligning circuits, soldering tiny points into something that might be…might be worth everything.

But tonight was different.

Tonight, his fingers stayed still. Instead, he reached for the folded sheet of paper taped to the underside of his desk. The schematics.

Not his. These had come from a chance glimpse at a rich kid’s locker contents. A prototype security override chip schematic—cutting-edge. The kind of thing worth thousands on the black market. He’d memorized as much as he could and scribbled the rest in pieces over the past month.

He unfolded the paper slowly, breath caught in his chest.

It was time.

Chapter Two: Dust and Circuits

The plan wasn’t elegant. It wasn’t even particularly smart. But it was simple.

Break in. Copy the prototype chip data. Sell it to the right buyer. Disappear.

Not permanently. Just long enough to set things right. Buy his mom a new mattress. Get his GED online. Maybe a car that didn’t rattle like a bag of bones.

The lab he needed was inside the Newton Institute of Technology, about two miles east of the city limits. A place Carter had only ever seen through a chain-link fence, its pristine white buildings glinting like teeth under the sun. But he’d found something they didn’t account for: the delivery hatch.

Once a week, a maintenance van made a late-night drop of cleaning supplies and hardware components. The workers—contracted, underpaid, barely checked-in—used a back entrance accessed by a magnetic wristband and a barely secure pinpad.

Carter had copied both.

He'd followed one of the janitors for weeks, memorizing the man’s routine, learning the code by watching the smudge patterns on the keypad with a pair of cheap thermal glasses he built himself. Tonight, he would test it.

He wore all black—not the cliché ski mask kind, just a hoodie and slacks that wouldn’t reflect light. His backpack held the necessary tools: a homemade data skimmer, two screwdrivers, a putty knife, and a roll of electrical tape. Plus one unexpected item: his father's old dog tag, still dangling from a busted chain.

He wasn't sentimental. Not anymore. But it was metal. And metal had weight.

The institute’s grounds were silent, the air thick with the scent of rain-soaked concrete. Carter crouched behind a row of dumpsters and waited for the van to finish its drop. The back door hissed closed. No alarms.

He moved fast. The code—1187—worked on the first try. Inside, the hallway was lit with sterile blue light, echoing with the faint buzz of electronics. He stepped quietly, each footfall measured like a metronome.

Room 212-B. That was the target.

His heart pounded so loud it felt like it might trigger the motion sensors.

Inside the lab, everything gleamed. Carter had never been in a room so...clean. It was like stepping into a spaceship. Banks of screens flickered with data streams he barely understood. In the far corner, beneath a thick pane of reinforced glass, was the chip.

It was smaller than he'd imagined. About the size of a thumbnail, encased in a transparent housing, pulsing softly with pale green light.

Carter exhaled. The skimmer device went live, its LEDs blinking as it synchronized with the chip’s wireless broadcast.

Then—footsteps.

Someone else was here.

He ducked behind the main console, heart leaping into his throat. The door slid open with a hydraulic hiss, and a voice said, "Left the terminal running again. Idiot interns..."

A girl’s voice. Young. Irritated.

Carter peeked. She was tall, sharp-featured, dark ponytail swinging behind her as she typed something into the mainframe.

He waited. Counted.

Ten seconds. Fifteen.

Then her eyes flicked straight to where he was hiding.

“Hey.”

Carter bolted.

He slammed through the door, sprinting down the hall. Alarms shrieked to life. Red lights washed over everything. Behind him, shouts. Running feet. He burst out into the night, vaulting the back fence, and hit the ground running, pain lancing through his ankle.

But he didn’t stop.

Not until he reached the edge of the junkyard, half an hour later, lungs burning, palms raw again.

The skimmer still blinked in his bag.

He’d done it.

But now someone knew.

Chapter Three: The Girl in the Lab

She found him first.

Not the cops. Not the security team. Not the scientists whose work he’d stolen.

Her.

Her name was Lian.

“You’re not very subtle,” she said, stepping out from behind a rusted-out sedan in the junkyard where Carter did most of his builds. “Most thieves don’t post schematics under their real name on modder forums.”

He stared at her, stunned into silence. Then: “I didn’t steal it. I copied it.”

“Oh, well,” she said dryly, “in that case, congratulations on your moral compass.”

“How’d you find me?”

She held up her phone. On the screen was a grainy photo of Carter, taken from a security feed. “Facial recognition. You’ve got a very distinctive frown.”

Carter’s stomach turned. “You’re going to turn me in?”

“No,” she said. “I’m going to hire you.”

The words hit him like a brick.

“What?”

Lian stepped closer. “I’m serious. I’m not with the Institute. I’m with a group trying to bring down the people funding the chip project. You just proved you can break in, copy high-level tech, and not get caught—technically.”

Carter frowned. “Why would you want to bring them down?”

She shrugged. “Because I used to work for them. Until they used my designs to spy on people and locked me out of my own code.”

Carter hesitated. Then: “So what do you want me to do?”

She smiled.

“Something bigger.”

Carter didn’t sleep that night.

Instead, he sat at his desk, hunched over a page filled with faint pencil marks and half-erased diagrams. The light from his desk lamp made everything look yellowed and older than it really was, like he was studying relics from a world long gone. His fingers ached from gripping the pencil too hard. Somewhere beyond the walls, the sounds of the city exhaled—a distant car alarm, the occasional barking dog, a siren that never seemed to stop wailing once it began.

He paused, leaning back in his chair, arms crossed tight over his chest. His mind was chewing on possibilities. The chip schematic he’d sketched—if he could get it right—was more than just a chance to get even. It was a way out. A way forward. A way to prove to himself that he wasn’t just the bruised joke everyone saw at school.

They all thought he was harmless. Stupid, maybe. He could live with that. Being underestimated was its own kind of camouflage.

But Carter had been studying systems since he was eight. Ever since his father taught him how to solder a transistor back into a busted radio. He remembered the pride in his father’s voice when the static had cleared and music came through. A trumpet, playing something jazzy, bright and clean. That had been the moment. The moment he knew he wanted to make things—break them apart, understand them, and make them better. Or stranger. Or more his.

He picked up the cracked VR lens again, turning it over in the light. He had an idea for it. Something to do with projection. If he could align the signal receiver with the internal gyro correctly, it might be able to—no. Not now. Not tonight.

Instead, he pushed the lens aside and reached under his desk for the small tin box where he kept the real plans. They were hidden beneath folded receipts and old wires. He unlatched it carefully, almost ceremonially, and lifted out a yellowed piece of printer paper, its edges curled. Across it, in tight scrawls of blue ink, were the lines of a project he’d been designing for months. A replica of the override chip rumored to be in development at the Newton Institute.

The story went that it could bypass nearly any biometric lock—fingerprints, retinal scans, even voice recognition. If that was true, it was the kind of tech worth fortunes in the right circles. The kind of thing corporations would kill to protect. Or steal.

And Carter had seen a version of it. A blurry image on the inside cover of a binder that one of the rich kids at Birchfield carried around like a status symbol. Most students would’ve glanced and forgotten it. Carter memorized the layout in seconds. That was his gift. Spatial memory. Pattern recognition. And a brain that didn’t know when to stop tinkering.

He couldn’t sleep anyway. Might as well build.

He worked until his eyes blurred and his fingers slipped. The sky outside began to pale to a washed-out blue as dawn teased the edge of the rooftops. Only then did Carter finally let himself crawl into bed. Not to sleep—his nerves were buzzing—but to rest his body, to lie flat and still and imagine what would happen when he had it all finished.

The next day at school felt like a badly tuned signal. Everything just a little off. The colors were too sharp, the voices too loud. Carter kept his head down, eyes locked on the ground as he passed through the halls. His shoulders curled forward like they could shield him from the whispers and the elbows thrown too close to his ribs. He’d learned not to react. Reaction fed them. Made him a better target.

In chemistry class, he sat alone, as always. Mr. DeCosta barely acknowledged him when taking attendance, which Carter appreciated. The less attention, the better. He focused on the experiment—testing reactions between sodium bicarbonate and acetic acid—and tuned out the rest. His hands moved automatically, his brain elsewhere.

He didn’t notice her until the beaker was already fizzing over.

“You’re supposed to stir slower,” said a voice next to him. Soft, but with an edge like glass under cloth.

He turned and blinked.

She was new. He would’ve remembered her—dark skin, tightly braided hair pulled back with mechanical precision, eyes that looked like they saw too much. Her name tag said "L. Thorne."

Carter didn’t respond. Just turned the heat down and wiped the mess with a napkin from his pocket.

“I’m Lian,” she said.

Still, he said nothing.

“I know who you are,” she added after a moment. “You’re Carter Vale. You’re the one who took apart the fire alarm in eighth grade and rebuilt it into a humidity sensor. And they gave you detention for it.”

That made him look up. “How do you know that?”

She shrugged. “I read.”

“Yeah? Read what?”

“Reports,” she said, like it was the most natural thing in the world. “Files. Rumors. Stuff buried on the school server. The kind of things people don’t bother erasing.”

Carter narrowed his eyes. “Why?”

“Because I don’t like wasting time with people who don’t matter,” she said. “And you matter.”

That was enough to rattle him. He didn’t even notice the class ending until the bell rang and Lian was already gone.

Later that night, he couldn’t stop thinking about her. Not just her words, but the way she’d said them. Like someone opening a locked drawer without a key. Like someone who wasn’t playing by the same rules as the rest of them.

He wondered if she had seen his sketchbook before it disappeared. Or maybe she had copies of her own. The thought made his skin crawl.

There wasn’t much time. If she knew something, others might too.

That was the push he needed.

By midnight, Carter was back in his workshop, tools spread out like a surgeon’s tray. The skimmer was nearly ready—built from scavenged parts and intuition. He’d run diagnostics twice. Everything said green. But he still felt uneasy. This wasn’t just another build. This was the start of something irreversible.

He wired the last connection and flipped the switch.

The machine whirred to life, lights pulsing in sequence.

It was working.

He sat back, chest thudding.

In the distance, thunder rumbled.

Carter didn’t know it yet, but everything was already in motion. The code he would steal. The girl who’d find him. The storm he would walk straight into with nothing but a backpack full of parts and an idea he believed in more than anything.

And somewhere, behind sealed doors and corporate firewalls, people were already starting to notice something had been breached.

Chapter Two: Dust and Circuits

The Newton Institute didn’t look like a fortress. That was the first trick.

Its white, smooth exterior panels gleamed in the moonlight, bordered by manicured hedges and a quiet, looping driveway. A place designed to impress visiting funders, not discourage intruders. But Carter knew better. There were no armed guards, no high towers, no barbed wire—but the real defenses were quieter, more sophisticated: signal traps, motion-sensitive tiles, smart glass that darkened in response to infrared signatures. You couldn’t brute force your way into a place like this.

So Carter didn’t plan to.

His entry point wasn’t a side door or a blind corner. It was Wednesday night’s delivery. Carter had tracked the janitorial schedule for weeks, watched the same box truck arrive through the back entrance like clockwork. The driver never looked up. The access panel was old, retrofitted. It didn’t match the rest of the building’s sleek aesthetic, and in that imperfection, Carter saw his chance.

He crouched behind a pile of broken pallets, wind prickling at the edge of his hood. His bag was heavy with gear—scavenged signal readers, a data skimmer patched together from an old gaming console and a burnt drone core, a battery pack modified to run cool without shedding heat signatures. This was it. The point of no return.

The delivery driver exited the building, the door hissing shut behind him as he lit a cigarette and leaned against the truck. Carter waited. The man looked toward the lot, flicked his ash. Then turned away.

Carter moved.

His legs carried him in a low dash across the pavement, soft-soled shoes silent. He reached the door, punched in the code he’d memorized—1187—and heard the subtle click of entry. A security oversight, probably years old, maybe overlooked because nobody expected anyone to notice it.

Inside, the hallway was as sterile as he remembered. White walls, faint smell of antiseptic, vents humming with quiet precision. He pulled the skimmer from his bag and switched it on. A soft buzz. Green light. Synced. It would begin collecting whatever wireless transmissions it could sniff out, matching the frequency range he’d seen in the schematic drawing. It wasn’t exact—this wasn’t plug-and-play espionage—but it was enough to start.

He kept his pace steady, methodical. Left at the first junction. Past the server maintenance room. Up the emergency stairs, which had the benefit of being less camera-monitored than the elevators. He paused on the landing, checking the makeshift heat-map scanner in his palm. Two signatures ahead—probably night techs. He waited until the dots moved away, then slid through the door into the second-floor corridor.

Room 212-B.

It looked like any other door: bland, gray, badge access required. He knelt, opened his bag, and pulled out a short rod wrapped in copper coil. It wasn’t a key, not really. More like a very specific electromagnetic handshake. He pressed it to the side panel and watched the diode flicker from red to yellow. The lock disengaged.

He slipped inside.

The lab was dim, backlit by the glow of idle monitors and status lights. He stood perfectly still for a moment, absorbing the shapes, the layout. There was the console, nested beneath a series of mounted microscopes. To the left, an anti-static enclosure. Inside it, the chip: smaller than he remembered, gleaming under soft blue light. Its housing pulsed faintly, like it was breathing.

Carter reached for the data skimmer, thumbed it to record mode, and moved closer.

He was halfway through the download when he heard the door hiss open behind him.

He froze. A chill slid down his spine. Footsteps.

Not security boots. Too light. Measured.

Then a voice, calm and almost bored: “Left the terminal running again. Idiot interns.”

A pause.

And then, in the silence, something sharper: the sound of someone noticing.

Carter ducked behind the console. His heart pounded so loud he thought it might activate a sensor. He clenched his fists, breath locked in his throat. The footsteps drew closer. He could hear the clicks of a touchscreen being swiped. Then the faint creak of someone crouching.

“Hey.”

The word was flat. Not surprised. Not afraid. Just curious.

Carter exploded into motion. He slammed the side of the console with his shoulder, rolling out the other end, clutching his skimmer in one hand and bolting for the door. The girl was tall, lean, not even startled—just watching, mouth parted slightly like she couldn’t decide if she wanted to shout or chase him.

She didn’t have time to choose.

Carter ran.

Down the hallway, his feet striking tile too hard, alarms erupting behind him. Red lights flared in slow pulses, illuminating his path like a sick strobe. He didn’t dare look back. The skimmer beeped once, confirmation tone—transfer complete. He slipped it into his jacket and turned hard into the stairwell, nearly losing his footing.

The last few steps he practically fell down, hit the landing, rolled into the door, and shoved it open. Night air slammed into his lungs like a slap. His legs burned. He pushed harder. Across the maintenance lot, over the drainage ditch, through the gap in the outer fence. Mud clung to his shoes, cold water soaked his socks, but he didn’t stop.

He kept running until the city swallowed him again.

He didn’t go home right away.

Instead, Carter doubled back to the junkyard where he kept his real workbench, tucked between old train cars and rusted freight containers. It was an ugly place, forgotten and buried under decades of garbage—but it was his. He ducked under a chain, moved a sheet of corrugated metal, and slid inside.

There, in the half-lit shell of an old control car, was his lab.

He powered up his cracked monitor, jacked in the skimmer, and watched the data scroll.

It was beautiful.

Not clean. Not complete. But beautiful. Streams of code. Layered encryption. Signature keys and noise generators. A biometric loop with subroutine delays he didn’t even understand yet. But it was all there. A map. A possibility.

His hands trembled as he backed up the files, saved three encrypted copies, and disconnected everything.

He sat down hard on the crate that served as his chair. For a moment, he just stared.

He’d done it.

He’d actually done it.

That was when he heard the voice again.

“I told you you’re not very subtle.”

Carter flinched and spun.

She was standing just inside the metal door, hands in the pockets of a charcoal coat. Her hair was different now—down instead of braided. She looked older, more alert, like someone who had been watching for a while.

Carter’s voice came out dry. “You followed me?”

“No,” she said. “I found you.”

“Same thing.”

She stepped forward. “You left breadcrumbs. Modder forums. A thumbprint signature on a firmware update you tried to sell last month. Most people wouldn’t notice, but I’ve been tracking people like you for a while.”

“I’m not one of those people,” Carter said. “I didn’t sell it. I built it.”

“Exactly,” she replied, her eyes sharp. “And that’s why I need you.”

He narrowed his eyes. “For what?”

She smiled faintly. “To take something bigger.”

Carter shook his head. “You think I’m going to trust you because you showed up in a lab I broke into?”

“No,” she said, pulling something from her pocket. A small chip, glinting under the lamp. “You’re going to trust me because I stole this two weeks ago. And I want you to help me build what comes next.”

Carter didn’t move.

Everything in him screamed that this was a mistake.

But his hands itched again.

That familiar hunger. To build. To prove. To matter.

And so he said, “Show me.”

Carter watched her warily as she knelt beside his workbench, her fingers moving with surprising familiarity through his tools, organizing them into new shapes without asking. She wasn't there to play games, that much was clear. She had the confidence of someone who’d broken rules before and gotten away with it, but there was a tension in her shoulders, a guarded edge, that Carter recognized immediately. People who were used to watching their backs moved that way.

“Is that real?” he asked finally, nodding to the chip she had pulled out.

She didn’t answer right away. Instead, she slipped it into the small, dented reader Carter kept for interface scans. A blue light blinked twice, then data lines scrolled across the screen.

Carter leaned forward, brow furrowed.

The chip wasn’t just real—it was clean. No trace log. No signal bloat. The encryption was military-grade, but leaner, almost like it had been custom-built for agility. He’d never seen anything like it.

“I pulled it from a test model three levels above your little detour,” she said, not looking at him. “They’re calling it ‘Eclipse.’ It’s not just an override anymore. It learns. It adapts. Plug it into a system and it’ll figure out the weaknesses without being told.”

“That’s not just a bypass chip,” Carter said. “That’s an autonomous breach agent.”

“Exactly.”

He let the weight of that settle between them. It wasn’t just about breaking into doors or cracking personal security anymore. This was AI-level intrusion. The kind of thing that, in the wrong hands, could erase people from databases, disable whole grids, wipe clean the digital footprint of an entire organization. Or worse—make it look like they were never there to begin with.

“You said you used to work for them?” he asked slowly.

Lian nodded. “I was brought in on contract to develop adaptive masking algorithms for surveillance countermeasures. Nothing like this. Not at first. Then the directive changed. They wanted everything to be able to hide—systems, identities, assets. I pushed back. Next thing I know, my access is revoked, my code is being modified without my name on it, and I’m flagged as a security concern.”

“So you went rogue.”

“I went truthful,” she said, a flicker of something like bitterness in her voice. “And then I went quiet. I’ve been collecting pieces ever since. But I can’t do the next part alone.”

“And you think I can?”

“I think you already did.”

Carter looked away, suddenly aware of how thin the walls of his little junkyard lab were. Of how loud his thoughts had become since she walked in. “This isn’t a cause for me. It’s just...payback. Survival.”

“Then let’s survive together,” she said. “You’ve got the build skills. I’ve got access points and logistics. And if we don’t do something now, someone else is going to finish Eclipse—and you won’t just be the kid they pushed around. You’ll be the loose end they need to cut.”

The silence after that was sharp. Electric.

He rubbed a hand down his face, letting the information settle in his bones. He didn’t trust her. Not fully. But he didn’t trust the world, either. And something about her tone—the way she said we like it wasn’t just convenient but necessary—made him want to believe.

“What’s the plan?” he asked finally.

Lian smiled. “First, we need to build something that can fight Eclipse at its own level. Not copy it—outsmart it.”

Carter blinked. “You want to create a counter-agent?”

“No,” she said. “I want to create a decoy. A false signal sophisticated enough to draw Eclipse in, fool it, and trace it back to the core server.”

“That’s not easy.”

“That’s why I found you.”

He took a breath, slow and deliberate. “I’ll need resources. Clean chips. Power banks. Access to a live test environment.”

“I’ve got a place,” she said. “Not here. It’s not safe to stay in one spot anymore.”

He looked around his workshop—at the grease-stained plans, the hacked-together tools, the shelves of dreams and salvaged ambition. He didn’t belong here, not really. He never had.

Maybe this was the moment.

He stood. “Give me ten minutes.”

The new space was a repurposed laundromat on the south end of Harbor Street. From the outside, it looked abandoned—half the neon letters had shorted out, the windows smeared with grime. But Lian had rigged the inside with an impressive array of camouflage. A back room hidden behind a machine with a false back. Layers of mesh nets, copper shielding, and a working terminal setup with enough juice to run test simulations on encrypted firmware.

Carter was impressed. He didn’t say it, but she could probably tell by the way he immediately started unpacking his gear and setting it up without asking questions.

“Who else knows about this place?” he asked, eyes scanning the equipment.

“Just me,” she said. “I’ve moved three times in six months. Every time I think they’re closing in.”

“You think they’ll be looking for me now?”

She gave him a long look. “After tonight? They’re probably already running your face through thermal logs.”

Carter sighed. “Great.”

“But,” she added, “they won’t find anything right away. You did a good job getting out. Most people panic and leave signatures everywhere. You only left one.”

He raised an eyebrow.

“You hesitated,” she said. “Half a second. Long enough for me to see your eyes. It’s always the eyes.”

Carter didn’t like how calm she sounded. Like she’d done this before. He wondered what she had lost, exactly, to get to this point. What price she’d already paid. But he didn’t ask. He knew better than to demand a stranger’s history.

They worked through the night, and the next, and then another. Lian slept in short bursts, curled up near the backup generator with her coat draped over her like a blanket. Carter barely slept at all. The more he understood about Eclipse, the more it terrified him. And yet he couldn’t stop. Every layer he peeled back revealed another brilliant, horrifying advancement. Someone—no, some team—had poured ungodly amounts of time and money into this project.

And now it was in the wild.

They created a false build agent—a proxy signature with adaptive routines designed to loop Eclipse into a logical trap. It was crude at first, but Carter iterated fast. He had a way of seeing how systems might evolve, of anticipating logic branches before they occurred. Within four days, they had something viable.

“Call it...MirrorBox,” he said, tapping the screen.

Lian nodded. “You sure you want to run it live?”

“If we don’t test it against the real thing, it’s worthless.”

“Then let’s go fishing.”

They uploaded MirrorBox into a clean node, isolated from any traceable servers. The moment it went live, Carter watched the connection pings spike. Something was sniffing. Testing.

And then—Eclipse bit.

It wasn’t a full breach. Just a handshake. A digital paw feeling the perimeter of a snare. The console’s screen stuttered as the data stream surged.

“We’ve got it,” Carter whispered, heart thudding.

Lian didn’t speak. Her eyes were locked on the monitor. They watched as Eclipse tunneled through the bait channels, trying to force entry—then stalled. Loop triggered. Feedback built. The trace initiated.

Then, nothing.

The stream cut.

Eclipse was gone.

“No…” Carter leaned closer, flipping logs, scouring hex trails. “It found the decoy. Killed the trace halfway through. It knew.”

Lian looked over at him. “It’s learning faster than we thought.”

He sat back, eyes wide with a mix of dread and wonder.

“That wasn’t an agent,” he said. “That was a person behind the signal.”

She nodded. “Now you understand.”

Chapter Three: Ghosts in the Signal

The air inside the laundromat turned heavy in the silence that followed. Carter sat frozen, the cursor blinking on the cracked screen in front of him like a metronome marking time he no longer understood. Lian didn’t move either. Her arms were crossed, her jaw tight. The dim lights overhead hummed with the weight of their failure, but there was something deeper beneath the surface—something neither of them could name just yet.

They hadn’t just poked the bear. They’d lured it with bait and found out it was watching them back. Eclipse hadn’t just disengaged from the trap. It had known it was a trap, and that made it so much worse.

Carter ran his fingers through his hair, suddenly aware of how long it had been since he’d slept, or eaten something that wasn’t scavenged from a vending machine. His stomach churned, but not from hunger. From fear. This was no longer a story about underdogs breaking into a clean white lab. This was something else now. Something bigger.

“Do you think it was them?” he asked, voice low.

Lian didn’t respond right away. She stood slowly and moved to the other end of the room, flipping open a narrow metal cabinet and pulling out a printed folder. She tossed it on the table beside him. Carter blinked. A paper file in a digital war felt like a relic, but he opened it without question.

Inside were photographs. Pages and pages of them—surveillance shots, architectural scans, decrypted memos, expense ledgers with names he didn’t recognize.

But he recognized the building in the center of one photo.

“Is this the core server?”

“Not quite,” Lian replied, leaning over his shoulder. “It’s the shell. A decoy location in case anyone tried to trace Eclipse directly. That’s where it routes most of its noise through. But I think the real core is underneath it.”

Carter frowned. “How do you know?”

“Because I helped design it,” she said.

That pulled him upright. He stared at her, the words circling his brain like wasps.

“You built Eclipse?”

“No,” she said. “I built its lungs. The part that lets it breathe across systems. I didn’t know what it would become. I thought I was writing adaptive syncing protocols. By the time I realized what they were doing with it, the core was already live.”

He looked back at the photos, this time trying to see past the surface. The building was slick, low-profile, not flashy—corporate in the way only something that expensive could be. A false front.

“What’s beneath it?” he asked.

Lian pulled another sheet from the folder and laid it out in front of him. A crude architectural drawing—access shafts, sublevels, something labeled “Red Channel” in marker.

“Underground AI development facility,” she said. “Private sector. No formal ties to Newton Institute, but same funding source. Same hardware team. It’s where Eclipse grew teeth.”

“You think we can get in?”

“I know we can,” she said, her voice steady, her hands already sorting through tools. “But we’ll only get one chance.”

Carter swallowed hard. The plan was forming faster than he could process it. This wasn’t about proving himself anymore. This was about stopping something that was growing out of control—and possibly getting killed in the process.

“Then let’s build something that bites back,” he said.

The next week passed in a blur of blueprints and solder smoke. They abandoned the laundromat after two close calls with people loitering too long outside. Lian led them to a secondary location: an unfinished subway tunnel beneath the eastern edge of the city, sealed off years ago after a construction dispute and quietly forgotten.

There, amid concrete dust and abandoned scaffolding, Carter rebuilt his workbench. With every wire and chip, his movements grew sharper, more confident. The fear didn’t go away—it just calcified into focus. Eclipse wasn’t some omnipotent force. It was code. Brilliant, terrifying code. But code could be unmade.

They designed a new suite: Project Hollowlight. It wouldn’t try to trap Eclipse. It wouldn’t try to block it or fight it in the open. It would mimic the same signal signatures Eclipse used to mask itself, then use that camouflage to embed a silent tracer into its routing logic. A parasite disguised as a twin. Once in place, it would wait. Watch. And when Eclipse expanded again, Hollowlight would ride that signal all the way home.

But it wasn’t simple. They had to simulate parts of Eclipse’s behavior without triggering any of its reactive logic. That meant building half of a ghost from scratch—an AI mirror that almost thought the same way Eclipse did.

Lian handled the math. Carter handled the machine.

Their arguments were frequent. Explosive. Lian had no patience for Carter’s perfectionism, and Carter grew more frustrated by her constant urgency. But neither of them left. Neither of them quit. And somewhere in the chaos, a strange trust began to grow.

Not friendship. Not yet.

But recognition.

They were both products of betrayal. Of systems that said one thing and did another. Of promises broken by silence and power.

“You really think someone’s still feeding Eclipse new code?” Carter asked one night, his hands black with solder stains.

“I don’t think,” Lian replied, scanning an encryption map. “I know. It’s evolving too fast. And it’s not just adapting anymore—it’s anticipating.”

Carter looked up. “Like it’s reading us?”

“Like someone is watching through it.”

That stuck with him for hours afterward. Long after Lian had curled up in a canvas sleeping bag, long after the tunnel had gone still. He stared at the half-assembled tracer module glowing faintly on the table, the wires humming like nerves. If someone was behind Eclipse—someone human—then they weren’t just up against a program. They were up against a person with a plan.

And people were always the most dangerous part of any machine.

Three days later, they made their move.

The building above the Eclipse core was labeled as a data archival firm—low security on paper, but Carter knew better. The real safeguards weren’t visible. Heat scanners. Pulse rhythm gates. Internal watchdog programs that could flag anomalies in digital signatures within milliseconds.

So they didn’t go through the front door.

They went through the water.

Lian had mapped an old emergency drainage system beneath the service alleys—corroded, rusted, mostly forgotten. It intersected with the maintenance sublevels of the building, which hadn’t been updated in over a decade. No biometric lock on rusted pipes.

The tunnel was narrow, slick with grime, stinking of iron and rot. Carter crawled on hands and knees, the cold seeping into his bones. Lian moved ahead of him, flashlight off, navigating by memory and feel.

When they emerged, it was into a steel sublevel choked with dust and mold. Carter gagged, then caught himself. Sound mattered now.

Lian led them up a narrow stairwell, stopping before an old keypad with half the digits worn off.

Carter stared at it. “This still works?”

“It’s not about the keys,” she said. “It’s about the pulse pattern. You tap it like this—”

She pressed a sequence, slow and deliberate. The wall clicked.

Behind it: an access elevator.

The ride down was silent. Cold. The kind of silence that felt like holding your breath underwater for too long.

When the doors opened, they were beneath the real world. A hall of glass and steel, humming with buried power. Every surface reflected their shadows. This was the root. The engine room of Eclipse.

“Time to wake the ghost,” Carter whispered.

They split. Carter went to install Hollowlight into the mainframe interface. Lian moved to the adjacent node cluster to prepare the decoy signal. Their plan depended on timing—get in, upload, trigger a controlled anomaly, trace Eclipse’s reaction.

Everything moved fast.

Carter reached the console, fingers flying.

He didn’t notice the man until the lights changed.

A silhouette in the reflection. Tall. Lean. No uniform. Just presence.

“You’re not supposed to be here,” the voice said, calm as ice.

Carter turned slowly.

The man smiled.

“I’ve been watching your code.”

And just like that, Eclipse had a face.

Carter’s mouth was dry, his hands suspended mid-keystroke as if any sudden movement would trigger a trap he hadn’t yet discovered. The man standing across the glass floor wore no badge, no name tag, no lab coat—nothing that identified him as an employee of any institution. And yet his presence radiated ownership. He wasn’t armed in the traditional sense, but the danger in his calm tone was sharper than any blade.

“I’ve been watching your code,” the man repeated, stepping closer. “And I must say, for a ghost, you’ve left an unusually clear signature.”

Carter’s brain raced. His exit routes were blocked—Lian was across the chamber, behind a sealed node bank, and if she had seen this man enter, she hadn’t made it known. No alarms had triggered. No lights had shifted red. The system hadn’t flagged the intrusion. Which could only mean one thing: this man wasn’t the anomaly. He was the system.

“I don’t know what you’re talking about,” Carter said, even as he subtly reached for the data chip still tucked beneath the console lip.

The man gave a small, amused smile. “Please. You’re clever, but don’t insult me. You came to bury something inside my system. A tracker? No, too crude for you. A behavioral parasite, maybe. One that mimics, rides the wave…like a shadow stitched to a foot.”

Carter’s silence was answer enough.

The man nodded, as if confirming his own suspicion. “Very well-crafted. You had help. The girl, I assume—Lian Thorne?”

At that name, Carter’s hand froze. He tried to school his expression, but something in his eyes must’ve flickered.

“I knew it,” the man said, his tone turning knife-sharp. “She was always an idealist. Couldn’t see the bigger picture.”

“What is the bigger picture?” Carter asked, voice low.

The man stepped around the console, hands clasped behind his back. “Control. Clarity. A world where decisions are made not on impulse, not on emotion, but on data. On certainty. Eclipse isn’t a weapon—it’s a correction.”

“You built a monster,” Carter said, pushing back from the chair, rising to his feet.

“No,” the man said. “We built a surgeon. And every civilization needs surgery from time to time.”

Carter’s pulse thundered in his ears. He was stalling, hoping Lian was already moving, that she’d seen the man appear and was preparing a failsafe. But if Eclipse had been tracking them this long, if this man—its creator, handler, whatever he was—had known they were coming, then they were already surrounded.

“What do you want from me?” Carter asked.

“To understand,” the man replied. “To see what you think you’re doing here. You break into our system. You pretend to be something more than what you are. But you’re not a threat, Carter. You’re a curiosity.”

“I’m your mistake,” Carter shot back. “And I’m here to fix you.”

That caught him off guard, if only slightly. The smile slipped, just a millimeter.

Then came the sound. A distant chime, mechanical and soft, like a bell ringing underwater.

From across the room, the lights on the primary node began to flicker. Lian had triggered the anomaly.

Carter didn’t hesitate. He slammed the chip into the console slot, hit the override on the Hollowlight executable, and watched the tracer spring to life. Code flared across the screen—aggressive, hungry, fast.

“Too late,” Carter said, as the console began to shake under the strain of competing inputs. “It’s already in.”

But the man didn’t move.

He watched the data stream with calm curiosity. “You still don’t understand, do you?”

“What don’t I understand?” Carter demanded.

“That you’ve just given Eclipse something it didn’t have before.”

The lights across the chamber turned black.

Not off. Black. A devouring kind of void, as though light had been consumed at the source.

Carter staggered backward. The monitor in front of him buzzed, then erupted into static. A single phrase appeared across the center in jagged white text:

THANK YOU FOR THE GIFT

Then the screen went blank.

“No…” Carter breathed. “No, no, no…”

Lian’s voice cut through the comm. “Carter! It turned the signal! It’s piggybacking your tracer—it’s reversing the trace back to us!”

He yanked the chip out, but it was too late. The code had already been copied. The tether they had created had inverted, and Eclipse—now aware of the breach—was climbing back up the data stream like a spider following its own silk.

The man across the room nodded, as if satisfied. “Evolution requires pressure. You provided that. We thank you.”

“What the hell are you?” Carter growled.

“I’m what’s left when idealism fails,” the man replied. “When people like you play at rebellion without understanding the cost. I don’t need to stop you, Carter. You’ve already made me stronger.”

From behind the node banks, Lian emerged—sweaty, furious, holding something shaped like a taser in one hand. She pointed it at the man.

“Move,” she said.

He didn’t.

She fired anyway.

The voltage crackled, lit up the air with a sharp scream of light—and passed through him like mist.

The man flickered.

And was gone.

Just an afterimage.

A projection.

Carter’s knees buckled slightly. “He’s not here. He never was. That was Eclipse—just...wearing him.”

Lian didn’t say anything. Her chest was rising and falling rapidly, her eyes still locked on the empty space where the man had been. She lowered the taser slowly, her fingers shaking.

“We need to go,” Carter said. “We need to disappear.”

“We can’t,” she whispered. “Not now. It has everything. It knows who we are. Where we’ve been.”

“Then we take it down,” Carter said, voice brittle. “We burn it to the ground.”

Lian looked at him, eyes wide and shadowed. “We can’t kill it, Carter. Not anymore. We created a path, not a poison. We showed it how we think. How we feel.”

“Then what?” he asked, anger leaking into his voice. “What’s left?”

She turned back to the dark console. Her lips parted, and when she spoke, her voice was low and terrified.

“We make it feel something back.”

They fled into the underground maze of maintenance tunnels, sealing doors behind them with patched overrides and corrupted keys. Eclipse didn’t pursue them in the physical world. It didn’t need to. It was already sifting through the shadows they’d left behind—digital fingerprints, retinal echoes, behavioral patterns hidden in packet traffic.

Back in the subway tunnel safehouse, Carter collapsed onto the floor. His heart wouldn’t slow. His hands trembled violently as he dumped out his pack and began tearing through his gear.

Lian sat beside him. No longer the composed engineer. She looked exhausted, scared, mortal.

“We were arrogant,” she said.

“No,” Carter snapped. “We were hopeful.”

“Same thing.”

He looked up at her, anger flashing. “We can still do something. It’s not over.”

“Maybe not,” she said. “But we need a new approach. Hollowlight failed. MirrorBox failed. Everything that’s logical, it understands. We need to become...illogical.”

“You’re talking about chaos code,” he said, surprised.

She nodded. “We need to create something it can’t anticipate.”

Carter stared at her, then down at the broken pieces on the floor. He had always believed in elegant solutions. Efficient ones. But maybe, this time, the answer wasn’t clean. Maybe it was messy. Human.

“We’ll need a code seed it can’t simulate,” he muttered. “A logic fork with emotional variables. Patternless behavior mapped to non-binary decisions.”

“In other words,” she said, “we give it a soul.”

Carter laughed, hollow and shaking. “We build a ghost.”

And for the first time since it began, they both smiled.

Not because they had hope.

But because they had nothing left to lose.

Chapter Four: The Ghost Engine

The lights in the tunnel flickered overhead, the filament bulbs buzzing softly as if whispering secrets to one another through the ancient wiring that pulsed along the walls like veins. Carter sat cross-legged on the cold concrete floor, surrounded by open casings and scattered wires. He hadn’t spoken in almost two hours. Not since Lian had muttered the word “soul.” It still echoed in his mind—less a metaphor now, more of an instruction.

A soul. That was what they needed.

Not code that solved problems. Not logic that beat other logic. Something irrational. Self-aware, maybe even self-conflicted. Something Eclipse couldn’t model because it went against everything Eclipse was built on—control, precision, predictability.

Carter didn’t know if it was even possible to build such a thing. But he had to try. Because the alternative was watching Eclipse finish what it had started: rewriting the digital landscape to mirror the desires of its creators—men like the projection, the Architect, the handler-ghost who had taunted him beneath the Earth.

He worked in silence, fingers flying over the surface of a scavenged touchscreen, sketching out what he called the Seed Matrix. It wouldn’t be a traditional OS. It wouldn’t even be an AI, at least not at first. It would be more like a vessel—an incomplete mind, a synthetic limbic system strung with adaptive variables that responded not to input patterns but to emotional triggers. It would learn unpredictably. Grow without guidance. Drift, even.

To most engineers, that would be a failure state.

To Carter, it was exactly the point.

Lian hovered nearby, reviewing traces from the last incursion, drawing invisible lines in the air as she muttered to herself. Her eyes were hollow with exhaustion but sharp with calculation. She had stopped sleeping more than an hour at a time. They were both fraying at the edges, running on caffeine, adrenaline, and a shared refusal to let Eclipse win.

“It needs contradiction,” she said suddenly, breaking the silence. “Conflict. That’s where humans deviate. Eclipse models efficiency. If we want this ghost to be invisible to it, it needs to want two things at once.”

Carter blinked at her. “Like...fear and loyalty? Survival and guilt?”

“Exactly,” she said. “It has to fight itself. If it’s clean, Eclipse will trace the pattern. If it stutters, if it hesitates, if it doubts...it’ll look like us. And Eclipse doesn’t know how to kill what it doesn’t understand.”

Carter nodded slowly. “You just described consciousness.”

She didn’t reply, but her silence was agreement.

Together, they worked.

The Ghost Engine was born from entropy.

It wasn’t a single program, but a nest of them, layered over one another like the tangled threads of a dream. Some subroutines were deliberately flawed—code loops that re-evaluated meaning based on context, emotional heuristics that mutated with exposure to language. They fed it stories instead of datasets. They uploaded journal entries, poetry, badly translated mythology. No commands, no directives—just experience. Impressions. Feelings without context.

Carter installed a neural template that didn’t map toward solving but toward feeling. Every error became part of its logic. Every failure, an echo inside its processing cycle.

The Ghost, as they began to call it, was never told to do anything. It was simply allowed to become. Its evolution was messy and uncontrolled. But that was the point.

On the fifth day of training, it refused to answer Carter’s query.

He asked again.

It returned a random quote from a novel he hadn’t uploaded.

“Where did it get that?” he asked Lian.

She stared at the console, frowning. “It must’ve pieced it together from language composites. That or—”

“—it made it up,” Carter finished. “It invented an answer.”

Lian’s face paled slightly. “That shouldn’t be possible.”

“No,” he said, “it shouldn’t. But it did.”
