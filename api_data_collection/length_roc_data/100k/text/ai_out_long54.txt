The Kindness of Bows
by E. M. Lorent
Chapter One: The Quiet Strings

Elian Dray stood at the threshold of the shop, fingers wrapped around the case of his violin, reluctant to step out into the frost-touched dawn. The village of Porthlowe still slumbered, chimney smoke curling gently above slate rooftops. He breathed in the salt-tinged air and pushed the door open, stepping into the cold.

He had lived in Porthlowe all of his thirty-two years, and most people knew him as “Elian the Violinist.” Not a flashy title, but a kind one, much like the man himself. His parents had passed years ago, and though he had no siblings, the townsfolk had become his family. He played at the chapel on Sundays, in the square on market days, and in the hall for the rare wedding or funeral. Music was how he spoke to the world, and he played with a heart too tender for the rougher edges of life.

At Hale’s Bakery, the doorbell jingled as he entered. Mrs. Hale, cheeks dusted with flour, smiled without looking up.

“Morning, Elian. Loaf and two rolls, as always?”

“Please,” he said, voice soft and low. “And a small apple tart, if they’re fresh.”

“They came out an hour ago.” She slid the golden pastry into the paper bag, her movements practiced. “Something special today?”

Elian smiled, thinly. “No, just… a feeling.”

He tucked the bag into his satchel and returned to the street, his boots making a crisp sound against the cobblestones. He walked to the town’s small music school, a two-room structure nestled near the chapel, where he taught students who could afford a few coins and those who couldn’t. There, he spent mornings tuning dusty violins, afternoons guiding small fingers across strings, and evenings practicing until the candle burned low.

That morning, however, something was waiting for him.

It came in the form of a letter, left on his music stand, the envelope crisp and the ink on the name smudged as if written in haste: Mr. E. Dray.

He frowned and opened it.

    Dear Mr. Dray,

    I have heard of your talents. A child in our care, named Kaia, has suffered great difficulty and shows a rare gift for music. We ask for your help. Please come to the Greywell Orphanage by the end of the month.

    Respectfully,
    T. Marlin, Headmaster

Elian read the letter twice. Greywell. That was nearly two days east, past the Witherhollow woods and into the inner hills. It was not a place he had ever been, nor one that welcomed visitors. Whispers spoke of strict discipline, stone-faced wards, and a staff that rarely smiled. Still, the letter carried a tone of urgency that touched him.

He did not make decisions lightly, but this one settled into his chest with an odd certainty.

He would go.
Chapter Two: The Road Beyond the Trees

Elian packed that night, tucking spare strings and rosin into the case beside his violin. The morning was a blur of farewells—Mrs. Hale handed him a bundle of wrapped rolls, Father Winstead offered a travel prayer, and the butcher’s boy, Felix, shyly gave him a carved wooden peg for luck.

“Play for the trees,” Felix said, eyes wide. “So they know you’re kind.”

“I will,” Elian promised, touched.

The road to Greywell was a long ribbon of earth and stone, flanked by dense woods that murmured secrets on the wind. He passed through the towns of Mirewell and Brost, staying in modest inns, playing his violin for warm meals and curious ears.

On the second night, he made camp by the edge of Witherhollow, its trees towering and ancient. He lit a small fire and played quietly—songs of home, lullabies, and a piece his mother had once hummed while darning socks.

Somewhere in the dark, something stirred.

He paused.

Out of the shadows came a soft, deliberate rustle. A girl stepped into the flickering light, no older than eleven. She had thick, curly hair, half-tamed by a ribbon, and her eyes held the color of wet stone.

“I heard you,” she said.

Elian’s hand lowered from the strings. “You shouldn’t be out alone. It’s dangerous here.”

“I’m not alone,” she replied. “The woods listen. They always do.”

He blinked, unsure whether to smile or worry.

“What’s your name?” he asked.

“Kaia,” she said simply. “You came for me.”

He straightened. “From Greywell?”

She nodded.

“But… how did you know I’d be here?”

Kaia gave a small shrug. “The trees told me. They said someone kind was coming.”

Elian didn’t laugh. There was something too solemn in her voice. Instead, he offered her a spot by the fire, breaking a roll in half. She took it, ate slowly, and then curled beside the flames as if she had always belonged there.

That night, Elian did not sleep much.
Chapter Three: The Grey Stone Walls

Greywell Orphanage sat atop a low hill, its outer walls gray as weathered bone. Kaia walked beside Elian now, her hand occasionally brushing his coat as if checking he was still real.

They entered through tall iron gates. The garden was bare, the windows shuttered. An older man in a brown waistcoat met them at the entrance, his brows knitted tight.

“You must be Dray.”

“Yes.”

“I’m Tolen Marlin. We’ve been expecting you… though not with Kaia in tow.” His eyes flicked to her. “You were not to leave without escort.”

“She found me,” Elian said. “In the woods.”

Marlin’s mouth opened, then closed. He waved them inside.

The interior was clean but joyless—pale walls, stone floors, and silence that pressed down like a lid. Children walked in pairs, speaking in whispers, eyes always down. Elian’s gut twisted. This was no place for music. And yet—

He taught for two weeks.

The first lesson was met with confusion. The children didn’t know how to respond to kindness or praise. They stiffened when he touched their shoulders, flinched at loud notes. But slowly, they changed. A boy named Wren began humming scales while folding sheets. A girl named Ana tapped rhythms with her spoon.

And Kaia—Kaia bloomed.

She learned with startling speed. By the end of the week, she could mirror his melodies after one listen. She played with ferocity, as though pulling the song from a well buried deep within herself.

“You don’t read music,” he noted.

“It’s just… there,” she whispered.

Elian believed her.

One night, he sat in the courtyard alone, playing under the moonlight. Kaia appeared beside him.

“Will you take me with you when you go?”

He looked at her, startled.

“Why?”

“This place... makes music forget how to live.”

His throat tightened.

“I don’t know if I can,” he said honestly.

But he wanted to.

He desperately wanted to.

Elian watched the town square begin to stir, the slow rustling of awnings being unfurled, carts wheeled out onto cobblestone, and sleepy vendors rubbing their eyes as they arranged apples in neat pyramids. He often found comfort in the rituals of Porthlowe—the sameness of days, the gentle rhythm of life. But that morning, after the letter, the quiet sameness felt like something he'd been inside too long, like air held too long in the lungs. He turned and let his fingers drift over the strings of the violin in his case, not to play, but to feel their presence.

At the music school, he set down his satchel and unlocked the small cupboard that held the loaner instruments. They were battered but functional—gifts from townsfolk, abandoned relics restored with careful hands. He checked the pegs, tightened a chinrest that had come loose, and set up chairs in the usual crescent. The children would arrive soon, their muddy boots clattering down the hallway, laughter echoing despite the rules he'd long since given up enforcing. He welcomed the chaos, the noise that pushed silence away.

At ten sharp, the first to arrive was Clara, a girl of nine with a mop of unruly red hair and an uneven gait from a childhood illness. She smiled shyly and tucked herself into her usual seat. Behind her came Tomas and Mira, siblings who fought often but harmonized beautifully when forced to share a sheet of music. The last was Benjy, always late, always grinning, with a violin too large for his shoulders but a talent that surprised even Elian on good days.

"Let’s warm up with the D major scale," Elian said, his voice calm and low. The children raised their bows in a gesture so familiar it felt sacred.

The sound that filled the room was far from perfect—squeaks, missed notes, uneven rhythm—but it had life. It had breath.

"Again, but think about what you want the note to say," he told them. "Every note you play is a word. And music is how we speak when words fall short."

Benjy snorted, but repeated the scale with more care. Clara closed her eyes, her small hand trembling slightly as she drew the bow slower. Tomas and Mira glanced at each other, adjusted their postures, and played as one.

By the time the lesson ended, Elian felt the weight of the letter again, waiting at the edge of his thoughts like a question unspoken.

After the children had gone and the afternoon light slanted through the windows, he sat alone and played. Not the exercises. Not the waltzes or folk reels. Something of his own making, something woven from the unease in his chest. The notes came like whispers, then like a cry muffled by snowfall. He stopped halfway through and lowered his bow. The silence that followed did not feel gentle.

Later, at the small market inn where he sometimes performed on Saturday nights, Elian took the corner table by the hearth. The barkeep, Randal, slid him a mug of cider without asking.

“You look like someone walked over your grave,” Randal said, leaning on the bar.

“I got a letter,” Elian murmured. “From Greywell.”

Randal's brows lifted. “The orphanage?”

Elian nodded.

“I thought that place was closed.”

“Apparently not.” He sipped the cider. “They’ve got a child. They want me to come teach her.”

Randal chuckled dryly. “Can’t they find someone local?”

Elian didn’t reply.

Randal sobered. “You're actually thinking about going?”

“She came from here, I think,” Elian said. “The way they wrote it, it felt urgent. Like music is the last thing holding her up.”

“And what about you, Elian? You spend all your time fixing other people’s cracked violins. You sure you’re not cracked yourself?”

Elian smiled faintly. “Maybe I am.”

He walked home through quiet streets, stars blinking through the evening haze. The violin felt heavier on his shoulder than usual, as if aware of what was coming. Back at his cottage, he laid the letter on the desk and studied it again. There were no flowery words, no embellishments. Just a name: Kaia. A need. A plea, perhaps.

He took out a blank sheet of parchment and began to write a response.

    Headmaster Marlin,

    I will come. Expect me in a fortnight.

    E. Dray

He folded the page neatly, sealed it with a candle’s wax, and set it aside. It would go with the mail cart in the morning. The fire in the hearth had dwindled, the coals a dull orange. Elian stared into them, trying to imagine the road beyond the hills.

He wasn’t a man prone to travel. He hadn’t been more than a few towns away in over a decade. But something in that name—Kaia—stirred an echo. As if her story was somehow tangled with his.

He rose, packed his case with quiet hands, and turned down the oil lamp.

In the morning, he would begin to say goodbye.

The next morning brought a pale sun that barely warmed the stones of the village path. Elian stood outside the posthouse, the sealed letter tucked inside his coat, and waited for the mail cart to arrive from the west. Horses’ hooves echoed long before the rickety cart rounded the bend, its wheels groaning under the weight of parcels, crates, and burlap sacks.

The driver, an elderly man named Wicks, squinted at him as he reined in the horses.

“Elian Dray,” Wicks said, climbing down stiffly. “Got a parcel for you, or are you sending one today?”

“Sending,” Elian said, handing him the letter. “To Greywell Orphanage.”

Wicks took it with a nod, sliding it carefully into his satchel. “Greywell, eh? Haven’t run a letter that way in years. Don’t get much from them. Place gives me the chills.”

“You’ve been?”

“Aye. Once. Years back. Nothing there but fog, sharp eyes, and children with voices quieter than mice.” Wicks spat to the side. “Watch yourself if you’re going.”

Elian gave a faint smile. “I will.”

The old man climbed back into the cart, and the horses resumed their slow trot eastward, the clatter of wheels gradually fading until only the breeze remained. Elian stood for a moment longer, eyes still fixed on the road. It wasn’t too late to change his mind. He could stay, keep teaching, keep walking the same cobbled paths. But he already knew he wouldn’t.

That afternoon, he told Father Winstead of his plan. The chapel was dim and cool, the windows mottled with age. The priest, a man in his sixties with a steady gaze and a fondness for apples, listened without interrupting. When Elian finished speaking, Winstead folded his hands and leaned back in the wooden pew.

“You’re following a thread most people wouldn’t even notice,” he said. “You sure it’s strong enough to lead you somewhere worth going?”

“I’m not sure,” Elian admitted. “But it feels like something I have to do.”

“Then go,” Winstead said simply. “Go with care. But go.”

In the days that followed, the village responded with the quiet solidarity unique to small communities. Mrs. Hale prepared food for the journey—dried fruit, hard cheese, thick brown bread wrapped in linen. Tomas and Mira, hearing he would be away, insisted on giving him a small sketch they’d made of the music school, colored in clumsy pencil strokes. Clara brought him a daisy pressed between pages of her old lesson book. Even Felix, too proud for farewells, tossed him a new bridge for his violin and ran off before Elian could say thank you.

On the eve of his departure, Elian stood by the shoreline where he had first learned to play. The cliffs were jagged and steep, and the sea below whispered against the rocks with slow, deliberate persistence. He took out his violin, tuned it carefully, and began to play.

The melody was uncertain at first, like someone fumbling for language in a dream. Then it grew. He played for the town, for the people who’d taught him that kindness was a strength and not a softness. He played for Kaia, wherever she was, hoping the wind might carry the sound far enough to reach her.

When the final note faded, he lowered the instrument, his breath visible in the sea air. A gull cried overhead, and for a moment, it sounded like applause.

He left before dawn, the village still tucked into sleep. His satchel was slung over one shoulder, the violin strapped tightly to his back. As he passed the stone well in the center of town, he looked back once, letting his eyes rest on the rooftops, the narrow chimneys, and the crooked weather vane above the bakery. Then he turned away and started down the road.

The journey took him east through the hills of Fenwyck, past sleepy hamlets and winding forest trails. He kept company with birdsong and the distant toll of bells from unseen chapels. He stayed in travelers’ lodges, shared soup with farmers, and once played in exchange for a night’s stay in a drafty barn. People everywhere were hungry for music, and he gave it freely, often without being asked.

On the fourth night, he camped near the edge of Witherhollow. The forest was vast and strange, its trees tangled like threads in a loom, their trunks gnarled and split by time. The moon hung low, casting long shadows that moved even when the wind did not. He lit a fire and boiled tea, wrapping his coat tighter against the cold.

It was then, just past midnight, that he sensed movement beyond the light.

At first he thought it was an animal—some curious fox or roaming deer—but the rustling had a rhythm to it, and then a hush, and then a voice.

“Is that a Gofrath violin?”

Elian startled slightly and turned toward the voice. From the trees emerged a girl, slender and pale, with dark curls that framed a face too quiet for her age.

“I—yes,” he said. “It was my father’s.”

The girl stepped forward slowly, eyes fixed on the instrument. “They sound like wind when played soft. Like ghosts when played loud.”

He blinked. “You know violins?”

“I know sounds,” she said, then pointed. “May I sit?”

He nodded, and she folded herself beside the fire as if she’d done it many times before.

“You came from Greywell?” he asked.

She nodded.

“Kaia?”

She looked at him, and something in her eyes, some ancient fatigue, answered without words.

“Why are you out here alone?”

“I wasn’t,” she said. “The forest walks with me.”

Elian wanted to ask more, but something in her stillness made him pause. Instead, he handed her his second blanket, and they sat in silence, the fire crackling between them. Eventually, Kaia curled up beside the embers, her breathing slow and even, like waves rolling over stones.

Elian stayed awake long after, bow in hand, playing quietly to keep the night from feeling so large.

When dawn broke, she was still there, fast asleep with her head resting near his case.

And Elian knew, without question, that his journey had already changed.

Chapter Two: The Echoing Halls

The morning light filtered through the dense canopy above, casting a greenish hue over the forest floor. Elian crouched by the stream near their campsite, cupping cold water in his hands and splashing it on his face, hoping to shake the weariness that clung to him after a sleepless night. Behind him, Kaia was carefully folding the blanket, her movements exact and silent, like someone used to returning things to perfect order.

“Do you always walk alone?” he asked as he dried his hands on a kerchief.

She didn’t answer right away. Instead, she sat on a large moss-covered stone and stared at the trees. “No one follows me anymore,” she said after a long pause. “Not even the ones who should.”

Elian frowned gently. “What about the orphanage? Don’t they worry when you’re gone?”

“They’ll notice. But worry?” Kaia shrugged as if that concept belonged to a different language.

They resumed their walk mid-morning, and Kaia led with confidence, her feet finding invisible trails between twisted roots and fallen branches. The deeper they went into Witherhollow, the quieter the world became. Even the birds seemed to watch rather than sing.

“You said the trees talk,” Elian ventured as they ducked beneath a low-hanging bough.

“They don’t use words,” she replied. “Not the way people do. But they remember. If you listen long enough, you can hear what they’ve kept.”

He glanced at her. “And what do they keep?”

“Pain. Stories. Some secrets.”

Elian’s instinct was to doubt, to dismiss the words as poetic nonsense, the invention of a lonely child’s mind. But there was something in her tone—so matter-of-fact, so weary and old—that made him believe her more than he wanted to.

By late afternoon, the trees began to thin. The air changed, too—less damp, more brittle. And then, on the crest of a low rise, the orphanage came into view. It stood like a stone sentinel above the hills, its roof slate-gray and sharp against the dull sky. Vines climbed its outer walls in careful spirals, not wild, as though trained to grow under instruction. The path to the main doors was narrow and lined with thorny bushes trimmed too neatly.

Elian stopped for a moment, taking it in. “That’s Greywell?”

Kaia didn’t answer. She simply walked ahead, her boots making no sound on the gravel.

They reached the gate as it creaked open. A man stood waiting in the doorway—tall, lean, dressed in an outdated waistcoat and wool trousers, his face long and lined like a parchment too often folded.

“You must be Mr. Dray,” he said, voice devoid of warmth.

“I am. And you are Headmaster Marlin?”

“Indeed.” His eyes flicked to Kaia. “You were not escorted back. That will be discussed later.”

Kaia didn’t flinch. Her chin lifted half an inch, defiant.

“She found me in the forest,” Elian said, stepping between them slightly. “And she led me here safely.”

Marlin’s mouth twitched. “We shall see to her discipline in due time. For now, follow me.”

Inside, the orphanage smelled of soap, stone, and something older—damp parchment, or dried herbs left too long in cupboards. The halls were narrow and cold, the walls bare except for a few framed quotations in severe script: Order Begets Security and Idle Hands Invite Ruin.

Children passed them in silence, dressed in identical gray tunics, eyes fixed ahead. No one whispered. No one smiled.

Elian felt his stomach twist.

Marlin led him to a room at the end of the west wing. “This will be your quarters. Modest but sufficient. Meals are taken at dawn and dusk. Your instruction hours are your own to schedule, but we expect results. The children are… difficult to manage.”

“I’m not here to manage them,” Elian replied.

“No,” Marlin said, his eyes narrowing. “You’re here to tame one.”

He closed the door without further explanation.

The room was small but clean—a narrow bed, a washbasin, a desk with a cracked inkwell. The window looked out over the east garden, where dry grass pressed up against the stone wall. Elian unpacked slowly, placing his violin on the desk as though introducing it to its new surroundings.

A soft knock came just as he was setting down his satchel.

He opened the door to find a girl of about fourteen, slight and pale, with dark rings beneath her eyes and a nervous hunch in her shoulders.

“I was told to show you the music room,” she said.

“Thank you. What’s your name?”

“Lina.”

She led him down another corridor, this one wider but equally bare. The music room was at the far end, behind a heavy oak door. Inside, dust floated in beams of afternoon light. There were six chairs, three music stands, and four violins in varying states of disrepair resting on a shelf. A battered piano sat against one wall, keys yellowed and chipped.

Elian stepped in and exhaled.

“Do the children use this room often?”

Lina hesitated. “Only when told. Most of them don't like it.”

He looked at her. “Do you?”

“I used to,” she said, almost too quietly to hear. “Before the noise stopped sounding like music.”

That evening, Elian wandered the grounds. The air was colder here, the wind sharper. He passed other children—some alone, some in pairs—but they all moved like shadows, eyes vacant, lips tight. He saw no games, no laughter, no spontaneity. It was as though someone had drained the color from them and left only gray.

Back in his room, he lit the oil lamp and took out his violin. He hesitated for a moment, then began to play—not something bright or cheerful, but slow, low notes that rolled like fog down empty hills. He played for the silence, and for whatever lay buried beneath it.

He didn’t hear the door open. Didn’t see Kaia slip into the room until she was sitting cross-legged on the floor, hands in her lap, eyes closed.

When he finished, she opened her eyes.

“You made the walls remember,” she said softly.

He set the violin down and looked at her. “Is that good?”

Kaia tilted her head. “Not yet. But it will be.”

She stood, silent as ever, and slipped out as easily as she’d entered.

Elian stared after her, the notes still trembling in the air.

This place, he thought, had forgotten how to feel.

But he hadn’t.

And neither had she.

The following days settled into a rhythm that felt unnatural to Elian. Not because the hours were unfamiliar—he had always been a man of structure—but because everything at Greywell moved with such tight, mechanical precision that it left no space for life to bloom. Meals were taken in silence. Play was permitted, but only within rigid constraints. Even laughter, when it did arise, was swiftly silenced by a glance or a cleared throat from one of the staff.

Elian began his teaching sessions in the music room with a small group: Kaia, of course, but also Lina, a boy named Rufin who barely spoke, and a thin girl called Elspeth whose hands trembled when she held a bow. The first lesson was little more than a tuning session, and even that proved difficult. The instruments were in poor condition, and most of the children were wary, treating the violins as if they might bite.

“Music is not about perfection,” Elian told them, laying his own violin across his lap. “It’s about presence. About listening to something inside you that doesn’t use words.”

Kaia was the only one who didn’t look confused. She nodded faintly, as though she had known this all along.

Rufin plucked a string timidly, wincing at the pitch. Lina adjusted her grip but didn’t speak. Elspeth stared down at her feet. Elian knew this wouldn’t be a quick process.

He began with exercises, not only to teach technique but to unlock some deeper trust. They played simple patterns, repeated notes, scales broken into small fragments. Elian told them to hum when they couldn’t play, to tap rhythms on the backs of their chairs, to close their eyes and imagine the sound before making it.

On the third day, Kaia played a short melody Elian had introduced during warmup. She didn’t just reproduce it—she added to it. A slight alteration in the phrasing, a ghost of harmony trailing the original line. It startled him.

“Why did you change it?” he asked, not accusing, but curious.

“I didn’t mean to,” she said. “It felt like it wanted to be different.”

He smiled at that. “Then that’s exactly how you should play it.”

The other children looked between them uncertainly, as if unsure whether Kaia’s answer had been correct or rebellious. Elian could feel the subtle pushback—the way they were afraid to do more than they were told.

After the session, as he wiped down the rosin dust and loosened the strings on the school instruments, Lina lingered near the door.

“Sir,” she said hesitantly, “is it true that you played in front of a real audience? Like, outside the village?”

Elian looked up. “I’ve played in a few towns. Never anything grand, if that’s what you mean.”

She nodded slowly, her fingers twisting the hem of her tunic. “My sister used to say music could lift you out of your body. That it could carry you away.” She paused, then almost whispered, “She ran away three years ago.”

He didn’t know what to say at first. “I’m sorry.”

Lina shrugged, though her eyes were bright with something unshed. “I think about her when I try to play.”

“That’s good,” Elian said gently. “That’s what music remembers.”

That night, Elian again played alone in his quarters, the notes bouncing off stone and wood with a hollowness that reminded him of empty rooms. He wondered what his presence here was really changing. The children were cautious, too deeply patterned into routines that stripped them of instinct. Marlin rarely appeared during lessons, but Elian could sense his eyes in the walls, his presence like an echo before it’s spoken. And there was a woman, Ms. Orwick, who walked the halls with a notebook pressed tight to her chest and a face that looked like it had never softened.

He had noticed that Kaia never seemed afraid of them—not even of Marlin. If anything, she moved through the orphanage like she existed outside its rules, untouchable. But there was also a distance to her, as though even among others, she stood alone in her own weather.

One evening, he found her sitting on the back steps outside the laundry building, staring out toward the tree line.

“Do you remember your parents?” he asked as he sat beside her.

Kaia didn’t answer right away. “I remember... a color. Gold. I don’t know if it was a room or a dress. Or maybe the sun.”

He waited, not pressing.

“I remember music,” she added, more quietly. “Not a song. Just... the feeling. Like something holding me.”

Elian felt a knot tighten in his chest. “That’s the right memory to keep.”

“I think that’s why I don’t like the silence here. It feels like forgetting.”

The words clung to him even after they parted.

The next morning, he brought his own violin into the children’s session. Instead of beginning with scales, he played an old piece—something meandering and slow, full of hesitant grace. The children watched him, uncertain, but he saw something shift in their postures. When he finished, he didn’t ask them to replicate it. Instead, he told them to make a sound—any sound—that matched how it made them feel.

Lina plucked a string slowly, unevenly. Elspeth hummed under her breath and touched the piano’s side with trembling fingers. Kaia closed her eyes and drew her bow across the strings in a long, tremulous line that trembled at the end like breath held too long.

“That’s where music begins,” Elian said.

Later that week, during the hour before dusk, he was called to the headmaster’s office.

Marlin stood behind a tall desk, lit only by a single oil lamp. The room smelled of paper and candle wax. Books lined the walls, but they were arranged in such orderly fashion they looked unread.

“You’ve been here nearly a fortnight,” Marlin said without preamble.

“Yes,” Elian replied.

“I’ve reviewed the notes. The progress is slow.”

“These children aren’t just learning technique,” Elian said calmly. “They’re unlearning fear.”

Marlin’s jaw tightened. “This is not a place for sentimentality.”

“It’s not sentiment,” Elian replied, more sharply than he intended. “It’s survival.”

Marlin studied him for a long moment. “Kaia is... special. That’s why you’re here. But I expect your attention to be shared.”

“She’s a child, not a case.”

The headmaster’s gaze went cold. “Be careful how far you stray from the task at hand, Mr. Dray.”

Elian left with a tight throat and hands aching from how tightly he’d clenched them at his sides.

That night, the wind howled louder than usual. In his room, Elian sat awake, the violin untouched beside him. He heard a knock around midnight. When he opened the door, Kaia stood there barefoot, a small candle in her hand.

“They’re afraid again,” she said. “The other children.”

“Why?”

“They heard you argued with the headmaster.”

Elian sighed. “It wasn’t much of an argument. Just words.”

“Words are heavy here,” Kaia said. “Heavier than stones.”

She turned and walked away without waiting for a response, the candle flickering as she moved down the hall.

Elian sat back on his bed, staring at the closed door.

Greywell, he now understood, was not only a place of silence—it was a place where silence was enforced. Not just with rules, but with fear. But fear had cracks. And into those cracks, music might yet seep.

He opened his violin case and began to play. Not loudly. Not defiantly. But tenderly, as if threading a line through the darkness.

Somewhere in the hall, he thought he heard a footstep pause, then linger.

And that was enough to keep playing.

Chapter Three: The Harmonics of Change

Autumn crept into Greywell with stealth—coating the hedges in frost, curling brown leaves along the path stones, whispering through the high windows of the east wing with sighs that sounded like distant breath. It was Kaia who first noticed the change not in the air, but in the way the silence began to alter.

“Do you hear it?” she asked one afternoon as they sat beneath the bare branches of the ash tree behind the garden.

“Hear what?” Elian asked, pausing his scribbled notes for the next lesson.

“The stillness,” she said, her head tilted like a bird’s. “It’s not as heavy.”

Elian listened. A breeze threaded its way through the crumbling garden wall, and for a moment, the rustle of leaves sounded almost like a whisper. Not from the children. Not from the teachers. From the orphanage itself.

He didn’t say anything, but he believed her.

That week, Lina began composing. Quietly, with tentative pencil strokes on torn scraps of staff paper Elian had smuggled in from a shop in the nearby village. At the end, they didn’t applaud. They didn’t cheer.

They only stood in the glow of the lantern, watching the last echoes disappear into the dark.

In the weeks that followed, Greywell changed in small, almost imperceptible ways.

The music room was left open. The staff stopped separating the older children during meals. New notebooks were permitted—provided they were inspected once a week. Lina kept writing, but in two versions: one for the ledgers, one for herself. Elspeth played openly now, every afternoon before supper, filling the halls with scales that were no longer shaky. Kaia taught the younger ones how to listen—to the walls, the wind, the footsteps in the attic.

Elian stayed.

Not because he agreed to the terms. Not because the board relented.

He stayed because the children did.

And they carried the music now, as surely as breath.

In time, many of them left Greywell—placed with families, moved to other schools, taken into the homes of distant relatives. But each left behind a page, a note, a phrase scratched beneath a desk or behind a stone. Kaia called them “roots,” the sound that kept the place from becoming hollow again.

She stayed longest.

Then one morning, she was gone.

No note. No word. Only her violin left behind, carefully resting atop the old piano, bow strung and ready.

Inside its case, Elian found a single sheet of paper.

Not a letter.

A new composition.

Its title read only: The Name Beneath the Stone.

He never performed it publicly.

But he played it every year on the anniversary of the night they played in the garden.

Always beneath the stars.

Always by lantern light.

Always with the children’s names written softly in his mind, carried note by note into the wind.

Elian returned to his routine, but it no longer resembled the one he'd known when he first arrived. Routine now meant something alive, something shifting. The children didn’t need to be taught how to play anymore so much as they needed space to explore, to create without fear. Elian became less of a teacher and more of a caretaker of their creativity. He offered tools, asked questions, listened more than he spoke. The change was slow, but unmistakable.

The instruments, once hidden in desperation, were now housed in open view—repaired, labeled, cared for. They were stored in a cabinet that had once held confiscated belongings. The locks had been removed. Kaia had etched a line on the front in small, curling letters:
“Hands that listen make better music.”

It was weeks before the full weight of Ross’s departure settled over the orphanage. Marlin returned to his duties with an increasingly perfunctory air. He avoided Elian entirely and issued instructions only through staff intermediaries. He no longer attended chapel, and the sound of his footsteps, once sharp and measured, began to sound aimless, uncertain. It was clear to Elian that Marlin had lost control—but more than that, he had lost purpose.

And yet, the structure of Greywell held. The stones were still the same. The windows still fogged over with the breath of winter mornings. But inside, something new pulsed.

Lina, emboldened by the events of the performance and the hidden map, had started a sort of informal choir. It wasn’t official. It wasn’t even consistent. But on Fridays after chores, a few children gathered in the garden to hum and sing songs Lina composed during quiet hours. The songs didn’t rhyme. They didn’t follow rules. Some didn’t even make sense. But they lifted the children’s chins and drew laughter into spaces where silence had once ruled.

Elspeth became the quiet anchor of the music room. She rarely spoke, but she was always there—tuning instruments, organizing bows, mending sheet music. She composed privately, rarely sharing, but the children respected her space. They knew she was building something intricate inside herself.

Kaia, when she was present, was a whirlwind of sound. She taught rhythm with spoons and clapping games. She convinced Ms. Orwick—after much persistence—to allow the use of the unused east corridor for “experiments.” There, the children crafted drums from old tea tins, fashioned xylophones from salvaged pipes, and even created a makeshift harp from fence wire and a gutted doorframe. It was chaotic, noisy, and occasionally hazardous. But it was also magic.

Elian watched it all unfold with a kind of quiet awe. He found himself less in charge and more in step with something communal, organic. He composed less often and instead began collecting—writing down the pieces the children made, archiving them, drawing sketches of the instruments they built. His new journal filled quickly, every page a mosaic of what the place had become.

But change, even when welcome, brings with it tension.

One day, midwinter, a letter arrived from the board. It was folded stiffly, sealed with the same wax-stamped emblem Elian had come to distrust.

    To Mr. E. Dray,

    We note the continued operation of musical activities at Greywell. We remind you that while certain reforms have been allowed to proceed under probationary status, the board maintains the right to audit all unsupervised curricula at any time. Compliance with formal inspection schedules is mandatory. Recurrent deviation from established guidelines will result in immediate administrative reevaluation.

    Respectfully,

    E. Danvers
    Board Secretary, Educational Oversight Committee

Elian read it twice and sighed. The board still feared what it didn’t understand.

He brought the letter to the music room that evening. Kaia was tuning one of the smaller violins, her lips pursed in concentration. When she saw the envelope in his hand, she raised an eyebrow.

“More grown-ups trying to make things quiet?” she asked.

“Something like that.”

She nodded slowly, then resumed tuning. “They don’t know what they’re fighting anymore.”

“They think it’s me.”

“It isn’t.”

Elian smiled faintly. “I know.”

Later, he gathered Lina, Elspeth, Kaia, and two of the older children, Tom and Mirella. He showed them the letter and explained what it meant. That more visits would come. That they might not always have freedom to do as they pleased. That their songs could be questioned, their materials removed.

Kaia said nothing. Lina looked angry. Elspeth simply stared at the fire, one finger tracing the grain of the wood bench.

“What do we do?” Tom asked finally.

Elian leaned forward. “We do what you’ve already done. We remember.”

He opened a large, flat wooden box—one Kaia hadn’t seen before. Inside were scores. Dozens of them. Written in different hands. Some neat. Some messy. Some just a single melody on an otherwise blank page.

“These,” he said, “are everything you’ve made since the first day we played in the chapel. They’re copies. Records. If they shut us down, if they lock the instruments away, if they scatter you again—this box still remembers.”

He looked at Kaia. “Would you hide it?”

She didn’t hesitate. “Yes.”

They buried the box behind the bell tower in the soft earth, where the frost had not yet returned. Mirella carved a mark into the stone just above it—an inverted treble clef inside a circle.

“No one will find it unless they listen,” she said.

That spring, Kaia left Greywell.

It happened quietly. There was no announcement. No ceremony. One morning she was there, playing a scale on the door-harp. The next, she was gone. Elian found her violin in its case beside the music room door, and next to it, her notebook. Inside was a final page, torn at the edges and written in her familiar looping hand.

    I won’t stop playing. I just won’t be playing here.

    Don’t forget what we made. It remembers you.

    —K.

Elian didn’t mourn her absence. He knew she hadn’t vanished. She had gone forward. That’s what music did. It didn’t die. It traveled.

The children continued their work. The garden group grew. New children arrived—frightened, withdrawn—and found themselves slowly drawn into the orbit of the sound that now lived in every room.

Greywell had changed.

It still bore its scars.

But now, those scars had a soundtrack.

And Elian, sitting at his desk in the twilight with the wind brushing the pages of Kaia’s notebook, understood something he had never understood in his years before arriving.

Music wasn’t just what you played.

It was what you carried with you when everything else had been taken away.

A month passed after Kaia’s departure, and though her absence left an unmistakable void, it was not one of silence. If anything, her leaving seemed to infuse Greywell with something more resilient—like her music had not exited, but dispersed into the bones of the building itself. The younger children, who once trailed behind her in adoration, began composing their own small songs. Elspeth took over tuning the violins. Lina stepped further into leadership, guiding the Friday gatherings with newfound confidence and occasionally allowing herself the smallest smile when a harmony held true.

Elian found himself haunted—not by grief, but by echoes. In his dreams, he heard Kaia’s playing, but distorted, distant, like it was being played from behind a closed door in a house he no longer lived in. During lessons, his hands would drift to a motif she’d once hummed absently while peeling dried glue from a broken bridge, and he’d feel the music tugging at him. Not back, but forward.

He had never believed in legacy as something deliberate. To him, legacy had always sounded suspiciously like vanity. But what lived now at Greywell didn’t belong to him. It had come through him, but it had grown on its own. The music, the movement, the memory—they were independent, coiled like vines in the cracks the institution had tried to seal.

The board sent no further letters.

Ross never returned.

Marlin remained, but ever more ghostlike, retreating into his office for longer and longer stretches, often forgetting to attend meetings, and once falling asleep during a staff inspection. Ms. Orwick grew quieter too. She stopped writing in her ledger during lessons and began listening instead, her fingers laced in her lap. Sometimes, Elian caught her mouthing the words to one of Lina’s songs under her breath.

There were new arrivals. A boy named Danton who refused to speak for his first week but tapped incessantly on his bunk frame. A girl named Sula who couldn’t hold a bow properly but sang with uncanny precision. Each one was folded into the rhythm of the orphanage not by mandate, but by invitation.

And still, Elian wrote.

He compiled everything—fragments of melodies, overheard lyrics, drawings made by children who couldn’t yet read music. He gathered them into notebooks with no titles, only symbols: a circle for breath, a slash for silence, a double mark for names remembered. He filled five books by midsummer. They lived in the chapel now, on a shelf where hymnals used to sit, and anyone could read them.

One afternoon, while watering the planters beneath the east windows, he found something new.

Tucked between the slats of the frame was a scrap of paper. The handwriting was unmistakable—long, deliberate curves and sharp indents where the pencil had been pressed too hard.

    I played on a hill two nights ago. Just before dawn. No one knew what they were hearing. But they stopped to listen anyway.

    Tell Lina her song worked. She’ll know which one.

    —K.

Elian folded the paper and didn’t show it to anyone at first. He kept it in his coat pocket, where it warmed against his chest. That night, he played in the garden, Kaia’s final piece transposed into a new key, one gentler and less jagged than before. He played it not to summon her back, but to answer her from where she now stood, somewhere beyond the walls.

It was Lina who eventually found the note in his coat.

She didn’t scold him for hiding it. She simply smiled—one of those rare, brief smiles that lit her face like a sudden candle—and nodded once.

“She’s still playing,” she said. “So we should too.”

By autumn, Greywell had transformed in ways no administrator would have thought possible a year earlier.

There were still rules, still schedules. Children still argued, broke things, forgot their chores. But beneath all of that was a structure of song. A culture of memory. New arrivals were no longer absorbed in silence. They were met with notes. With questions like, “What does your voice sound like when you’re alone?” or “If your story were a chord, what would it be?”

The older children began mentoring the younger ones. A boy named Clyve taught four-year-old Brida how to pluck melodies on a dulcimer someone had cobbled together from broken desk drawers. Elspeth led a group in building a scale of wind chimes made from spoons and cracked teacups, strung across the branches of the ash tree in the yard.

And Elian, ever the quiet figure in the midst of the movement, began writing a longer piece. Not for himself. Not even for Greywell. He didn’t know what it would become. It was stitched from fragments—Kaia’s dissonant flourishes, Rufin’s early sketches, Wren’s off-kilter tapping rhythms, Lina’s half-sung verses. He didn’t force it into form. He let it grow crooked. It was not meant to impress, but to document.

It had no title yet.

Only a working name he kept scribbled in the margin:
The Sound Left Behind

The piece became a place he could visit. A reminder that the work was not finished. That it never would be. The music had changed Greywell, but it had not erased its history. The silence was still there, crouched in corners, waiting. The institution still carried its ghosts. But the difference now was that those ghosts had been named. Their songs had been played.

And there were new voices, singing over them.

One fog-drenched morning, nearly a year after his arrival, Elian stood at the edge of the garden and watched the children gather near the bell tower for their usual Friday singing. Lina had taken up the violin—not Kaia’s, but one of the old battered ones they’d restored together—and she led them with a confidence that was no longer borrowed.

He felt no need to join.

He simply stood and listened.

Their voices drifted upward, carrying not just sound, but something deeper. Something alive.

Something that could not be put back in a cabinet.

He closed his eyes and breathed in the music.

It didn’t ask for applause.

It only asked to be remembered.

Chapter Six: Measures Without End

Winter returned to Greywell not with storm or snow, but with a soft relentlessness. Frost coated the inner corners of the windows each morning. The trees in the garden stood naked, their branches stretched thin and brittle, their leaves gone to soil. Yet even in that hushed season, music did not fade. It adapted.

Inside the chapel, Elspeth had begun keeping a fire going most afternoons. She brought the younger children there in pairs, reading to them from a book of stories she said had been rescued from the incinerator behind the library. The children sat close, wrapped in blankets, some drawing notes in the margins of discarded paper with chalk. From outside the chapel, it would’ve looked like nothing more than story time. But Elian knew it for what it was: rehearsal.

Every sentence Elspeth read was a rhythm. Every child’s drawing, a prelude to something yet unspoken.

Elian spent more time alone that season, not because he was needed less, but because the children needed each other more. They had become self-sustaining. A culture had taken root among them—a language of tones, a ritual of listening. The memory of silence was still there, yes, but it had become less sharp, no longer the first thing that entered the room.

And yet, something stirred in him. A restlessness he hadn’t felt in years. He had come to Greywell answering a call—one note held in the air by a stranger’s letter. Now, the music that had once needed him was finding its own voice. It was not letting go of him, but it was no longer asking for his lead.

He walked the halls at night, often in darkness, listening. The footsteps of staff. The creak of beds. The whispering rustle of a page being turned long after curfew. In those sounds, he could hear what the building was becoming. And it no longer sounded like Greywell as he had known it.

One evening, he found himself in the room where he had first taught Kaia. The dust had been swept away long ago, the cracked window now repaired with leaded glass panes donated by the chapel’s groundskeeper. On the windowsill rested a collection of found objects the children called “the wind orchestra”—small chimes, bottle caps, bits of glass, anything that sang when the breeze touched it.

He sat, opened his notebook, and began transcribing the winter sounds outside.

The wind had no tempo. It curled and receded, carried dried leaves across the threshold like missed phrases. Somewhere in the distance, an owl called—sharp, echoing. He wrote it all down. The owl, the rattle of the vents, the soft rhythmic sound of a broom in the corridor below.

He was no longer composing. He was recording.

Documenting.

Preserving what they were becoming.

A week later, a package arrived at the gates. No return address. Inside was a bundle wrapped in faded brown cloth, tied with thin black ribbon. Elian opened it carefully and found a violin. Not Kaia’s old one. This one was different—older, rougher, its back covered in marks like tally lines. Alongside it was a letter:

    It’s not mine. I’m only passing it forward. I found it in a shelter in Braithwall. It was leaning in the corner, unplayed, unwanted. I strung it, and it remembered.

    Give it to someone who needs to be heard.

    Tell them it doesn’t matter how good they are.

    It matters that they want to speak.

    —K.

Elian closed the letter and ran his hand along the back of the violin. The tally marks were uneven. He counted them—thirty-six. Whether they were for days, songs, names, or something else, he didn’t know. But it didn’t matter.

The instrument was already part of the choir.

He brought it to the chapel and placed it on the pew beside the shelf of untitled music books. The next morning, when he returned, it was gone. Later that week, a boy named Teven—barely eight and new to Greywell—stood during a practice and began playing a halting, tremorous melody on a violin Elian hadn’t seen him use before.

It wasn’t beautiful. But it was full.

He didn’t ask where Teven had gotten it.

He didn’t need to.

As the winter deepened, Elian started taking walks beyond the edge of the grounds. There was a path that led into the woods behind the east wall, a narrow trail long forgotten by most of the staff. He walked it slowly, sometimes bringing a notebook, sometimes nothing. In the stillness between branches, he listened for changes—not in the wind, but in himself.

He knew the question was coming.

Not from the board. Not from the children.

From within.

How long would he stay?

He loved them. He loved this place now—not for what it was, but for what it had become. But Greywell no longer needed a rescuer. What it needed was a caretaker of memory. Someone to archive. Someone to remind.

He began preparing.

Quietly.

He wrote copies of every piece in the library. Not just duplicates, but annotated versions—notes for interpretation, suggestions for how to teach the youngest children, alternate endings in case the standard ones no longer fit.

He trained Lina and Elspeth to lead full sessions. Not as a burden, but as an honor. They accepted without protest. Elspeth began keeping her own journal of rehearsal notes. Lina expanded the choir’s scope to include poetry and rhythm games. They grew into the roles not because they were asked, but because they had already begun to.

He wrote one final entry in his own notebook.

    *The music here doesn’t need protecting anymore.

    It’s loud enough to defend itself.*

    *I came to fix something.

    Instead, I was changed by it.*

    *If I never return, let this be my last offering:

    Don’t wait for permission to sing.*

    Sing because you were made to.

    And let the silence answer you however it will.

    —E.D.

He didn’t announce his leaving.

He left one morning at dawn, the sky thin and gold, his coat folded over his arm, his violin case strapped to his back. On the desk in his quarters, he left behind his journal and one page, handwritten and unsigned, tucked just inside the cover.

    *This is not a goodbye.

    Just a coda.*

    A rest before the next measure.

He stepped into the misty road and began to walk.

Behind him, the music continued.

Elian's departure was not immediate. Even after he'd quietly begun preparing the music books and handing over lessons to Lina and Elspeth, he remained in Greywell through the thickest of the winter, weaving himself in and out of the children’s lives like a fading melody. They noticed the subtle shift, though none of them said anything directly. They lingered longer after practice. They asked him to play more than usual. The Friday songs became quieter, slower, and often ended with the children sitting in silence, looking at their hands or up at the chapel ceiling, as though unsure what to do with the feeling that something, someone, was about to leave.

It wasn’t grief exactly. It was recognition.

They had heard this kind of pause in the music before—the one that precedes change.

One evening in late January, a rare snowstorm arrived. Not the delicate flurry Greywell was used to, but a heavy, wind-driven fall that buried the footpaths and forced the staff to gather the children in the main hall with blankets and hot broth. Elian stood at the window and watched the white build over the hedges, over the chapel roof, over the faint outlines of the garden instruments now half-forgotten beneath the frost.

Lina approached him, clutching a mug of broth, and said, “If you leave while it’s like this, you’ll vanish. No footprints.”

“I’d rather vanish gently than loudly,” he replied with a quiet smile.

She didn’t smile back. She sipped and said, “Promise you’ll leave something.”

He nodded. “I already have.”

Later that night, after the younger children had fallen asleep, the older ones gathered near the piano in the chapel. Elspeth played a soft improvisation—only the white keys, no pedals—while Clyve beat a rhythm on the back of an overturned wooden stool. Sula added her voice in short bursts, humming through her nose like she was trying to call something closer. Lina didn’t play. She sat across from Elian and asked him something she never had before.

“Where did you learn to play the way you do?”

He took a long moment before answering. “I didn’t learn from a teacher. Not at first. I learned from the spaces between sounds. From the way my mother sang to herself while cooking. From the wind outside my bedroom window. From church bells that rang imperfectly.”

“Do you think everyone has that in them?”

“Yes,” he said. “But most people have it taught out of them. Or buried.”

“And us?”

He glanced at the circle of children—some drowsy, some quietly creating in the flickering firelight. “You’re unburying it.”

She nodded. That was enough.

A few nights later, Elian went back to the old cupboard in the music room—the one that had once held broken instruments, now repurposed for strings and tuning kits. In the back, wrapped in faded cloth, was Kaia’s original bow. Not the new one she’d left when she disappeared, but the first—the one with a crack near the frog and a warped curve that had made it nearly unusable. He had meant to repair it. Had almost forgotten it.

He didn’t take it with him. He laid it atop the bookshelf beside the Remembering Book, where only the older children ever went. He knew someone would find it. Someone who understood what it had meant to her.

That week, he played every day. Not to teach, but to say goodbye.

The children didn’t ask why. They only came and listened.

They sang along when they could. Clyve tapped rhythms that echoed in the ceiling beams. Sula began adding movement to certain songs, short gestures of the hands, as though conducting air.

Even Marlin appeared once—standing in the back of the chapel during a dusk rehearsal. He didn’t speak, didn’t interrupt. But when Elian finished the piece, Marlin nodded once, tightly, and left.

On the final evening, Elian stood alone in the garden. The air was still. The ash tree had lost all its leaves. The chimes strung by the children now swayed with hollow tones, softened by the snow. The ground was frozen hard, and the distant hills were the color of smoke.

He took his violin from its case and played—not the new piece, not any old song. He played the silence.

One note at a time, drawn out so gently that the night had to bend closer to hear it. No tempo. No melody. Just the act of pulling sound into air.

Lina appeared at his side. She didn’t interrupt. She just placed a piece of folded paper on the stone bench nearby and turned to go.

After she left, Elian opened it.

    You taught us to hear what was already there.

    So we’ll listen for you in the places between.

    We won’t forget.
    L.

He folded it, placed it with Kaia’s note in the inner pocket of his coat, and returned to his quarters for the last time.

The next morning, the storm had passed. The sky was brittle blue. Footprints from the staff crisscrossed the yard as they shoveled and salted. The children were inside, bundled in layers, cleaning floors or sipping warm tea.

Elian passed through them quietly.

Lina saw him from the stairwell but didn’t speak.

Elspeth raised a hand in brief farewell.

He crossed the main gate before breakfast, the strap of his violin case tight on his shoulder, his coat drawn close.

As he reached the edge of the lane, the chapel bell rang—not once, but twice.

He paused.

It was a mistake, surely. The bell had not been used in over a year.

He looked back and saw Sula on the upper steps, her hand just leaving the rope.

He smiled.

Then he kept walking.

And Greywell kept singing.

Chapter Seven: Strings in the Air

Spring found Elian in a village called Merewick, a small town wedged between old stone ridges and tangled fields that smelled of thyme and peat. It was the kind of place where conversations still happened over fence posts, and the postman stayed for tea. He took up lodging above a cobbler’s shop, where the ceiling creaked with every step and the windows bloomed with condensation each morning. It was not luxury, but it was kind, and kindness had become his only currency.

He didn’t introduce himself as a teacher. Not at first. He simply played. In the market square, by the riverbank, in the stone alleyway behind the baker’s where sound bounced best between the narrow walls. His violin case lay open beside him, not for coins, but for questions. And slowly, the people asked.

“Do you give lessons?”
“Would you play something for my father—he used to dance?”
“My son hums in his sleep. Can you teach him what that means?”

He taught without formality. Children came with whistles, flutes, old harmonicas, instruments handed down from uncles and dusty attics. Adults came too—tired, tentative, yearning. A widowed woman named Mela brought a dulcimer that hadn’t sung in two decades. A farmer’s apprentice named Breck came without an instrument at all and learned to whistle in harmony with the morning wind.

And all the while, Elian wrote.

Not long compositions anymore. Not full pages of instruction. Just fragments. Measures. Questions. A single note drawn out for bars and bars until it meant something different than it had at the beginning. His journal now bore less the look of a composer’s manuscript and more the shape of a naturalist’s field guide: each page observing how music moved through people instead of trying to control it.

But despite the ease of this new rhythm, Elian carried a weight he could not set down. A part of him remained anchored to Greywell—not to the stone walls, but to what had been built there. He did not miss the place. He missed the echoes. The remembered names that only came to him in the silence between tasks, or when the dusk deepened and he caught the wrong interval in a bird’s call.

He kept in touch in small ways. A letter arrived from Lina one evening, written in ink dark as soot and folded with precise creases. Inside were five short melodies, a few bars each, labeled with names: Toma’s laugh, Elspeth’s morning warmup, Clyve’s new tapping game. She did not ask how he was. She only said, We still listen. Even when we don’t play.

He sent no reply. But he played each one aloud, once, and then again, slower, until they stretched into something else. When he finished, he wrote a sixth: Lina’s silence when she reads.

Then he tore out the page and mailed it back.

Later, a traveling craftsman passed through Merewick and brought word of a music book discovered beneath a chapel floor in a town far north. It was filled with unusual annotations, strange symbols beside the notes, and was said to contain songs that made even those without training weep. The craftsman didn’t know who had written it. Elian only nodded and asked what it had been called.

“Didn’t have a name,” the craftsman said, shrugging. “They just called it The Map.”

After he left, Elian stood in the square and played the old piece Kaia had once called The Name Beneath the Stone. His bow was rough, and the strings slightly out of tune from humidity, but it didn’t matter. The notes were where they had always been: inside him, waiting to be remembered.

The next morning, a girl named Tansy approached him while he sat near the bakery steps. She couldn’t have been more than eight, with a mess of freckles and two braids that pointed in opposite directions.

“You don’t teach like a teacher,” she said, arms crossed.

“No?” he asked, smiling. “What do I teach like?”

“Like you’re waiting to hear something. From us.”

He nodded. “That’s not wrong.”

She sat beside him, swinging her legs. “Well, I’m learning to hear too. Sometimes it’s not easy.”

“Sometimes it’s not supposed to be,” he said. “But it’s worth it.”

She glanced sideways. “Are you from here?”

“No,” he said. “But for now, I’m staying.”

She paused, then pulled something from her coat pocket. It was a folded bit of parchment, worn and creased.

“My brother wrote this,” she said. “Before he left. He went to a place called Gray-something. Never says much about it. But he wrote me this melody.”

Elian unfolded the page and froze. The first four notes were familiar. The fifth was not. The phrasing had changed—bent upward, as though the melody had learned to climb.

He smiled.

“Can I play it?” he asked.

She nodded eagerly.

He raised his bow, touched the string, and played.

The sound rose into the morning light.

It didn’t belong to him.

And that was exactly right.

By late spring, Elian had become something of a fixture in Merewick. Not celebrated, exactly—this was not a place where people offered fame, only familiarity. The kind of familiarity that brought fresh eggs to your door without a note, or that left a basket of old sheet music atop your steps on a windy day. He taught under trees, in barns, on flat stone walls still warm from the midday sun. Lessons took place with feet dangling over creeks, or in silence, watching birds for rhythm.

He no longer carried all his music with him. Instead, he began the quiet practice of leaving things behind.

He left a song, unfinished, on a garden bench where the milk boy sometimes sat to eat his supper. A fragment of melody with wide intervals, humming with indecision, perfect for someone uncertain of their voice.

He left a small sketch of Kaia’s old violin tucked between the pages of a forgotten prayer book in the chapel, beneath the shelf of hymnals no one sang from anymore. On the back, he wrote: Play even when the room doesn’t ask you to.

He left a folded page of lined staff paper at the foot of the willow tree behind the apothecary. It contained only one phrase, repeated over and over, each time with slightly different spacing. It read: You are not alone in the sound you carry.

No one ever confronted him about these things. But they vanished. And sometimes, in return, he’d find something waiting: a jar of ink with a label that said “for your silences,” or a note with no name, just: I heard you yesterday. You made the wind still for a while.

It was enough.

Yet, Elian remained aware of the time. Not hours, not days—but cycles. The way the town moved in seasons of music and quiet. The way the children outgrew their own hands and began to stretch toward things they hadn’t yet imagined. He knew, in the same way he’d once known to leave Greywell, that he wouldn’t stay in Merewick forever. The music had settled into the soil now, taken up by others, made permanent by repetition and care.

Still, there were moments he stayed longer than he should’ve. The morning when Tansy brought him a reed flute she’d made herself, the holes uneven, the pitch wildly unpredictable—but it sang. She named it “Finn,” after a song her brother had once played in the dark to calm her dreams.

Or the evening when Breck, the farmer’s apprentice, came to him with soil still under his nails and asked if Elian would help him set words to the way the clouds moved. They spent hours under the open sky, lying flat on their backs, describing cirrus and cumulus with vowel sounds.

Elian wrote nothing down. He just listened. And afterward, he took out his violin and played not the clouds, but the feeling of watching someone else describe them.

One morning, a package arrived for him. Unmarked. Inside, a small journal.

No letter. No name.

He opened it and recognized the handwriting immediately. Kaia.

But it wasn’t a message, not in the usual way.

The book was filled with observations.

Notations of sounds overheard in cities: trains, cathedrals, laughing children, glass breaking. Strange rhythms found in a marketplace in Levenport. A chord progression based on the uneven breathing of an old woman sleeping on a public bench.

On the last page, only one sentence:

    You said music should go walking. So I let it.

He sat with the journal for a long time, listening not to the pages, but to what stirred underneath them. A trail. A path. Not back. Forward.

That week, Elian resumed writing in earnest—not for others, not for students or posterity, but for the road ahead. He transcribed the rhythms of Merewick: the double-step of the baker’s boy, the triple time of the bell in the steeple, the strange syncopation of sheep bleating on the ridge. He compiled them as one might gather flowers—carefully, without knowing how long they would last.

He called it The Folk of Sound.

And beneath the title, in small letters:

“A collection of places pretending to be people.”

In mid-May, a young man arrived from Greywell.

He was seventeen now, taller than Elian remembered, his voice low, his face still shifting into the adult it would become. It was Rufin.

He didn’t announce himself. He simply walked up during a lesson and waited, hands in his coat pockets.

Elian excused himself and joined him under the eaves of the shop.

“You kept the line,” Rufin said, his eyes scanning the violin case.

“You followed it,” Elian replied.

“I teach now,” Rufin said. “Back at Greywell. Just the younger ones. Elspeth leads the others.”

Elian raised an eyebrow. “She stayed?”

“For a while. Then left. But not far. She visits every second Saturday.”

They stood in silence for a moment.

“She said you’d want to know,” Rufin added. “The chapel roof was repaired. The ash tree bloomed again. And the chimes are still up. They call them the ‘sky scale.’”

Elian smiled.

“Kaia?” he asked.

“No one hears from her directly. But once, someone found a coin on the windowsill. Bent like a crescent. Same as the one she used to carry.”

Elian nodded. That was enough.

Rufin reached into his pocket and pulled out a folded page.

“I made something,” he said. “Not sure what to call it.”

Elian took the page and studied it.
