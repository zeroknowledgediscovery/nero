The Shifting Crown
Chapter One: The Whispering Walls

The storm broke just past midnight. It rolled in over the Sea of Varanne like a curse, shaking the old stones of Castle Tharros with its howling breath. Thunder rattled the stained glass windows of the Tower of Alms, and the guards along the parapets muttered quiet prayers, clutching their cloaks tighter. Deep in the heart of the castle, beyond velvet draperies and gold-laced doors, a girl sat upright in her bed, her heart drumming in time with the wind’s crescendo.

Princess Elaria of Tharros, youngest daughter of King Maelen the Resolute, had always been told she was born during a storm. The servants said the skies had howled in anger when her first cry rang through the chamber, that lightning split the great oak in the eastern courtyard just as her eyes opened. Her mother, Queen Renya, said it was just rain—but Elaria always felt the storm was in her blood.

She rose, wrapping her cloak over her nightgown, and moved to the window. The sea churned like a wounded beast. Lightning flashed, and in its sharp light she caught sight of something impossible—movement atop the cliffs across the bay. A figure, silhouetted by the storm, stood unmoved by the wind. Watching. Waiting.

A knock at the door made her jump. “My lady,” came a voice, soft and hesitant. “Are you awake?”

Elaria opened the door to find her handmaiden, Brynn, holding a candle whose flame danced wildly. Brynn’s dark eyes were wide with unease. “The king’s called an emergency council. Lord Tharo’s ship returned tonight… with only a quarter of its crew.”

Elaria’s brow furrowed. “I thought they were bound for Narren’s Reach, to trade salt and gems.”

“They were. But something… happened. He came back raving. About blood. About betrayal.” Brynn lowered her voice. “He said the Reach has fallen. That a shadow walks there now.”

A chill moved through Elaria like ice in her bones.

The council chamber was ablaze with torchlight when Elaria arrived. Her father sat at the head of the table, iron-crowned and grim-faced, flanked by his most trusted advisors—Lord Valen, the High General; Sarn of the Silver Tithe; and her eldest brother, Prince Audric. Lord Tharo, a man once proud and hawkish, knelt before them. His cloak was torn, his armor cracked and crusted with dried salt.

“They rose from the deep,” Tharo whispered. “Men with eyes like drowned stars. Their voices… it wasn’t words they spoke. It was the sound of bones cracking.”

“Madness,” muttered Sarn. “You disgrace the king with this tale.”

But King Maelen did not dismiss him. His hand clenched the hilt of his ceremonial blade, and his gaze flicked to Elaria as she stepped inside. “You saw them?” he asked.

Tharo nodded. “We landed on the shore. All was quiet. Then—screaming. The villagers were gone. The towers gutted. And then they came—cloaked in mist, blades that bled light…”

The room fell silent. Then Audric slammed his fist against the table. “We must strike back. Sail at once with the fleet.”

“We do not yet know what we face,” the king said. “And we do not move blindly into the jaws of an enemy we cannot name.”

Elaria’s voice cut through the quiet. “But if they come here, we must be ready.”

Eyes turned to her. The princess rarely spoke in council. Not because she lacked intelligence, but because she lacked favor. As the youngest, and a girl, her role had always been symbolic—graceful, gentle, gilded.

But tonight, her words hung in the air like prophecy.

That night, unable to sleep, she found herself walking the outer halls of the western wing. Here, the walls murmured with old secrets. The portraits of long-dead queens lined the corridors, their painted eyes glinting in flickering torchlight. She paused before one: Queen Alareth, the Flamekeeper. Her gaze was fierce even in paint, her fingers resting on a jeweled sword—one that hadn’t been seen in Tharros for a hundred years.

“You’re not supposed to be here,” said a voice behind her.

Elaria turned. A boy stood in the shadowed archway—tall, narrow-faced, wearing the black garb of the royal archivists. His name was Kael, and she recognized him vaguely; he had once brought her a book on the history of the southern tribes, unbidden.

“I could say the same of you,” she replied.

He smiled faintly. “Books don’t keep time.”

“What are you doing here?”

“I was following the storm.”

She tilted her head. “You sound like a mad poet.”

“Better than a sane soldier.”

There was something strange about him. Not dangerous, but… unusual. He had the look of someone who lived half in dreams.

He walked closer and nodded at the painting. “You know she vanished, yes? Alareth. Took her sword and rode north, into the Varnwood. Never returned.”

“She was searching for the Mirror Tower,” Elaria said.

“Ah, so you do read.”

“I do many things my father wishes I wouldn’t.”

Kael laughed. It was a quick, dry sound, like flint on stone.

They stood in silence for a moment before he said, “If you want to know what happened at Narren’s Reach, I may be able to help.”

Elaria turned sharply. “How?”

He glanced around, then lowered his voice. “Because the signs were written before the fleet ever left. In the old texts. The Dream Codex. There’s a pattern. A cycle. The sea gives, and the sea takes. But this time, it may not give back.”

Elaria stared at him. “Why are you telling me this?”

“Because you’re the only one who listened to Tharo without laughing.”

Two days passed. Storms lingered on the horizon, but none approached the coast. Yet unease spread through the castle like rot. The queen locked herself in her solar, murmuring prayers to gods no longer named. Prince Audric trained with twice his usual fury. The king ordered a doubling of the night watch, and whispers said he had sent a raven to the old witches of Aelwyn, though their tower had not answered a summons in thirty years.

Elaria, meanwhile, found herself drawn to the ancient library, where Kael worked. The vaults below the castle were deeper than she imagined—tunnels carved into the stone centuries ago, back when Tharros was only a citadel. Here, among scrolls sealed with dust and books bound in cracked leather, they read by candlelight.

“There,” Kael said one evening, pointing at a map drawn in red ink. “The Mirror Tower. Alareth wasn’t the first to seek it. But she might’ve come closest.”

“Why is it called the Mirror Tower?”

“Because it shows you who you are. And if you're strong enough, who you might become.”

“That sounds like a myth.”

Kael’s gaze sharpened. “All myths begin as truths.”

Elaria traced the edge of the parchment. “What does this have to do with the ships?”

“There’s a gate beneath the sea. The Codex calls it ‘the Eye of Varn’. It opens every hundred years. And when it does… things slip through.”

“You mean these… drowned men Tharo spoke of?”

Kael nodded. “And worse. But there’s something else. The Mirror Tower is said to be able to… change the tide. To close the gate. If it can be found.”

Elaria sat back. The world was shifting beneath her feet, old truths crumbling like dried leaves. “And you think I should go there?”

“I think you’re the only one who can.”

Silence fell between them. Then, from somewhere above, a bell tolled—three sharp clangs. The signal for a breach.

The castle was under attack.

—

The bells rang like the voice of a god, loud and insistent, shattering the hush of night with their sudden, terrible clamor. Elaria and Kael rose at once, instinct trumping fear. Dust drifted down from the vaulted ceiling as the library trembled underfoot, and far above them, shouts echoed through the stone like thunder in a valley.

They ran.

Up twisting stairwells and narrow passages lit by the red flare of alarm-torches, past startled sentries and fleeing stewards, until they burst into the corridor near the Great Hall. Elaria’s breath burned in her chest. She yanked her cloak tighter, Kael a step behind her, as they rounded the final bend—and stopped short.

The doors of the Great Hall had been thrown open. Inside, a battle raged.

Black shapes swarmed through shattered windows, long-limbed and half-cloaked in mist. Their skin glistened like kelp, stretched taut over wiry frames, and their eyes—bright, endless, and wet with a glow like lanterns drowned in oil—pierced the chaos. Soldiers of Tharros fought with steel and fire, and still the invaders came, spilling over tables and crashing through braziers, leaving trails of frost and brine.

Elaria ducked as a blade whistled past her head, ricocheting off a column. Kael grabbed her arm and pulled her behind the stone pedestal of an old knight’s statue. “They’re inside,” he breathed, as if saying it aloud would make it real.

“I can see that,” she snapped, then immediately regretted the tone.

A moment passed before they moved again, this time lower to the ground, crawling to the side wall where a servant’s passage wound toward the king’s dais. “My father,” she said, “he’ll be—”

Kael didn’t let her finish. “We’ll find him. But we need a weapon first.”

It wasn’t bravery that pushed her forward—it was certainty, a feeling in her marrow that if she didn’t act now, she’d lose something she could never regain. They slipped through the hidden passage and emerged behind the tapestry of King Alenyr’s Coronation. The dais stretched before them, cloaked in smoke. Through the haze, Elaria saw a figure standing tall in the middle of the hall—her father, sword in hand, face streaked with ash and blood. He moved like a bear among wolves, cutting through the fog-beasts with powerful, deliberate strokes. Beside him, Audric fought with grim intensity, already wounded in the leg but refusing to falter.

The battle teetered. A scream ripped the air—one of the creatures had leapt onto the balustrade and flung itself at the king, both claws outstretched. Elaria didn’t think. She rose from cover, snatched a dropped pike from the floor, and hurled it like a javelin.

It struck true. The creature howled, twitching and shriveling, before crumbling into a slurry of pale steam and seawater. The king turned, and for one moment, their eyes met—his wide with surprise, hers bright with terror.

“Elaria, back!” Audric roared.

But she was already running forward.

The invaders began to retreat not long after. As suddenly as they had come, they withdrew, slipping through cracks and windows and doors like spilled smoke. By the time the last of them vanished, leaving behind only dead silence and puddles of brackish water, the castle was broken. Blood slicked the marble. The scent of burnt salt filled the air. And Tharros would never be the same again.

In the aftermath, the wounded were gathered. The dead were counted. Elaria stood on the battlements, staring down at the cliffs across the bay—where once again, the figure stood. This time, she was sure it watched her directly. As lightning crackled, it raised one arm, and for the briefest of moments, Elaria felt a pressure against her thoughts, as if something cold and immense brushed the edges of her mind. Then it was gone.

Back in the council chamber, the atmosphere was brittle. The high windows had been boarded. The walls smelled faintly of mildew and smoke. Her father sat heavier than before, as though the weight of his crown had doubled.

“We cannot face this with steel,” said Lord Valen. “These are not raiders, not beasts of war. They’re old. Ancient. And they are not meant for our world.”

“Then what would you have us do?” Audric growled. His leg was bound in fresh linen, a red stain already spreading. “Pray?”

“We consult the Codex,” Kael interjected, stepping forward for the first time.

Several lords turned, frowning at the unfamiliar voice.

“This boy speaks out of turn,” said Sarn coldly.

But Elaria stood as well. “He knows things none of us do. We’d be wise to listen.”

The king studied them both. “Speak, then, Kael.”

Kael’s voice remained steady. “The Eye of Varn has opened. It opens every century. Each time, something slips through, and each time, it is worse than the last. These creatures—they’re only the vanguard.”

“Of what?” Maelen asked.

Kael hesitated. “Of the Deep King.”

A hush fell.

“The name is forbidden,” murmured one of the councilors.

“Because we fear it,” Kael replied. “But we cannot afford fear now. The Deep King was sealed beyond the Mirror Tower at the end of the Second Cycle. If he returns—he will not just conquer. He will unmake.”

“You propose we chase after a ghost?” Valen scoffed. “You want us to ride into myth?”

Kael said nothing. But Elaria spoke.

“I’ll go.”

Every head turned.

She stood tall, chin raised, voice clear. “If there is even a sliver of truth in the Codex, then we must find the Mirror Tower. I’ll lead the expedition.”

“Impossible,” Maelen said. “You’re not trained. You’re not—”

“I am not a child,” she interrupted. “You’ve trained me to curtsey, to smile, to dance. But you forgot I also watched every sparring session. I read every battle chronicle. I know the woods, the paths, the old ruins better than any man here.”

Audric stood now too. “Then let me go. It should be me.”

“No,” Maelen said again. “I need you here. If I fall, you are king. Tharros must endure.”

The room trembled with the weight of the decision.

At last, the king turned to Elaria. “You’ll take a small party. Five riders. You leave at dawn. If you do not return within one moon's cycle, we will assume the worst.”

Elaria nodded.

“And Kael,” the king added grimly, “go with her. If this is your storm, then see it through.”

She did not sleep that night. She sat on her bed with the window open, listening to the ocean hum like an old lullaby. The sea had taken much. It would take more. But in that moment, Elaria felt something not quite like fear and not quite like hope.

In the early light, she was dressed in leather and cloak, her sword belted tight, her dark hair braided in a crown. Her party assembled in the courtyard—Kael, quiet and resolute; Brynn, who had refused to stay behind; Ser Calen, the old knight of the Eastern Reach; Linna of the Huntwood, a tracker with sharp eyes and sharper arrows; and Elion, a soft-spoken healer who had once lived among the mystics.

Six souls, bound by oath and fire.

As they rode out beneath the rising sun, the gates of Tharros closing behind them, Elaria did not look back.

She knew, somehow, that nothing behind her would ever be the same.

She was no longer the girl in the tower.

She was the wind-born daughter of storm and stone.

And the crown was shifting.

Chapter Two: The Salt Road

They followed the old road east, a path long overgrown with creeping vines and broken stone. Once, it had been the Salt Road, a trade route stretching from the cliffs of Tharros to the moss-covered ruins of Ildarel, winding through the riverlands and forested hills of the kingdom’s forgotten spine. Now it was half-swallowed by the earth, the milestones etched with glyphs no one had read in generations. The land here seemed to hold its breath. Birds fell silent when they passed. The wind carried the scent of brine, even so far from the sea.

Elaria rode at the head of the column, her face hidden beneath the cowl of her deep blue cloak. She had said little since they left Tharros, her mind tangled with memory and anticipation. Brynn kept close to her side, while Kael rode a few paces behind, half-absorbed in a journal he scribbled in when the horses slowed. The others—Ser Calen, Linna, and Elion—rode in a tight triangle, eyes constantly scanning the underbrush, hands never straying far from sword or bow.

On the third day, the rain began.

It came suddenly, as if the sky had simply decided to tip itself over. A cold, relentless downpour that turned the road to mud and reduced their progress to a crawl. They camped that night beneath the crumbling arch of a ruined viaduct, once part of an ancient aqueduct that had brought water to the old capital, before it had sunk into the swamp-lands now called the Mirror Fen.

Elaria sat with Kael beneath the shallow shelter of a collapsed stone lip. A small fire flickered between them, fed with dry twigs that Linna had managed to scavenge under the thick roots of a weeping elm. The flames cast shadows on the underside of the viaduct, painting strange shapes on the walls.

Kael closed his book and glanced over. “We’ll reach the river fork by midday tomorrow, if the rain eases.”

She nodded but said nothing.

He studied her for a moment. “You’ve been quiet.”

“I’ve been thinking.”

“That much is obvious. About what?”

She didn’t answer right away. The fire snapped, sending a brief spray of embers into the air. “The figure I saw on the cliff. The night of the attack. It wasn’t a dream. It knew I was watching. And it wanted me to see it.”

“You think it was… part of them?”

“I think it was more than that. It wasn’t like the others. There was a mind behind it. Purpose.” She looked at him. “And I think it’s still watching us.”

Kael’s mouth tightened, but he nodded slowly. “The Codex spoke of the King-Below, a creature that wears the sea like a cloak. But the ancient scribes also mention something else. Something older. A voice that whispers into the bones of men.”

“Whispers what?”

“Truths they cannot bear.”

Elaria shivered despite the fire. She stood and looked out into the night. The forest was dark and slick with rain, and beyond it, the shadows curled like living things.

At the river fork, they found the bridge gone.

Where once a proud stone span had arched across the boiling waters of the Syrican, there was now only jagged rubble and a blackened stump where the watchtower had stood. Charred bones littered the ground, and the earth stank of old fire. Linna knelt near the edge, running her fingers through the soot.

“This wasn’t bandits,” she said, standing. “Too clean. Too precise.”

Ser Calen frowned. “No battle signs. No footprints leading in or out.”

“Something rose from the river,” Elion said quietly, pointing to the unnatural scarring on the stone. “Look at the way the moss has burned. As if it was boiled from below.”

Kael flipped rapidly through his journal, finding a page filled with notes. “The southern manuscripts speak of a ‘Wrath Beneath’, a creature of heat and hunger said to dwell in the rivers when the Eye stirs. The last recorded sighting was five hundred years ago, just before the last tide war.”

“We’re not going around,” Elaria said, pulling her cloak tighter. “We’ll build a raft.”

The others stared at her. The river was wide, fast, and roaring with snowmelt from the mountains.

Kael grimaced. “That’s suicide.”

“Then find me another way.”

No one spoke for a long moment. Then Ser Calen nodded. “She’s right. There’s no time to lose. We’ll need vines, logs, and pitch.”

By nightfall, they had lashed together two crude rafts. The river protested their passage with all the fury of a waking god. Twice, the ropes snapped and had to be retied mid-current. Brynn nearly fell in once, saved only by Linna’s quick hand and an arrow driven into a passing tree stump as a makeshift rope anchor.

They crossed, soaked and freezing, and set camp on the far side beneath the protective curve of a granite outcrop. No one spoke that night. Even the fire seemed subdued, as though aware it had no dominion here.

On the seventh day, they reached the edge of the Mirror Fen.

It rose before them like a bruise on the land—flat, endless, wet. A thousand shallow pools stitched together by threads of broken ground, reeds, and ghost-white trees that bent like old men under the weight of memory. The fog never lifted. It clung to the water like skin.

They stopped at the Fen’s edge. Even Ser Calen looked uncertain. “I’ve ridden through the Dead Hollow and faced the fire dogs of the Brass Hills, but this place…”

“It smells like endings,” Brynn whispered.

“There’s a path,” Linna said, gesturing. “There, through the trees.”

Sure enough, a thin causeway of raised earth wound into the fen. Stones lined its edge, each marked with worn runes. Elaria studied them. They reminded her of the markings from the archives—protection glyphs, old and frayed by time.

“Whatever’s inside,” Kael said, “someone once tried to keep it out.”

“Or keep it in,” Elion added.

They entered single file, every step a decision. The fog thickened as they moved, and strange sounds echoed across the water—whispers, laughter, the flap of unseen wings. Once, Elaria thought she saw a woman standing waist-deep in a black pool, hair trailing like ribbons, her eyes mirrors of silver. But when she blinked, there was nothing there.

The path narrowed, and the ground grew softer. Twice, someone slipped, and the water hissed as if in hunger. They moved slower after that.

That night, they lit no fire. The light wouldn’t catch. It flickered, then vanished, swallowed by the fog. Kael muttered something about ley-line disruption. Elion sat with his back to the others, his eyes darting. Ser Calen stood watch, unmoving, like a statue of old iron.

Elaria dreamed.

She stood in a great chamber of stone and glass. Above her, a sky of fire and stars. Before her, a mirror taller than a tower, framed in bone and gold. In it, she saw herself—not as she was, but as she could be. Crowned. Armored. Eyes burning with some strange light. She reached for the glass—and it shattered.

She woke with a cry, startling the others.

Kael was at her side in a moment. “What did you see?”

She hesitated, then shook her head. “Nothing. A dream.”

But even as she said it, she could feel the fragments of that vision inside her, sharp and trembling, lodged somewhere between her thoughts.

By the ninth day, the Fen began to change.

The water grew deeper. The trees more gnarled. The whispers louder. Linna, usually the calmest among them, began speaking to someone who wasn’t there. Elion tried to cleanse the air with burning sage, but the smoke twisted back on itself, curling into shapes that resembled hands.

Then came the tower.

It rose from the heart of the Fen like a jagged tooth, pale stone slick with moisture, leaning slightly to one side as though weary from standing so long. Its windows were black, its door sealed with a sigil that pulsed faintly in the dark.

Kael dropped to one knee, muttering in awe. “The Mirror Tower.”

Elaria felt something pull at her—like the tide at her ankles. She stepped forward, one hand outstretched.

The sigil flared.

And the door opened.

—

The air inside the Mirror Tower was heavier than the bog itself. It pressed against the skin like wet cloth, thick with the scent of iron and old water. The passage beyond the door sloped downward in a slow spiral, walls lined with smooth obsidian veins that shimmered faintly in the torchlight. They descended in silence, the only sound the slosh of their boots through shallow, cold water and the occasional breath too loud in the otherwise tomb-still air.

Elaria led the way, one hand resting on the hilt of her sword, the other running lightly along the damp stone wall. She felt drawn forward by something unseen—not a voice, not a sound, but a sensation of direction, a tug within her chest like a string being pulled from somewhere below. Behind her, the others followed in uneasy formation. Kael kept scribbling in his journal even as he walked, glancing at etched runes carved into the wall every few steps. Brynn looked back constantly, clearly unsettled by the idea of something following them down. Ser Calen muttered to himself under his breath, perhaps prayers, perhaps curses. Linna had drawn her long knife and never sheathed it again.

After what felt like hours, the passage opened into a vast, circular chamber. The walls rose like the inside of a broken egg, curving high above them until they vanished into darkness. In the center stood a pedestal of pale stone, and on it sat a shard of mirror, no larger than a man’s hand. Around the pedestal were six statues—hooded figures, faceless and hunched, their arms reaching out toward the shard like supplicants frozen mid-prayer.

No one spoke. They didn’t need to. The stillness of the room demanded silence.

Kael stepped forward slowly, examining the shard. “This is it. Not the full mirror, no—but a piece. A key, maybe. Or an anchor.”

“To what?” Elaria asked, voice low.

“To whatever the rest of the tower hides.”

Brynn stepped beside her and pointed. “Look. The reflections. They’re wrong.”

Elaria turned her head, and her blood chilled.

The shard showed a perfect image of the chamber—except the statues were not still. In the reflection, they moved subtly, slowly. One turned its head. Another lowered its arms. Elaria stepped closer to the mirror and saw herself there, but not as she stood. Her reflection smiled when she did not. Its eyes were hollow.

She backed away at once.

“Don’t touch it,” Elion warned, his voice sharp. “That’s not just a mirror. It’s a gate. Or worse—bait.”

Kael approached more carefully now, running a hand along the runes at the base of the pedestal. “These symbols—they’re similar to those near the gate of the Eye. This shard could be a lock. If it’s taken, it might weaken the seal.”

“Then we leave it,” Ser Calen said gruffly.

“No,” Elaria said, her voice stronger than she felt. “We came here for answers. If this is part of the Mirror Tower’s purpose, we can’t walk away from it.”

She reached out—hesitated—and then grasped the shard.

There was a sound like a thousand whispers inhaling at once. The statues cracked at the joints. Not stone. Not lifeless. Not anymore.

The nearest one moved, its neck twisting with a sound like ice breaking. It turned toward Elaria, a hand lifting slowly.

“Run!” Kael shouted, and suddenly the whole chamber came alive with motion.

The statues lunged—not fast, but relentless. Ser Calen drew steel and stood his ground, cleaving through one with a single powerful arc. The thing shattered like brittle clay, but the others closed in.

Elaria clutched the shard to her chest and fled down a side tunnel, the others hot on her heels. The passage was narrower here, the walls close, and the ceiling so low they had to crouch. Behind them, the sounds of pursuit echoed—the scraping of limbs on stone, the hollow clicks of those faceless heads turning.

They emerged into another chamber, this one smaller and filled with strange devices—gears, pulleys, and a massive stone disc inscribed with a star pattern that looked eerily familiar.

“I’ve seen this before,” Kael breathed, eyes wide. “It’s the Seal of Varn. The center of the old gate.”

“Then use it,” Elaria said, shoving the shard into his hands. “Lock the Eye. Do whatever you have to.”

Kael dropped to his knees, placing the shard at the heart of the disc. The room trembled. The air grew colder. One of the statues reached the entrance just as a column of blue-white light erupted from the seal, striking the ceiling and vanishing into the sky above.

The statue froze mid-step. It turned slowly, jerkily, and then retreated.

The others followed suit, their faceless heads bowed as they melted back into the dark.

For a long moment, no one moved.

Then the light vanished, and all fell still.

They emerged from the tower at dawn, their skin pale, clothes damp with sweat and dew. The Mirror Fen had changed. The mist had thinned, and sunlight broke through the trees in weak but real beams. Birds sang again. The water no longer seemed so eager to swallow them whole.

Elaria looked down at the shard in her hand. It no longer pulsed, but it remained cold as ice.

Kael pulled out his journal, hands trembling. “We’ve disrupted the Eye. Delayed it. Maybe even sealed it again for a time. But this is only a fragment of the whole. The true Mirror—the one that seals the Deep King—still lies beyond. Deeper. Older.”

Brynn collapsed against a tree, exhausted. “Then why does it feel like we won?”

“Because we survived,” Ser Calen said. “And sometimes, that’s enough.”

But Elaria knew better. She had seen what lay on the other side of the mirror’s glass. A future fractured and on fire. She had felt its pull.

And she had seen the shadow watching her again—this time in the reflection of the water as they crossed the last stretch of the Fen.

It did not speak. But it no longer needed to.

She was part of the cycle now.

That night, they camped in a dry glade just beyond the Fen. The air was clearer here. The ground firm. It almost felt like peace.

Linna kept watch while the others slept, her eyes scanning the trees. Elaria sat apart from the group, holding the shard and tracing its edges.

Kael joined her quietly. “You did well.”

She didn’t look at him. “I touched something in there. Something not meant to be touched. And now I can’t stop thinking about it.”

“You saw something.”

“A version of me. Crowned. Alone. Burning.”

Kael nodded, unsurprised. “The mirror doesn’t lie. But it doesn’t tell the truth either. It shows what you fear. Or desire. Or both.”

“I don’t want a crown,” she said, though it sounded hollow.

“Maybe not yet. But the storm you were born into—it’s coming again.”

Elaria stood. “Then we ride at first light.”

“To where?”

She stared into the trees. “To the Whispering Vale. That’s where Alareth disappeared. And where the next shard may lie.”

Kael said nothing, but his breath caught slightly.

The path ahead would be darker still.

But now, there was no turning back.

Chapter Three: Ash and Amber

The Whispering Vale was not marked on any living map. Kael had discovered its name only once in the margins of a crumbling journal bound in leather made from something that didn’t seem quite animal. The entry was brief, nearly illegible, and spoke of “a valley between worlds, where time folds like cloth and breath is weighed in coin.” A riddle, perhaps. Or a warning. Yet the name lingered in Elaria’s thoughts long after they’d left the Mirror Fen behind.

The landscape changed slowly. The wetlands gave way to dry hills speckled with scrub pine and leafless birches that rattled like bones in the wind. Roads grew rarer. Villages too. What homes they passed were shuttered, abandoned or hollowed by rot, their windows covered not with curtains but with sigils drawn in red wax and ash. The people here had seen something—or remembered something passed down in blood and nightmare.

On the twelfth day of their journey, they crossed an ancient stone bridge that spanned the River Thalmir. It groaned under their horses' weight but held. Beyond it, the hills sharpened into ridges and cliffs, and the trees began to whisper.

It began subtly at first—just the rustle of leaves when the wind had fallen still, or the sense that footsteps echoed with too many feet. Then came the words. Faint at first, overlapping, soft as breath: “Not yours… not yours… not yours…” Over and over, in a dozen voices, some familiar, some impossibly old. Brynn covered her ears and rode faster. Elion began to mumble prayers again, hands trembling. Even Ser Calen’s broad back grew hunched with tension.

Elaria tried to ignore them. The whispers clawed at the edges of her thoughts, tugging like nettles against cloth. Her mother’s voice echoed once—“Elaria, come back inside now.” Then another, deeper: “Daughter of the storm, break the seal and be undone.”

“We’re being watched,” Linna said flatly that night, as they set camp on a narrow shelf of land beneath a tall red cliff. “Not by beasts. By the Vale itself. It doesn’t want us here.”

“We need to go further,” Kael insisted, spreading out his maps. “The Codex places the point of convergence at the heart of the Vale—here.” He tapped a crescent-shaped hollow nestled between the peaks.

Elaria leaned over the parchment. “And what is supposed to be there?”

Kael hesitated. “The Amber Spire.”

The name meant nothing to the others, but it pulled at Elaria like a memory. She sat back, remembering the dream in the Mirror Tower—the sky of fire, the mirror, the shard that glowed not silver, but amber. The same hue as the stone now etched into her palm where the shard had burned her.

The next morning, they found the first marker.

It was a stone monolith, six feet tall and veined with gold, half-buried in moss at the center of a narrow glade. The wind here was still, and the whispers ceased as they approached. The stone pulsed faintly. A rune spiraled its surface—one Kael didn’t recognize.

“I think it’s a wayfinder,” he murmured. “A tether between planes. Old sorcery. Maybe older than the Codex.”

As he reached out to touch it, Elion grabbed his wrist. “No. This is not a map. It’s a warning. It’s been sealed for a reason.”

“It’s been forgotten,” Kael retorted. “And that’s the difference. We’re not here to preserve rot—we’re here to understand what was lost.”

Elaria stepped between them. “Enough. We go forward. But we don’t touch anything unless I say so.”

Tension sparked between them, but Kael relented, and they continued deeper into the Vale.

The land began to warp as they pressed on. Distance stretched. A mile felt like three. Trees turned inward, their branches entwining overhead to blot out the sun. Shadows lingered too long. At times, they came upon strange phenomena: a pool where birds flew backward, a ring of stones around a tree that wept clear oil, and once, a hare that spoke in a child’s voice and fled into the underbrush laughing.

Brynn grew pale and withdrawn. Elion’s chants turned frantic. Linna refused to sleep.

And then, finally, they saw it.

The Amber Spire rose from the earth like a finger of light frozen in glass. It stood alone at the center of the crescent valley, surrounded by a ring of dead trees, each one blackened and brittle as coal. The tower shimmered, not with light, but with memory—images flickered across its surface like trapped echoes: a battle in a field of stars, a woman burning in gold fire, a dragon devouring a moon.

They dismounted slowly. No one spoke.

The air was different here. Thin, dry, and heavy with the scent of citrus and ash. Elaria stepped forward, shard in hand. The mark on her palm glowed in response, and the spire pulsed once, like a heart recognizing its kin.

“The tower’s not sealed,” Kael said, staring up at it in wonder. “But it should be.”

“Why?” Elaria asked.

“Because this was not built to hold something in. It was built to hold someone out.”

Inside, the air was crystalline and silent. Light filtered in from no discernible source. The walls gleamed with translucent panels of amber, within which shapes moved—faces, hands, fragments of motion, like insects frozen in sap. A grand staircase spiraled around the inner walls, rising into darkness.

They climbed.

The whispers returned, stronger than before, now shaped like voices they knew—her father calling out in warning, Kael’s mentor crying in pain, Brynn’s mother singing an old lullaby. Elaria staggered once, clutching her head. She saw herself standing over a burning field, crown aflame, eyes blank.

Kael caught her. “It’s illusion. Memory spun into thread. You must not listen.”

“I don’t want to see it.”

“You don’t have to believe it. That’s how it feeds.”

They pressed on.

At the top of the spire was a chamber made of dark glass. At its center, a dais held a second shard—larger than the first, this one shaped like a spearhead. Around it, three figures stood in silence. They were not statues. They were alive, but unmoving—veiled in robes of smoke and gold, faces hidden beneath lacquered masks.

One turned its head toward them.

“You should not be here,” it said.

Elaria drew her sword. “We seek the shards. We seek to seal the Eye.”

“There is no sealing what has already awakened.”

“Then we fight.”

“No,” said the second figure, its voice layered with strange harmonics. “You choose.”

“What do you mean?”

“The Mirror Tower. The Amber Spire. The Pale Gate. Three keys. Three locks. But each taken breaks the seal further. Only one may hold.”

Kael stepped forward, voice tight. “The Codex never mentioned this.”

“The Codex is incomplete. You cannot shut a door that was never yours to open.”

Elaria looked to the shard on the dais. It pulsed like the one before, but warmer—hungrier.

She stepped forward.

“I will take it,” she said.

“Then understand: every choice has a cost. And this cost will be memory.”

As her fingers closed around the shard, a blinding light erupted from the floor.

And she remembered.

She was ten again, standing in the east courtyard, watching her mother argue with her father. She was fifteen, riding alone under moonlight, tears streaming from a name she could not forget. She was twenty, leading soldiers against a faceless horde, fire all around, the sky bleeding stars.

Then—emptiness.

Half her childhood faded. Faces blurred. The pain remained, but the names were gone.

She gasped as she fell back. Kael caught her again, staring at her with wide eyes. “Elaria?”

“I… I don’t remember my mother’s face.”

The shard in her hand burned with golden fire.

And the veil between worlds thinned a little more.

—

The descent from the Amber Spire was not marked by celebration, only silence. Elaria moved like someone half-formed, her eyes distant and unfocused, the golden shard clutched tightly in her gloved hand. She had wrapped it in cloth, but its warmth still radiated through the layers, a pulse against her skin that seemed to echo her heartbeat. The others noticed her change. They watched her with wary glances and unspoken questions, but none dared ask aloud what price she had paid for the shard—or how much of her had been left behind in that glass chamber.

Brynn was the first to break the silence that followed their return to the valley floor. She moved beside Elaria as they walked through the ashen grove that surrounded the spire, stepping carefully over the blackened roots of the dead trees.

“You don’t remember, do you?” she asked softly.

Elaria didn’t answer right away. When she did, her voice was like gravel dragged across stone. “No. Not all of it.”

Brynn lowered her gaze. “When we were children, you and I used to sneak into the east garden to steal apples from the royal orchard. Your mother caught us once. She made us eat every single one we took until we were sick.”

Elaria paused in her stride, brow furrowed. “I remember the orchard. The apple trees. But not… her. Just laughter. A shadow in sunlight.”

“It was early summer. You were barefoot, I remember. Your hair was down.”

Elaria looked at her with a fragile sort of gratitude. “Thank you.”

Kael came up behind them then, flipping through the pages of his journal with growing frustration. “This complicates everything,” he muttered. “If only one lock can hold, and we now possess two shards, we might have already doomed the cycle.”

“Unless the Deep King hasn’t yet crossed the threshold,” Elion said from where he rode at the rear. His voice was hoarse, but steadier than it had been in days. “If the seals are staggered—one for each phase of his awakening—we might still be in the second stage.”

“Speculation,” Kael muttered, slapping the journal closed. “And thin speculation at that.”

Ser Calen halted his horse on the ridge just ahead and pointed down into the valley they were approaching. “Whatever time we have left, we’ll be using it in this place. That smoke—it’s not campfire.”

Elaria followed his gesture.

Below, nestled in the crags of the Whispering Vale, was a village she hadn’t expected to see. It was not marked on any of their maps. From a distance, it seemed abandoned—roofs sagging, gates hanging askew, windows open like broken mouths. But smoke rose in lazy curls from three chimneys. No movement. No sound.

They descended slowly, weapons close at hand. The path wound through steep rock and spindly trees until the village rose around them like a trap that had already been sprung. The signs of life were clear—clothes hung on lines, food lay half-eaten on tables, and a goat tethered to a post blinked lazily at their arrival.

But there were no people.

“This isn’t right,” Linna murmured. “This isn’t natural.”

Kael approached the nearest house, a squat stone structure with a thatched roof and wooden shutters, all ajar. Inside, the table was set for a meal. Plates still held spoons and dried crusts of bread. The fire in the hearth had burned down to embers, but hadn’t been completely extinguished.

“They left in a hurry,” he called back.

“No,” Elion corrected grimly as he emerged from another doorway. “They didn’t leave at all. They’re still here.”

He pointed to the ground.

There, in the soft dirt between houses, was a series of shallow impressions—human-shaped outlines pressed into the earth as if someone had lain down and simply… evaporated. Around the edges of the impressions, grass grew thick and dark, tinged with an oily sheen. Some of the shapes were curled in fetal positions. Others were sprawled, arms outstretched as if reaching for something.

Brynn turned away, pressing her knuckles to her mouth.

Kael knelt beside one of the impressions. “There’s no blood. No signs of trauma. Whatever did this—it took them without force.”

“It called them,” Elion said. “That’s what this feels like. A summons. Like the Vale itself devoured them.”

Elaria stood very still, the shard in her hand growing hotter with each passing moment. “It’s reacting. The shard.”

“To what?” Ser Calen asked.

“To something beneath us.”

They found the entrance to the hollow just before dusk, concealed behind an old well in the center of the village. The well’s stones were worn smooth, but the pulley and bucket were untouched, as if no one had drawn water from it in decades. Elaria felt the pull the moment she stepped near it. The golden shard vibrated softly, and the mark on her palm throbbed in answer.

“It’s not just a well,” she said. “It’s a threshold.”

Kael examined the rim. “The runes etched along the inner stones—they’re binding marks. Very old. Some look… broken.”

Ser Calen swung his lantern low. “Only one way to find out what’s inside.”

They descended with torches and ropes, the well widening after the first twenty feet into a narrow vertical shaft that dropped deep into the earth. The air grew warmer the farther they went. At the bottom, the passage opened into a vast underground chamber—part natural cave, part carved tunnel, its walls lined with fossilized roots and amber-like growths that pulsed with faint internal light.

The air shimmered. Elaria felt her vision double for a heartbeat, then clear.

“What is this place?” Brynn whispered.

Kael was already scrawling notes. “Some sort of memory vault. Maybe an old sanctum for the Mirror cult. These amber nodes—they’re holding something.”

Elaria approached one, peering into its glowing heart. Inside, barely visible, was a face—eyes wide in terror, mouth open in a scream. It was frozen mid-moment, captured in perfect clarity, like an insect in resin.

She backed away, bile rising in her throat. “They’re not just memories. They’re people.”

“Preserved,” Kael murmured. “Or trapped.”

Elion’s face twisted. “This is a prison.”

“And it’s still functioning,” Linna added. “Which means it’s being maintained. Or fed.”

A sound echoed through the chamber then—not a scream, not quite. More like the groan of an ancient tree snapping under its own weight. The ground vibrated beneath their feet. Dust fell from the ceiling.

Elaria turned slowly toward the sound.

At the far end of the chamber, something was moving.

A figure—no, a mass—was unfolding from the wall. It was humanoid in shape, but impossibly tall, its skin glistening like wet wax, its eyes twin orbs of molten gold. It moved with the slowness of thought, each motion deliberate, inevitable.

“The Warden,” Kael breathed. “They spoke of it in the oldest texts. Guardian of the vault. Servant of the Seal.”

The creature opened its mouth—not in speech, but in resonance. A deep hum filled the chamber, vibrating through bone and thought alike. The amber nodes flared in answer, and the trapped faces began to twitch, to move, to mouth words none could hear.

“Back!” Ser Calen roared, stepping in front of the others.

Elaria raised the shard. It blazed in her hand, a searing light that momentarily held the Warden at bay. The creature recoiled, not in fear, but in recognition. It stepped forward again, slower now, its head tilting as it regarded her.

“You,” it said, voice like rock grinding against itself. “Bearer of the Second Flame. You walk a path not meant for mortal flesh.”

Elaria met its gaze. “I don’t care what path I was meant for. I’m walking it.”

“You are breaking it.”

“Then tell me how to fix it.”

The Warden paused, golden eyes flickering.

“There is one shard left. The Pale Gate. Beyond the Crown of Winds. Take it, and the Eye closes forever. But only if the others remain untouched. Two shards burn. A third will break the weave.”

“Then we leave the others?” Kael asked, voice rising. “Just abandon the seals?”

“Choose one. Lose the others. The Deep King stirs. The Eye cannot be locked again unless the Gate is whole.”

“What happens if we fail?” Brynn whispered.

The Warden said nothing. It turned, slowly, and walked back into the amber wall, fading like mist into stone.

Elaria looked down at the shard in her palm.

It was no longer warm.

It was ice.

They left the vault before dawn. No one spoke much. Each step out of the hollow felt heavier than the one before. The village above remained empty, silent, frozen in its abandoned stillness. And as they rode out of the Whispering Vale, Elaria knew they carried something with them.

A burden.

A countdown.

A choice.

Far to the north, the Crown of Winds waited—where the Pale Gate would reveal the final piece.

And somewhere beyond that, the Eye was opening.

—

Chapter Four: The Crown of Winds

The wind began to shift before they saw the mountains. It came first as a low moan rolling over the plains—soft, mournful, and unrelenting. Then it grew teeth. By the time they reached the foothills of the northern range, the air had turned bitter, and the horses struggled against gusts strong enough to peel skin. Snow did not fall. The sky remained stubbornly clear. But the wind came in waves, each colder than the last, whistling through jagged stones like the voice of an old god growing impatient.

The locals called this place the Crown of Winds, a name as much earned as given. Jagged peaks encircled a vast frozen basin like the rim of a shattered crown, their tops lost in perpetual clouds. Villages once dotted the lower slopes, trading in ice, herbs, and mountain ore. Now there were only ruins—scattered stones, blackened timber, and the bones of houses swallowed by snowdrifts.

It took them five days to ascend into the highlands. Progress was slow and brutal. The terrain was unforgiving—narrow ledges, hidden crevices, and trails that vanished without warning into sheer drops. The horses could go no farther by the fourth morning, and they were forced to leave them in a makeshift pen beneath a cliff’s overhang. From that point on, they moved on foot, wrapped in furs and silence.

The cold affected each of them differently. Brynn grew quieter, retreating into herself. Linna grew sharper, her instincts on edge, bow in hand at all times. Ser Calen endured the climb with grim stoicism, refusing to show any sign of discomfort. Elion, always the weakest of them, suffered the most—his lips cracked, breath shallow, eyes red from windburn.

Only Kael seemed unaffected, as if the storm were no more than an annoyance. He moved with singular focus, his gaze always fixed on the horizon, journal clutched in his gloved hands. Elaria kept pace beside him, her golden shard tucked safely beneath her cloak, though she often felt it pulse against her chest like a second heart.

They crested the final ridge on the sixth day and saw it—half-buried in snow, rising from a plateau of ice like the crown jewel of the range.

The Pale Gate.

It was not a tower, as she had imagined, nor a spire or fortress. It was a circle of stone arches, ancient and cracked, standing in a perfect ring at the summit of the world. In the center, a thin veil of light shimmered, like the surface of a still lake. The wind screamed as it passed through the arches, creating a keening that clawed at the mind.

Kael dropped to his knees, overcome. “It’s real. By the stars, it’s real.”

Ser Calen’s hand was already on his sword. “I don’t like the feel of it. It’s not just old. It’s alive.”

“No,” Elion corrected, voice barely above a whisper. “It’s awake.”

Elaria stepped forward, her eyes locked on the central veil. She felt the pull immediately—stronger than anything before. This wasn’t like the Mirror Tower or the Amber Spire. The shards had beckoned. This… this commanded.

The moment she crossed the threshold of the outer ring, everything changed.

The wind vanished. The noise dropped away as if someone had thrown a shroud over the world. The sky beyond the arches darkened, colors bleeding from the clouds. The veil of light in the center rippled once, and then showed her a vision.

Not a dream. A memory.

She stood in Tharros, but not as it was now. The castle burned. The sky was red. Shadows poured over the parapets like water through broken stone. Her father lay dying in the courtyard, a sword through his side. Her brother fought in vain against a beast made of wings and teeth and sorrow. And there—atop the tower—stood a figure wearing her face.

It was her.

Crowned in ice and gold.

Her eyes were dead.

And she was smiling.

The vision snapped away.

She staggered back, out of the ring. Kael caught her before she fell.

“What did you see?” he asked urgently.

Elaria shook her head. “A future. Or a warning. I don’t know anymore.”

Brynn moved forward, her voice trembling. “Is that… is that the Gate itself?”

Kael stood, brushing frost from his knees. “No. It’s a mirror of possibility. The true Gate lies beyond that veil. The last shard is within.”

Ser Calen growled. “Then let’s take it and finish this.”

Elaria raised her hand. “No. The Warden said the third shard would break the weave. That only one lock can hold.”

“Then we’re out of time,” Kael said. “Because something is coming.”

They felt it then. A tremor beneath the ice. Distant, at first—like the footfalls of something enormous walking beneath the world. It grew stronger with every heartbeat. The arches began to tremble. The light in the veil flickered.

A scream echoed through the air—not human. Not beast. It sounded like a language that had no tongue to speak it.

Elaria turned to the group.

“We have one chance. I go through. Alone.”

“No,” Brynn said, stepping forward. “We go together.”

“If the shard breaks the seals—if I fail—it needs to be only me. The rest of you need to carry what we’ve learned back to Tharros. Warn them. Prepare.”

Kael opened his mouth to argue, but she silenced him with a look.

“I chose this when I took the first shard,” she said. “I’ll finish it.”

Without another word, she stepped into the veil.

The world turned inside out.

She fell—no, shifted—through a space that wasn’t space, a moment that stretched and folded like parchment on fire. Light twisted around her. Time blurred. She emerged into silence.

She stood at the center of a vast, empty plain. The sky was silver. The ground was glass. There were no stars. No sun. Only the Gate.

It rose before her—a monolith of pure white stone, carved with shifting glyphs that rearranged themselves even as she tried to read them. At its base, a pedestal. And on that pedestal, the final shard.

This one was pale—almost colorless—but not dead. It pulsed like breath. It shimmered with a light that came from within.

As she reached for it, the ground beneath her cracked.

A figure stepped from the Gate.

He wore no armor. He bore no crown. But she knew him instantly.

The Deep King.

Not a creature of horror. Not a beast. A man—tall, beautiful, with eyes like dying stars and a voice like velvet over razors.

“You would defy me,” he said.

“I will stop you.”

“You cannot stop what you are becoming.”

Elaria’s fingers closed around the shard.

And the world screamed.

The ground split. Light poured up. The Gate exploded outward in shards of energy that struck the sky like lightning. The Deep King vanished.

And Elaria fell again.

This time, she saw herself not as she was, but as she could be.

Whole.

Or broken.

She opened her eyes on the snowy plateau.

The veil was gone.

The arches stood empty.

The Gate had been sealed—or destroyed.

The others rushed to her, relief and terror warring in their eyes.

“Did it work?” Brynn asked.

Elaria didn’t know.

She held up the pale shard.

It was dark.

No light. No pulse.

Only silence.

That night, they did not sleep. The storm broke above them. Snow fell, thick and blinding. But beneath it, the wind no longer howled with hunger. It moaned with mourning.

Kael sat beside her, watching the fire. “You did it.”

“I’m not sure what I did,” Elaria said. “But the world feels different now.”

“Maybe that’s enough.”

She looked at the three shards in her satchel. One burned. One glowed. One was still.

Three pieces of a truth she had only begun to understand.

The Eye was closed.

But the cost had not yet revealed itself.

And some doors, once opened, never fully shut.

—

The storm that swept through the Crown of Winds lingered for two days. Snow piled high against the stone arches, burying the path that had led them there. They huddled in a hollowed-out cavern in the cliffside, lighting small, reluctant fires with what little dry wood they had left. The cold crept in no matter how tightly they wrapped themselves. It pressed against the walls and whispered through the cracks. Even with the Gate sealed—or shattered, they could not yet tell—something old still stirred in the wind.

Elaria spoke little during those days. She sat apart from the others, the pale shard cupped in her hands. It had not changed since she took it. Unlike the golden shard, which now pulsed softly when she drew near the amber one, this third piece of the cycle was inert, dull. No glow, no warmth. It felt like a husk. But Elaria couldn’t shake the feeling that it wasn’t dead. It was waiting.

Kael made the first attempt at explanation. He laid out his books, copied fragments of runes, re-translated what the Codex had said about the nature of seals. “Maybe the shards aren’t locks after all,” he said one morning, breath fogging in the cold air. “Maybe they’re conduits. Refractions of something larger. And by taking all three, maybe we didn’t break the weave—we became it.”

“That’s not comforting,” Elion muttered, eyes hollow with fatigue. “That means she’s the seal now.”

Elaria didn’t respond. She could feel something building in her—just beneath the skin, behind the eyes. Not madness. Not power. A kind of awareness. Like a door in her mind had opened slightly, and through it blew a breeze from a place without name.

On the third morning, the snow stopped.

The silence that followed was heavier than the wind. A sky of milky white stretched above them, flat and depthless, as if painted on canvas.

Ser Calen led them down from the mountain. The trail was easier on the descent, though the cold had frozen the shale in jagged formations, and more than once they had to carve their way through the ice with blades. It was during one of these moments—while Calen split a path through the wall of frozen scree—that Brynn pulled Elaria aside.

“You haven’t told them what you saw,” she said, her voice low.

Elaria met her eyes, slow and deliberate. “No.”

“You saw what comes next, didn’t you? Beyond the Gate.”

“Not what comes next. What comes if we fail.”

“And the King?”

Elaria looked out over the blinding whiteness. “He isn’t what I thought. He’s not a conqueror. He doesn’t want a throne.”

“Then what does he want?”

She hesitated, then whispered, “Oblivion.”

They reached the lower ridges by sunset. Their horses had survived, huddled and frostbitten but alive. The return to the lower plains felt surreal, like waking from a dream that refused to loosen its hold. The world below had moved on without them. The skies were warmer. The wind gentler. Villages reappeared, though they were thinner, stranger. People looked at them with cautious eyes, as if sensing something otherworldly had clung to them on their journey.

In one town—Rosdrin’s Hollow—they stopped for supplies. The villagers were kind enough, but afraid. One woman, old and blind in one eye, approached Elaria at the edge of the well. “You carry the deep in your shadow,” she said. “The crown without the head. Be careful where you stand, or the world may tilt beneath your feet.”

Elaria thanked her and said nothing.

They left the town by midday, riding south.

That night, as the others slept, Elaria sat alone with the three shards spread before her. They looked harmless now—nothing more than stones etched by time. But when placed side by side, they formed a broken circle. Not perfect. Not symmetrical. But deliberate.

Kael stirred and joined her by the dying fire.

“You’re still awake,” he said softly.

“So are you.”

They sat together in silence. Somewhere far off, a bird cried—lonely, out of place in the springless sky.

“You know it’s not over,” Kael said finally.

Elaria nodded. “I felt something in the Gate. Something beneath even him.”

“The Deep King?”

“No. Older. I don’t have a name for it. But it saw me. Not through the Gate, but in it.”

Kael frowned. “The Codex doesn’t speak of anything beyond the Eye.”

“Maybe it didn’t know how.”

“Or maybe it was afraid.”

She looked down at the shards. “We’re not done.”

“No,” Kael said. “We’ve bought time. That’s all. The seals are holding, for now. But something is still pulling at the edges.”

Elaria drew in a breath, closed her eyes. The air shimmered faintly around her. She saw visions behind her eyelids: a tower of bone rising from the ocean; a black sun spinning above a frozen field; a hand made of flame reaching through a hole in the sky.

When she opened her eyes again, Kael was watching her with a look she didn’t recognize.

“What?”

“You’re not the same,” he said. “Since the Gate.”

“I know.”

“Are you still… you?”

“I hope so.”

Kael didn’t answer. He rose, dusted off his coat, and returned to his tent.

Elaria sat for a long time, watching the fire burn to embers.

In the morning, they would ride for Tharros. They had messages to deliver, warnings to carry, truths to tell.

But in the back of her mind, the Gate still shimmered. And in its reflection, the Deep King still waited.

He was patient.

He had all the time in the world.

And now, so did she.

—

The road to Tharros unwound like a scar across the land, a line of familiarity stretched thin across a landscape that no longer felt like home. The southern plains should have been green by now, fields thick with spring barley, orchards blooming with soft white petals. Instead, they found the land quiet, subdued. Trees bore no fruit. The grasses were brown at the edges. The air itself felt uncertain, as if the world held its breath for something yet to come.

They passed travelers who would not meet their eyes. A few bore signs of fleeing—bundles tied hastily, children asleep on backs, carts filled with what little they could carry. These people said little but shared stories in passing: strange tides pulling rivers upstream, villages losing time, dreams that didn’t feel like dreams. In one town, a shepherd told Kael he’d woken with black sand in his mouth and the sound of voices singing beneath the ground.

Elaria heard it all in silence. She recorded none of it, nor did she ask the others to. There were too many signs now, and they all pointed in one direction: the Eye might be closed, but the cracks in the world remained. Something deep was bleeding through.

They crossed the Thalden Bridge on the twelfth day, its pale stones still standing strong above the river they had last crossed months earlier. But even the water below had changed—its color darker, a hint of shadow running through its flow like ink. Elion, who had grown quieter with each passing league, paused on the bridge’s midpoint and touched the stone railing.

“There used to be birds here,” he said. “Do you remember?”

Linna looked up from adjusting her pack. “Sparrows. Thousands of them. They’d nest under the beams.”

“Now there’s nothing.”

Elaria didn’t speak. She was listening again—always listening now. Not for birds or wind or voices, but for that deeper murmur. A presence. She could no longer call it a sound, but it moved in the edges of her awareness like a distant tide pulling toward shore.

Kael rode up beside her later that day, pages fluttering in his lap as he re-read his notes. “We need to tell the king what we saw,” he said, keeping his voice low. “Not just the shards. Everything. The Vault. The Vale. The Gate.”

“He won’t believe us.”

“He has to. If Tharros isn’t preparing for war, then it’s preparing for ruin.”

“He’s already seen it,” Elaria said quietly. “Or felt it. He’s a king. He knows when the world is changing under his feet. He just doesn’t want to name it yet.”

Brynn, overhearing, joined them. “He’ll believe you. He saw what you did in the hall. He knows you’re not the same girl who wore velvet and played courtly games.”

“Maybe not,” Elaria said. “But he might think that girl was safer.”

They reached the gates of Tharros by dusk two days later. The city walls loomed tall and familiar, but the air around them was wrong. The guards at the outer gate bore new sigils on their armor—symbols for vigilance, for warding, and for remembrance. Their eyes were drawn. Tense. When they recognized Elaria, they stood straighter, but they did not smile.

The city within was quieter than they remembered. The markets still bustled, but the voices were subdued. Colors had dulled. Doors bore chalk markings. Taverns whispered curfews, and posters hung at every corner listing names of the missing: merchants, ship crews, children.

At the castle, they were ushered quickly to the king.

Elaria stepped into the throne room and found her father older than he had any right to be. His beard, once streaked with grey, was now wholly silver. His crown sat lower on his brow. His hands, though strong, trembled faintly against the arms of his chair.

He rose as she entered and stepped down from the dais without ceremony.

“Daughter,” he said. His voice cracked at the edge of the word. “You’ve returned.”

She bowed but did not kneel. “Not unchanged.”

“No,” he said. “That’s plain to all.”

He looked at the others, each of them gaunt, hardened, marked. Then back to her. “You have much to tell.”

“We do,” Kael said, stepping forward and unrolling a scroll. “But some of it is better shown than spoken.”

Kael placed the three shards on the floor before the throne. Each glowed faintly now, even the Pale one, which shimmered with a soft, pale blue light as if stirred from its dormancy.

The court murmured. Some of the high advisors stepped back.

The king narrowed his eyes. “What are they?”

“Locks,” Kael said. “Keys. Anchors. We still don’t fully understand their nature. But they’re pieces of something ancient. Something that holds back a force older than the empire, older than language.”

Elaria stepped in then, her voice calm. “We faced it. Not fully. But enough to know it cannot be defeated in war. This is not a battle of swords or numbers. It’s a wound in the world, and these are stitches made of flame and memory.”

King Maelen looked at her long and hard. “And what are you now, Elaria? What are you stitched with?”

She did not flinch. “Resolve.”

There was a long silence in the hall.

Then he nodded. “Then you’ll speak to the Circle.”

The Circle of Nine—Tharros’s highest council of mystics, lorekeepers, and seers—had not met in full session for two generations. They were relics of the Old Compact, held more for tradition than purpose. But now, summoned in full, they sat in the council chamber with books open, hands ink-stained, eyes sharper than their years suggested.

Elaria spoke of the Mirror Tower, the Amber Spire, the Pale Gate. She told of the Deep King and the Warden, of the shards and the vaults, and of the choice she had made when she touched the third shard.

When she finished, the chamber was utterly silent.

Then a woman with silver eyes—High Arcanist Vessa—stood. “You’ve walked the axis of the world,” she said. “And brought back its price. But the wheel still turns.”

Kael leaned forward. “What do you mean?”

Vessa pointed to the shards. “There are three. But the Codex once spoke of four.”

Kael’s face drained of color. “The fourth. I thought that was myth.”

“Not myth,” Vessa said. “Consequence.”

Elaria stood. “Where is it?”

“Where it has always been,” she said. “At the heart of the sea.”

The court erupted in protest. One advisor shouted about treason. Another about madness. But Elaria didn’t listen. Her mind was already moving. She could feel the fourth piece waking. It was deep. It was vast. And it was calling.

The king rose slowly. “And if it is found?”

“It ends,” Vessa said simply.

“Ends how?” Brynn asked. “The Eye? The world?”

“No one can say,” the Arcanist replied. “That is why it was never sought.”

Elaria turned to her father. “Then we’ll be the first.”

He met her gaze. “You may not return.”

“I already haven’t,” she said. “Not as I was.”

And this time, he did not argue.

That night, Elaria stood on the highest tower of Tharros, watching the moon trace a pale arc over the ocean. The wind off the sea was no longer cold—it was heavy, charged, filled with something she couldn’t name. Behind her, the city lights flickered like dying stars.

Kael joined her a few minutes later. He said nothing at first, only looked out at the black horizon.

“You believe there’s a fourth,” she said.

“I believe you were meant to find it.”

“What if it breaks me?”

“It already has. The question is whether you’ll become something better… or something worse.”

She nodded.

“The sea doesn’t forgive,” Kael said softly. “But sometimes, it gives back what was taken.”

They watched the waves roll in, steady and slow.

Tomorrow, they would sail.

And beneath the surface, the Eye would open once more.

Chapter Five: Beneath the Silent Deep

The ship they boarded was unlike any vessel docked in Tharros’s harbors. Sleek and narrow, its hull lined with black ironwood and glyphs etched in salt-rusted silver, it was built for deep waters and darker purposes. It bore no name on its prow, only a sigil in the shape of an eye partially submerged beneath three wavy lines. Elaria recognized the mark from a page in Kael’s journal—a forgotten house that once served as sea-wardens, oath-bound to guard the gate beneath the waves.

The ship’s captain was a woman called Mereth. She had skin like old parchment, hair coiled into braids the color of stormclouds, and eyes too pale to be trusted. When they showed her the three shards, she said only, “I’ll take you where the sea stops lying. After that, it’s between you and the deep.”

They left at dawn.

The waters beyond the bay were calm at first. The sails filled with a steady wind, and the horizon stretched smooth and featureless under a sky the color of smelted silver. But after the third day, the weather turned.

Clouds gathered. Not storm clouds, but something heavier. They hung low and unmoving, casting a strange, dim light over the waves. The sea changed, too. The surface grew sluggish, thickened. Birds vanished from the sky. Fish no longer jumped. The air tasted of copper and memory.

“Dead waters,” Mereth muttered. “We’ve crossed into it now.”

“Dead to what?” Brynn asked.

“Dead to time. Dead to the gods. The Deep remembers nothing, and it permits less.”

On the sixth day, they saw it.

Kael was the first to cry out. He pointed across the deck, and the others followed his gaze. Rising from the water ahead was a stone pillar, impossibly tall, covered in spiral carvings and wrapped in chains. More pillars followed, a scattered ring of them breaking the ocean’s surface like the spines of a drowned colossus.

They were at the edge of something old.

“The sea vault,” Kael said, half-whispering, awestruck. “It’s real.”

Mereth anchored the ship between the southernmost spires. “This is as close as we go,” she said. “No wind beyond this line. No surface. Only descent.”

Linna turned to Elaria. “How deep is it?”

Elaria had no answer. She felt the shards in her satchel shift, the golden one humming, the amber flickering, the pale one cold and sharp. Together, they pulsed in time with something vast and slow beneath them.

Elion and Kael prepared the diving vessel—a sort of glass-walled chamber suspended by chains and reinforced with binding runes. It had been built in secret by Tharros’s Arcanists for another purpose entirely, but now it served this final one. Two people could descend. No more.

“I’ll go with you,” Kael said.

“No,” Elaria said firmly. “This is mine.”

“You don’t know what’s waiting.”

“Neither do you. That’s why you need to stay. If I don’t come back, someone must remember what happened.”

Kael clenched his fists, jaw tight. But he did not argue.

Brynn helped her don the water armor—woven kelpsteel and rune-threaded linen, meant to shield the body from the crush of pressure and the pull of deeper things. She hugged Elaria before sealing the final latch. “If you see her… your mother… tell her you remember the orchard.”

Elaria nodded.

She stepped into the chamber.

The descent began.

The ocean closed above her like a mouth.

At first, there was light—shafts of sunlight slanting through the blue, illuminating trails of drifting plankton and distant silver fins. But with each foot of descent, the light faded, and the water thickened, grew darker, until there was only the slow pulse of the shard in her chest and the faint glow of the runes etched along the chamber walls.

She passed through layers of cold. Passed schools of silent fish that didn’t blink. Passed structures half-formed, coral-wrapped statues with faces like hers. Passed fields of broken swords and shields drifting on invisible currents. Memories, all of them. Echoes preserved in salt.

And then the chamber stopped.

Not because it reached the bottom—there was no bottom. It simply stopped descending. Outside, the water no longer moved. It had become still, more like air than sea. A plane of suspension.

And in the center of that suspended world was the Gate.

It floated in the dark, a ring of obsidian etched with light, turning slowly. Inside the ring, nothing. A blackness that pulled even thought toward it. It was not absence. It was not emptiness. It was completion. The final shard.

The fourth.

Elaria opened the chamber.

The water did not flood in.

Instead, she stepped into silence, floating across a floor that wasn’t there, toward a light that didn’t glow. The three shards floated free of her cloak and arranged themselves around her—gold, amber, pale—and as they aligned, the fourth appeared.

It was black.

But not the black of shadow or night.

The black of forgetting.

She reached for it.

And it spoke.

“Name yourself.”

“I am Elaria of Tharros. Daughter of storm. Bearer of three shards.”

“You are not whole.”

“I am what remains.”

“And what will you give?”

She did not hesitate. “Whatever it takes.”

The fourth shard drifted into her palm.

She screamed.

Not in pain. In knowing.

The Gate shattered.

The ring disintegrated into stars. Light tore across the ocean, up through the layers of depth, into the world above. The chamber cracked. The runes broke. The sea roared in silence.

And something rose.

The Deep King. No longer a man. No longer a memory. Now vast. Now real. He opened eyes that split the water like thunder.

“You bring me the key,” he said.

“I bring you the end.”

“You do not understand.”

“I do now.”

She held up the four shards.

They merged.

One stone.

One truth.

One final seal.

And she pressed it into the heart of the rising king.

Light exploded.

When she awoke, she lay on the deck of the ship.

The sky was blue.

The water was calm.

The ring of spires was gone.

So was the captain.

So was the Deep.

Kael was leaning over her, hands shaking. “You came back,” he said.

Elaria blinked. “Not all of me.”

She looked at the horizon. Something in her chest pulsed once, then stopped.

It was done.

For now.

But she knew the world had changed.

And someday, when the wind stopped whispering, the sea would speak again.

—

The days following their return from the depths passed in fragments, like pieces of a mirror shattered and swept across a floor. Elaria remembered them in glimpses: the ship sailing over unnaturally calm waters, the whisperless wind, the silence of the spires no longer standing. She remembered the look on Kael’s face when he helped her from the broken remains of the diving vessel—both awe and sorrow. She remembered Brynn’s arms around her, the weight of familiarity pulling her back to the world of breath and names. But above all, she remembered the sensation of the fourth shard vanishing into her palm like a coal dissolving into snow.

They did not speak of what had happened beneath the water, not at first. Not until they’d cleared the Dead Waters and the sea began to behave like sea again. Then, and only then, did the others begin to ask questions.

“What did you see?” Elion asked one night, his voice thin and steady. “What was beyond the Gate?”

Elaria sat at the prow, legs tucked beneath her, eyes fixed on the stars. “Nothing,” she said. “And everything. The fourth shard wasn’t a lock. It was a mirror. A perfect one. It showed me what I was… and what I could never be again.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“No,” she agreed. “But it’s the only truth I have left.”

Kael approached slowly, the pages of his journal clutched tightly in one hand. He sat beside her, glancing up at the sails and the wind that now moved them without protest. “You were in the water for more than an hour. We thought we’d lost you. But then the shards flared. The entire sea lit up from beneath like someone had struck the ocean with lightning.”

“It wasn’t me,” she said quietly. “It was the Gate. It chose to close.”

Kael looked down at his notes, flipping to a half-finished sketch of the four shards fused together, ringed with runes that didn’t quite follow any known pattern. “And yet the Deep King rose. That part wasn’t in any prophecy. None of the lore ever spoke of him becoming real, not like that.”

“He wasn’t born of prophecy,” she said. “He was born of forgetting. The shards were what held the world’s memory together. Each one sealed away not just magic or power, but the memory of what had once tried to devour everything.”

“So what happens now?” Brynn asked, joining them. She sat cross-legged, her face more weathered than when they’d left Tharros, but her voice clear. “If the Gate is gone, the shards are merged, and he’s… what, dead?”

“No,” Elaria said. “He’s not dead. He’s returned to the place where gods go to sleep. And if we’re careless—if we forget what was done—he’ll wake again.”

Silence settled among them, the kind of silence that doesn’t demand an answer, only acknowledgment.

The ship reached Tharros by the eighth day. From a distance, the city looked untouched—its towers unbroken, its walls strong, its harbors bustling with sails. But as they docked, the signs were there. People walked more slowly. Priests stood at every corner, offering charms against dreams. Soldiers bore new crests—runes of vigilance and remembrance. The sea had changed them too.

The king welcomed them in private, behind closed doors with only three advisors present. His face was gaunter, his shoulders slouched as though even the crown had grown heavier. He rose when Elaria entered, and for a moment he simply looked at her, seeing something he could not name in her eyes.

“You carry silence like a mantle,” he said.

“I carry what you told me to find,” she replied, placing the fused shard—now a dull, grey-white stone—on the table between them.

He didn’t reach for it. No one did.

Kael gave the report. He spoke of the pillars, the descent, the Gate, the fourth shard. He explained how the memory of the world had been fragmented across the locks, how the Deep King fed not on blood or conquest but on the forgetting of truth. When he finished, no one spoke.

Finally, Maelen asked, “And is it over?”

“For now,” Elaria said. “But we must remember. That is the price. Not vigilance. Not war. Memory.”

The king dismissed the advisors.

When they were alone, he stepped close to her. “You’re not my daughter anymore, are you?”

Elaria did not look away. “I am. And I’m not. I remember her. But I also remember something else now. Something too old for a name.”

“You frighten them.”

“I should.”

He sighed. “And what of you? Do you frighten yourself?”

“Yes,” she said. “But I know how to carry it now.”

In the weeks that followed, Tharros changed.

The Shard Vault was constructed beneath the western wing of the castle—no magic, no keys, no enchantments. Just stone, steel, and the vow of ten witnesses sworn to memory. The fused shard was placed at its center, not locked away, but held in reverence. Every ten days, a different member of the Circle of Nine stood vigil beside it. Elaria herself took the first watch.

Kael published the first of his volumes—Chronicle of the Gate: What Was Remembered—and sent copies to every known university and archive from the Icebound Sanctum to the Desert of Keshar. Some called it myth. Others believed. But it did not matter. The record was planted.

Brynn returned to her family’s orchard and began replanting what had withered. Elion traveled east to a monastery built along the edge of a cliff, where he said the sea sounded less angry. Linna disappeared north into the mountains, leaving only a note: If they wake again, I’ll be waiting where the wind still sings.

Elaria remained in Tharros.

She took no title. No crown. But people bowed to her all the same. Children whispered her name and old seers touched their temples when she passed. The priests offered her seats at their tables. She declined. She preferred the ramparts, the libraries, and the shoreline where the sea spoke in its eternal, quiet rhythm.

One night, Kael found her there.

“You were right,” he said.

“About what?”

“We didn’t seal a door. We became the door.”

Elaria looked out over the waves. “Then we’ll need to stand open. And never close.”

He nodded. “You’ll write it down, won’t you? Your own version?”

“I already have,” she said. “But not on paper.”

Kael tilted his head. “Then where?”

She tapped her chest, where the scar of the final shard pulsed faintly beneath her ribs. “Here. It’s the only place that remembers everything.”

The waves rolled in.

And far below, in the deep places where memory was once broken, the world remembered itself whole again.

Chapter Six: The Thorned Crown

Spring returned to Tharros not with flowers or festivals, but with rain—steady, chill, and gray. The city glistened beneath its curtain of water, rooftops dark with wet slate, courtyards full of shallow pools, gutters murmuring like secret mouths. It was not the kind of season that lent itself to joy, yet the people moved with quiet resolve. The old rites had returned, altered but not abandoned. At the southern gate, lanterns burned through the night not to ward off beasts, but to remind passersby to remember. The palace banners had changed too. Where once the royal crest stood alone, now it shared space with a four-fold mark: gold, amber, white, and black, braided like a cord.

The world was not healed. But it was holding.

Elaria stood at the edge of the courtyard that had once been her childhood refuge. The cherry trees were slow to bloom this year. Their branches, though heavy with buds, resisted the coaxing touch of sun and wind. She placed her fingers lightly against the bark of the oldest one, its trunk scored by time and lightning.

“I used to climb this,” she said aloud.

She wasn’t speaking to anyone in particular, but Kael was there anyway, watching her from the shade of the colonnade. He approached carefully, his boots silent against the damp flagstones.

“It’s still standing,” he said. “Even after everything.”

“I wonder if it remembers me.”

“You’ve changed,” he admitted. “But you’re still you.”

Elaria turned her head toward him, her expression unreadable. “Am I?”

He didn’t answer immediately. They stood in silence, broken only by the slow rhythm of rain on stone. Eventually, he said, “A new word is forming in the far libraries. One the seers are whispering. Not Deep King. Not Gateborn. Something else. Something older.”

Elaria’s hand fell from the tree. “Names again.”

“They matter. A name can bind. Or it can free.”

She turned and walked toward him. “Then what are they calling me now?”

Kael hesitated. “The Thorned Crown.”

She laughed—quietly, bitterly. “Fitting.”

“It’s not an accusation. It’s awe.”

“I don’t want awe, Kael. I want truth.”

“Then here it is,” he said, voice firmer now. “The people believe you saved them. Even if they don’t understand what from. The Council fears you. The priests revere you. And the king… he no longer knows how to treat you.”

“I don’t want a throne,” she said.

“I believe you.”

“But they’re building one anyway, aren’t they?”

Kael’s silence confirmed it.

A throne of black stone and silver roots had appeared in the Hall of Stars three days prior. No one claimed to have commissioned it. No masons recalled carving it. It had simply been there when the hall was opened for court. Some called it a gift. Others, a warning.

It had thorns.

Not sharp enough to draw blood, but just enough to hurt if you sat without care.

That night, thunder rolled in from the east.

Elaria stood on the parapets, hair slick with rain, cloak billowing like smoke in the wind. The city stretched beneath her—quiet, gleaming, full of lamplight and worry. She felt the shards even now, though they were no longer separate. They were part of her, woven into the fabric of her memory, shaping her dreams and her waking moments alike. Sometimes she heard echoes of the Deep King’s voice in the crashing of waves. Sometimes she felt the pull of the Gate like a thread stitched through her soul.

But tonight, she felt something new.

It came on the wind—not a threat, not quite a promise. A presence. Not beneath the sea or within the broken weave, but high above. Beyond the storm. A watching force. Not hungry. Not angry. Curious.

She narrowed her eyes toward the lightning-slashed clouds.

“I know you,” she whispered. “And you’re waiting.”

The next morning, she summoned the Council.

The throne remained empty as she entered. Her father was absent—retired, in all but name, to the eastern wing, where he sat with old books and older regrets. The lords and advisors gathered in tense silence as Elaria stood at the center of the room. No crown. No sword. Only truth.

“The seals are holding,” she said. “The shards are united. The Gate beneath the sea is dormant. But we all feel it. The world is still watching. Something else is stirring.”

The High Arcanist leaned forward. “What kind of stirring?”

“Not destruction,” Elaria said. “Change. There are places now where the rules of things have softened. Valleys where time slips. Forests where the dead whisper back. Dreams that don’t belong to the dreamer.”

Another lord spoke. “You would have us prepare for war again?”

“No,” she said. “I would have us prepare for understanding. Build shrines not to fear, but to memory. Let the world remember what it was. Let it choose what it becomes.”

“And what of you?” a woman asked. “What role do you play in this new order?”

Elaria met her gaze. “I remember. That is enough.”

Afterward, Kael found her alone in the Hall of Stars. She stood before the Thorned Throne, her fingers tracing the edge of one armrest. A line of pale blood shone across her knuckle.

“You touched it,” he said.

“I needed to know it was real.”

“And?”

“It is. But it’s not mine.”

“Then whose is it?”

Elaria looked up at the stained glass dome above, where morning light spilled through in broken color. “Someone who hasn’t yet come. Someone who’ll know how to sit on it without bleeding.”

Kael didn’t argue.

Instead, he said, “There’s talk from the west. A library uncovered in the dunes. Pre-collapse. Maybe older. The seers think it might contain echoes of the fifth.”

Elaria turned sharply. “The fifth?”

“No name. No shard. Just a pattern that suggests incompleteness.”

She smiled faintly. “Then the cycle isn’t done.”

“No. And you’re still in it.”

That evening, she sat in the eastern courtyard and reread the letter her mother had once written her—the one she had found folded in the back of an old almanac weeks after returning from the sea. The ink was faded, but the words held fast.

"You were born in a storm, my girl. Not from it, but for it. Let the winds come. Let them test you. But never forget the stillness beneath them. That’s where the true strength lies."

Elaria folded the letter and slipped it back into the leather pouch she wore across her chest, beside the fragment of the first shard.

She rose, her steps light, her breath steady, and walked into the night.

There would be other shards.

Other names.

Other doors to open.

But she would not fear them.

She would remember.

—

The rain finally broke on the third morning, leaving the streets of Tharros gleaming as though washed by time itself. It was a fragile sort of peace that settled afterward—not victory, not ease, but the quiet that comes when the wind forgets to howl. For the first time in months, the city stirred without fear. The fishermen returned to the harbors. The bells of the low temple rang for the first time since the Vale. Children played again in the east quarter, their laughter like cracks in the long silence. But Elaria knew it would not last.

She walked the city daily now, not as princess or guardian of the seals, but simply as herself. The people bowed to her, yes, but they also touched their foreheads, an old gesture from before the crown, older even than her name. A gesture for remembrance. She didn’t correct them.

One morning, she passed a vendor selling stormglass trinkets—shards of blue resin twisted to resemble the real thing, wrapped in silver wire, said to warn the wearer of change. Children clutched them like charms. Elaria stopped and examined one. It was shaped like the Pale Gate shard—too round, not quite hollow, but unmistakable in its intent.

The vendor, an older woman with sea-wrinkled hands, didn’t recognize her. “It’s just a story, love,” she said. “But people feel better with a little story in their pocket, these days.”

Elaria bought one and tied it to her belt.

Back at the castle, Kael was waiting with a map spread across a marble table in the observatory. His eyes were tired, and ink smudged his fingers, but his mind was sharper than ever.

“We’ve charted new faultlines,” he said, motioning to the red marks. “Not literal, not tectonic. These are places where dreams linger after waking. Where memory echoes. The priests call them echoes of the Fourth Passage. I think they’re aftershocks of the fusion.”

Elaria leaned over the table. The dots formed an uneven ring around Tharros, stretching as far as the spine of the northern highlands and the ravines south of Narren’s Reach. She placed her hand over one mark near the riverlands. “This one. What happened here?”

“Entire village walked into the water and disappeared. No signs of violence. No remains. Just clothing folded on the banks. A fisherman swears he saw them smiling beneath the surface.”

“Did they drown?”

“No. Just… left.”

She straightened. “We need to go.”

Kael frowned. “Now?”

“If the seal failed, even for a moment, we need to know what spilled through.”

They departed before dusk.

Brynn joined them this time, along with Linna, who had returned without explanation the night before, her eyes sharper and quieter than ever. Ser Calen stayed behind, now more scholar than soldier, guiding the Shard Vault’s defenses and the Circle’s apprentices in the art of warding. Elion had vanished again—his note pinned to the chapel doors with a simple line: Not gone. Just ahead.

They rode east into the riverlands, following the old Salt Road until it forked near a stand of willows. The land here was green, vibrant even, but unnaturally so. Trees bloomed out of season. Insects hovered in slow, perfect spirals. Birds repeated the same song for hours without pause. The wind was warm, and the scent of honeysuckle hung over everything like a dream trying not to end.

The village, once called Dereth’s Hollow, had not been burned or broken. Its houses stood intact. Its fields were tended. But there was no one.

They dismounted in silence.

Kael moved toward the well at the center of town, scribbling observations. Brynn ducked into a cottage, returning minutes later with a book of children’s stories lying open on a kitchen table, its pages warped by dew. Linna followed tracks leading out into the meadow—and came back pale.

“They walked east,” she said. “Single file. Through the high grass. No struggle.”

Elaria knelt at the edge of the path. The ground pulsed faintly under her palm, like a sleeping heart. She reached into her satchel and pulled out the fused shard. It remained dormant. No glow. No hum. But her fingers tingled where they touched it.

“They followed something,” she said softly. “A voice. A thread.”

“A calling?” Kael asked.

Elaria nodded. “But not from the Deep.”

That night, they camped in the town square, unable to shake the sensation of eyes that weren’t there. The air whispered, just faintly, with laughter that wasn’t echo, nor wind. In the middle of the night, Elaria woke suddenly, the shard glowing cold white beside her bedroll.

Someone stood in the road.

Not a ghost. Not a figure from memory.

A girl.

She looked no older than ten, barefoot, her eyes too large for her face, pale hair drifting upward as if she stood underwater. She stared at Elaria without fear.

“You shouldn’t have sealed it,” she said.

Elaria rose slowly, her voice careful. “Sealed what?”

“The world,” the girl replied. “You locked it shut. But the lock rusts. And the key sings in the dark.”

“What are you?”

The girl tilted her head. “I am what you forgot.”

She turned and walked down the road, vanishing before Elaria could follow.

Kael found her awake at dawn, sitting with the shard in her lap.

“She spoke to me,” Elaria said.

“She’s the fifth, isn’t she?”

Elaria didn’t answer.

Because she wasn’t sure.

Not yet.

But in her mind, the girl’s words echoed.

The key sings in the dark.

Whatever she was—child, echo, memory—she was part of something older than even the Deep King.

And she had not come to warn.

She had come to remind.

Chapter Seven: The Girl from the River

They followed the trail east, across fields thick with dew and valleys where mist clung to the earth long past morning. The land felt dreamlike here, as if reality were growing tired of itself. Trees bowed lower than they should. Streams trickled upstream for a few heartbeats before righting themselves again. Once, Kael reached for his waterskin and found it already full, though he hadn’t refilled it in a day. The sun remained in a perpetual haze, never truly rising or setting. Time, it seemed, had bent its spine in this place.

Elaria didn’t speak much as they walked. She kept her eyes ahead, fingers always close to the shard that pulsed faintly through the leather of her satchel. Since the night the girl appeared in Dereth’s Hollow, the shard had not gone dormant again. It didn’t glow, not exactly—it thrummed with a kind of presence, an awareness. Like it was listening.

Kael studied her closely but said little. He could see the change in her, not just the silence or the way she drifted ahead of the others when they traveled, but in her posture—something newly heavy in her shoulders, something wary in her gaze. The others noticed too. Brynn watched with open concern, while Linna remained quiet, as always, observing with the eyes of someone who had spent too long in forests where everything watched back.

On the fifth day, they came upon the river.

It was wider than Kael remembered it from the maps. A slow-moving braid of greenish water, flanked on both sides by groves of sycamore and strange red reeds that bent with no wind. The bridge that once spanned it was gone, its stone supports collapsed into the shallows, now overgrown with vines and moss.

There were footprints in the mud—barefoot, small, dozens of them, heading into the river but not out again.

“She’s been here,” Brynn said, crouching near the edge.

Elaria stepped into the shallows without hesitation. The water lapped around her boots, cold as memory. “They all have,” she murmured. “The villagers. The ones who vanished. They followed her here.”

Linna moved to the edge of the grove, bow in hand. “Tracks go upstream too. Something came out before them. Big. Not human.”

They followed the river northward, skirting its edge for a full day. The forest thickened, and by nightfall, they reached a place where the trees grew too close together for the moon to reach the ground. The wind died completely. Insects vanished. There was no birdsong. Just stillness.

They made camp in a narrow clearing. Brynn built the fire. Kael prepared tea from the herbs they’d brought. Elaria stood watch. She didn’t sleep that night. She didn’t even blink.

At dawn, they found the pool.

It was circular and perfectly still, a mirror among the trees. Fog coiled above it like smoke. At its center stood a single stone pedestal, and atop it, a silver bowl filled with dark water that did not ripple.

“She’s here,” Elaria said.

“Who?” Kael asked, though he already knew.

The girl stepped from the trees across the pool. She wore a simple gray shift, her hair still weightless in the unmoving air. Her feet made no sound as they touched the ground. Her eyes were too large, too dark, and her voice—when it came—was as flat as the water in the bowl.

“You brought it.”

Elaria stepped forward, careful, cautious. “The shard?”

“All of them. Woven into one. I knew you would. You were made for endings.”

“I came for answers,” Elaria said.

“And you’ll have them. But not from me.”

“Then from who?”

The girl turned and pointed to the pool.

“From yourself.”

The water in the bowl darkened.

Elaria stepped toward it. Her companions held their breath as she placed her hand over the surface. The moment her fingertips broke the skin of the water, the forest vanished.

She stood in a chamber she did not recognize—circular, high-walled, covered in mirrors that showed not her reflection, but moments. One was her birth. One was her first steps. One was the day she first held steel. Another showed her kneeling beside her mother’s bed the night she died. And another… another showed her standing atop the Thorned Throne, alone, wearing a crown of white flame.

“What is this place?” she whispered.

“This is where the world remembers itself.”

The voice echoed from every surface. It was her voice. It was also not. It was older. Wiser. Broken.

“You carry the memory of what was sealed. But there is more beneath it.”

“More than the Deep King?”

“He was a symptom. Not a cause.”

“Then what caused it?”

“The forgetting.”

Elaria turned slowly, her breath sharp. “The world chose to forget?”

“It begged to. Too much pain. Too much loss. The shards were not just seals. They were relief. Mercy crafted into stone. The Deep was not locked away by force. He was offered. Offered up, like a scapegoat, so the rest of the world could move on.”

“Then why return him at all?”

“Because mercy is not healing. And forgetting is not peace.”

Elaria reached for one of the mirrors, but her fingers passed through. “Why me?”

“Because you remembered. Even when you didn’t want to.”

The water vanished beneath her feet.

She was back in the clearing. The girl was gone. The bowl was empty.

Her companions stood around her, tense.

“What happened?” Kael asked.

Elaria didn’t answer right away. She looked to the north. “There’s a place. A city beneath the ground. Older than the Deep King. Older than time. It’s where they took the memories.”

“Who?” Linna asked.

Elaria turned to her, her eyes colder than ever. “The architects.”

Kael blinked. “From the myths?”

“They’re real. And they built the first forgetting.”

Brynn’s voice was tight. “And now?”

“Now we find them.”

“Why?” Kael asked.

“Because we’re not done,” Elaria said. “Not until we remember everything.”

They left the forest by dusk, the sky breaking open above them in streaks of amber and violet. Behind them, the pool stilled again. The silver bowl vanished into mist. And somewhere far below, deeper than even the sea could reach, something shifted.

A gate long thought buried blinked open.

Not with malice.

With memory.

And it was waiting.

—

The road to the lost city was no road at all. There were no maps for it, no markers, no ruins left to trace its bones. It existed in fragments—in the fevered ramblings of old sailors, the footnotes of forbidden texts, and half-burned journals that ended mid-sentence. Kael had read them all, chasing clues like shadows through a forest of silence. The most consistent detail across all accounts was simple: you do not find the city by seeking it. You find it by remembering it was always there.

They traveled west again, this time deeper than before, into the land where the low hills began to dip and twist, where stones jutted from the ground like broken ribs and the air smelled faintly of wet metal. It was not a place that welcomed travelers. The sun did not reach it. No birds sang. No insects stirred. Yet the ground bore no signs of rot or death. It was not dead, only... paused.

“Elaria,” Kael said one night as they camped in a narrow ravine, “I’ve been thinking about the girl. The one at the river.”

Elaria looked up from the fire, her hands clasped around the shard—still quiet, but no longer cold. “What about her?”

“She wasn’t just a messenger. She was part of it. Part of what’s left behind. The fifth shard, if we even want to call it that... it isn’t an object. It’s a mind. Or maybe a wound.”

Brynn stirred beside them. “Then are we walking into it?”

“No,” Elaria said. “We’re walking into ourselves. That city beneath the ground—it’s a construct, yes. A place. But it’s also a prison. A vault for memory so dangerous it was buried beyond reach. We are not going there to explore. We’re going there to reclaim what was taken from us all.”

Kael hesitated. “And if we weren’t meant to remember?”

“Then we’ll break the world again. But this time we’ll do it with our eyes open.”

It took another week to find the entrance. They didn’t know they’d found it at first. It looked like a simple stone field, dotted with broken columns and weather-worn tablets etched in a script no one recognized. But Elaria felt the pull again—not from the shard, but from something beneath it. She knelt, pressed her palm to the earth, and whispered a name she had not known she remembered:

“Rynakal.”

The ground responded.

The stones shifted, not with the grinding of weight, but with the sigh of something released. A circle appeared in the earth, wide and deep, rimmed with steps that led downward in a perfect spiral. No light spilled from below. The air was dry and old.

Brynn crossed herself. “It feels like we’re about to walk into a tomb.”

Kael checked his satchel, made sure the wards were ready. “A tomb that dreams.”

Linna drew both her knives. “We’re already inside the graveyard. Might as well meet the ghosts.”

They descended.
