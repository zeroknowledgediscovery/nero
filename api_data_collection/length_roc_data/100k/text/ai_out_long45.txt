Title: The Prince and the Ashen Star
Chapter One: The Letter in the Flame

The morning air of Eldareth was perfumed with the scent of cinnamon and dying leaves. Autumn had cloaked the palace grounds in crimson and gold, the colors of change, though the court still pretended it was summer. Prince Kaelen, second son of the High Throne, found little comfort in the pageantry of the season. His boots crunched over frost-tipped grass as he crossed the Eastern courtyard alone, arms crossed and eyes distant.

He hadn’t slept. Again.

“Your Highness.” A soft voice echoed behind him.

Kaelen turned, recognizing Sir Rorin—his shadow more than his guard, the only man in the palace who dared call him by name when protocol demanded title.

“You’ll catch fever,” Rorin said, offering a fur-lined cloak.

Kaelen accepted it, more out of respect than chill. “Did the ravens come?”

“No word from Caer Vardis. Nor from the Northward Pass. But…” Rorin hesitated. “Something else arrived.”

The knight reached into the folds of his surcoat and produced a folded parchment, its edges blackened as if licked by flame. The seal bore no crest, just an indigo smear shaped like a dying star.

Kaelen frowned. “What is this?”

“It appeared in your chambers. No one saw who delivered it.”

The prince opened the letter. The parchment was warm—absurdly so—and bore no ink, only a single line burnt into the paper as though by invisible fire:

“The world is ending. You must come. Midnight’s Hollow. The door opens for no other.”

He blinked. “A jest?”

“I don’t think so,” said Rorin. “It reeks of old magic.”

Kaelen looked up, eyes stormed. “Get the horses.”

Chapter Two: Midnight’s Hollow

They rode hard through the Rivenwood, avoiding the high roads where questions gathered like flies. Only Rorin and Kaelen knew the journey’s purpose, though the prince himself wasn’t sure he understood it. A message without a name, a warning with no reason, and a place that did not exist on any map he knew. Midnight’s Hollow. A fable, whispered by old women to children to keep them from wandering into the hills.

They reached the place after five nights. The Hollow was not marked by any sign or landmark, but they knew it when they saw it—a clearing where the trees leaned away rather than toward, and the sky above was unnaturally dark, as if time forgot this piece of land.

In the center, a door stood upright, embedded in nothing, its frame made of bone and bramble.

“Gods,” Rorin murmured.

Kaelen approached it slowly. There was no handle. No seam.

He reached out. The surface shimmered under his hand and, without sound, opened.

Beyond was not another part of the forest—but a chamber of lightless stone, carved in impossible angles, walls humming with ancient energy. And standing at its heart, a woman draped in silver threads, her eyes black as coals and burning just the same.

“Welcome, Prince of the Cracked Crown,” she said.

Kaelen stiffened. “You know me.”

She nodded. “I called you.”

“Why?”

“The world dies, and you must stop it.”

Chapter Three: The Fractured World

Her name was Virell, Keeper of the Eclipsed Flame. She spoke in broken riddles at first, her tongue old as forgotten suns. But she told him the truth, or at least her truth: that the world—Aravell—was not whole. Not anymore. It had fractured long ago during the War of the Splintering Sky, and only now, centuries later, were the wounds beginning to bleed again.

“There are seven shards,” she explained, gesturing to a constellation map etched into the stone. “Each a fragment of the Prime Source. And each guarded, hidden, cursed.”

“And you want me to collect them?” Kaelen asked, his brow furrowed.

“No,” she replied, voice quiet. “You must earn them.”

One was kept in the Hollow Monastery, guarded by monks who spoke only in dreams. Another in the Ruins of Sylveth, beneath the bones of the first dragon. A third lay sealed in the chest of a sleeping god.

Kaelen felt laughter rising in his throat. “You’re mad.”

“No more than the world,” she said. “But you’ve already begun. The moment you opened the door, your path was inked.”

“I have no army. No magic.”

“You have choice. That is the rarest power.”

Chapter Four: A Blade Named Mourning

The Hollow spat them out onto a different path, far south of Eldareth. The skies here were bruised purple, the sun a sickle barely scraping the edge of the horizon. The land was barren but not empty.

They came upon a village hollowed by silence—no people, no livestock, just houses whose doors bled rust. On the altar of a broken chapel lay a sword, black as obsidian, hilt wrapped in silver thread. It vibrated faintly, humming like a whispered name.

Kaelen touched it—and saw fire.

A battlefield. Screams. Himself, older, broken, kneeling before a burning city.

Then, nothing.

When he awoke, Rorin stood over him.

“The sword,” Kaelen gasped. “Did you touch it?”

“No,” Rorin said. “But it sings to you.”

Virell’s voice echoed in his skull. “That is Mourning, the blade that remembers. It will show you the cost before the choice.”

Chapter Five: The Monk’s Dream

They reached the Hollow Monastery after ten more days, guided by visions Kaelen could not explain. Dreams and symbols, voices in the wind. The monks greeted him not with words but by pressing their foreheads to his and filling his mind with images: a child with no face, drowning in black water; a tower of screaming mirrors; a field of clocks all ticking backward.

The trial was simple. Kaelen was given a vial of blood and told to drink it.

“What is it?” he asked.

Rorin frowned. “Poison?”

“A memory,” one monk said aloud, breaking their vow. “Of someone you’ve never been. You must become him, and find your way back.”

Kaelen drank.

He was suddenly elsewhere—a thief in a city of smoke, hunted by guards for a crime he didn’t commit. He had no sword, no name. He had only instinct.

It took him seven days in the dream to remember he was a prince. When he awoke, the shard lay on his chest, a sliver of gold encased in a stone heart.

Chapter Six: Betrayal in Sylveth

Sylveth was a ruin of bones and shattered marble. Once a city of dragons, now a graveyard.

They weren’t alone.

A group of mercenaries had arrived days before, led by a woman Kaelen once called friend—Captain Serah Valen, now exiled from Eldareth for reasons too tangled to speak of.

“Kaelen,” she said, drawing her blade. “Or do you call yourself something else now?”

“I need the shard,” he said.

She smiled. “Then you’ll have to bleed for it.”

What followed was chaos—steel on stone, shouts, fire. Rorin took a spear to the side. Kaelen dueled Serah beneath a crumbling arch, their swords ringing like bells.

She was better. Always had been.

But Kaelen was different now.

He let Mourning guide him, saw the futures behind her eyes. Saw her die. Saw her live.

He chose mercy. Knocked the sword from her hand. Offered his instead.

She stared at it. “You’re a fool.”

“Maybe.”

She took his hand.

And together, they entered the dragon’s tomb.

Chapter Seven: When the Sky Split

With three shards in hand, the sky began to change. Colors bled wrong. Birds flew backward. Children spoke in dead tongues.

Virell returned in flame.

“Time frays,” she warned. “You must hurry. The next shard is buried in the Wastes of Harn, where time forgot itself.”

“And the others?” Kaelen asked.

“They will come to you. But beware—the Starborn have seen you.”

“Who?”

She faded.

The Starborn came that night. Creatures of glass and breath, with no faces but thousands of mouths. They whispered Kaelen’s name in every voice he’d ever heard, even those long dead.

He did not sleep.

He did not dare.

Kaelen stood still for a long time after the woman’s form vanished into smoke. The torchlight in the strange chamber flickered against the wet, dark stone, casting shadows that danced with a rhythm that seemed off, like a heartbeat too slow or too fast. He tried to tell himself this was a dream, some elaborate jest played by sorcerers or madmen—but deep in his bones, he already knew that was not true. The air in this place was too heavy with meaning, too dense with the kind of silence that only ancient truths could summon.

He looked to Rorin, who hadn’t stepped inside the door but had come close enough to lean against its invisible threshold. The knight’s brow was creased, his mouth pressed into a thin line, but his hand did not reach for his sword. That alone spoke volumes.

“What did she say?” Rorin asked, eyes scanning the room as if trying to understand how it fit into the world they knew.

Kaelen didn’t answer immediately. He turned, walking back through the door, half-expecting it to shut behind him and leave him lost forever in that unnatural chamber. But the door remained open until he crossed the threshold—and then, without fanfare, it simply closed. A blink, and it was gone. The frame had vanished, no scorch mark left on the grass, no shimmer in the air. Just trees again, leaning in as if the moment had never happened.

“She said the world is ending,” Kaelen said finally, his voice hollow.

“Well,” Rorin muttered, adjusting the hilt of his blade, “at least it’s not another arranged betrothal.”

Kaelen didn’t laugh. Instead, he turned back toward the horses. “We’ll need supplies. Provisions. And no questions asked.”

“You’re not thinking of returning to the palace?” Rorin asked, tone bordering on alarm.

“No,” Kaelen replied. “We go east. There’s an old sage near the Glass Hills. He used to talk of things like this. Runes, stars, cracks in the veil. My brother always called him a madman.”

“Your brother calls squirrels mad when they don’t run,” Rorin said. He paused. “Does this have something to do with the plague in Vesseran? Or those stories out of the marshlands—the black rain and blood-colored fog?”

Kaelen shook his head. “I don’t know. But if I wait to understand, we’ll already be too late.”

They rode again before dawn, their horses restless and snorting in the chill. The path they took was old—so old the trees seemed to lean aside for it, and no birds dared call from the branches above. The road was cracked, overtaken by roots and moss, but it still remembered its purpose, and so did the air.

After two days, they passed a ruin that neither of them could name. The stones looked like teeth chewed by time. Rorin spotted a shape watching them from the tallest broken arch, but it vanished when they approached. Kaelen did not ask what he saw. They both knew some things watched only the marked.

At night, the prince stared into the fire and remembered her words. You must come. The world is ending. And that strange place beyond the door, the way it felt like it was not built but grown, not lit but remembered.

He also remembered her eyes. Not cold, not cruel—just full of knowing, like someone who had watched the sun die and kept the story to herself.

“You should sleep,” Rorin said, tossing him a rough-woven blanket.

“I can’t,” Kaelen replied, watching sparks lift into the sky. “Every time I close my eyes, I see a tower with no top and no bottom. It’s burning, but the flames are blue, and they burn up not down.”

“Sounds like prophecy.”

“Sounds like a trap.”

They reached the edge of the Glass Hills on the fifth day, the land shimmering as though a mirror had cracked across it. The sage they sought—Arreth—lived in a hut made of discarded memories. That’s what Kaelen had called it once, after seeing the walls made from broken windowpanes, scrolls too faded to read, and bones that hummed when touched.

Arreth emerged before they could call out, as though he’d been waiting for years. His beard reached past his knees, and his eyes were shut with old scars.

“I told you not to come back, princeling,” he said without smiling.

“I was summoned.”

The blind man tilted his head. “You heard the Ashen Star, then.”

Kaelen’s breath caught. “You know of it?”

“I know it,” Arreth said, his voice rasping like dry parchment. “But no one’s heard it for an age and a half. Not since the last Sundering.”

Kaelen stepped forward. “What is it? Why now? Why me?”

“Because,” the old sage whispered, “the world is fraying at its seams, and you are one of the few still holding a thread.”

Arreth invited them in without waiting for a reply. The inside of the hut was darker than it should have been, as though light feared to stay too long. The shelves were crowded with books that smelled of smoke and seawater, and jars that pulsed faintly when Kaelen passed.

The sage sat with difficulty, motioning for the prince to do the same. “You were touched by the Hollow. That much I can sense. You’ve crossed a door that most believe is metaphor. You’ve entered the space-between. That makes you dangerous.”

“To who?” Kaelen asked.

Arreth didn’t answer right away. He reached into a jar and pulled out a lump of dark glass, polished and warm. He passed it to Kaelen.

In it, the prince saw a reflection—but not his. The face was his, but older, hollow-eyed, scarred by fire and grief. Behind that face, the stars were falling.

“Yourself,” Arreth said. “You are your own danger now.”

Rorin stood near the entrance, arms crossed, watching with growing discomfort. “He’s no sorcerer. No priest. He’s not chosen.”

“No,” Arreth said. “But he is seen. That matters more than being chosen.”

Kaelen tightened his grip on the glass, feeling it burn into his palm.

“Then tell me what to do,” he said. “Give me something. A name, a place, anything.”

The old man sighed and gestured toward a map pinned to the wall by thorns. “The shards. Seven in all. One to the north, buried in a monastery of silent monks. Another in the ruined city of Sylveth, under the bones of the last sky-dragon. One guarded by a river that flows backward through time. You’ll need them if you want to mend what’s breaking.”

“And what’s breaking?” Kaelen asked.

Arreth leaned forward, breath sour with age. “The veil. The line between what is and what should not be. Once it tears, nothing will keep the unmade things from crawling through.”

Kaelen looked at the map, tracing lines from one shard to another.

“When do I start?” he asked.

“You already have,” said the sage.

That night, as they made camp at the base of the hills, the moon turned red—not the red of blood, but the red of heat and iron. The horses grew restless, and even Rorin looked unnerved. Something was stirring in the bones of the world.

Kaelen sat beside the fire, Mourning across his knees, and for the first time, he spoke aloud to no one.

“I will follow this path,” he said, “but I don’t believe in fate. If this world ends, it won’t be because I didn’t fight for it.”

The flames flared higher as if in response, and far in the distance, something screamed—long and low, like the sky itself cracking open.

Chapter Two: Whispers in the Hollow

The eastern winds carried a strange bitterness as Kaelen and Rorin pressed on into the lowlands. The trees had begun to change, not just in hue but in form—trunks curling as though they twisted away from the earth, their leaves paper-thin and colorless, fluttering like torn veils. Kaelen tried not to think about the way the path behind them seemed different whenever he looked back. One moment the trail was dirt; the next, moss-covered stone. At times there were no footprints at all.

“We’re being watched,” Rorin said quietly, pulling his cloak tighter. His hand never strayed far from the hilt of his sword, a blade far more ordinary than Mourning, but no less trusted.

Kaelen nodded. He had felt it too. A presence, not close but constant, like a name someone kept trying to say in a crowded room. His sleep had become fragmented, haunted by glimpses of a tower in the middle of a black ocean, its top wreathed in lightning that danced in reverse. He would awaken with Mourning already drawn, breath ragged, fingers clenched white around the hilt.

They passed through three villages in the next five days. Each more silent than the last. Doors were left open, fires still smoldered in hearths, meals half-eaten upon stone tables. The people had vanished. No signs of struggle, no blood—only absence. At the last of them, Kaelen found a single word scrawled across the wall of a meeting house, etched into the wood with something sharp and scorched at the edges.

UNSEEN.

“What does it mean?” Kaelen asked, his voice low.

Rorin didn’t answer right away. He bent down and ran his fingers over the soot-lined mark. “It means they didn’t vanish,” he said finally. “They were taken. Or… turned away.”

“Turned?”

Rorin stood, brushing off his gloves. “There are old stories, from before even the Writings of Elennor. Of things that lived in the gaps between what we know. Things that couldn’t be seen—not because they were invisible, but because we never knew how to look at them. They were... wrong. And when they came near, the world refused them. The wind would stop. The animals would bolt. Flames would die.”

“And they’re back?”

“If they ever left,” the knight said grimly.

They did not linger.

Their next destination was the Hollow Monastery, a place Kaelen had only heard of in whispered tales told by initiates too green to know better. It was said the monks there had long since given up the need for speech, choosing instead to commune through dreams and symbols, their minds no longer bound by the spoken word. Virell’s instructions had been maddeningly simple: Find the dreaming monks. They guard the first shard, though they do not know it.

As they approached the monastery’s vale, the land dipped into a shallow basin surrounded by cliffs so smooth they appeared melted. The monastery itself was carved directly into the cliff face, a spiral of arches and bridges that defied logic, appearing taller from within than it seemed from without. There were no gates, no guards, just a long stone path flanked by bowls of fire that did not burn but glowed with a deep blue radiance.

Kaelen paused before stepping onto the path. “No turning back, is there?”

“You turned the moment you read the letter,” Rorin said. “The rest is just walking forward.”

The silence inside the monastery was not the absence of sound, but its inverse. Every footstep felt muffled, as if the space itself rejected noise. The monks appeared without warning—hooded figures in robes of bone-white cloth, their faces concealed behind veils made of thin silver chains. They moved as one, never speaking, never gesturing. And yet, somehow, Kaelen understood where to go.

He was led through a long corridor that seemed to curve endlessly inward, past murals carved directly into the walls—scenes of starfalls, oceans burning, and figures faceless but crowned. At the end of the corridor, a domed chamber awaited, the ceiling painted with a map of a sky that did not exist.

The monks formed a circle around him. One stepped forward and held out a small cup filled with dark crimson liquid. Without a word, Kaelen took it. The taste was sharp, metallic, and the world immediately pitched sideways.

He fell.

But not to the ground.

He awoke in a different skin.

The clothes on his back were tattered, his hands smaller, rough with dirt and cuts. Around him rose a city of glass towers and smoke-choked alleys, humming with strange, half-familiar energy. He realized with a slow, dawning horror that this was not a vision but a memory—one that did not belong to him. The sounds, the scents, the heaviness of his limbs—they were all real.

He tried to speak, but his voice came out with an accent he didn’t know. He was someone else. Somewhere else. And he had no sword, no title, no past.

His name, he learned, was Daran. A thief. A liar. A survivor of the lowest ward in the city of Varenth—a place that existed only in the apocryphal texts Kaelen had once mocked as fantasies. He spent his first night in this false life bleeding from a cut across the ribs, hiding beneath a rusted stairwell as soldiers passed by, searching for a stolen artifact he had no memory of stealing.

He lived as Daran for seven days. And each day, the weight of this borrowed life grew heavier. There was no sign of the monks. No clues. Just fear and smoke and the growing suspicion that he was losing himself to this identity.

But on the seventh night, he stood before a mirror that had not worked since he arrived. For the first time, it reflected something other than the city behind him. It showed his true face. Kaelen. Crownless, sleepless, and now—aware. In that moment, he remembered why he was here.

And the world around him cracked like glass.

He awoke gasping in the monk’s chamber, sweat-soaked and trembling. Rorin was by his side, hand on his shoulder. The monks were gone.

In his lap lay the first shard.

It was no larger than a child’s tooth, shaped like a twisted spiral and glowing with a light too deep to be color. Kaelen picked it up, and the stone around them trembled as if recognizing the shard’s presence.

“Did you find it?” Rorin asked.

Kaelen stared at the fragment. “I didn’t find it. I became it.”

They left the monastery without words, the monks watching silently as they passed. Outside, the wind had changed. The sky was darker than it had been, though the sun still hung above the horizon. Birds flew in spirals instead of lines. And far to the north, a shimmer of light rippled across the horizon, like a curtain tearing.

“What now?” Rorin asked.

Kaelen didn’t answer right away. He held the shard in his palm, felt the pull of it—toward the east, toward a place he could not yet name but already feared.

“We find the next,” he said finally. “And we pray that when we do, we’re still ourselves.”

They mounted their horses and rode on, and behind them, the Hollow Monastery vanished—folding itself back into the cliff as though it had never been.

The road that led away from the Hollow Monastery stretched like a thread unraveling from the hem of the world. What had once been familiar countryside gave way to uncanny terrain, where trees bore leaves shaped like hands and streams ran uphill for brief, disorienting moments. Time moved irregularly here—sometimes the sun would seem to hover in one spot for hours, other times it would jerk across the sky like a marionette pulled by unseen strings. Even Rorin, whose iron calm had never faltered through battlefield or blizzard, began to shift uneasily in his saddle.

“We need to get out of this region before our minds start melting out our ears,” he muttered one evening, as he attempted to light a fire for the fourth time with flint that refused to spark.

Kaelen sat nearby, staring into the darkness between the trees. “The shard pulled us here. The next may be doing the same.”

“Then we follow it and hope it doesn’t lead us off a cliff.”

Kaelen didn’t respond. In truth, he felt something gnawing at the corners of his perception—an itch he couldn’t reach, like a word on the tip of the tongue that refused to manifest. The shard they had recovered no longer glowed, but it pulsed with a warmth that increased when they headed east. He had taken to wearing it beneath his shirt, tied in a leather pouch around his neck. Though small, it felt heavier than it should, as though each step toward the next shard added weight not only to his body but to his soul.

Three nights later, they encountered their first living soul in nearly a week.

She stood in the middle of the road as if she had grown from the stone itself—tall, cloaked in brown robes stained with ash and dust, her head bare and her eyes rimmed in black kohl. She did not flinch when the horses drew near, nor did she speak. But when Kaelen dismounted and approached, she held out her hand and made a strange sign in the air—three fingers up, one curled, the thumb angled toward the sky.

Kaelen returned the gesture instinctively, though he did not know why.

“Child of the Ashen Star,” the woman said, her voice hollow and echoing as if from a deep cavern. “You walk the Path of Unmaking.”

“I walk a path I was shown,” Kaelen replied. “I did not choose it.”

“All choices lead to the same end,” she said, lowering her hand. “Only the footprints differ.”

Kaelen glanced to Rorin, who looked about ready to draw steel.

“Do you guard the next shard?” the prince asked.

“I am a witness,” the woman said. “Not a guardian. What comes next is neither test nor gift. It is a reckoning.”

Before he could speak again, she turned and walked into the woods without another word.

Kaelen followed without question.

They came to a clearing that was not a clearing at all. The trees parted only to reveal a crater in the earth, vast and filled with mist that shimmered silver and gold. In the center stood a structure—no, a skeleton of a structure. It looked like the ruins of a cathedral, its spires snapped like bones, its windows shattered into constellations of glass that reflected nothing.

“What is this place?” Kaelen asked softly.

The woman did not answer, but the answer came nonetheless.

He heard it in his mind—no voice, just intent.

Here lies the second wound.

He stepped forward, and the mists parted as if in deference.

Inside the ruin, time stuttered. One moment Kaelen was stepping over fallen stone; the next, he was a child again, running through the palace gardens with his brother, laughing. Then he was old—too old—sitting in a broken throne room with dust in his mouth and fire in his eyes. Then he was himself again, gasping, sweating, and kneeling at the base of a shattered altar.

A figure waited at the altar. Tall, pale, draped in robes of deep indigo stitched with symbols that hurt to look at.

“Prince of Eldareth,” it said. “Bearer of one. Seeker of many. Do you know what lies beneath your feet?”

“No,” Kaelen replied.

“Your grave,” the being said. “And theirs.”

It raised its hand, and the ground quaked.

From the dust rose forms—ghosts, maybe, or memories. They looked like people Kaelen had failed. Soldiers who had died while protecting him. Servants poisoned in a plot meant for his father. Even his mother, her face drawn with the sorrow she’d carried until the day she disappeared.

“What is this?” he asked.

“This is the price,” the figure said. “All power is drawn from loss. All balance paid in kind. You cannot take from the world and expect it to remain still.”

Kaelen stood slowly. “Then I’ll pay it. Whatever it is. But I won’t run.”

The figure tilted its head. “Brave words. Let us see if they endure.”

The forms rushed him—not to attack, but to envelop. Kaelen was submerged in them, a tide of regrets, of missed chances and whispered blame. He felt his lungs closing, his knees buckling. But then, through it all, he remembered the firelight from the monastery. The feeling of the shard in his palm. The knowing that he had already begun to change.

He screamed—not in fear, but in defiance—and the ghosts shattered like glass underfoot.

When the world settled, he stood alone. The altar had crumbled to dust, and in its place lay the second shard. This one was shaped like a fang, serrated along one edge and cold to the touch.

The cloaked woman waited outside the ruin.

“You have passed the Reckoning,” she said. “But you are no closer to truth.”

“I don’t want truth,” Kaelen muttered. “I want to stop what’s coming.”

“Then you must understand what it is you fight,” she said. “Follow the storm. There you will find the Sleeper.”

She walked into the mist and was gone.

Kaelen turned to Rorin, who had remained behind, sword drawn and face pale.

“Did you see them?” the prince asked.

Rorin nodded once. “I saw enough.”

They did not speak further that night. They camped by the edge of the crater, the sky above blanketed by rolling clouds lit from within by green lightning. Kaelen dreamed of oceans made of glass and a voice calling to him from beneath the waves.

When morning came, they found the forest gone.

Some contained statues that seemed to watch Kaelen as he passed; others held mirrors that refused to reflect him. In one chamber, Rorin nearly stepped into a pit that looked shallow but reached infinitely down. Only the shard’s pull—warm and constant—kept them moving forward.

Eventually, they reached a central chamber. A dome of pure night curved overhead, lit not by flame or torch, but by the illusion of stars—each blinking in patterns too precise to be natural. At the chamber’s heart stood a man.

Or rather, something that had once been a man.

He was suspended above the floor, bound by chains made of runes, his eyes closed, his chest rising and falling as if caught in the rhythm of a distant tide. His hair was white, not with age but with fire that had long since gone cold. Around his neck hung a shard—the third—this one long and flat, shaped like the blade of a ceremonial dagger.

Kaelen stepped forward, drawn not by desire, but inevitability.

As he did, the Sleeper opened his eyes.

They were black. Not the black of pupils or the void, but the black of grief—a color so deep it pulled at Kaelen’s soul like a tide.

“You carry their echoes,” the Sleeper said, his voice too many at once.

“I’ve come for the shard,” Kaelen replied, forcing the words through a throat tight with ancient fear.

“No,” the Sleeper said. “You’ve come for yourself.”

“I don’t know what that means.”

“You will,” the Sleeper whispered. “But not yet.”

The chains binding him glowed with blue fire. Each rune on them pulsed once, then dimmed. Kaelen felt something twist behind his eyes.

“You are not ready to bear what I carry. But you will be. Take it.”

The shard snapped from the Sleeper’s neck and flew toward Kaelen, embedding itself between the two he already bore. For a moment, Kaelen’s vision swam. The ground beneath him cracked. The sky inside the dome tore open.

He saw a city burning.

A woman with silver eyes kneeling in chains.

His brother, crownless and dead.

A blade not yet forged.

Then nothing.

He awoke outside the temple.

Rorin crouched beside him, bruised but alive. “You collapsed. Everything inside that place just—fell in on itself.”

Kaelen reached into his pouch. Three shards now. Each humming. Each warm. Each heavier than before.

“Did you see the Sleeper?” he asked.

Rorin shook his head. “There was no one there. Just… darkness.”

Kaelen stared up at the storm. It was gone. The clouds were breaking apart, light filtering down in ragged shafts.

“We have three,” he said. “Four remain.”

Rorin helped him to his feet. “And what happens when you have all seven?”

Kaelen looked toward the east, where the sky was beginning to glow red—not with dawn, but with fire.

“Then I find out what they want me to become.”

They left the chasm behind, unaware that behind them, buried beneath a mountain of falling obsidian, the Sleeper still dreamed.

Chapter Three: The Crownless Road

The land they emerged into from the ash chasm bore little resemblance to the Aravell Kaelen once knew. Where once there had been forests and rivers, now stretched a wide, barren valley pocked with scars in the earth like claw marks. Whole swaths of ground appeared scorched from within, cracked open to reveal glowing magma beneath brittle soil. The air hung heavy with ozone, and strange lights flickered just above the horizon—blues and greens like the auroras spoken of only in the ancient epics. It felt like a place unmoored from the world, a land still arguing with its own right to exist.

Rorin scanned the horizon with weary eyes, chewing absently on a strip of dried meat that had long since lost all flavor. “There’s nothing out here. No villages, no roads. No people.”

“There used to be,” Kaelen murmured. “This was the Lowvale Basin. The breadbasket of half the eastern provinces.”

Rorin gave a snort. “Looks more like the gods dropped a forge on it.”

They traveled in silence for some time. Kaelen could feel the shards pressing against one another in his pouch, like living things beginning to wake from a long sleep. Their song had changed—not louder, but more layered, more complex. Sometimes it seemed as if they whispered not to him, but to each other, in a language just outside the edge of understanding. He found himself glancing over his shoulder more often than before, not out of fear, but from a strange certainty that the past was following them, each step leaving not just a mark on the world but an echo.

By the third day of crossing the Crownless Road—so called, Kaelen had learned, because no royal caravan had dared travel it since the Sundering of Halden’s Pact—they came upon a structure standing alone on the plain. It was a simple tower, round and squat, covered in ivy that had long since died but clung to the stone as though trying to remember life. Its door hung crookedly on one hinge, and a dull crimson light pulsed faintly from within, like a heartbeat slowed almost to stillness.

Kaelen dismounted and approached cautiously, Mourning drawn but held low. Rorin followed, crossbow at the ready. The door opened without resistance, groaning under its own age.

Inside was a spiral stair that led downward. The light grew brighter with each step. And when they reached the bottom, they found not a crypt or cellar—but a vast underground chamber filled with mirrors.

Thousands of them.

Some stood tall, etched with sigils Kaelen did not recognize. Others were cracked or dust-covered. Still others floated, suspended in the air as though unaware of gravity’s rules. The red light came not from torches or fire, but from the mirrors themselves—each reflecting a different sky, a different time.

Kaelen stepped closer to one and gasped.

It showed him, but not him. In this reflection, he wore a crown of thorns and flame, and his eyes glowed white. Behind him, legions of armored warriors marched across a blackened plain, banners raised, their faces veiled.

Another mirror showed him dead—pinned to a tree by spears of light, his hands charred, his eyes hollow.

Rorin moved among them warily. “I don’t like this. This reeks of arc-sorcery.”

A voice echoed through the chamber, quiet yet omnipresent. “They are possibilities.”

The mirrors shimmered, and a figure stepped from one of them—a woman cloaked in midnight, her face obscured by a mask made of woven stars.

Kaelen’s grip tightened on Mourning. “Are you another guardian?”

“I am a curator,” she said, her voice like wind through a cavern. “These are the fates not chosen. Paths not taken. Each shard you gather limits the futures available to you. You become more certain. More defined. Less possible.”

“I didn’t come to weigh futures,” Kaelen said. “Only to stop the end of this one.”

The woman stepped closer. “Then choose carefully.”

She raised a hand, and a new mirror rose from the floor. This one was darker than the others, almost black, and it shimmered as though submerged in water. Within it, Kaelen saw no reflection—only a void that pulsed, slow and patient.

“This is the Gate of the Null Path,” she said. “Where all things are unmade. There is a shard on the other side. But it is not guarded. It is forgotten. To retrieve it, you must step through. But beware—what passes through the gate does not always return the same.”

Kaelen approached, every hair on his body rising. The gate pulled at him, not physically, but emotionally—like it knew his grief, his questions, and offered an end to them all.

“What do I become if I take it?” he asked.

“Less,” she replied. “But also more. A paradox. You will forget some of yourself. In return, you may gain understanding no mortal mind should carry.”

Rorin grabbed his arm. “Kael, this is madness. We’ve already seen too much. You step through that thing, you may come out a stranger—or not at all.”

Kaelen looked from Rorin to the gate. He thought of the monks. Of the Sleeper. Of the ghosts that still haunted his dreams.

Then he stepped through.

For a moment, he became everything.

He saw himself as a child, crying in a nursery his mother had abandoned. He saw himself laughing with his brother, unaware of the blade poised above both their heads. He saw thousands of versions of himself—beggar, tyrant, knight, assassin, king, martyr. He became a constellation, a broken sword, a whisper on the lips of a dying god. Time ceased. Space inverted.

Then—he was nothing.

And then—

He was again.

Kaelen staggered out of the mirror, gasping, falling to his knees. Rorin caught him, barely. The chamber had gone dark, the mirrors silent. Only one remained lit—the one he had passed through—and within it, his reflection slowly faded to black.

He opened his hand. A new shard lay in his palm.

It was unlike the others. Not jagged or crystalline, but smooth. Round. Like an eye.

Rorin helped him to his feet. “You were gone for hours.”

“It felt like centuries,” Kaelen whispered.

The curator was gone. Only her voice remained, drifting like smoke.

“The fourth is yours. But the fifth waits where the dead speak. Tread lightly. Memory is a weapon, and your enemies carry blades made of yours.”

They left the mirror tower at dusk. The sky above had turned gold, and flocks of birds—real birds, not illusions—passed overhead for the first time in days.

Kaelen did not sleep that night. The dreams that came were not his own.

They belonged to another Kaelen.

And that Kaelen had done terrible, irreversible things.

They crossed the outer edge of the Glassreach the next day, entering a swathe of land known on no official chart, though the locals of bygone eras had called it the Whispershade. Kaelen had read of it once, in the margins of a book too damaged for the royal scribes to catalog—a passage scrawled in ink the color of dried blood: Here speak the dead not in cries, but in memory, drawn from those who dare to listen.

The fifth shard, he now believed, waited here.

Unlike the scorched plains or the mirror chamber, the Whispershade was not overtly hostile. It bore a quiet sorrow, like a room long abandoned, still carrying the scent of its last occupant. The forest, if it could be called such, was dense with trees that had no bark—only smooth grey skin, warm to the touch and eerily pliable. Their leaves were translucent and shaped like tongues. At first, Kaelen thought it imagination, but then he realized the trees were whispering—not in wind, but in voices.

He heard his name first. Then his brother’s. Then his mother’s lullaby, sung in the old tongue. Each time he paused, the voices multiplied, swelling into layered murmurs that pressed against his skull like too-tight bandages. Rorin held out better, jaw clenched and eyes forward, but even he flinched when the trees began speaking in his own voice, repeating words he’d spoken years ago in the quiet aftermath of battle: It should’ve been me. Gods, it should’ve been me.

They camped in a hollow where the voices thinned, though never ceased. That night, Kaelen stood watch, sleep having become a luxury he could no longer afford. The fourth shard, ever since leaving the mirror tower, had become... active. It spun faintly in its pouch, vibrating against the others, creating a tone he could almost hear, like a tuning fork held to his soul.

He pulled the pouch free and laid the shards out before him in a circle.

Each glowed a different hue—red, silver, violet, black. The colors pulsed in rhythm, and as he stared, an image took shape between them—not projected, but formed in his mind. A mountain wreathed in stormlight. A black gate carved into its face. A figure kneeling before it, offering something unseen.

He saw the fifth shard there.

Not buried. Not guarded.

But weeping.

Kaelen shook the image from his mind as dawn broke, the trees exhaling long moans as if protesting the sun’s return. They moved quickly, cutting through thickets that bled sap when touched. The terrain worsened. Pools of brackish water appeared without warning, their surfaces reflecting not the sky, but faces—some Kaelen recognized, others he could not. One showed Serah Valen, the exiled captain from Sylveth, her mouth moving in frantic speech though no sound came through. Another showed Arreth, the blind sage, his eyes open and bleeding fire.

Finally, they came upon a mound—no, a barrow. A burial place.

It stood at the center of a clearing where no trees dared grow. Moss covered its sides, and a single stone marked its entrance, etched with symbols Kaelen could not decipher. As he approached, the air thickened, not with heat, but with memory. Not his own.

Suddenly, he saw through another’s eyes—a woman, kneeling at this very barrow, tears cutting lines through soot on her cheeks. Her hands were red with blood. She whispered a name into the earth, and Kaelen felt it echo in his chest, though he could not understand it.

“She buried it,” Kaelen murmured, coming back to himself. “The shard. It was her sacrifice.”

Rorin studied the barrow warily. “What kind of place is this? I feel like we’re trespassing in a dream.”

“We are,” Kaelen said. “But dreams remember.”

They entered the barrow, ducking beneath the low stone lintel. Inside was pitch black, but the shards lit the space softly, casting shadows that trembled like candleflame. The walls were lined with bones—not arranged, but scattered, as though thrown in haste. There was a dais at the center, unadorned, save for a single crown resting atop it. The crown was broken—split down the center, its sides dented and scorched. Beneath it, nestled in a hollow, was the fifth shard.

Kaelen reached for it, but as his fingers touched the crown, the chamber sighed.

A figure rose from the far corner—a ghost not made of light, but of silence. Its features were Kaelen’s own, though older, thinner, haunted. Its eyes glowed pale blue, and when it spoke, its voice was identical to Kaelen’s.

“You forget too easily.”

“I don’t understand,” Kaelen said.

“I am the you who gave up. Who fled. Who abandoned the world because the weight of it was too much.”

Kaelen stepped back. “I never—”

“Not yet,” the echo interrupted. “But you could. And that’s why this shard weeps.”

The specter reached for the crown, lifting it with a tenderness that made Kaelen’s throat tighten. “This is the symbol of what you were born into. And what you must choose to leave behind. You cannot save the world as a prince. Only as something more. Or something less.”

Kaelen stared at the ghost, at the shard, at the broken crown.

And then, slowly, he reached down, lifted the crown from its resting place, and placed it on the ground beside him.

“I’ll carry the weight,” he said, voice steady. “But not the crown.”

The ghost nodded.

The fifth shard leapt into his hand.

And the barrow collapsed.

Rorin dragged him from the rubble just before the entrance caved in completely. They lay in the clearing, coughing, staring up at a sky now streaked with gold and crimson, as though dawn and dusk had arrived at once.

Kaelen held the fifth shard against his chest. It no longer wept.

But he did.

Only for a moment.

That night, as they set camp on the ridge beyond the Whispershade, Kaelen placed all five shards before him, arranging them in a circle. They hummed in harmony now, each color melting into the next, forming a pattern he couldn’t name but somehow understood. He saw a door in his mind—a real door, not the illusion of one. It waited atop a distant cliff, carved from stormglass and framed by ancient silver trees.

“The sixth is waiting,” Kaelen said.

Rorin looked up from sharpening his blade. “Where?”

Kaelen stared at the fire. “At the edge of the world.”

Chapter Four: The Silver Trees

The edge of the world was not a place one arrived at. It was a place one remembered—a half-formed idea left behind in the stories of madmen and poets, hinted at in the spaces between maps, or in the old songs where lines trailed off into silence. Yet as Kaelen and Rorin journeyed westward from the Whispershade, guided now by a force neither of them could name, the landscape itself began to betray the rules it once obeyed. Rivers flowed upward, skipping across the sky in arching bands before falling into the mouths of canyons below. Mountains rearranged themselves subtly each morning, so that familiar silhouettes became strangers overnight. The stars above burned with too many colors, and the constellations spun out of their orbits like dancers breaking formation.

They were crossing into myth.

The air grew thinner with each day, not with altitude, but with something else—some quiet unweaving of the world’s fabric. The ground beneath them still held weight, but Kaelen could feel it resisting, as though their presence strained the patience of the land. The fifth shard had changed as well; it no longer hummed like the others. It pulsed once every few minutes, sending faint shocks into Kaelen’s chest, as though marking the seconds until some final threshold would be crossed.

They came upon the first silver tree on the ninth morning, just as the wind had begun to whisper in voices again—not as it had in the Whispershade, but clearer, sharper, like bells tolling names. The tree stood alone at the top of a rocky hill, its bark gleaming like polished moonlight, its leaves shaped like narrow blades, each one catching the light and splitting it. There was no sound in its presence, only stillness.

Rorin touched the bark with a gloved hand and hissed, pulling back. “Cold,” he muttered. “Not just cold—wrong. Like something too old to remember warmth.”

Kaelen stepped closer, placing his hand on the trunk. The tree didn’t resist. It welcomed him.

And suddenly, he was somewhere else.

He stood in a circle of identical trees, each bending toward the center, forming a dome of silver limbs. In the middle sat a man. Or a god. Or something in between. His skin was pale as the bark, and his eyes burned with layered light.

"You carry the fifth,” the figure said. “The Weeping Shard. The memory of surrender.”

Kaelen nodded. “And I’m looking for the sixth.”

“It has already found you,” the man said, lifting one hand. “But to wield it, you must pass the Trial of Truth. You must know yourself. Not the version you present. Not the prince. Not the seeker. You.”

Kaelen braced for pain, for fire, for the crushing weight of sorrow as before. But instead, the dome of trees filled with silence so complete it became pressure—pushing into his ears, his lungs, his eyes.

And then he saw himself.

Truly.

Not in fragments or dreams. But as a boy who never understood why his mother left without saying goodbye. As a brother who both loved and resented the heir. As a man who had always walked behind others, never fully belonging in court, in war, or in peace. He saw his cowardice. His quiet envy. His hungers. His gentleness. His doubt.

He fell to his knees. Not from pain. From recognition.

“I don’t know if I’m strong enough,” he whispered.

“You aren’t,” said the figure.

Kaelen looked up, startled.

“But strength is not your offering,” the figure continued. “You are choice. In a world desperate for certainty, you carry the seed of divergence. And that is why the sixth shard calls to you.”

The world snapped back.

Kaelen gasped, stumbling away from the tree. The sixth shard sat on the ground before him, shaped like a coin with a crack running through it. One half gleamed silver. The other, obsidian.

Rorin picked it up gingerly, as though it might bite. “That makes six,” he said. “What now?”

Kaelen turned slowly, his breath visible in the air though the sun burned above. He could feel them—the shards—humming together now. Harmonizing. And then he heard it: a seventh note. Distant. Imperfect. But unmistakable.

“The last one,” Kaelen said. “It’s in Eldareth.”

Rorin blinked. “Back home?”

“Not the city,” Kaelen said, shaking his head. “Beneath it.”

They made their way eastward, through a corridor of shifting lands. The silver trees multiplied, forming a forest with no visible end. Each one stood like a sentinel, silent and watchful. Kaelen found that the further they traveled, the more frequently he was drawn into their memories—glimpses of ancient times, of wars fought before language, of skies burning blue with suns that no longer existed. The trees were not of this world. They were roots of something older, something beneath the world. Their presence was not natural, but necessary.

At night, Kaelen dreamed of Eldareth. Not the city as it was, but as it had been—built atop something vast and hidden. He saw a vault. A chamber lined with runes of gold. A throne of bones. And at its center, a door made of nothing at all—an absence so complete that it seemed to devour light.

The seventh shard waited there.

He didn’t know how he knew. But he did know.

Two weeks later, they crossed the border into familiar lands. But nothing was as they’d left it. Smoke rose from villages that had once stood tall and proud. Roads were torn, as if great beasts had clawed their way through stone. Travelers no longer wandered. The wind carried rumors—half-spoken things heard in passing.

“The king is dead.”

“No, he’s worse. He’s changed.”

“They say the stars bled red the night the prince vanished…”

Kaelen did not reveal himself. Not yet. But the rumors clung to him, whispering truths he had long feared. The palace would be watching. The capital would not welcome him as heir returned. It would greet him as myth walking.

By the time they reached the outskirts of the city, it was dusk. Eldareth’s spires rose into the blood-colored sky, gilded in gold and arrogance. But beneath them—deep in the foundation stones, hidden in tunnels so old even the masons had forgotten them—something pulsed.

Kaelen felt the shard calling.

And something else.

Something waiting.

Eldareth looked unchanged from a distance—its towers still pierced the sky like silver lances, and the banners of the royal house fluttered proudly in the dying light of the sun. But as Kaelen and Rorin crept closer under the cover of a merchant’s cloak and a shadowed hood, the illusion of continuity shattered with every step. The streets were wrong. Guards no longer stood watch at familiar posts. Whole districts lay eerily silent, their windows shuttered not for the night, but as if to ward off some unseen blight.

When they passed through the South Gate—unchallenged, for no one manned it—Kaelen felt a surge of nausea. It wasn’t sickness. It was dissonance. The kind born not from memory, but from grief. This was his city. His home. The capital of Aravell. And it no longer recognized him.

The people had changed too. Faces looked hollow, drawn with suspicion and weariness. Children didn’t play in the alleys. Merchants whispered behind closed stalls, and wherever Kaelen looked, he saw eyes glance away too quickly, as if afraid of being caught in a thought. The scent of fear was everywhere, layered with soot and iron.

“Something’s happened,” Kaelen said quietly as they passed what remained of the old artisan quarter.

Rorin nodded grimly. “We’ve been gone long enough for rot to set in. And look at the sky.”

Kaelen glanced upward.

It had begun to split again.

A thin red line bisected the heavens like an open wound, stretching from horizon to horizon. No one else seemed to see it. Or perhaps they did, and no longer had the strength to acknowledge it.

The palace, once unreachable without ceremony, now stood open. The gates had been torn from their hinges. Guards stood in place, but their eyes were hollow and unfocused, as though moved not by will, but by rhythm.

Rorin stared at one of them for a moment too long and muttered, “They’re not breathing.”

Kaelen approached slowly, expecting resistance, but none came. The guards did not move, blink, or speak. They simply stood, frozen in immaculate armor now dulled by dust.

Within the palace walls, the air was cold. Not cold as in winter, but cold like deep water—silent, pressing. The halls echoed with memories Kaelen had buried: his father’s voice barking orders, his brother’s laughter during feast nights, the soft rustle of his mother’s gown as she paced in silence.

But now, there were no voices. No music. Just the endless thud of his own footsteps and the quiet murmur of the shards within his cloak.

They made their way down into the lower levels, bypassing chambers and galleries long untouched. The farther they descended, the older the stones became. Runes marked the walls, faint beneath the dust—symbols of a time before the First Conclave, before even the founders of Eldareth named themselves kings. Kaelen felt the call intensify with each stair. The seventh shard was near.

At last, they reached a sealed door.

It bore no handle. No hinge. Just a circle carved into the stone, around which seven empty slots had been shaped, each waiting to receive what Kaelen now carried.

He took out the shards, hands trembling. Rorin stood silently behind him, face pale and jaw set. Without words, Kaelen placed each shard into its place: the jagged sliver from the monastery; the black fang of Sylveth; the eye from the dream-mirror; the weeping stone from the barrow; the cracked coin from the silver forest; and the final, gleaming sliver that had called him home.

Each shard clicked into place with a subtle vibration. When the sixth slid into its hollow, the door groaned, and the seventh—missing—revealed itself.

It hovered there.

A shape of light.

A shard not of matter, but of memory.

Kaelen reached forward, and it passed into him.

Suddenly, the world bent.

He was not in the corridor anymore. He stood in a grand chamber lit by no torches. Seven thrones circled a great pit, and in each throne sat a shadow. Not human. Not wholly formed. They pulsed with power but lacked solidity.

One of them leaned forward, and its voice poured into Kaelen like molten thought.

“You have gathered the remnants. You bear the memory of what was broken. Now comes the last choice.”

Kaelen braced himself. “What choice?”

“To remake. Or to release.”

The other shadows stirred. Another voice, softer, yet no less commanding, followed. “To remake the world is to bind it. To keep it whole. To carry its weight forever. You become its anchor.”

A third voice: “To release it is to free the fabric. Let the seams fall. Let it dissolve. End the cycle.”

Kaelen’s thoughts raced. “And what happens to the people?”

“If you remake the world,” the first voice said, “they endure. But changed. Bound to your memory of what should be.”

“And if I release it?”

“They vanish,” the last shadow said. “Not in pain. Not in suffering. But as if they never were. Peace, at last. True and utter peace.”

Kaelen’s knees buckled. He thought of Rorin, of Serah, of Arreth. Of the nameless monks. Of his brother, dead on a battlefield yet still walking in dreams.

He looked into the pit at the center of the thrones.

Inside it, he saw a seed.

Not a seed of wood or root—but a shard, the final one. The first one.

His own soul, split long ago.

“This is what I was meant to do,” Kaelen whispered. “This was always about more than saving the world.”

“It was never about saving it,” the first shadow said. “It was about deciding if it deserved to be saved at all.”

Kaelen stepped forward.

He touched the shard.

Light erupted—neither blinding nor warm, but clarifying.

And then—

He was back in the corridor.

The door was open.

And inside, the chamber pulsed with life.

Rorin stared at him. “What did you choose?”

Kaelen’s voice trembled. “I chose not to choose yet.”

He stepped through the door, and behind him, the palace began to shake.

Chapter Five: The Fire Between Worlds

The chamber beyond the sealed door bore no resemblance to anything above it. Gone were the stone-cut corridors and the sigils of old kings. This was a space out of time, out of form, out of anything Kaelen could name. The floor was made of glass, but beneath it flowed fire—slow-moving, viscous, and golden, like the blood of the sun. Pillars rose into nothingness, vanishing into darkness above, and between them drifted the echoes of voices, disembodied and half-spoken, repeating fragments of lives no longer lived.

Kaelen stepped cautiously forward, feeling the glass beneath his feet tremble with each step, not with weakness, but with awareness. The place was watching him. Judging him. Waiting.

Rorin entered after a long pause, his face tight and drawn. “This isn’t a vault,” he said. “It’s a wound.”

Kaelen said nothing. He moved toward the center of the chamber, where a dais stood, floating slightly above the floor, reached by a narrow bridge of molten light. The shards in his pouch buzzed now, not in fear or in power, but in anticipation. All seven had been gathered, all memories recovered, but none of it would mean anything unless he understood what came next.

On the dais sat a woman.

Not Virell. Not a ghost. Not a goddess.

But her.

The one who had sent the first message. The invitation burned into the letter. Her silver-threaded robes flowed behind her like water suspended in air, and her face, though young, bore the fatigue of ages.

“I wondered how long you would take,” she said, her voice quiet, almost amused.

Kaelen stepped onto the bridge. “You knew I’d come?”

“No,” she replied. “I hoped. And I prepared for both outcomes.”

She rose slowly, revealing a blade in her hand—thin, almost ceremonial, its edge the color of moonlight. “You carry the seven. But the eighth lies here.”

“I thought there were only seven,” Kaelen said.

“There were. Until you.”

Her gaze was steady, though sadness pooled at its edges. “Each shard you recovered was a fracture of the original source. Long ago, before memory was memory, there was only one truth. It was broken by those who feared its power. The pieces scattered. But every time they are gathered, a new fragment is born—a mirror to the one who gathers them.”

Kaelen felt his breath catch. “You're saying I’m the final shard?”

“No,” she said. “I am.”

He froze.

Her smile was gentle. “The last echo. The guardian of the remnant. I was created the moment you stepped through the first door. My purpose is to end this path.”

She raised the blade.

“And to test you.”

Kaelen stepped forward, Mourning in hand. “You want to fight?”

“I don’t,” she said. “But I must. The final shard can only be claimed by the worthy. And worthiness is not measured in trials or strength. It is measured in willingness to bleed for what cannot be owned.”

She struck.

The clash was quiet—not a roar of metal, but a sigh. The chamber darkened as their blades met, the fire beneath the glass rippling with each parry. Her movements were graceful, exacting. She did not waste a breath. Kaelen, though skilled, found himself on the defensive, Mourning barely holding against her relentless pressure.

“You’ve walked far,” she said between strikes. “But walking isn’t the same as bearing.”

“I’ve lost to get here!” he shouted, pressing forward, slashing low.

“Loss is not proof. Sacrifice is.”

They moved like dancers through flame. Kaelen’s mind drifted—back to the Hollow Monastery, to the dream-mirrors, to the Sleeper’s words: You are your own danger now.

He understood.

He had been tested not by what he faced, but by what he carried.

As her blade sliced across his shoulder, drawing blood, he didn’t cry out. He let go of the fear. The image of who he had to be. The crownless prince. The reluctant savior. The child of a broken throne.

And he chose.

He dropped Mourning.

She paused, blade poised. “You yield?”

“No,” Kaelen said. “I refuse.”

“To what?”

“To keep fighting when I understand the answer.”

He stepped forward.

She pressed the blade to his chest.

He did not flinch.

Her eyes widened.

And she smiled.

“Well done,” she whispered.

The blade dissolved. So did she.

In her place, a shard floated. Not crystal. Not metal. Just light.

Kaelen reached for it.

And the world changed.

He stood in a field of stars. Below him, Aravell twisted—torn, fraying, bleeding. Whole cities crumbled into rivers of shadow. The sky boiled with storms. He saw Serah standing before an army of glass-skinned invaders. He saw Rorin, weeping at the foot of a dying tree. He saw himself, over and over, making different choices, walking different paths.

The final shard hovered before him.

It pulsed once.

And then the voices returned.

Remake.

Release.

Repeat.

He saw the circle of thrones again. The pit. The seed.

He saw the door that had opened at the start of this all.

He saw the invitation.

The world is ending. You must come.

Kaelen understood now.

He wasn’t meant to stop the end.

He was meant to choose what came next.

He took the shard.

The sky exploded with fire and light.

He awoke beneath the stars.

The palace was gone. The city, silent.

Beside him, Rorin stirred. Alive.

Above them, the red wound in the sky had sealed.

Kaelen looked to the horizon.

A new sun rose.

The shards were gone.

But the memory remained.

And the world… was beginning.

Again.

The fire between the worlds faded slowly, as if reluctant to relinquish its hold on Kaelen’s vision. He lay still for some time, uncertain whether he stood in the ruins of the palace or the ash-drenched basin of his own mind. There was a ringing in his ears, not sharp, but deep, resonant—as though something ancient had struck a bell within him that would never stop sounding. The chamber had changed. Gone were the thrones and the pit, the shadows and riddles. What remained was simple: a garden.

Or at least, something that wanted to be one.

Wild roots clawed through stone tiles. Pale flowers bloomed in silent patches, glowing faintly where no sunlight reached. Water trickled up through cracks, running not downhill but skyward, coiling into spirals and vanishing into unseen clouds. It was a garden not meant for life as Kaelen knew it, but something... adjacent. Something else.

He stood slowly, hand brushing the glass floor. His shoulder still ached from the battle with the final guardian—the woman of starlight whose blade had cut not flesh, but illusion. She had been real and not real, truth and test and mirror all at once. He had passed. Or survived. Sometimes, those were the same.

Rorin approached from the shadow of a collapsed archway, bruised but whole. His armor was scraped, his eyes hollowed by hours of tension. And yet he smiled when he saw Kaelen standing.

“Thought I’d find a pile of ash,” he muttered. “You don’t disappoint.”

Kaelen shook his head. “Did it end?”

“I don’t know. I only know the shaking stopped. The sky sealed. The stars burned cleaner. And the palace…” Rorin gestured up.

Kaelen followed his gaze. Above them, where once had loomed the towering halls of the royal house, now arched only open sky—vast and unmarred, stars spinning in clear and perfect patterns. The city had not collapsed. It had lifted. The palace and its bones had vanished, leaving only this strange place where stone had turned to soil and silence had become song.

“I made a choice,” Kaelen said softly. “But I’m not sure what it was.”

Rorin clapped a hand on his shoulder, wincing. “We’re alive. For now, that’s good enough.”

But Kaelen knew it wasn’t. Not truly.

He felt the shards within him, no longer separate entities, but one thing—merged, complete. The memories they carried had not faded. If anything, they pressed deeper now. Visions of other Kaelens—king and coward, tyrant and sacrifice—haunted his thoughts. And beyond them, the voice from the thrones still echoed.

Remake. Release. Repeat.

The world had begun again. That much was clear. But how it had done so remained hidden. And there was still one final thread unpulled.

They departed the chamber the way they had come, though the path had shifted. The halls were no longer halls but colonnades of silver wood. The air no longer held dust but shimmered with invisible particles, like the residue of divine breath. As they walked, Kaelen caught glimpses of others—faces, flickering through trees, vanishing when he turned. Some were people he’d met. Others, people he had never known, but somehow missed.

Outside, the city had been transformed.

Eldareth’s towers remained, but they leaned now, not out of disrepair, but as though listening. The river no longer ran but hovered in coils over the bridges. The sky held not one sun but two, rotating around one another in a slow dance. And its people—

They were waking.

Not with panic. With recognition.

Kaelen saw it in their eyes: some shimmer of memory, some truth they had never spoken, now settling into place. Children looked older. The elderly stood taller. Merchants opened stalls not to sell, but to give.

Rorin exhaled. “Gods. You did something.”

Kaelen nodded. “But I don’t think it was just me.”

They found Serah in the market square, speaking quietly with a group of cloaked strangers Kaelen didn’t recognize. She turned when she saw them, her eyes widening slightly.

“You’re alive,” she said.

“I wasn’t sure I would be,” Kaelen replied. “How long—?”

“Three days,” she said. “No one knows what happened. The sky tore. The palace shook. Then... quiet. And when people looked again, everything was here but new. It’s like we were peeled from time and stitched into another version.”

Kaelen nodded. “That’s close to what it felt like.”

“Did we win?” she asked.

“I don’t know,” he said honestly. “I think we were never meant to win. Just... to choose.”

She studied him a long moment, then gestured to the strangers. “There are others. From other places. Other versions maybe. They came when the world bent. Some of them speak of the Ashen Star. Others of different names. They know you, Kaelen.”

He blinked. “They know me?”

“Not exactly,” she said. “But something like you. Something that could have been you. And they say... the cycle isn’t finished.”

Rorin groaned. “Of course it isn’t.”

Kaelen looked up at the twin suns. One burned gold. The other flickered silver.

“The shards were memory,” he said. “But memory is a seed. It grows. And sometimes it grows back into the same thing.”

Serah stepped forward. “So what now?”

Kaelen looked to the horizon. The sky there bent again—barely perceptible, like heat rippling across stone. A new door. A new fracture.

“We follow the next invitation,” he said.

And somewhere, in the quiet of the reshaped world, something burned anew.

The next morning arrived without ceremony. The twin suns hung in a sky painted not with clouds, but with great sweeping bands of color—ochre, plum, deep teal—moving like slow tides across the firmament. Eldareth stirred with tentative rhythm, its people re-learning how to live in a city that had not simply changed, but been recreated. Streets once broken were smooth and luminous, veined with vines of light that pulsed faintly beneath the stones. The fountains ran with clear water, yet when Kaelen passed one and looked into its pool, the reflection staring back at him was… older. Not in years, but in understanding. The burden of choice, it seemed, aged faster than time.

He walked in silence through the city with Rorin beside him, hooded still, though the need for secrecy had long passed. The people they passed no longer whispered his name, not out of ignorance or fear, but because they no longer needed to. Somehow, without edict or banner, they knew who he was. Or what he had done. And Kaelen knew that while his name might still echo, he had become something else now—an idea, an echo of possibility that had taken shape in flesh only long enough to set something new in motion.

“I’m not sure I belong here anymore,” he said, pausing at the steps of the old Library of Ethedrel, which now gleamed with walls of transparent crystal rather than sandstone.

Rorin grunted. “That’s fair. The world barely knows where it belongs anymore. And I’ve stopped trying to understand how we’re still walking around after standing in a room that didn’t obey gravity.”

Kaelen turned to face him fully. “You were always more grounded than I was.”

Rorin offered a rare, half-hearted smile. “That’s what makes me the one who survives the madness.”

They entered the library not to read, but to speak. Kaelen had asked for Serah to meet him there, away from the open streets and the gathering murmurs. She arrived just as the second sun reached its noonward arc, flanked by two others—one a woman in scaled armor that shimmered like frost, the other a tall, mute man with a cloth over his eyes and a shard-shaped pendant at his throat. Kaelen sensed it the moment they entered: the same tension, the same vibration in the air that had once followed the shards. These people were not born of this Eldareth.

“I told you,” Serah said, her voice low, her eyes locked on Kaelen’s. “They’ve come from elsewhere. From folds in the world that don’t match ours. Some speak of a cycle. A repetition.”

The woman stepped forward. “I am Kythe,” she said. “I held the third shard in my world before it fell back into silence. You are Kaelen, the breaker.”

Kaelen frowned. “Breaker?”

“The one who doesn’t follow the pattern,” she replied. “The one who chooses something else.”

Rorin raised an eyebrow. “And what pattern would that be?”

The mute man removed the cloth from his eyes. Where sight should have been was darkness—an absence so deep it bent the light around it. And yet, he spoke clearly into their minds.

The worlds turn. Each time, someone is chosen. Each time, they gather the pieces. And each time, they must choose: restoration… or release.

Kaelen sat heavily on one of the reading benches. The quiet throb of the seven shards still echoed in his chest. “And I chose neither.”

“You delayed the end,” Kythe said. “But the cycle continues. It always does. Somewhere, even now, another door opens. Another letter is read. Another prince walks alone.”

Serah leaned forward. “So what happens now? Do we wait until the cycle catches up to us?”

Kaelen closed his eyes. “We prepare for what comes through.”

He felt the weight of what that meant. He had not saved the world in the way stories often promised. There had been no final battle to end all things, no climactic moment where darkness was struck down and light triumphed. There had only been the question—and the refusal to answer it in the way the world expected.

He had bought time. Nothing more.

But time was a precious thing.

That evening, the new court met beneath the remains of the old throne room, which now opened onto the sky. The stars had returned to their rightful places, but their light carried new weight. They looked down on a people waking into a world whose rules had shifted, subtly but permanently.

Kaelen stood before them—not as a king, for the crown was broken and the throne buried—but as the one who had held the choice and refused to let it be made for him.

“I won’t name myself ruler,” he said. “Nor will I command you. The world you walk in now is different, and you must each choose how to live in it. But know this: something stirs beyond the veil. Not darkness. Not light. Just... return.”

A hush fell.

Rorin spoke next. “We defend what we can. And we remember who we were.”

Kaelen nodded. “And when the cycle turns again, when the invitation comes to someone else, perhaps they’ll find that this time, the story changed.”

That night, Kaelen returned to the place where the first door had opened—outside the Eastern courtyard, where once the air had smelled of cinnamon and dying leaves. The grass was green again now, and no door remained. But he felt its echo. A thin seam in the world. A promise waiting to be rewritten.

He sat beside it as the stars turned.

And in the distance, beyond mountains that no longer bore names, something opened.

Again.

Chapter Six: The Door That Waited

The city grew quiet again in the weeks that followed. Not with the silence of fear, but the hush of rebuilding, of learning how to breathe in a world that no longer carried the weight of inevitability. The people of Eldareth—those who remembered the sky cracking open, the palace vanishing, the strange red dawns and blue-lit nights—began to shape a new rhythm. Markets returned, though the currency was no longer coin but trade and story. Schools reopened, not with old texts, but with scrolls still being written. Children ran through the city with wild wonder, often stopping beneath the old skybridge that now curved like a broken rib over the rebuilt central square, whispering that it had once housed a prince who had vanished into flame.

Kaelen remained among them, but distant. He no longer walked the palace halls; instead, he lived in a small tower at the edge of the city, once the dwelling of an astronomer who had recorded starlight before the first Sundering. The observatory’s lens had shattered long ago, but Kaelen repaired it slowly, piece by piece, as though stitching together a way to see what could not be written.

He spent most days in silence, and most nights awake, staring into the sky that shimmered with strange constellations. He was waiting, though he could not have said for what.

The shards were gone—absorbed, perhaps, or buried in the choice he had made—but their memory lingered. Sometimes, when he closed his eyes, he could still feel them: the twisted fang of Sylveth, the cracked coin from the silver grove, the shard of light taken from the woman of stars. They were echoes now, no longer humming, but still present, like the scent of a fire long extinguished.

Rorin visited often, though never stayed long. He’d taken to leading the border efforts, rebuilding roads, escorting trade caravans that emerged from the twisted lands reshaped by Kaelen’s decision. The knight’s shoulders bore new scars, and his smile had grown rare, but when it came, it was genuine.

“Things are changing,” he said one evening, setting down a map marked with new borders and strange symbols. “There are cities now that didn’t exist before. Forests growing from stone. Places where time loops midday for an hour and then lets go.”

Kaelen studied the map in silence. “And the people?”

“They’re adapting,” Rorin said. “But there are... visitors.”

Kaelen looked up. “From beyond?”

Rorin nodded. “A few speak your name. A few speak names we’ve never heard. One carries a sword made of sound. Another has no mouth, but dreams speak through her. They say the threads are fraying again.”

Kaelen returned his gaze to the stars.

“They never stopped,” he said.

Later that night, when the moon passed behind the shattered rings that now hung above the world like frozen tides, Kaelen left his tower.

He carried nothing. No sword. No scroll. Only a name he had never spoken aloud—one that had burned behind his lips since the moment he stepped from the seventh shard’s chamber.

Lorthar.

He didn’t know where it came from. Only that it waited.

The name was the key. And the key had found its lock.

He walked for three days into the highlands, beyond the edge of every map he had ever studied. The terrain bent strangely, as if reluctant to remember its shape. Rocks that had once been cliffs now floated, suspended over ridges that split without quake. The wind spoke in sighs, not voices, and the stars blinked slower the further he walked.

He reached the ridge at dusk, and there—framed by two trees whose bark shimmered like quicksilver—stood the door.

It was not the same door he had first passed through in the forest beyond Eldareth. This one pulsed. It breathed. And its surface was not wood or bone or bramble, but a polished blackness that reflected no light. No image. Just emptiness.

He approached and laid his hand on it.

It did not open.

Instead, it whispered: You are no longer the one who was summoned.

Kaelen stepped back. “Then who is?”

The door said nothing.

Kaelen waited until dawn. Then he turned to leave.

But before he crossed back over the ridge, he looked once more over his shoulder.

The door was gone.

Back in Eldareth, he found a letter waiting for him.

The parchment was old. The ink fresh. The seal—a familiar smear shaped like a falling star—shimmered faintly in the sunlight.

It bore no address.

Inside, a single line:

“The world is ending. You must come.”

But it was not written to him.

It was addressed to another name.

Not Kaelen.

Lorthar.

He stared at it for a long time.

Something had begun again.

The cycle had turned.

But this time, he would not walk the path alone.

Kaelen kept the letter in a simple leather pouch, the kind once used by scribes to carry unfinished scrolls—unassuming, anonymous. He did not open it again. He did not need to. The words were etched not only in ink, but in the air around him, in the subtle shift of light when dusk approached, in the way the earth seemed to hold its breath when he passed. The message was no longer his, and yet it moved with him like a shadow that refused to dissolve. He had once thought the ending of the world would come with fire, or ice, or silence. Now, he knew it came with invitation.

It was not a singular cataclysm that brought about change—it was the soft, repeating beat of decision, echoing through the centuries like a drum just beneath hearing. Kaelen had not destroyed the old world. Nor had he saved it. He had chosen it. He had reshaped it, and now, the shape waited for another to press against it, bend it further, break it wider, heal it deeper.

He spent the following days in the lower districts of Eldareth. The rebuilt ones. The parts where rubble had turned into gardens, and crumbling streets into spaces where artists carved their thoughts into stone and children learned to ask questions no one had answers for. It was there he saw the boy.

He was perhaps eleven. Small, but with a quickness to his gaze that Kaelen recognized instantly—not intelligence alone, but knowing. As if the boy were walking two steps ahead of time, always aware of what would be asked before it came.

He wore a pendant shaped like a shard.

Kaelen crouched beside a fountain where the boy sat watching the water.

“You don’t belong here,” the boy said without looking up.

Kaelen tilted his head. “Why not?”

“Because you’re already gone,” the boy said. “You just haven’t left yet.”

Kaelen said nothing.

The boy reached into a satchel and handed Kaelen a smooth stone. Its surface shimmered, though it bore no symbols, no mark. But as Kaelen touched it, he felt the hum.

A shard.

But not broken.

Whole.

“You’ll need that,” the boy said.

Kaelen took it gently. “You know what this is?”

“I know what you are,” the boy replied. “And I know what I am. You were a question. I’m an answer. But not the last one.”

Kaelen felt his mouth dry. “What’s your name?”

“Lorthar,” the boy said.

Kaelen nodded. Of course it was.

“You’ve had the dream?” he asked.

“Every night,” the boy said. “A sky of broken glass. A tower made of bones. A woman with silver eyes holding a door closed. And someone—someone who is me but isn’t—calling through it.”

Kaelen stood. The shard in his palm was cold. Not lifeless, but waiting.

He knelt, placing a hand on the boy’s shoulder. “When the door opens,” he said, “don’t wait. Go through. But remember this: you don’t have to be the one who fixes everything. You only have to make the next choice.”

Lorthar looked up at him. “Will you be there?”

Kaelen tried to speak, and couldn’t. His throat closed. His mind filled with a thousand images—of versions of himself that had lived and died in countless branching worlds, each hoping, choosing, breaking, believing.

“No,” he said at last. “But everything I’ve done will be.”

Lorthar nodded. He turned back to the water, watching as ripples moved without wind.

Kaelen walked away.

That night, he returned to the place where the stars shone most clearly—an outcrop beyond the city walls, where silver sand blew in quiet spirals and the sky seemed closer. He sat beside the hollow where once the first door had stood. The seam was still there. Faint. Barely visible. But he no longer needed to pass through.

Someone else would.

He opened his hand. The new shard shimmered. It hummed once, then pulsed gently.

Behind him, the wind changed.

A second presence joined him. Not Rorin. Not Serah. Not Virell. Not a ghost or echo or dream.

It was future.

He did not turn to face it. He only placed the shard into the earth, where it nestled like a seed.

“Grow,” he whispered.

The stars did not answer.

But something beyond them shifted.

And Kaelen finally, truly, rested.

By the fourth morning, Kaelen began to dream of the sea.

Not the sea of his childhood—the calm blue waters of Eldareth’s coastlines with their gentle tides and perfumed breeze—but something vast and ancient, a black ocean that churned with stars and memories. In these dreams, he stood on a shore made of voices, each wave crashing into the sand with a different whisper. Remember me, they seemed to say. Forget me. Choose again.

He awoke from one such vision with salt on his lips, though no sea lay within a hundred leagues.

The city continued to breathe, slow and uncertain, like a wounded animal unsure whether to rise or sleep. And Kaelen, in turn, moved among it as both stranger and symbol. They didn’t call him prince anymore. Nor did they dare to name him king. He had become something less defined, more elemental. Not a man of rule, but a man of possibility. Wherever he walked, people paused. Some bowed. Others fled. But many simply stared, unsure of what he was or what they were seeing. He did not blame them. He wasn’t sure either.

Word spread of others—new arrivals, wanderers from other folds of the world who bore strange powers and stranger truths. A woman whose voice caused trees to bloom in winter. A blind child who recited prophecies that no one remembered writing. A man who claimed he could summon entire days from the past and trap them in jars. Kaelen listened, but did not intervene. These were signs. Seeds of what came next. The world had not simply survived his choice. It had been made fertile by it.

Still, there was one place he had not yet dared return.

The tomb of his brother.

It lay in the western quarter, beneath the Chapel of the Thirteenth Flame—a place left largely untouched by the reshaping, as if the past had anchored it in place. The chapel remained modest, its white stone facade cracked with age and the firebowls at its entrance long extinguished. Kaelen arrived alone, wearing no cloak, bearing no blade. The heavy oak doors opened with a low groan, revealing the quiet sanctuary within.

There was no priest. No offerings. Just dust, silence, and the faintest echo of a song no one remembered the words to.

The tomb was marked only by a circle—a ring of obsidian set into the floor. No name. No inscription. Kaelen knelt by it, lowering his hand onto the cold stone.

“I thought you’d hate me for leaving,” he whispered. “But I think maybe you understand now.”

He waited, listening. For what, he didn’t know.

Then, something stirred.

Not sound.

A presence.

Faint, but unmistakable.

A warmth on his shoulder.

A flicker of memory not his own.

He saw his brother—Theren—standing beside him as they once were, high on the parapets during their father’s coronation. The day Kaelen had turned away from duty, and Theren had stepped into its shadow.

You never failed, the memory said.

Kaelen’s throat tightened. “I couldn’t save you.”

You weren’t meant to. That wasn’t the shape of your story.

The presence faded, leaving only the cold and the silence.

Kaelen stood. He did not cry. Not out of pride, but because he no longer needed to. Grief had been part of the shaping. Now it was part of the soil.

Outside, the wind had changed again.

Rorin met him at the base of the chapel steps, arms crossed, his hair wind-tangled, armor dusty.

“There’s been another door,” he said. “Far to the south. Past the Misted Reach.”

Kaelen nodded.

“Do you think it’s for you?” Rorin asked.

Kaelen looked at the sky. The twin suns had begun to draw apart again, like siblings leaving home. “No,” he said. “It’s for someone else.”

“But you’ll follow it.”

Kaelen smiled faintly. “No. I’ll meet it halfway.”

The two men stood there a while longer, watching as a flock of birds—black-feathered and gleaming—cut across the sky in perfect spiral. A sign. A warning. A beginning.

Kaelen closed his eyes.

He still remembered the first letter.

The world is ending. You must come.

Now, he imagined a different message.

The world is beginning. And you are already here.

He turned and walked toward the path beyond the chapel, the road that led into the southern wilds.

He would not carry the shards again.

But he would carry memory.

Because that, too, was a kind of power.

And maybe, this time, it would be enough.

Kaelen's path led him southward, away from the last remnants of the throne that had once shaped his fate. Past the bright avenues of Eldareth, through meadows where the grass shimmered silver in the morning frost, and into the long valleys that lay cradled beneath the shadow of the Grethar Mountains. These were lands untouched by the recent unraveling, but not unaware. The people here spoke cautiously of the changes—the subtle ones, the quiet ones. Wells that whispered. Trees that grew taller in moonlight. Children who dreamed together and woke with matching scars.

They did not know Kaelen’s name. He walked as a traveler, not a legend. That suited him. He no longer needed to carry the weight of a world’s gaze. His purpose had shifted. He had become a thread, not a banner. Something to weave through the seams of the world rather than pull them taut.

At dusk one evening, he arrived at a village so small it had no name—just a row of houses built from riverstone, nestled around a crooked bridge that arched over a stream whose surface never rippled. A fire glowed in the hearth of the common house. He was welcomed not with suspicion, but with the simple hospitality of people too tired to ask questions.

“Stormwalker,” a young girl called him, pointing to the odd light in his eyes.

Her mother hushed her, but Kaelen only smiled.

He stayed there that night, listening to the wind through the shutters and the strange songs of crickets that had begun to echo like distant flutes. That evening, a boy knocked gently at his door. Twelve years old, perhaps. Wary, quiet-eyed, holding something tightly wrapped in linen.

“I was told to give this to you,” the boy said.

Kaelen knelt. “By who?”

The boy shrugged. “She never said her name. She just said you’d know what to do.”

Kaelen unwrapped the cloth.

Inside was a shard.

Not of memory, not of glass. Something else.

A sliver of petrified wood, dark and veined, warm to the touch.

It pulsed in his palm.

A new shard.

He sat heavily on the edge of the cot, the weight of realization coiling in his gut. The cycle had not ended. But it had shifted. It no longer turned like a wheel—bound, predictable. Now it spiraled outward, erratic, growing with each revolution.

This shard was not a piece of the past.

It was something future-born.

He looked at the boy. “She—did she say anything else?”

The boy shook his head, but hesitated. “Only that you’d understand. When the trees begin to speak.”

Kaelen stayed in the village for three more days. He walked the glimmering meadows in the morning and watched the twin moons pass over each other at night, their soft glow painting the hills in overlapping shadows. He spoke little, but listened always.

And on the third night, the trees spoke.

It was not a voice, not quite. Not words. But intent. The will of something vast, slumbering, now just beginning to stir. The trees leaned toward each other as though in counsel, and the wind danced with messages that Kaelen felt more than heard.

He rose and followed.

Into the deeper woods, through tangled roots and hanging moss, into a hollow where the air tasted like iron and old rain. There he found a circle of stones—ancient, moss-covered, cracked from age and upheaval.

At their center stood a woman.

She wore no robe, no crown, no armor. Her hair was the color of twilight, her skin inked with constellations that pulsed softly under her breath. She turned as he entered, her eyes already fixed on his.

“You were never meant to carry the end,” she said.

“I didn’t,” Kaelen replied. “I passed it on.”

She nodded. “As was needed.”

She stepped aside, revealing a pool in the earth behind her. Its surface was silver, but did not reflect. It absorbed. Held. Waited.

“This is the new gate,” she said. “But it will not open for you. Your path was to shape the door. Not to walk through it.”

Kaelen stepped forward. The pool stirred, sending gentle ripples out toward the stones.

“Then who will?”

She looked at him with something almost like sorrow. “Those who come next. Those who’ve seen what you could not. Who carry not the memory of the world—but its possibility.”

Kaelen looked down at the shard in his palm. The petrified sliver pulsed once.

“They’ll need more than stories,” he said.

“They’ll have seeds,” she replied.

She reached out and touched his chest, above his heart.

From her touch bloomed a second light, a glow that spread through his body in delicate arcs, like a constellation being born.

“You are no longer bound to the shards,” she said. “But you are one of them now.”

He felt it.

His memories were no longer only his own.

They would pass forward.

Into hands unshaped. Into hearts yet born.

When he left the hollow, dawn was cresting the ridge, and the stars were fading slowly from his skin.

Chapter Seven: Inheritance

The roads beyond the Misted Reach were older than memory and less traveled than myth. Here the sky often refused to open entirely, clinging instead to thick veils of pale vapor that never quite became fog, nor dissipated into air. Beneath these clouds, the land sloped and bent with strange insistence, and what little sunlight broke through found itself swallowed by soil that drank not only water but sound. Kaelen walked alone now, though the weight of company never left him. It lingered in the hum of the shard he carried, in the slow reverberation of his heartbeat that seemed sometimes to echo with more than one rhythm.

He was not the first to walk this road. He knew that. Nor would he be the last.

The village he found—unnamed, like most places this far from Eldareth—had no signs, no boundary stones. It simply emerged from the mist, a cluster of low structures built from reeds, thatch, and driftwood gathered from rivers that had long since changed course. The people spoke little. Their eyes were wary, but not cruel. They offered food in exchange for stories, and Kaelen gave them one—about a boy who once believed kings wore crowns of gold, and learned instead that real power walked barefoot in the mud, carrying silence.

That night, he slept beneath an open sky for the first time in weeks. The stars here seemed unfamiliar—constellations drawn in unfamiliar geometry, as though this region of the world had inherited a different heaven entirely. Yet the moon remained constant, its fractured twin drifting behind it in slow orbit, casting a pale mirror-light across the land.

When Kaelen dreamed, it was not of doors or thrones or battles.

He dreamed of a child.

She stood on a cliff, overlooking a sea that burned green under the moon. Her hair was wild and dark. Her hands were stained with ink. She turned to him and spoke his name—not the one he carried now, but the one he had never said aloud. The name that had been buried beneath layers of identity, obligation, memory.

And she smiled.

“You didn’t end anything,” she said.

He sat up gasping, the cold night air sharp in his throat. The shard in his palm glowed faintly, its warmth rising as if in recognition.

By morning, he knew where he had to go.

It wasn’t a palace.

It wasn’t a battlefield.

It was a school.

The Tower of Adrial had once been a place for elite instruction—where noble sons were trained in politics, warfare, and the art of shaping language into chains. But after the Reshaping, it had been repurposed. No longer a fortress of rank and rule, it had become something stranger and far more dangerous: a sanctuary of ideas.

Kaelen arrived at dawn, hooded and dust-covered, a traveler with no herald. The gates opened before he knocked.

The children who ran the halls looked nothing like the pages or squires he had once trained beside. These children wore patchwork robes and carried books tied with vines. They chanted in multiple tongues and argued over the meanings of stars. They watched Kaelen with a curiosity that didn’t come from reverence but challenge.

One girl, no older than ten, stepped before him with a wooden staff.

“You’ve come to give it up,” she said. “Haven’t you?”

He looked at her—really looked—and realized she wasn’t guessing.

“Yes,” he said. “If it will be accepted.”

The girl reached out her hand, and Kaelen placed the shard in her palm.

It pulsed once, then quieted.

“It remembers,” she said. “Not what you did, but what you didn’t.”

Kaelen knelt. “Then teach it what comes next.”

She studied him for a long moment. “You don’t have to stay.”

“I know.”

“But you can.”

“I know.”

He stood, and she turned, leading him deeper into the tower.

Each room they passed echoed with energy. Children in circles, breathing in rhythm with crystal orbs. Walls that changed shape depending on the stories spoken within them. Windows that peered not outside, but into other possible tomorrows. The world was not being restored—it was being reimagined, re-authored by those not yet told what was impossible.

At the top of the tower, they reached a room filled with wind. No doors. Just sky.

She turned to him, eyes bright with inheritance.

“Do you know what we’re building?” she asked.

Kaelen shook his head. “No. But I think it’s better that way.”

She smiled again. “You’re not a prince anymore.”

“No,” he said. “And I never needed to be.”

He stepped into the wind.

Behind him, the tower shimmered.

Ahead, the world opened wide.

Kaelen did not remain in the Tower of Adrial.

He stayed long enough to witness the first of the new rituals—children kneeling in quiet circles, not in worship but in conversation, their voices woven together like threads forming a tapestry of intent. They were not learning spells or strategy or systems of control. They were learning how to listen. How to hold memory without clinging to it. How to ask questions that no longer had to lead to war.

The shard he had given them was placed in a basin carved from a stone that bled light. The basin sat at the center of the highest chamber, and over the days, Kaelen watched it change. The shard pulsed faintly at first, then grew quieter. It sank into the stone—not buried, not lost, but absorbed. Its light did not disappear. It spread. Into the walls, into the floor, into the voices of those who came near. It did not command. It taught. The fragment of his journey, his decision, his unwillingness to choose the expected path had become something transferable. A kind of unspoken guidance.

He left at dawn, without farewell.

There was no need. Those who carried the future did not need reminders from the past. They needed space to grow.

Southward, the land grew warmer, softer. Hills gave way to open valleys, the kind that cradled mist in the mornings and released it with a sigh by noon. Kaelen moved slowly, not because he was tired, but because he was beginning to understand how not to rush. There was no throne waiting. No crown. No war to meet or end to forestall. There was only presence. The shape of a man who had once stood at the edge of the world and chosen not to be its savior, but its steward.

He met others along the way.

A woman walking backward who spoke in riddles and wept when he gave her his name.

A merchant who had once dreamed of the sky bleeding and recognized Kaelen’s face from a memory that had not yet happened.

A child who claimed to be dreaming for three.

Each left him with something—a word, a song, a breath. Small offerings that seemed meaningless at first but settled into him like seeds waiting for rain.

One night, Kaelen camped by a river that shimmered green beneath a crescent moon. The water ran uphill for a time, then corrected itself. He no longer questioned such things. The world had shifted. Logic had been rearranged. Meaning had become layered.
