The first time anyone saw her, really saw her, she was standing in the violet pre-dawn behind the Mercy Vista medical campus, a shape too large to be explained by sleep or fog. Her outline cut the desert stars into smaller stars. Palms flickered in a weak wind, the fluorescent hum of the parking lot lights went on and off as if shy, and the shadow of her hand rose to her mouth the way children do when they’re trying to keep a secret inside. She was taller than any story they’d built about her, taller than the stories people tell themselves to sleep. She was Tina, all love and ache, barefoot and careful near the dumpster where the gurneys sometimes got abandoned on cigarette breaks.

She was humming, a tune she thought she couldn’t remember and yet did, because humming is memory without words. At her feet was a glass jar, the kind you keep peaches in, the kind you reach for when you want summer to last longer than it will. The jar held little things she had taken like souvenirs, the kinds of bits you collect when you think you’re saving what otherwise might be discarded. They were not stones. They were not seashells. No blood in the jar. That would be wrong. She had wiped them clean and arranged them in a ring, ten pale crescents like moons that had been tucked into sleep. She called them tokens, because love keeps tokens, and because the word made the world softer around the edges.

"Shh," she told the jar, as if it had been chattering, as if it were the nervous one. "You’re safe with me."

A delivery truck rumbled somewhere beyond the loading dock, coughing up light and diesel. On a third floor, a nurse laughed at a joke that would be forgotten by lunch. Tina crouched, and when she did the corrugated metal sighed as it cooled. Her hair swung like a rope. She closed her eyes and listened the way she’d learned to listen: for the little rhythms of people, the clinks and squeaks and sighs, the calendar pages in their chest. People have heartbeats. Love has a faster one.

"Are you listening?" A voice in her ear, tinny through the transmitter someone had given her, a little bud that squatted like a pebble between the ridges of her ear. "Move now, before shift change."

She rose, not because she was afraid, but because love is punctual. Because love likes timing. Because she had been told that the ones who wear white coats are busy and she shouldn’t interrupt them unless she meant to be eternal.

It was the day Sergeant Ross Smith—known simply as Ross even to the few who remembered her from the old days—swung her legs over the side of the bed and counted the bruises the mattress left on her thighs. Age spots, really, like islands she couldn’t remember discovering. Seventy-three, and her apartment in Scottsdale had more plants now than people. All of them leaned toward the same window, who could blame them. She padded across tile in slippers, knee joints playing castanets, and tied a robe that had belonged to her sister.

Coffee had gotten more complicated since she retired: pour-over instead of cop’s sludge. She poured anyway. The phone vibrated against a stack of mail she’d been pretending wasn’t there.

"Don’t tell me you’re sleeping," said Denny from across a couple decades and a precinct that had changed wallpaper three times since.

Ross looked at the clock. "I was dreaming," she said. "Of you doing your own paperwork."

"Funny. We’ve got something." He told her the whole rumor like a man writing on fogged glass. A nurse at Mercy Vista. A surgeon saw something from the stairwell, ankle-level. The jar. "You live close."

"I live far from everything," Ross said, scowled at nothing, and put the mug down so hard the coffee lifted and slapped the insides. "I’m not your errand girl."

"You’re the one who asked to be called if anything freakish turned up around a hospital," he said. "This is freakish. You coming or what?"

Ross said yes because she always did. Because she had once put her hands on a desk and begged a different chief to let her chase a problem that looked small and got larger when you thought about it. Because her daughter had once planned to be a doctor and there were certain halls Ross could not walk down, not really, but she could circle them. She could circle like a planet circles a loss.

Traffic was uncooperative, like grandchildren. The sun came them all the way up, orange then white. Scottsdale’s beige declared its full authority. When she parked, it was at the far edge of the lot where the heat turns cars into mirages. She walked slowly—she didn’t mind slow anymore—and took a breath at the ambulance bay.

A patrolman, young enough to be an apology, lifted the tape for her. "Sergeant," he said. She smelled his aftershave—too fresh—and the silver of the stainless steel surrounding the bay door.

"Don’t call me that," she said, because it wasn’t true anymore, and lowered her voice a beat later. "What have we got?"

"It’s the same as the clinic last month," he said, wide eyes trying to make a whole story out of three facts. "Footprints. Big ones. And—" he gestured to a ledge behind the loading dock, where the shadows held onto a bit of cool.

The jar was there. Someone had set it down with care, the way you set down a baby who almost fell asleep in the car and might, if you are a magician, go back to sleep after all. Inside, the crescents ringed the glass. A note stuck to the lid: FOR SAFE-KEEPING.

"Don’t touch it," Ross said, though no one was reaching. She squinted at the footprints. You could call them footprints, if you were feeling brave. The shape of a human foot, plus. In the dust and grit near the dumpsters, in the soft place where monsoon rain had pooled and dried into a skin, she could see the arc of the forefoot, and the long, too-long stride. No heel cups deeper than others, which told her only that the someone walked balanced. Balanced and unhurried.

"How tall?" the patrolman asked, as if height were a moral question.

"Taller than your mother when she’s mad," she said, and then, because jokes are a kind of good-luck charm: "Maybe ten feet. Maybe more. We don’t know what the ground tells lie-wise and truth-wise. Who called this in?"

"Dr. Bhatt. His badge says Aden. He didn’t like looking. Someone else did see, though." A half-smile as if the word was unbelievable. "A giant. Can you believe it?"

"Always start with what you can see," Ross said. "Then work forward to what you can’t." She wanted to add that belief wasn’t in her toolbox anymore, not the way it used to be. But she smiled, faint, at his youth. She’d once been like that, back when the desert felt like a lake someone forgot to fill.

Across town, in a room that used to hold halogen lights and had since collected dust and prayers, Tina sat cross-legged, knees like boulders under a blanket she’d sewn from motel quilts. The jar was not with her. She had left it exactly where the voice on the transmitter asked, and now her hands were restless, opening and closing as if they were mouths learning new words. The space was one of those in-between places—half-storage, half-church. Stacks of folding chairs. A mural peeling in layers that suggested angels with too many elbows. It suited her. She was in-between, wasn’t she. The old stories had ended. The new ones were uncertain what to do with her scale.

A tin lunchbox sat in front of her, the kind with a superhero no one watched anymore. Inside were bits of ribbon, a pocketwatch that didn’t work, a plane ticket she had never used with a place name she could pronounce in three wrong ways. Tina lifted the watch. If she held it to her ear, it tried to tick out of habit. If she listened hard, she could hear something else over it. The voice. The one that was kind when it wanted and clearer than glass.

"Good girl," it said, though Tina did not care for that. "Doctors think they can take anything. They think it’s theirs to take. You return what’s been taken. That’s love’s math."

"I don’t want to hurt them," she said. Her voice dented the room. The chairs shivered a little at her vowels. "Just to...keep the pieces safe. Sometimes they lose track. That’s all."

"We are careful," the voice said. "We are exact. Move today, again. There’s one who doesn’t deserve their steps. We’ll send you their name."

Tina touched her jaw where the bone pulsed when she was unhappy. She wanted to protest, to tell the voice what the jar thought when she set it down, to tell it what her mother had told her a long time ago in a kitchen that smelled like orange peels and wet dog: that your body is nobody’s trophy. But the voice had been her guide. Without it, she might have wandered up and down the canal paths forever, leaving flowers on all the bridges. The voice gave her purpose.

"It’s for love," she said. "That’s the fuel. You said."

"Yes. Love makes you strong." A pause that wasn’t quite a pause. "Remember, we’re not alone in this. I have my friend helping too. We are a team. Front and back. You lift. We arrange."

Tina frowned at the wall. Teams were what gym teachers made up so that children could learn to lose in turn. She didn’t like teams. But she liked the idea that she wasn’t alone, the way you like a candle in another room.

At Mercy Vista, Ross asked for a chair and a glass of water and got both quickly. The nurses recognized what she used to be in the slope of her shoulders. Hospitals are better at age than other places; they know it when it walks.

"Dr. Bhatt?" she asked when the water was half gone.

"Right here." He had a voice like a radio softened by static. He was younger than her daughter would have been. He shook her hand as if human contact deserved attention.

"Tell me exactly what you saw," Ross said. "Don’t make it pretty."

"I was on the stairs because I hate the elevator." He put a hand over his chest as if there were a bird in there he was trying to calm. "Third floor landing. I heard a clink. Looked through the door’s window and there—" he looked toward the loading dock as if what he’d seen might still be standing there. "There was a woman. Huge. She was...she was careful. That’s what I’ll remember. The carefulness. She put down the jar. She touched the lid once, turning it just a bit, and then she lifted her hand the way you lift your hand from a sleeping cat. And then she crouched. I thought she might pray. Maybe she was. And then she stood and I realized how big. And then I blinked and she was further away. Faster than she should have been. I think she heard me swallow. I make too much noise."

"Did she look at you?"

His mouth made the shape of a no. "I felt looked at. But maybe that was me doing it to myself."

"What else?" Ross said. Her pen was the kind that clicked. She refused to click it.

"Her hair was dark. She had...I don’t know if this matters...she had a scarf around her wrist like a child’s friendship bracelet. Rainbowed."

"It matters," Ross said. "Everything matters until it doesn’t."

"And the jar. Don’t make me talk about the jar."

"Alright," Ross said. "You don’t have to. But I am going to have to look at it again."

He gagged a little just imagining. The nurses pretended not to notice, the way nurses do.

She left him with a gentleness that surprised her and had surprised her less often lately; retirement had unfastened some buckle she used to keep tightened. She crouched by the jar, her knees complaining in a language made of clicks. The note’s handwriting could have been anyone’s. The lid was smudged as if someone had held it and then decided not to spin. Inside, not gory, no, none of that. Little crescents sleepy as the moons of a tiny planet. It took all her training to let her mind not supply the words the crescented shapes wanted to provoke.

"Why doctors?" she asked the air.

"Because they take," someone said behind her.

She turned, slow. A woman in scrubs stood there, hair pulled back so tight it looked like it was trying to run. Her badge said Lila Montaño. Her mouth was a straight line, but her eyes were hills and valleys.

"And who are you?" Ross asked.

"Someone who thinks maybe we deserve it," Lila said. "Don’t quote me. I’ll deny it. Just—" she blew air up to lift a strand of hair that wasn’t there. "We cut and we fix and we cut and we fix. Sometimes we cut more than we fix. Maybe somebody got tired of us acting like our hands are holy."

"I’ve never met a doctor who believed their hands were holy," Ross said. "Busy, sure. Arrogant, sure. Holy? That’s a different department."

Lila’s smile was not a smile. "The rumor is she has a helper."

"She?"

"The giant," Lila whispered, as if the word would come if she spoke it too loudly. "Someone who tells her where to go. Coordinates. Like she’s...being guided."

"Who told you that?"

"My cousin heard it in a chatter room the kids use. Someone said someone knows someone who—"

"That’s a lot of someones," Ross said, but the idea was already walking around her, trying on her furniture. Not alone. Someone who made lists. A second villain, if you needed to say it plain. The idea sat down and crossed its legs.

By late afternoon, the jar had been boxed and carried like a relic. The soles of the prints had been photographed with a measuring tape like a narrow snake beside them. Ross went home and sat on the floor because chairs were liars. She opened the sliding door, braced her feet against the warm rail, and told the plants she was going to water them tomorrow for real this time.

"Two," she said to the room.

Her apartment hummed back, the old hum of refrigeration and the new high singing of neighbors’ televisions. She could hear the news through the wall. Freak weather in the east. A video of a cat riding a vacuum like a valkyrie. Nobody was talking about a woman taller than the stop signs. Sometimes the news didn’t know where to look.

Her daughter smiled from a photograph that had no right to be so confident about its own truth. Ross got up, because sitting with the picture too long made something inside her shiver like a sick dog. She pulled an old storage bin from under her bed. "Stop it," she told herself, good humored. "Not tonight." She opened it anyway. Old case files, long since boxed when she retired. Newspaper clippings. The story about the podiatrist conference that had been interrupted by a fire alarm and panic. Why did feet keep coming up? A clipping about a missing resident named Eli Mae Turner who had left her shoes in a locker room. The memory of a young officer who’d floated so many weird ideas you could have called him helium. File notes in her own handwriting blinking up at her like a blackout trying to remember itself.

She slept—a little—as the sky bruised and then calmed. The desert does its best work at dusk; no one had told it that the middle of the night was supposed to be the strangest.

Near midnight, Tina stood under the Indian School Road bridge and waited for the voice to tell her what to do with her hands. They big as Thanksgiving platters. They tender as linen. The canal was almost asleep, ducks tucked beak into wing like commas. On the bridge, bicycles hissed past like good ideas you’ll never catch again.

"It’s a hospital on the far side of town," the voice told her. "The private one, with glass like you could drink it. There’s a surgeon there who took too much. You know the drill. Be clean. Be quick."

"I am not a drill," she said. She touched the scarf at her wrist to quiet the remark. Her fingers trembled, not from fear, but from love’s burn—she could feel it, a flush in her skin, the way she sometimes felt right before she lifted something heavy, a sign that her heart was bigger than bodies knew. She wondered if the voice understood or just liked the results.

"We," the voice said, patient. "Are almost done."

"Almost?" she asked. "That’s what you said before. ‘Almost done’ is one of those phrases that makes children or dogs hop in the car."

Silence. A silence that had a face if you stared at it long enough.

She went anyway.

The night emptied the streets for her. She moved through alleys where kitchens gave up their heat, past apartment blocks where someone was practicing a flute. As she passed, porch lights clicked off in a scolded line. When she got to the private hospital with its glass like poured water, she put a hand on the wall for balance. Somewhere a security guard turned his head toward a noise he did not identify. Tina thought of the jar she had left, how it would sit in its box, misunderstood. She thought of her mother’s hands kneading dough, pressing their fingerprints into the day’s bread, how the dough could keep the shape of a hand even after it had been baked till gold. She moved.

"What are you doing?"

The voice in her ear returned not just louder but closer, and she froze. The tone had moved from coaxing to caution. There was another tone, too, layered beneath it, like the way certain songs carry a harmony only dogs hear. A second voice, lower, more metallic. It made the skin between her shoulder blades wake up.

"Who is that?" she asked.

"Our friend," the first voice said, too quickly. "We have help, remember? Listen. No time to dither. He’s crossing the east atrium now. Scrubs still on. Follow the pattern. Take only what’s needed."

She obeyed because love is sometimes an obedient animal. On the east side of the hospital, a man in green walked briskly toward his car, and when he dropped his key fob the sound was a ping that would ring in Ross’s ears later like a small bell that announces large arrivals. Tina took two steps, which was six of his. He did not see her until she was shadow and then she was story and then she was a door he could not push. She reached down and lifted not him but his fear. Her hand was careful. She did what she had been taught to do, the motions clean, ritual as a dance. There was no gore. She carried the tokens in her mouth the way cats did with kittens they were moving, gentle, and then spit them gently into her palm like pearls. She put them in the jar she had carried this time, a different jar because you didn’t mix love like that.

The man she had stopped slid down a car until he sat on his own feet and hugged himself like he had been told to practice self-soothing. He did not cry. That would come later. "Why?" he asked the air. "Why me?"

Tina stood over him like a building that was fond of the people inside it. "You took more than you needed," she said. She said it as a line someone else had written. "You took because you could. I’m just putting the little parts on a high shelf so they cannot be taken again."

He looked up. He saw her eyes. For a second, he calmed. Then the alarm went off somewhere else and he remembered how stories end.

Tina took two steps back. The ground accepted her like a trampolinist it trusted. Careful. Always careful. She looked for a place to leave the jar and found a planter where a palm had given up. She set it in the moonlight and stroked the lid with the back of her finger, because tenderness is not a finite resource.

She felt watched when she turned around, and not because of the cameras. The feeling was behind her teeth. She touched the transmitter. "Friend?" she asked. "Are you watching?"

"Always," the first voice said. "Always with you." And then the second voice came through, not words so much as a scrape, and she shivered like someone poured ice water into her honey.

The next morning, Ross stood with Denny in a parking structure that smelled like old oil and new fear. The doctor from the night before sat in a chair a paramedic had produced from nowhere, blanket around his shoulders as if he’d been dragged from a shipwreck. He would not put his shoes on. He stared at them like they were enemies. His name was Lavin or Levin depending. His hands shook and not because he was cold.

"It happened to him," Denny said. "What happened to the podiatrist and the ENT and the nurse in Tempe. Someone’s collecting."

"Yes," Ross said, because sometimes repeating the obvious makes people think you’ve done something. "He said anything helpful?"

"He said she was enormous, and careful, and her eyes were sad and then not-sad. He said he did not scream because his breath forgot how."

Ross nodded. "What was taken?"

"Don’t make me say it," he said, like Dr. Bhatt said the day before. The paramedic lifted an eyebrow at Ross as if to ask whether she didn’t already know. She did. Ten little moons that would fit in a jar like coins in a church box. Not gore. Ritual. Terrible without theatrics.

"And the rumour mill?" Ross asked Denny. "Any fresh water?"

"We’re running all the cameras. We’ve got somebody combing for patterns on the hospital schedules. We’re also monitoring the online chatter—"

"Chatter," she said softly, thinking of the nurse Lila’s cousin’s cousin. "Find out where it starts."

"It starts everywhere," Denny said. "It’s a weed, not a tree."

"Hm," Ross said. She leaned over the parking structure’s low wall and let the heat rise to her cheeks. The city looked like a floor someone had mopped and missed only the corners. "Two," she said to herself again. "Two villains. One who collects, one who directs." She tried saying it like a prayer to see if it would answer itself.

"You talking to me?" Denny asked.

"Always," she said. "Even when I’m not."

Later, she went to see Lila Montaño alone. The nurse had texted; Ross was not entirely sure how the woman had gotten her number. Nurses have their ways. They met in a coffee shop that had named all its drinks after neighborhoods.

"You shouldn’t have asked to see me at work," Lila said without preamble, as if Ross had committed a small sin on her way in.

"I didn’t," Ross said. "You texted. Then suggested coffee. Then told me your break was at three." She looked around. The boy at the counter had a moustache that could have belonged to a previous century. "Make it quick, then."

Lila stirred something that didn’t need stirring. "I think I saw the other one."

"The other?"

"The helper. At least someone who seemed to be waiting. The night it happened here, the first jar. There was a car with its engine on near the alley behind the cardiology offices. Too nice a car for that neighborhood. The windows were dark. No one got out, but the interior light came on for a second, and I could make out something. A hat. A little brim. And a hand. Pale. A ring with a rectangular stone. Not a doctor. They don’t wear jewelry you can catch on things. But there’s something about...a way of holding a phone like it’s both a bomb and a baby."

"Do you remember the car?" Ross asked. "Make, model."

"No. I know blood types, not cars. It purred more than it growled. That helps no one."

"It helps me," Ross said, and meant it. She lived on crumbs; sometimes crumbs make a map.

"I told a cop who was taking statements," Lila said, looking away. "He smiled like he was collecting bottle caps. I knew he wasn’t keeping them for good."

"You told me now," Ross said. "I keep things until they’re done with me."

Lila looked at her, long, then shorter. "Why you?" she asked. "You’re retired. You have the look of someone who’d rather be home with a jigsaw of a lighthouse."

"I hate lighthouses," Ross said. "They’re too proud. I’m here because no one else wants to say they believe in a giant and a secretary in a hat."

"A secretary?" Lila repeated.

"Or a counsel," Ross said. "Or a friend. There are two, I think. One who steps and one who plans. One hard to miss, one designed to be missed."

"That sounds right," Lila said. "Even if it sounds like fiction."

"Fiction pays attention," Ross said. "It’s facts that scuttle away when you look."

When Ross returned to her apartment, she found an envelope tucked under her door. No return address, but the paper had the powdery feel of something expensive. On the inside, a note, typed, old-fashioned:

If you want her to stop, you’ll have to ask nicely.
Some of us only speak love.

There were fingerprints on the paper. Someone had handled it after the typing, and not too carefully. One print smudged as if someone had tried to wipe away their own touch and only made a cloud from it. She looked closer. Ink or oil? She couldn’t tell. She smelled it, because smell is a shortcut to things we’re not allowed to know. A whiff of something like antiseptic and something like vanilla.

She sat down on the floor again, because gravity is stubborn. She held the letter on her knee and thought of the jar like a small planet with moons that had been rehomed. She thought of Tina, though she did not know her name. She thought of the helper in the car, the ring with the rectangular stone, the voice that could split itself and layer over itself like a bad duet. She said, quiet, so the letter would not be startled: "I can be nice."

The sun soaked the last of the day. Maybe tomorrow she would follow the canal paths where giants leave the smallest wakes. Maybe she would sit at hospitals like a gargoyle and wait. Maybe she would go to where women sew quilts into blankets that can disguise a shape the size of a house. She would not sleep much. She would boil an egg and forget to eat it. She would call Denny and not call him and then call anyway. Her daughter would smile in her photo and ask nothing of her in a voice Ross pretended to hear.

Under Indian School Road, Tina waited for the second voice to come and tell her what mercy looked like tonight. The ducks had finished their commas and become periods. The moon, a clipping of itself, hung on a loose thread. Somewhere, in a car with windows that wanted to be a mirror, a hand adjusted a brim and a mouth said, "Almost done. Almost almost," like a spell it had forgotten the ending to. And Tina, fueled by a love that made her taller than she intended, squared her shoulders like architecture and wondered if being careful counted as being kind.The first time she heard the second voice sing without the first one tied to it, Tina was perched on the lip of the old feed store’s roof, knees tucked to her chest like pried-up boulders, watching a cat watch a moth and trying to decide which of them was better at pretending not to care. The city was in that hour when lights are either bravely on or bravely off, no middle. She’d learned to trust that hour. Fewer errands in it. Fewer men with leaf blowers soldiering their little hurricanes around. She had one earbud turned with the soft of her thumb the way you turn a coin at a magician’s table, making it flash, making it disappear.

“Almost,” the second voice rasped when it came, a sandpaper scrape that had politely wrapped itself in cotton to seem less itself. “You did very well. The jar is a poem. I showed it to someone who needed to know what mercy was spelled like.”

“I don’t spell,” she said, a smile trying and failing to be a frown. “I hum.”

The first voice arrived a beat later, warm as bread. “Don’t let him make you shy, sweetheart. He thinks in blueprints. You and I are chalk on sidewalks.”

“I’m taller than sidewalks,” she said, and swung her feet a little, the roof’s corrugation answering with a note that would be a chord if someone braver joined it. In parking lots, her heels could startle birds. On roofs, her feet were fair-weather clouds.

She told herself the second voice was a friend of the first, like when a neighbor brings another neighbor to your porch with a pan of brownies and you talk to both of them even though the second one keeps looking past you into your living room, counting the things you own. She told herself a lot of things. Sometimes they all wanted to be true.

Across the city in the square room where she kept everything even after the world asked her to practice letting go, Ross was trying to ask the internet for directions without admitting she needed them. She had discovered the chat rooms a cousin-once-removed had called servers as if one could order fries there, and had clicked on one the nurses whispered about. Teens and interns called it a hub. Her laptop wheezed like a patient up a flight of stairs when she opened an app whose icon looked like a swollen cartoon ghost. It was all so much cuter than the old bulletin boards had been, those places where men had argued about train schedules and conspiracy while pretending to be alone.

After a few false entrances under a name that sounded too much like a tax attorney, she typed SANTAROSSA73 because sometimes you surrender and make the password part obvious on purpose. The hub was a vertical road of nicknames in a window that twitched like a porch screen. People used animal names, and punctuation like jewelry. A thread called GIANTORNOT hummed. Another, SIGHTINGS, had three maps and one heated debate about whether clouds count as witnesses.

She scrolled with one finger hovering like a dowser after water. There was a clip of a parking lot, nothing but shadow and someone’s breathing, and below it, a string of hearts because people cry with whatever their hands can find. A user named wren-with-a-3 wrote that she had boosted the audio and found something interesting. Then a link. Ross clicked. Twice. She listened.

Under the hiss and the glitch, the voice she thought of as The First was there, sweet and careful, the way you talk to someone carrying a pie on a bicycle. And then, under that, if you turned your head inside your head: a lower sound. Not even voice at first. A hum like someone’s car idling. Then words, unfinished notes more than sentences, almost done, almost almost, and she hit replay because sleep had not taught her how to stop wanting more of the things that might save her.

She typed, heart doing the little trot it did when she was pretending to be braver than seventy-three allows. “wren, rookie question. How did you boost it?” She signed it R.

Within a minute, a reply appeared. “rookie answers only. hi R. u gotta pull the band below 300hz but notch the HVAC whine. lol my mom says stop saying notch in casual conversation. I can DM you if you want settings.”

Ross stared at the chat as if it might ask for ID. The kids were generous. It undid her. “Please DM,” she typed, and waited, and when a window popped up, it was like a tiny bird had perched in her palm and was trying to explain magnetism.

“Do you have the original file?” wren-with-a-3 asked. “Also, why do I suddenly feel like I’m helping the world’s coolest librarian?”

“Because I used to wear sensible shoes,” Ross wrote, and then, because she did not owe anyone the weight of the truth but sometimes had to hand somebody the small coins in her pocket, she added, “And because I think if we want her to stop we are going to have to ask nicely.”

“The giant?” the child wrote back.

“The one with the jar,” Ross said. “And the voices.”

“I like her,” Wren wrote. “My aunt lost a foot in a thing. Doctors saved her life, I know, don’t @ me. But she always says people look at her like the wrong thing is missing. Maybe the giant is saving the tiny things no one else thinks of. Maybe the tiny things deserve a parade.”

Ross rubbed her eyes and saw lightning in the black. “I like her too,” she wrote. “But I also like people keeping their parts.”

“If you want to say please,” Wren wrote, “you have to learn how to shout quietly.”

Never had a teenager given her better advice. She copied the audio settings into a notebook that still smelled like a hardware store tab and then drew, without realizing, a little rectangle with a hat brim. Hats had been on her mind since the nurse told her about the car like a sleeping cat. She added a ring. Square-cut. The doodle felt like a seed.

“Do the kids still say ‘stay safe’?” she typed, and Wren sent her a gif of a raccoon wearing a seatbelt. They have different prayers now, the children. Maybe that’s for the best.

By noon, Ross had driven to the clinic where the missing resident’s locker room had been. The building had new signage but the same old ghosts; when you hang fresh art on nail holes it doesn’t convince the wall. A woman with a keycard named Shelley let her in because Ross said please as if it were a badge. Inside, the smell had not adapted to new times. Everyone had a towel with a name. The metal benches still sounded like tin theology. Ross opened the locker Eli Mae Turner had used, though she had no right and Shelley had left her alone on purpose.

Inside were practical things. Sneakers sensible as Swiss men. A folded sweatshirt with rhinestones spelling out a city that thought itself sexy. A plastic zip bag with a twisted friendship bracelet like the one Dr. Bhatt had described at the giant’s wrist. Rainbowed. The sort of thing kids made at camp when camp was still a place you sent children to learn about mud and songs.

There was also a handout from a meeting stapled in the corner and dotted with coffee. Support Group for Unaccounted Grief. Thursdays, Unitarian Church, back room by the piano. Bring your own tissues; we have tea. The name made Ross’s hands go still. Unaccounted grief was the only kind she knew how to count.

On the way out, she asked Shelley whether Eli Mae had known anyone with a small hat and a large ring. Shelley laughed the brittle laugh of someone stepping on old ice. “This place has three thousand people,” she said. “It has at least twenty hats.”

“But the ring?” Ross asked, and when Shelley looked at her hand, silver band thin as an apology, and then shook her head, Ross knew she was coming up on the corner of a map that would require a legend somebody had lost.

She drove to the Unitarian church in a heat that made the white lines on the road look like snakes exhausted by their own alarms. Inside, it was cool and smelled faintly of lemon and binding glue. The back room by the piano had a mural too, angels with too many elbows like the place where Tina sometimes sat cross-legged and tried to be less than what she was. There were quilts folded on the chairs, because people who grieve prefer to have something to do with their hands. A woman with hair the color of snowfall after a long day looked up from a basket of thread and narrowed her eyes kindly.

“Are you here to cry or to spy?” she asked. “Be straight with me. Or crooked with conviction.”

“Both,” Ross said. “In that order.”

The woman made a sound that could have been a laugh if she hadn’t been so tired of laughing. “Mags,” she said, touching her chest. “I sew for babies and disasters. Sometimes those categories overlap.”

“Ross,” Ross said. “I used to be a cop. Now I’m a person who tells plants I will change.”

“We all used to be something eleven jobs ago,” Mags said. “You want tea?”

Ross said yes. The tea was amber and honest. The quilts looked like maps a sky might use if it got lost. “Do you have anyone,” Ross asked carefully, “who sits on the floor and makes rooms nervous? Someone who is taller than the word tall. Someone who knows about ears and feet and how to be careful.”

Mags put her teacup down and let it pitch its small porcelain version of thunder. “We have all kinds,” she said. “We are a wide-mouth jar. Sometimes a stranger comes who hums like she’s reminding the world it used to be kinder. She sits with her back against the baptismal font because she says it is heavy and she is tired of being the heaviest thing in every room.”

“Does she have a name?” Ross asked, keeping her voice at the register you use when reading to animals you’d like to keep near.

“We gave her one,” Mags said. “She came with one, but she keeps it wrapped in a scarf like you do when you’re saving the last piece of your mother’s china for a future you don’t trust. We call her Tina because you can stitch it in thread you find in most boxes.”

Tina. The name sounded exactly like itself. It landed softly in Ross’s chest and then refused to leave. She blew on her tea even though it had cooled. “Is she safe?” she asked, and hated, in the same breath, the way she had asked it as if she had rights.

Mags lifted a shoulder the way people do when the answer is yes and no and if we’re lucky and it’s a Wednesday. “She knows how to fold herself,” the woman said. “She can make a building think it is taller. But she has a friend who talks in her ear like a wind you forgot to shut the window against. And there is another who stands where the streetlight fails on purpose. He leaves tire on the road, a handsome smear. I do not like the way he looks at lids.”

“Lids?” Ross asked.

“Jars,” Mags said. “The way he turned one like he was unscrewing the world. We have a jar of buttons; he looked at it like it owed him something.”

“Do you know his ring?” Ross asked. “The shape of it.”

“Rectangle,” Mags said immediately. “A small window pretending to be a mountain. He thinks jewels are nouns. He wears a hat to make his face look like a verdict.”

Ross felt very briefly twenty and crashy again, wanting to run when all the running could get her was hot. “If she comes,” she asked, “if Tina comes, will you tell her I want to talk? Nicely. Tell her I liked her careful. Tell her I have no jars she needs to be afraid of.”

“I tell everyone who walks in here something,” Mags said. “Otherwise they forget why they have feet. You write her a note. Better yet, write her a song, but I suspect you have lost your singing voice the way the dry season loses its shame. Write, then. I’ll give it to the roses if she doesn’t come. They pass messages. They gossip better than girls.”

Ross took an index card with a faint arithmetic table printed on the back and thought about words for a very long time. She wrote, what she wrote, as if the card might break under the weight of an extra word. She wrote that she believed the world loses too much by accident and on purpose and that sometimes love is a thief that returns what it should not take. She wrote that if the voice told her she had to do something that ended with paper towels and bleach, she could say no. She wrote that she, Ross, would meet her where the ducks turn into commas under Indian School Road, and that she would come alone, and that she would bring nothing you could unscrew. She ended with a please that surprised her with how much it felt like a door.

Mags read it, nodded as if she were agreeing with how the sky had chosen to be blue that day, and tucked it in a hymnbook. “I know the song to hide it in,” she said. “It’s the one about ships that never arrive and still leave salt on your mouth.”

Ross left the church more accompanied than when she arrived. The sun did that thing where it made a halo of dust around each car’s tires. People say halos are white. They are wrong. They are everything sun touches twice. On her way home she bought peaches even though it was not perfect peach time. It is an act of faith to cut into a peach you know will disappoint and to eat it anyway with a knife balanced on your thumb like a tightrope walker who refuses to wear glitter.

The second voice had a habit of showing up when Tina’s hands were the fullest or mind the emptiest. In between, it let the first do the wooing. She was walking the narrow seam between two cinderblock fences, toes careful, arms out, the way you have to when you are your own horizon. The scarf around her wrist caught on a rough place and she seemed not to feel it. She was thinking about what it meant to be almost finished, whether almost done was a geography you could walk out of. Her mouth tasted of copper and cinnamon, the taste humming leaves when a storm is half a city away.

“Pet,” the first voice said. “You’ve been so good. One more tonight and then we can rest. He’s not a doctor, but he takes. He believes all the pain in the world owes him its wallet. We will help him understand his hands are not borders.”

“Am I helping?” Tina asked. She did not wait for an answer. The first voice always said yes. It would yes her even into a fire if that’s what the plan called for. “My mother taught me, you know. The crease of a palm, what it says. Love lines, life lines. People made a game of it. But I saw the little ways bodies apologize. Wrists told me how they slept. Nails told me if they ate for hunger or habit. They tell secrets, nails do. They curl when someone wants to stop touching anything.”

“You are keeping the secrets safe,” the voice said. “You are putting them where hands cannot reach.”

“Jars are not far,” she said. “Jars are simply near behind glass. It is different.”

“We are just,” the second voice groaned faintly, and the word was a bus driving past with a destination that had been dialed wrong. He coughed with a smile. “Do you want him on an empty staircase or by his car? He has a car with manners. It purrs. It reads as innocence.”

“Cars are sad,” she said, and jumped down into a yard where an orange tree was trying not to become a rumor. “They think they are better than walking.”

She said yes because she always had, until you learn a word you didn’t think you could pronounce without breaking something. She followed directions the way a sheet follows wind, not because the wind is right, but because it is the only one with hands.

The man that night did not wear scrubs. He wore a name like a corporation and a shirt the color of waiting rooms. He had a briefcase you could imagine him practicing opening with one hand. He walked like the ground owed him more ground. He was thinking, as Tina settled into his shadow, about a game on his phone and a woman named Terri he had not called. She did not like him. That was not the compass she used. She hummed because humming smoothed the corners of things. He heard it and it made him forget what his name felt like from the inside.

“Hello,” he said to the air, because people are polite until they aren’t.

She came into his wind. He saw her and then saw her again, and the second seeing was worse because it meant the first seeing had been true. He held up his hands like small flags. “What are you,” he said, because men think naming you tames you, and she did not answer that game. Her fingers were careful, as always. She lifted what the voice said to lift, the small defenses, the crescents he cut himself and flicked under desks, the ten quiet moons he did not know he had missed until he was lighter and suddenly angry. She put them in the jar. She turned the lid once and then, for the first time, she did not turn it the rest of the way.

“Enough,” she said, the word heavy in her mouth as a coin you’re sure is counterfeit. She stepped back and the world put itself in her footprint.

Behind her, down the block, in the dark cavity between two parked cars, glass caught light like a fish. A small brim turned. The second voice had a face in the real world and it smiled exactly with its teeth. He lingered in the corner of cameras and made his car’s engine a rumor. He wore the rectangular ring, three clean reflections running up and down its surface like elevator doors mimicking thought. He watched her with the devotion of a scientist whose experiment is about to produce the answer he paid to get. He did not like that she had failed to screw the lid all the way down. He almost moved and then didn’t. Control feels like silence when it’s new.

Later, perched again at the quilted church where angels were elbows and elbows were wings, Tina found Mags and asked for water and something that would teach her to swallow the sound of the second voice. Mags filled a plastic pitcher that used to live at a picnic, handed it to her with both hands the way you hand over a baby, and closed the door to keep the rest of the world from seeing what friendship looks like when it is too large. “The roses told me,” Mags said, because of course they did. “You have a suitor. The kind who writes notes and means them. Also the kind I don’t like, who drives around looking for stories to be the center of.”

“I’m not a center,” Tina said. “I’m the edge of things.”

Mags reached up and touched the scarf like a wristband. Her fingers were small as commas. “You can say no,” the old woman said. “You can lift the word no like you lift a car, sweetheart. You’ve been lifting the wrong things.”

Tina flinched, not because of the rebuke but because the word sweetheart and the word no in the same sentence made her feel the whole length of herself, a sail suddenly aware of the ocean. “There was a note?” she asked, and Mags slid the index card out of the hymnbook.

“It smells like peach and stubbornness,” Mags said. “Two of my favorite colognes.” She watched Tina read because there are few sacred rituals left and watching someone hear mercy is one of them.

The card held its breath while Tina read. The handwriting was everything she liked: steady, unpretty, trying. It did not reach for metaphors. It hauled words like buckets. It asked her to meet with a please. The please made her knees warmer. She folded the card without folding it where the fold would break the words, and tucked it inside the scarf at her wrist where she kept the little things she could not keep in jars. “She is kind,” she said. “She thinks jars are magic. They are not. They are just glass.”

“She thinks jars are excuses,” Mags corrected softly. “She thinks your careful is important. She thinks you can be kind without being obedient. Will you meet her? Don’t tell me. Tell the place you’re going to meet. Places like to be included.”

Under Indian School Road, Ross stood with a thermos and the kind of patience you get only after the desert has refused you water three summers in a row. Ducks reassembled into commas and then into a sentence that looked like a flock going home. The canal put on its best impression of a thought—deep, quiet, moving enough to matter. She had left a pair of old sneakers on the bank, not because she planned to offer her feet to any ritual but because somewhere in the grim humor of the afternoon it had occurred to her that when someone takes what they believe has been taken, maybe you begin by taking off your shoes.

It was late enough that the police scanners in other people’s apartments were static lullabies. She had told no one except the plant whose leaves she had finally watered with abject apologies. The moon had not committed to being full or thin. It had chosen, wisely, to be undecided.

She heard the humming first. The sound came like a memory that had been aired out and was almost but not entirely clean. She closed her eyes because sometimes you see better that way. When she opened them, the world was closer. A shadow had placed its feet at the edge of the canal the way a god might if gods had the sense to be careful. She looked up because there is no not looking up when a roof decides to be sky.

“Hello,” Ross said, and put the thermos down so it wouldn’t be mistaken for a weapon. “Thank you for the note you didn’t know you sent me when you left that jar where someone would find it.”

The voice that answered had been trying to be brave for a very long time. It sounded like a mother who had forgiven someone she did not plan to forgive again. “Hello,” Tina said. “I have been told to be done. I have been told that love is the kind of math where you give the teacher back their chalk. I thought you would be shorter.”

“I used to be taller,” Ross said, because humor is a hook you throw out in a flood. “It’s an age thing. Sit, if you want to. Or don’t. Nobody makes chairs for either of us anymore.”

Tina lowered herself the way a building refuses to collapse. The canal flexed, delighted by the new shoreline. The ducks rearranged their punctuation. “Are you going to ask me to stop?” she asked.

“I am going to ask you if you’d like to,” Ross said. “And then I am going to ask whether you meant to start.”

Tina’s laughter startled a bicycle on the bridge into ringing its bell like a spell. “I meant to keep things safe,” she said. “No one else was guarding the small moons. Everyone was busy with blood and stories. I thought, if I keep the little parts, maybe the big hurt won’t find them.”

Ross nodded as if this were the first reasonable thing she had heard all day and maybe it was. “Who told you about the taking?” she asked. “Whose mouth burrowed into your ear and nested there.”

“Two mouths,” Tina said. “One says yes like sugar. One says yes like a boot. They know about schedules and parking lots and which hallways are the loneliest. They say doctors take. They say we are giving back justice. I think maybe they don’t know what ‘we’ means.”

“They’re not we,” Ross said. “They’re an I that wants to feel like company.”

“You sound like a teacher,” Tina said. “Or a girl at a peaches stand telling me this one isn’t ready, but if you put it in a paper bag it might be sweet by Thursday.”

“I am just someone who lost someone. And then kept losing. Turns out you can lose the same thing many times.” She lifted her thermos. “Coffee?”

Tina shook her head, hair making night even darker. “It would taste like a joke in my mouth.”

“Fair. Mine tastes like my ex-wife’s compromise.” Ross took a sip because she could. “Do you know a man with a small hat and a rectangular ring who likes to watch lids?”

Tina closed her eyes and the bridge’s bolts shivered. “He stands where light fails,” she said. “He watches with his hand. He likes to say ‘we are almost done.’ He says almost in threes like a nursery rhyme. He smells like lotion and something that used to clean better before it got sued.”

“It’s antiseptic,” Ross said. “And vanilla. He wrote me a note that tried to be charming and forgot to get permission from its better self.” She reached into her pocket, slowly, because everything was slower now, slowly was the only way to keep time from spilling. “He said, ‘If you want her to stop, ask nicely. Some of us only speak love.’ I am asking nicely. Would you like to stop for a little while and see if the voices become less voracious when you are quiet.”

“I was not noisy,” Tina said, and then laid her two hands on her knees like two plates that had fed a family and were stacked stubbornly together. “I am so careful. I could thread a needle with a spider web.”

“I know,” Ross said, and for a long time they listened to the ducks turn punctuation into swimming. The city smoothed its back and pretended to be asleep. After a while, when a while had been long enough to count as a choice, Tina nodded.

“Alright,” she said. “I will try. For the roses. For the woman who wrote as if she were taking off her shoes in a new house. For love that does not lick blood off its own teeth. But if someone takes,” she said, the word not imitating the voice but auditioning for its own part, “I will know.”

“Yes,” Ross said. “Knowing is our curse and our skill. In the meantime, might you tell me how to find the man with the ring. I have questions for him that will not fit in a jar.”

Tina looked toward the street where cars made light into instructions. “He said a name once, by mistake,” she murmured. “The way someone says their own name if they have been pretending to be the sky too long and forget the difference. He said ‘Vince,’” she said, and the sound of the name made a sprinkler come on down the block like it had been waiting to applaud. “Then he said ‘No, no, not here,’ as if the night could overhear. His car purrs like a cat that doesn’t trust you yet. The hood is low like the hat he wears. The seats smell like someone doesn’t let children in them.”

Ross filed the information under corners she would turn. Vince, a ring, a brim, a car that purred. She would give it to Denny as a present he would wrap in too much tape. She would hand it to Wren and the children would build a diagram that looked like a spiderweb with a nap. She would go home and circle the name in three colors and whisper it to the picture on her shelf. She would ask it nicely with her fist if asking nicely didn’t work.

When she looked back to say the thank-yous she would never admit she had rehearsed in the car, Tina was watching her with a look that did not belong to giants or to stories. It was the look you give someone who has just added a room to the house that exists behind your ribs. “Be careful,” Tina said. “You are not large. You are only brave.”

“Brave is simply tired with a better outfit,” Ross said. “And I will not buy any more clothes. I promise nothing except I will keep my hands open.”

“Open hands,” Tina said. “Jars with no lids. I have to go. If I am quiet too long, he notices the wrong thing.”

“Let him notice,” Ross said, the way a woman talks to a dog that has never learned the difference between leash and love. “Let him notice and let him misunderstand.”

After Tina left, the space she had occupied took a while to forget her. The canal smoothed its dress with both palms and pretended nothing had happened. Ross put her shoes back on, pouring a little gravel out of one as if hope were always sand that insists on coming home with you. She walked to her car with her thermos empty and full and did not turn on the radio because the night was already speaking and it felt rude to interrupt.

The man who called himself Vince when he was careless sat three miles away in a car that cost more than Mags’s mortgage and listened to the recording of a woman giant saying the word enough. He ran a finger along his ring and thought about optics. People like him always think about optics when it is the organ of conscience they are meant to be consulting. Next to him, on the passenger seat, a typed envelope waited for ink. He liked envelopes. They were little doors he got to lock from both sides.

He took off his hat because the interior light was unkind, and in the pale circle of it, his face was the kind of handsome that was good for about five blocks from his apartment. He dialed a number without looking. “She’s slipping,” he said into the air. “We have to make a performance of it. They like performance. They forgive if they can name the show.”

On the line, a voice patient and practiced as a nurse. “Do not touch her,” the first voice warned, softer than any blade. “I will handle it. You and your ring can stare at lids. That is what you are good at.”

Vince’s smile went sideways and then obedient. He fogged the window with his breath and drew a careful crescent with one finger. It was an old habit, the way boys practice signatures when they are sure the world is waiting to autograph itself. Somewhere in that same city, in another room with a piano that had forgotten its own songs, a jar of buttons sat with its lid just shy of tight. Some things are better for the grace of almost.

In the morning, Ross wrote a list of names on the back of a bill that had been dressed like a pamphlet. Vince. Lila. Eli Mae. Wren. Mags. Bhatt. Levin. She wrote Tina and she did not circle it. She drew a small square and set a hat on top of it and then, because she is who she is, she called Denny and said, “I’ve got a lead. Don’t make me use words like suspect. It’s too early.”

“You are a rumor I wanted to believe,” Denny said. “Tell me everything you’re not supposed to.”

She told him some. She kept the important piece—the place under the bridge where the ducks punctuate the water, the place where she had remembered how to say please—in the part of her throat that does not talk for anyone but the person who taught you to tie your shoes.

By lunch, a nurse in Glendale had found a jar near a staff entrance that smelled like grapefruit rind. By lunch, Wren had uploaded audio of a man rehearsing the word almost like a magic trick he had not earned. By lunch, someone had made a joke that needed to sit in the shade and think about itself. By lunch, Tina had found a quiet building to hide in that used to be a carpet store and had been dreaming of footsteps since. She lay down on a roll of padding and stared at a ceiling where water stains had made galaxies no one had named. She held the index card in her hand and thought about jars with no lids and about love that did not require arrangements to prove itself. The city pressed its back against the day and counted to ten. The Glendale nurse entrance smelled like peeled citrus and the inside of a new refrigerator. The jar had been collected already and it left a ring on the planter that felt like a bruise on the concrete. A groundskeeper with a moustache of undetermined era pointed at the cameras with the air of someone who had been ignored by his children and would not forgive the world for repeating their mistake. The security office was a narrow room with a television wall blinking through better stories than the ones they were living. She watched the feed and found the streak of motion that earned its name. You didn’t see a woman with the shape of a myth; you saw a shadow deciding where to fall. On the edge of the frame, a car glided and paused with predator patience, and when the dome light flicked for a blink she saw a hand and the flash of a ring that wore itself like a knuckle. She pressed her fingers to the monitor as if static could be a confession.

Later, over a bowl of soup that could not decide whether it wanted to be broth or apology, Ross listened as Wren talked her through something deeper than her laptop ought to be allowed. The child’s voice had sleep at the edges and enthusiasm in the middle, like a dog in a window. “We traced the guy’s online footprint,” Wren said, which made her laugh because the words did their own little choreography. “Not the giant. He was in a mod chat last night. He used a handle that says Vince without saying Vince. He typed, ‘We need an event. A reveal. “They feed the city what they want to see eaten,” Ross said. “And then call it natural. Can you send me what you’ve got? Slowly, as if my computer were a saltine.”

“I’ll deliver it like a gentle pizza,” Wren said. “Also, Miss Ross?”

“Don’t Miss me,” Ross said, automatically and kindly.

“Okay, Ross,” Wren said, tasting the name and keeping it. “You were right about the first voice. I think she’s local. The way she says street names makes me think she learned to drive here. She says ‘the 51’ the way only people here do.”

Tina spent the morning hiding in a place that did not know it was being kind to her. The old carpet store had rolls of foam that gave under her back without complaint, the way a friend gives under your need. She read the card again and pretended she didn’t read it twice. The please sat there like a coin that the sun had warmed and someone had left on the windowsill. She held the earbud between two fingers as if it were a beetle with delicate legs, and watched the way it tried to catch the light as a trick for forgiveness. She wanted to put it in a jar, to keep that small mouth from the bigger mouth, to undo the way voices could find each other even when she didn’t want them to.

The first voice found her anyway. It always did. It had a habit of arriving mid-sentence so that you couldn’t claim it had started anything. “I’m glad you’re resting,” it said, and the relief that leaked out of it made Tina forgive its trespasses for one more hour. “The night was too busy. My fault. I was greedy. That won’t happen again. Sit quiet today. Let your hands remember what their own weight is. You deserve to be heavy on your own terms.”

“I met someone,” Tina said. “Her voice kept the birds in the air. She asked me to stop as if I might enjoy stopping. She had a thermos like a small rocket. She called me careful and you know I like that word.”

“I know,” the first voice said, and it sounded tired for the first time, as if kindness had been a job she wasn’t always hired for. “Be careful of her. She will be loved for being brave. They make saints out of women who are just in the habit of not sleeping. She will try to take you from me. She doesn’t know about our math. She thinks love is an unkept ledger. She is wrong.”

“What if I want to be wrong,” Tina murmured. The earbud made a small apologetic squeak under her fingers. “What if I want to be wrong enough to sleep like a person who doesn’t flinch at their own dream.”

The second voice scraped on cue as if he had been listening with his mouth open. “Almost time,” he said. “Festival this weekend. Surgery convention at the resort with the blue umbrellas. Easy. Cameras all over. That’s what we want. We’ll place the jar for them. We’ll control what they control. You like crowds. They make you visible and invisible at once. It’s perfect.”

“I do not like crowds,” Tina said. “They make me a mirror and I am tired of showing people their own fear.” She rolled off the foam and stood in the middle of the room to measure her own edges. She was a horizon they kept trying to fold.

“You won’t have to go inside,” the first voice soothed. “I will go. You rest. We can let him do the part he’s good at—optics, oh his beloved optics. And you can give me what I ask for when I ask for it. We’re almost done.”

“Stop saying almost,” Tina said, and the anger surprised even her. It was not a blaze. It was a clean sound, like a glass being set down with authority. “Almost is a word you use when you don’t want the truth to feel alone.” She looked at the jar Mags had given her to put buttons in and found it already half-full of small circles that had the nerve to be cheerful. That afternoon, the three doors belonged to a surgeon with a trembling on/off switch, a nurse who held everything too far from her body to avoid attachment, and a man in orthopedic sales who had never thought of his hands as anything other than utensils. Tina did not go up to the doors. She stood across the street and leaned against a lamppost and looked like a building that had decided to spend the day outside. Eli sat on the curb once and let her body be something that takes a break while you ask it to.

She was, however, done with almost, which meant she could stand in exactly one spot and feel the world come to her without mistaking it for permission.

The lawsuit that a clever man promised had become a stack of paper with metaphors that scowled at one another. Vince settled in a way that left him poorer and unfamiliar with his own calendar, and the hat lived on a shelf that did not permit it the dignity of being looked at. He found himself listening to a podcast he would have mocked three months ago, a gentle thing about compost and patience, and he did not understand how a human being could say the word soil without feeling like a caricature, and then he heard the way the guest laughed over a joke that didn’t try to be good, and he closed his eyes and realized he had never been tired in a way that mattered before. They never got all the crescents back and maybe that was correct. Bodies keep mysteries in places we can’t call rooms. But some mornings you could swear the walkways at the hospital had more feet on them than yesterday, and that was enough to keep Mags threading needles she couldn’t see well, to keep Lila bending forms until they fit in a pocket instead of a file, to keep Eli learning the difference between precision and cruelty, to keep Wren reminding the world that shouting quietly is a sustainable practice. The morning after the big rains, the streets kept the sky’s fingerprints like bruises that didn’t mind being touched, and the city smelled briefly of green things remembering their names. Ross drove slowly on purpose, watching the palm fronds gossip with one another and the low clouds act like quilts that weren’t ready to be folded yet. At the canal, the ducks made a sentence so long it required a breath in the middle, and she took that as a sign that the day could be generous if you remembered to inhale when given the chance.

Denny had called at dawn to say that the warrant had turned into paper that meant something, and Vince had turned into a man who used words like compliance without being paid per syllable. He stood in a room with a microphone that was not a friend and said things that wanted to be wisdom and ended up as weather reports again, and in the space where almost usually snuck out of his mouth, there was a pause that embarrassed him enough to glance at his hat on a chair. Wren had clipped the audio for a class at the university and titled it The Geometry of Almost, which made the professor laugh into their sleeve as if the sound might escape and ruin the study of ethics permanently. When Ross parked at the church with the jar of buttons clacking in her trunk, she felt the unusual and comforting sensation of being carried by other people’s competence, and she considered writing a thank-you note to the day and leaving it under a rock for the ants to pass along.

Inside, the back room had taken on the smell of old books and a very particular kind of lemon, the kind that doesn’t care what your grandmother called it. Mags was sewing insultingly small stitches into a border that looked like river water deciding how to behave, and Lila was reading a consent sentence aloud in three different tones to see which one made her own hands stop wanting to ball into fists. Eli sat with her elbows on her knees in a position that would have gotten her scolded in childhood and saved her from three bad decisions as an adult. “We’ve had three more doors,” Lila said, without ceremony, because kindness can become a schedule if you let it. “Two took notes like gifts. “The medical board wants me Thursday,” Eli said, and the way she said Thursday made the day itself rearrange its furniture. “There’s a woman on it who wears the kind of shoes you keep for weddings, and she will look sad on purpose and ask me to narrate the line between suggestion and instruction as if it were a curb nobody tripped over. The name punched through three different conversations at once because it had been a thread running under someone else’s day. At Mercy Vista, they had made an accidental tradition of opening the blinds in the rehab ward when the first person took the first step. The light was never as flattering as magazines think, but it was honest without being cruel, and it showed up regardless of who believed in it. “You don’t have to say anything to me,” Terri began, which is the midpoint of courage, not the beginning. “I had a week of people saying a lot and then saying sorry for saying it. “I have no city law to offer you,” Tina said, softly enough that the parallel bars could keep their own count. “I can stand where you ask. I can carry when you like. I can not carry if that is the secret you are trying to say.”

“We don’t lie in the ward,” Lila said, stepping into her proper authority without apology. “We tell prettier truths. Your feet are the size of women who have had enough. That’s an odd shoe, but we stock it.”

The first step was the kind of dull victory that would never trend because it required too many witnesses and too little drama. Terri leaned into the bar and remembered a weight that belonged to her, not the hospital, not the man whose name she’d chosen in a year that smelled like wedding cake, not the giant who had become a rumor she’d been forced to tell her therapist about. At the board hearing, the chairs had arms that bit into your elbows if you slouched, and the men took notes in pens that believed themselves to be better instruments than mouths. They asked about consent the way people ask about the ocean when they’ve only seen it on postcards, and Eli answered with small water: what it is to ask a man twice because his yes was for show, what it is to listen to a woman answer with her shoulders instead of her mouth, what it is to say we are going to count to five and not to keep counting when fear makes you greedy for control.

Afterward, Ross and Mags sat on a bench outside the double doors that were both a welcome and a warning. “You’ve learned to enjoy door jambs,” Mags said. “You always looked like one. It’s nice to see you admitting it. The trick will be not letting men with hats convince you they installed the hinges.”

Wren had set up what they called a “quiet shout,” and it threaded through the city in the way only young people with passwords can manage without being insufferable. Vince’s apartment learned what it is to be left out of a room. He sat on the edge of a couch that had been curated to within an inch of its life and read the emails he had written late at night when your fingers are cowardly and your mouth can still pretend it was someone else. The mod chat archive was making its way through circles he had used to hire attention, and twice a man called to say I didn’t know it had gotten that ugly, which was a lie with good manners. He found himself scrolling to the picture of the ring’s imprint in its velvet slot and had the sudden and humble thought that he had been trying to make light stand still for so long he had forgotten the basic mercy of a dimmer. He opened a window and let the heat in on purpose as a sort of penance, and when a neighbor’s radio pushed a country song about dirt through the screen, he did not sneer. He put his hat in a paper bag because he could not yet bear to throw it away, and he did not label the bag, which was the closest he could come to acknowledging the world didn’t need him to remember everything he owned.

At the church, a new jar appeared with no note, and it took a minute for the room to realize what it had been asked to hold. Inside were slips of paper with three words written large in different familiar hands: I kept something. People reached in with the hesitance you use when petting a cat you have not yet been introduced to, and the slips told tiny truths without asking to be thought profound. I kept my breath. The night after the board hearing, the power flickered across a few old neighborhoods, and the sky in those parts did a brief, scandalous striptease, revealing more stars than people in the city consider polite. It took a while for the ducks to find their way back to ordinary punctuation after the storm, and their sentence wandered, easy with itself, along the canal in a better tempo. Eli started a small ritual in the rehab ward that no one put in a manual because manuals dislike superstition unless you describe it as protocol. One night, with nothing to prove and slowness to spend, the group gathered at the canal with paper boats that had names written on them in the thick, almost rude letters of kindergarten. Wren had insisted on the boats because they were tired of computers pretending to be rivers, and Mags had folded each one with a fuss that turned out to be prayer. Tina’s boat said almost because she was amused by ceremony but not above it, and Eli’s said please and no on opposite sides because she had finally accepted that opposites can be neighbors if you let them. When wind came up and made a brief mess of their flotilla, they laughed because the point had never been precision. Mags clapped once and then again, and the canal converted the sound into ripples with the competence of a translator who refuses to add adjectives. There was no moral that night and no catastrophe to take away. The city did not applaud, and no man with a hat appeared to ruin the ending with a speech, and no one wrote a headline because you can’t sell a thing that doesn’t break or marry. Ross drove home with her window down because the air had remembered how to be sympathetic without being dramatic, and in her kitchen she twisted the lid off the jar with the square of paper that said please and held it to her ear like a shell. It didn’t make a sound that you could record, but the apartment leaned in just enough that she heard what a room sounds like when everyone who had been insisting on being useful took a night off. She turned out her light and slept in the honest dark, and when morning came asking for tasks, she handed it an index card with only three names and one drawing of a hat, and she did not explain the sketch to anyone.
