Wokingham is not a town you can learn in a day. It looks easy enough—red brick, sensible pavements, the old corn stores now caramelised into flats, oak and ash with their elbows on footpaths. But walk it twice and hear how the kerbs hum and you start to notice the seam. There’s a narrow fold of air just under the unforgiving polish of tarmac, a thin channel between the visible and the lived-in. Most folks never feel it brush their ankles. One did, once, when he was six and licking a sherbet dip by the station, and he grew up with music listening through his bones. He moved away. These things happen.

I was born in that seam. I say born—sprouted, spun, whistled into tug. The oak in Joel Park gave up a curl of bark and there I was, damp with sap and making the sort of noise an ant-hill makes when it has ideas. I could look you in the eye if you knelt, and in the shoulder if you lay down. Not mouse-small; I am a sprite of the mid-scale, sized to sit on your shoulder and not dislodge. Neighbours call me Pip, because names should be short in case you shout them while a bus is reversing. People think we all have wings. We don’t. I have knees and a very competent set of hands.

A partcilar set of skills, if you want the butter polished. Threadwork. Knot-lore. The unjamming of hinges at distances you would think delusional to attempt. I don’t lift buildings, I reverse hours, very slightly, to nudge a missed glance back into place. It’s technical. And I listen. If you give me an evening and a broken thing, I will hear inside it what it is trying to be.

My house was a nest of keys and snail shells in the hollow of a horse-chestnut, the part of the park where the dew lingers. I had a wooden comb for training breezes and a pin that once belonged to a mayor, which I used for opening delicate potentialities. Mornings I worked the hedgerows along the path to The Crispin, tidying goblin-handles—from the outside they’re hawthorn thorns—and whispering the thuds out of over-tired rabbits. After that I put in hours at the railway where the signal cabins mutter like old men. There are days when Wokingham station cries, silently, in a way that hurts the enamel. Those are the days I take a braid of spider silk and tie the corners of the platform to a sensible sort of calm.

Yes, yes, yes—how silly. But if I didn’t, trains would run late and tempers would flare and somewhere between here and Winnersh a lad with a new job would sigh and drop a letter he should have posted, and a different boy in a different town would never learn to whistle the one tune that stops his father from slamming a door. Do you see? All this blunting and brightening works outward like tea. That is our job.

It was the spring of the year that the kestrel nested on the Tesco sign, which is one of the funnier little rebellions I’ve witnessed. The human town woke up and put on dapper clothes and turned out on a weekend to look at tulips that had not asked for inspection. I threaded dew into the threads of their sleeves, so they would remember to be soft with one another. It was very effective. Then I pulled my collar up against a breath that didn’t belong.

Plastic smells like crushed weather. It tastes like a whisper that won’t make sense. The park’s edges had been catching it all winter—white bellies snagged on hawthorn, handles strangling the loss of wind—but now that breath was thickened, layered. A waft like stale reality. I stood up in the chestnut, and my shins prickled.

That was the first day I heard the rustle laugh.

You know the sound that happens when you push your fingers into a pocket full of crisp packets? That dry approval? Add a thread of spite and pull it taut. That.

“Beth,” I told the magpie when she arrived to inventory my bright scatter. “You are late and that noise is back.”

Beth, whose beak was a polished opinion, hopped and stood on the mayors-pin. “Hush. If I am late, I am fashionable. Pippers, you hoard too many keys, there’s no telling which is special if everything is. And don’t say noise, say nemesis. It gives you spine.”

“It does not. Besides, you’re only saying nemesis so I will be exciting for your mates.”

“True,” she said, elegantly swiping a biscuit crumb. “But it’s a shape. I have seen him. Rustler. Bag-brother. Plastic-eater. Whatever he goes by. Down by the Emm where the overhanging elder lets its hair in the water—he was there, last night. Laced up in layers and laughing with his shoulders. I thought it was you, from a distance.”

“Beth.”

“Well, you do have shoulders. Don’t be touchy.” She was young, she liked irritation as a seasoning.

We all had a word for him we’d not said aloud. Sprites are not a homogenous workforce; we’re a federation of micro-trades and stubbornness. But if you are good at what I am good at, and you’ve stood by the river Loddon and listened to it try to get a word in against the roar of fields full of binbag, you know what the rustle laugh belongs to. There is a who when there should be a what.

You will call him litterbug. You will say irresponsible. Oh dear, dear. You will inventory his offences on a clipboard and watch as the wind writes an extra column. He is a sprite who found a way to make more of himself, by adopting the trappings of forgetfulness and calling it armour.

I went to the river at dusk, because that is the time threads show and also the time dogs are off their leads (and a well-timed quirk of your own shoulder, if you know how, will keep a cocker spaniel out of a vole’s private space). Past the earthy little allotments, past the boy who always jams his left hand in his pocket exactly when I wish he wouldn’t, a drift of spring warmth sliding. The elder bent over the Emm like a grandparent telling a joke. I shimmied into the seam and made myself cinder light.

At first, I saw plastic bag ghosts caught on nettles, dragging the river thin. Then I saw something moving that wasn’t either water or plant. Not big. Not shouting. A receding and returning that did not belong to current. He sat like a child playing house, with a very particular sense of tidiness. He had draped four or five bags—the white kind with little blue squares on them and a slogan promising a thousand uses—around his shoulders, tied with precise knots under his arms. Two more were laid puddled like a floor. He had a twist of something metallic in his hair to keep it out of his eyes. Those eyes were pale and eager.

He was packing air. Making parcels of it. He would open a bag, press it to his mouth, blow, seal, tuck, smile a little. The smile was not cruel. That would have been easier. It was hungry and mathematical.

“Hello,” I said, which is, after all, what we’re meant to say.

He startled so visibly the river forgot to pretend it hadn’t noticed. He turned and there was his lift of the chin that made me want to unlace something at his neck.

“You’re Pip,” he said. That voice in that body; light as shimmer. “You fixed the gates at All Saints so that the heavy immer of grief would not make the wood swell. The vicar thinks she did it. You’re very neat.”

“And you,” I said, “are wearing six too many bags.”

“A quibble,” he said. “I like a sound when I move. It reminds me I am casting shadows with my hands.”

He went back to his packages. Pop pop pop of air captured. I crouched closer, and my knee slid in mud cleverly in love with me.

“Is this for an occasion? Balloons for a hedgehog’s birthday? Because hedgehogs will prick them.”

“Not a party. A practice. You tie things so they stay still; I am tying things so they fly, when I tell them. Also for warmth. The bags. We can do jokes about it. Everyone does.”

“It’s a habit,” I said. “No, that’s too kind—it's a sickness. You have wrapped yourself in habits that will not rot and you have begun to believe you are the present they are protecting.”

He stopped. He was quick; I saw him pocket three of the seams of himself before he looked up. He had a tick where the jaw meets the ear, small but rhythmic, like a trapped watch.

“Do you think you’re the only artisan in this borough?” he asked, almost politely. “Everything we do is habit. I make things too. Mine are louder.”

“My name is Pip.”

“Rustle,” he said. “If you need something to call me so you can call me away from ruin. It’s a joke. I don’t like it. I had a better one once. I forget. They forget. Why keep a name when you can have three hundred empty bottles singing? Why bother with a thread when the whole town is a net?”

“Because threads can be untied,” I said. “Nets strangle.”

“Pious,” he said, and those sleeves of his lovely, hateful bags crackled in happy agreement.

I shouldn’t have gone alone, that first evening. There are times when courage is just a refusal to take helpful advice. But what better bait than a tidy little fixer and his pin? I sat down in his sight and folded my hands and said, “We should talk about the way you are stacking wind.”

He did not wave away the offer. He made a space for it, which is worse. In that distance between us the river developed four extra small musics like coins in a pocket.

“You know what I am doing,” Rustle said. “You’ve listened to town centres. You’ve felt that it is—what do they say—onwards. And I have learnt to take the onward and wrap it around me. I am a piece of town. I am tarmac-colour. I am practical and endlessly providing. I am the promise printed in blue.”

“You are a sprite wrecking streams and hedges with the cast-offs of larger creatures who are bad at waste,” I said, because if you don’t call a spade a spade early, you spend the book un-digging.

“My larger creatures,” he said, smiling, and there it was again—that hunger rot. “We serve them. It’s the old bargain. Do you think you’re an exception? Your neatness keeps their days glued in; my noise keeps them awake.”

“That’s not awake,” I said. “That’s—”

“Irresistible,” he said, and he released one of his air-bundles into the water, where it bounced and wobbled against the elder’s reflection like a trapped cloud.

I went home after that. I don’t mind telling you I went home in a sulk. I unpicked a dozen micro-knots I’d left in my sleeves because I did not want the memory of his voice caught anywhere on me. Beth followed, dignified in the way of birds who think they are in a play.

“Do not peck me tonight,” I told her. “The skin at the back of my neck is an ill-tempered instrument.”

“I will anyway,” she said, perching. “This is one of those moments you are going to try to fold into a lesson, and I am going to tattle to the others. They should come. Do you have biscuits?”

“I have a bag of crumbs.”

“You see,” she said, and she puffed a very black shoulder. “We all have bags.”

We did convene, a little. In the old brickwork behind the library where a line of stinging nettles thinks it’s an army, we huddled and warmed our hands on a pilfered thermos cap of coffee. Sprites of the Subtle Knotworks, the Bit-Menders, two Drain Spirals who smelled of iron and ended their sentences with upsweeps, and a tired Weather Pocket from the bypass who was trying to quit nicotine and had therefore become unhelpfully literal.

“We could leave him,” said Ned-of-Kerbstone, who is the nearest thing we have to cheerful since his beard grew in grey. “A phase. Folks stop handin’ ‘im their filandor—what-do-you-call. Filament. No, those thin whispers. The unseen stuff you both wave about. Anyway. Let him tire.”

“He won’t tire,” said Martha Lint, who makes holes into the exact size you need for a screw to fit properly, and whose eyebrows are two extraordinary opinions. “He is/collecting/velocity. He will learn to bind it round something. He’ll make a—” She wanted a word. She is strict with words. “He will build himself a storm.”

“He already built it,” I said. “And he wore it like a cape and went to the river to practice.”

“What did you do?” asked one of the Drain Spirals, with the upsweep, so that it sounded like flattery. “Hero.”

“Don’t call me that. I made an introduction.”

Beth pecked my neck.

“You’re sulking because he didn’t immediately apologise,” she said. “Your tidy little wounds, they like to be fussed.”

The coffee was a mistake. The smell of it takes me to kitchens with well-worn mugs and people who wipe the table with dramatic sighs and do not know what to do when the silence returns. I felt human sadness in my throat like a fruit seed. We, too, take on the taste of what we serve.

“We can shape it,” said Martha. “If he is making parcels of air, we can make tiny, elegant holes in his parcels.”

“And then he will simply make ten more,” said the Weather Pocket through clenched lips. “You cannot out-scale a manufacturer, Martha.”

“Then we do not out-scale,” I said. “We change the want. The sound he wants from the bags. That crackle he likes. Can we make it less—”

“This is not a therapy group,” said Ned, who has loved me too long to let me speak soft. “Either you believe in your particular life’s work or you go wash it off and take up carpentry.”

“Carpentry is a life’s work,” Martha said, affronted.

“I did not mean—”

“Guidance,” Beth intoned, because she was worried we’d drift into storystitched names for a week. “Go on, Pippers. Tell us how you plan to gently persuade a magpie dressed as a bin to embrace a simpler daily. I will listen because I am asked to bring back objects from schoolrooms and need to know whether to avoid a certain corner of town.”

“Not magpie,” I said. “Sprite.”

They didn’t laugh. We don’t laugh at who we are; there’s enough out there doing it.

“We will watch him,” I said. “We will set unflattering mirrors where he goes. We will—Martha you can tune a hinge so it refuses to tolerate the sound of crackle, can’t you? Those doors at the leisure centre that moan when people bring wet bags in—make them grieve and people will stop. It’s ugly, yes. But grief moves at a different speed. We can adjust speed.” I was building a plan as I spoke it. Plans make a promise to your hands. “We will watch. And I will go back to the river. When he has enough air to float, I will tie a thread to one of the sky’s ankles and show him how unlovely the fall is.”

“You’re braver in groups,” Ned said, kindly cruel.

“Shut up.”

We did not settle it that night. We left in ones and twos, wearing clothes that were not clothes and voices that sounded like other voices in case a human turned their head at the wrong time. I went home and lay in my shell bed and listened to the town change its mind about sleep, twice. The wind attacked the fence around a building site and made the most marvellous umbrella of noise. I counted thirteen breathing patterns from thirteen different windows and matched each to a knot to remind myself they belonged to people, and then I cried a little bit because I was not a hero. I was a secretary of neatness in a town that has been quietly eating itself for a century and a half.

In the morning, I got up and did my ordinary.

A baby hedgehog had curled foolishly in the middle of the footpath by The Holt. I persuaded a dog that it didn’t need to discover whether hedgehogs taste crunchy. A boy had to catch the bus or he would not face his friend’s avoidance that would otherwise take place in period three (you’re thinking: my god the melodrama. Yes. Humans and we share that scale), so I breathed a second into his shoelace, heavy with rain, and tied it without hands. He paused, looked back toward home, looked forward toward Whatever, and went forward.

At mid-morning, the sky did that thing where it pretended to be a very pale sheet of thinking paper. In that light, my hands looked older, which is not a property we should possess. I tipped a jar of buttons into a circle around me and said their names, to remind myself how bound and binding works: pebble, mother-of-pearl, horn, plastic, god help me plastic, bone, wood, wood, wood.

“Are you reciting again?” came a voice from lower in the tree, which is to say the human voice of Clem, who would be a boy forever if he was eight in this mood.

“I am persuading my mind to behave,” I said.

“Is that like when my mum says she’ll only count to ten and then counts to twelve and then says she meant sixteen all along?”

“Similar,” I said, and I stepped out, because with Clem there is no seam, there is just a fact. He is a partner to coincidence, and he does not need a name for it to want to come sit with him. “You look troubled. Is it the lesson on Roman Britain you were meant to enjoy and did not?”

“That was fine. Sir talked too much about drainage. I wanted roads. There’s a man by the river who wears crisp packets.”

“Mmm,” I said, keeping my face in a soft shape. “What of him.”

“He gave Ben a bag for his hamster. To sleep in. Ben put it in the cage and the hamster went inside and then Ben’s mum came home and made a noise you wouldn’t believe.”

“And the hamster?” My hearts—the figurative and the literal—put themselves in different rooms.

“It got out,” Clem said, with a sort of savage relief, because he enjoys not dying. “It is fine. But I thought the man was nice. He said he made balloons out of sighs.”

“Oh,” I said, and I wanted to put my hands on the bark of the tree until I could read something into it that would be enough. “He is not not nice. He is many things at once. That is a lesson. Do not take gifts from him. If he gives you words, put them under a bowl until they stop moving.”

Clem frowned, which on his face looked like thinking had sprouted eyebrows.

“Is he your enemy?” he asked. Bless him, he wanted to be in a story.

“I don’t know. I haven’t given him the job title.”

“When I’m grown,” Clem said, “I’m going to have a tool belt with too many tools and a van that says Clem’s Fixing, OK? And I will not need your—anyone’s—magic. But I still want you to be here, because the sound of you is like a crisp morning in winter when your breath is full of your breath.”

“What a lovely boy you are,” I said, and I wanted to laugh, and I did, and it sounded like it should.

At noon I went to the council offices and hung about in the potted plant by a radiator because sometimes, you have to do the bureaucratic work in person. The woman behind the desk had a name that wanted to be embroidered on a cardigan—Val—and a way of tapping indicative of someone who wanted to eat at different times from the rest of the world. I put a glimmer at the edge of the form she would sign tomorrow that made the numbers for recycling rates look less pathetic, which I am not proud of. But if it bought me six weeks in which Rustle had less to wear, then I would argue with the gods later. The gods and I are on nodding terms; they have bigger pans on the hob.

It was on my return to the Park that I noticed what he’d done with the noticeboard.

There is a board where people post the lost things of their hearts and the found dogs and the ads for second-hand prams that you must fetch quickly because someone else has a better phone. It had been neatly re-papered with a mosaic. White squares. Blue and red. GROCERY NAMES. And over them all, in tidy permanent marker, a quotation: I MAKE A CRACKLE THEREFORE I AM.

I stood for a very long time. Ned-of-Kerbstone came and slanted his head so that his beard put itself in a different climate. Martha arrived as if she had been on her way elsewhere and would not be delayed by foolishness. Beth hopped onto the board and tried to remove the R from CRACKLE because she is a crow at heart.

“He is capable of sentences,” said the Weather Pocket miserably, tempted.

“He is planting his flag,” I said. “On thought.”

We removed the paper. We did it carefully, because that is how you separate a muscle from something that thinks it is a bone. We left the pin holes. Wounds should show, a little, to make sensible conversation later.

Afternoon pulled the town by its shoulder to the left a little. Rustle did not appear. I went again to the river and walked its tidy bits where men run with bright shoes and their pride. I threaded down under the plank footbridge, where the graffiti is trying out adolescence, and asked the water to tell me where my mistake had begun.

The water, being the water, said: All your lines go forward; some should go back. So I went back to childhood.

The thing about childhood is that it is never as sweet as it thinks it is. Sprites are born into work because work is the way we know we exist. My first memory is of a woman crying and me making the cupboard handle write a little comfort in her palm. It did not work. But the act sharpened me as nothing else had. That was the day I thought of myself as hero. I didn’t use the word then. I wore the shape.

By the time the sun had given up on being anything other than a consolation prize, I had done exactly nothing about Rustle that would change the world. That hurt.

At twilight, I stood at the corner by the newsagent where the sweets are displayed with a guilt shameless in its colours and the cigarettes behind the counter are coy. Here humans arrange handovers. Here we cannot resist overhearing. Two girls were talking about a lad, and the lad was talking about a song, and the song was talking about a fight in a basement, and over all of it ran that dry happy sound.

“Are you trailing me?” I asked, and I didn’t turn.

“If I say no you will say you did not mean me,” said Rustle, and he was beside me and not beside me and precisely where he wanted to be. His layers hissed as he shifted weight.

“You’re in the way,” I said.

“You’re in the way,” he said. “Of the future.”

“And you are the worst version of the present.”

“I am the truest version of the present,” he said, once more with that infuriating politeness, as if I was a priest and he wanted to posture his sin correctly. “Do you know what I learned today? I went to a supermarket and pretended to be air, and do you know what they gave me? More of me. For free.”

“Then I will ask them to stop,” I said.

“Yes, because human institutions are so very good at doing the sensible thing, quickly,” he said, and he laughed in a way that got behind my ribs and tickled where nothing polite should be.

We could have fought then. I could have reached for the particular thread at the back of his left shoulder where the knot is always less confident, the part of his armour he made while he thought of his own cleverness. I didn’t. I put my hands in the pockets of a coat I wasn’t wearing and said, “Do you want to do something besides chafe the world?”

“Is that a job offer?” he asked.

“Mmm.”

He considered. The street bustled around his silence like bees around a jam pot. “What would you have me do, Pip, fixer of little things?”

“We could use your speed,” I said. “Your multiplication. Come with me up to the top of town and stand very near to the right lampposts and persuade them to flicker out for three minutes and then hold steady after, because some people are sleeping near them and some are walking under them and there’s a rhythm to light that makes people hopeless when it goes wrong.”

“You want me to be a list of chores,” he said, and for a second I heard the thread that linked him to me, thin but there. “You want me to be your shame.”

“I want you to stop hurting the river,” I said. “And the hedges and the small breathing. If the price is that I ask you like a supplicant, then fine. Please. Please come. Be useful. Be gentle.”

“Gentle,” he said, tasting it like custard. “I remember gentle.”

“Then remember hard.”

He did not answer immediately. He did a very unnerving thing: he took off one of the bags he was wearing and folded it neatly and tucked it inside his shirt, as if he wanted it closer. “I will come,” he said. “Because I like the way you say please.”

We walked the town. It was evening proper now, when the pubs begin to fizz and the chip shops let out their elegant steam. The lampposts were tired. He nudged, I tuned. We learned each other’s timing. He cracked at the lights with his noise; I stitched the cracks so that after the tremble they went still. He was smart. He was, god help me, good company.

“How did you learn to—this?” he asked, at the point by Langborough Road where the old lady always leaves food for a fox she thinks is a cat. “Who taught you to knot?”

“My hands. A man called Percy at the dry cleaners in 1978. A woman named Lal who sings while she sharpens scissors. Wind. Keys. Possibly God. You?”

“The car park by the cinema,” he said, seriously. “And a girl who would not speak to me unless I rustled.”

We laughed. You will hate me for this. You want black hats and white hats, tidy. No. He was cinder-bright. He had a way of using my name that made it shine and then dull in one breath.

At the very end of this long evening, at the very edge of what I had in me to offer, we got to the street he wanted to show me. He did not say we were going there; he walked me like a dog. We stood in a shadow across from a semi-detached house with a very confident hedge.

He lifted his hand.

In the upstairs window, shadows moved. A man bent double, holding his own head as if it might discuss leaving. A woman walked back and forth with shoulders like chairs that have lost bolts. Between them was a small object in a brightly coloured cage. You guessed it. The hamster.

“Ben’s house,” I said.

“Ben’s house,” he said. “Did you think he wouldn’t go home with my gift? Did you think the story ends when a mother shouts?”

The father let his hands go. He sat on the bed and put his face on his knees. He was noisy about it. The woman put the cage down and gathered the bag in two hands and crushed it flat and then, because this is how it goes, smoothed it and laid it out again because it was a bag-for-life, and you don’t throw life away, do you, you wipe it and keep it with the others like a promise.

“They are my clergy,” Rustle said very quietly. “The keepers of my shrine. They do my work for me without loving me. I am holy anyway. Isn’t that delicious.”

It was not. It made me want to stand in that house and scream. It made me want to put my hands in the hedge and pull until the roots came up. It made me want to kiss the boy’s forehead, which is not my right and not my way. Instead, because it is my way, I found the thread from the streetlamp to the boy’s pillow and shifted it half a notch so that he would dream of water running clear, which is a cheat, but sometimes you cheat back.

“We will not be friends,” I said to Rustle, whose profile was beautiful like a knife.

He nodded, as if I was paying him a compliment. “I didn’t say we would.”

He left me on the pavement. I went home. The night made itself at home in my home like a cat, unbothered by whether I wanted it. I lay on my back and watched the ceiling of my chestnut for seven hours of not-sleep and finally rose with a plan that had nothing to do with kindness. I am not proud. I am necessary.

The next day I started a series of small wars. I took the whisper from every carrier hook in the town and lost it under the floorboards of the Town Hall. I taught three gates to detect the faint exhalation of plastic and have opinions. I found the tributary where Rustle collected pond water because it makes the bags shine and I salted it with a story about a girl who tore a bag in half and laughed and did not die and did not cry. That is not a story that would have killed him. But it would have made his sleepless hours taste different.

I also did something else. I went to the bus stop where teenagers gather and leaned my shoulder into the evening alongside them and let them listen to me. The hard ones, the ones who posture tenderness with knives in their mouths, they can smell a sprite from a long way because they too are uninvited to quiet. We talked. They pretended not to. I taught them to fold a bag into a triangle to make it smaller than the promise it shouts. They laughed at me and did it and then did it again and then flicked the triangles at one another like wealth. The smallest hand, a girl who had drawn crosses on her wrists in pen, tucked hers in her pocket as if it might one day be a sail for a boat.

This is the work.

This is also the point at which Rustle decided to take something I liked and show me what it sounds like when it tears.

Late afternoon. A wind that felt like a letter. The oak that was not my home but would have me if I needed it. The magpies doing their jokes. A laugh that was not a laugh. And the noticeboard again, new paper, a different pen, and one sentence that made the life I had built out of string and wit and minor theft shudder.

HEROES RETIRE. It said.

Not yet. Not then. Not now—but the crack ran, hair-fine, down to my feet.

“Don’t let him,” said Beth, pecking at the plastic coverings on the pins, as if breaking their skins would free us. “Don’t let him choose your last chapter before the middle.”

You will accuse me of taking it personally. I did.

I went to the river with a knife disguised as a very fine way of parting reeds and I said his name twice and then not at all, because names summon and sometimes I wanted the opposite. I sat. I watched. I waited.

A dog chased a stick with the dedication of a tax inspector. A boy tended to himself and tried to look elsewhere and I gently retied his belt. The water played catch with the sky. Two bags, bloated with air, but there they were, anchored overhead to the elder by a neatness that was almost mine. No Rustle.

“No,” I said to the voice that was not yet a word in my head. “No. We are not doing this. You do not leave signs and absences like a suitor. If you want a fight, you come.”

The laughter came again and went away. It has a way of saying: later. It is, in that way, like all the worst things that have ever happened to a town.

In the days that followed, the scale of it widened. People I liked carried their shopping in plastic and told themselves out loud it was fine, it was only a few. My hands felt heavier. The lampposts sulked in the rain. At the station the signal box coughed and needed soothing twice what usual. I began to pick my fights. The Work—that capital letter and its big clever boots—it wants you to say yes to everything. That is the straight road to failure.

I was not failing. I was, I tell myself, choosing my theatres. In the morning I would tie three threads and let the fourth trip and I would say sorry to the fourth and promise tea later. In the afternoon I would lull a whole pavement of shoes into remembering their ankles. In the evening I would leave four dozen holes in plans where the wind might speak. And all of it under the crackle. All of it under his breathing-in slow.

One evening, on the market square where the van selling doughnuts perfumes everyone into generosity, a woman dropped her bag of spuds and Rustle, who I had not seen in three days, caught one and tossed it back to her. He made her laugh. He made me want to throw up.

“You said please so beautifully the other night,” he murmured when I was close enough to hear. “Do you remember?”

“I remember liking you,” I said. It is a hard confession for a person like me to make and still be able to walk home upright. “I suggest you do something unforgivable soon, so that I may regain my moral simplicity.”

“Hee,” he said, and his bags whispered secrets. “Do you think that absolves you? Do you think your skill with threads builds a halo? You and I. We serve. The difference is I admit it.”

The engineer did what engineers do when someone brings a religion to a physics class. He nodded, made a note, said they would consider trials. Val caught my eye and lifted one shoulder a millimetre toward heaven. He had his hands in his pockets and the right one was making small apologetic shapes. “You wanted to hiss,” he said, cheerfully grim. “I heard your shoulders. You can hiss now if you like. “I don’t wire lamps,” I said, already calmer because he is right about my tendency to combust during dull tasks. “I adjust the opinions of their switches. And I will hiss when you deserve it. Right now you did not make it worse. I am as surprised as you are.”

He tilted his head, and for once the crackle in him rested. “I am trying to be the me who helps,” he said, and there was a tear in the seam of that sentence that nearly let me fall into pity, and I stopped myself by thinking of geese. “It itches,” he added, because we are not, between us, wholly sentimental.

The itch had a venue. Three days later, as if summoned by bad planning and goodwill in a tug-of-war, the town held its charity fete on the field behind St Crispin’s. There were bunting and dogs and a man frying onions whose aroma reminded everyone of being nineteen and invulnerable for six months. There was a gymnastics demonstration and a lady who sells soap with jokes and a teenage metal band called, gloriously, The Metaphors, who played so accurately out of tune that several lawyers reconsidered their relationships with their own teenagers. There were balloons. Not quite a release—someone on the committee had sense enough to veto sky lanterns—but a forest of helium waiting to be tied to wrists and chairs and moral fibre.

Paper crackled. Banners boasted. A stall advertised reusable this and sustainable that with a thin under-print of absolution. I settled myself by the tombola with the tranquillity of a clerk and began the day’s small militia: this string to be tied in a way that undoes without knife; that peg to be swopped for a gentler one; that dog to be persuaded that bouncing is a theological error when old men are holding tea. Clem won a novelty wig and put it on backwards and achieved the rare honour of making Beth drop a washer out of sheer mirth. Ben bought three tickets and lost twice and then, with a face set to receive humiliation gracefully, won a rubber duck that said Woky in a typeface I will not dignify with a description.

Rustle did what men like him do at community gatherings. He stood slightly to the side and was somehow always being watched. He leaned into his quiet shirt and made small talk with competence and did not, for two hours that felt a year in his jaw, rustle. Then he saw the helium. You could see the noise come up in him like fever. He walked toward the tent with the balloons as if it were a mirror he trusted. A nice woman with a cardigan that believed in charity asked him would he like to help and for a fatal half-second he looked at me. I saw him do the arithmetic: good cause, crowd assembled, permission baked into sugar and guilt. I did not shake my head. I did not drop his name into the Church Clock. I walked to the roll of ribbon and put my finger on the end of it and said, evenly, “Yes. But we do bows that fall apart. And we tie them to bags someone will take home made of cloth.”

He is very clever, so he did not make it a duel. He made it a workshop. He taught ten people in ten minutes to make bows that unfasten without teeth. He told three teenagers, with respect, that string is a language and you must not lie with it. He looked at the helium the way a priest looks at a font in the week between christenings. He tied balloons to cloth handles and the cloth was kind and held without resentment. For an hour, the field learned how to enjoy spectacle that would not migrate to hedges later. Then a boy let go of a string because boys do, and the balloon rose with smugness in its throat. Fifty faces went up like flowers following the sun. Rustle’s left hand twitched and I felt the seam ripple because he could have brought that balloon down with a shrug of noise and I held my breath ready to be impressed and furious. He did nothing. The balloon went skyward and became a dot and then nothing and the river, far off, added it to a list and would collect later and I will admit I went behind the raffle table and had five seconds of savage sobbing where no one could see, because this is the work: watching the dots go and not letting the hole in your chest become doctrine.

In compensation or penance—choose your hymn—Rustle came with me after the fete to the river, without invitation, without sleeves. The Emm had been fattening herself with weeks of story, ready to spill opinions onto the path if the gods fancied. The weather, insulted by someone’s picnic on its watch, built a sulk that would become rain. At the elder the door looked across the path and minded its own business with a ferocity I admire. The cupboard wore its green with a day’s dust it had earned honestly. We stood and the river muttered, and I had that flick of joy again that I get when I am allowed to be bored where I belong.

The flood came two days later and I intend to tell you exactly how dull it was so you will love the people who stood in it and did not shout. The sky opened with the decorum of a council chamber that has given up on decorum. The Emm shrugged her banks three times like a woman thinking about resigning and then resigned. Water went where it wanted with an energy that explained religion to atheists. It was not a catastrophe. It was an argument. The booms had not yet been installed, praise and pity both, which meant the river’s gossip went straight for the elder and the path and our door.

I can hear you think: ah, now the door will be heroic. No. The door did what properly made things do; it helped nearby. It gave people a frame for their bodies, told the elderly where to step to be less afraid, and presented the possibility of shelter to two birds who thought for a ridiculous six seconds about becoming domestic. The cupboard lent out its cloth without fanfare. People came because cupboards are more convincing than sermons. Bags left on shoulders and came back wet and left again, and no one asked for deposit or thanks. The latch, with its milk-ring ornament, held and did not cut anything that had a throat.

We made a boom with the things polite people forget they own. Old curtains, torn just so. A king-size sheet that arrived without its pride. Aprons that remembered fire and were happy to learn river. Ned anchored one end to a post that had been waiting its whole life to be necessary and showed a man trained by desks how to keep a rope from telling lies. Martha drilled holes where metal wished to be elegant and told me, without stopping her work, that the screws were fine, thank you, and I did not argue because if you have a Martha you do not argue while water is walking uphill. The Drain Spirals sang to the iron in voices that climbed ladders like cats. The Weather Pocket was magnificent and disastrous and magnificent again, because he remembered that wind can be held in state for twenty minutes if you make it feel admired.

Rustle was a machine and a poem. He moved without his bags and he did not rustle; he stitched. We needed speed to plait the torn cloth into a rope thick enough to embarrass the water into going the long way round, and he gave us speed like a man turning his body in to pay a debt. His hands learned our knots the way a clever boy learns swear words and admits to none of them where teachers can hear. He did not whip the flood into theatre. He refused himself three tricks that would have pleased a crowd. He asked me, like a child asking where to put the next brick, “Here?” and did as told. Once he laughed—a short honest break when Beth dropped something shiny into the water for amusements’ sake and a fox offered an opinion on the matter out loud—and it did not scratch paint from anything. Twice his jaw ticked and twice he breathed it down. The third time it ran away with him and he stepped back and scrubbed his face with his palms as if he could scour want from his cheeks.

The boom worked enough, which is a kind of miracle I prefer. It caught gossip and let water be rude past it. It pretended to be a snake because rivers respect a certain sort of animal. It kept a plastic crate from docking itself in a vole’s front room. It made a collection point that did not swear when you spoke to it. People came in ordinary shoes and went home with their socks obligingly soaked and the feeling of right-sized heroism you get when you move something that weighs more than your own mood. I breathed into the seam until I could taste the elder’s jokes in the spray. The river, whom I have offended and comforted in equal measure, conceded two centimetres and a metaphor. We tied the boom to the elder, who accepted the gift like a matriarch offered a necklace by grandchildren she knows will be ugly and loves anyway.

When the water shrugged her shoulders back into their usual posture, the town did the lashless blinking of a creature that has remembered its eyebrows. Rustle stood with his arms around himself as if he had grown cold, and perhaps he had. He was wet to the shins and mud had decided to make a map on his calves. He looked young. The itch had had nowhere to hook into him for two hours. He was he, without crackle, without costume, and he stood it. Then he reached into his shirt and pulled out one of his old squares of plastic he had folded and kept like a coin and looked at it. He turned it in his hand twice. He walked to the cupboard and, with the weariness of a man who has agreed to a rule he did not draft, pushed the square under the hinge where it could do no harm and a little good by making the door remember who watches it.

“Do not praise me,” he said, and the instruction saved me from myself. “I will go home and itch. I tell you this so you do not put me on your saint board and then hate me when I take a pin from it later.”

“We do not have a saint board,” I said. “We have a noticeboard and we use it for jumble sales.”

“Exactly,” he said, and he found in that a sanity that did him good enough to show. He nodded to the door as if it were someone else’s baby that had been well-behaved in a difficult restaurant. He nodded to the elder, because even he knows to respect a tree with a dirty laugh. He nodded, last, to the river, and the river blew him a kiss it did not mean and that is as kind as rivers get.

The day after a flood is when your fingers tell you their age. Mine ached with the satisfaction of being allowed to hurt for the right reasons. The cupboard had a smell of wet cloth that took me back to places I have not written down because there are chapters that belong to other people. The door had a new humility written into the way it hung, which is a brag in our profession. The noticeboard carried three mundane announcements and one slow handwriting that said: Thank you to whoever held the line yesterday, and someone else had added: The line was cloth and we all pulled. Val signed off on a petty cash request for screws with an indulgence that made me suspect she had slept properly for once. Clem arrived with a penknife of his grandfather’s and cut the milk ring off the latch because it was going to be brittle and he said, responsibly, that brittle is a form of future accident, and we nodded at him like responsible men we are not.

I took the square from under the cupboard hinge and made it into the least pretty thing. I cut it into triangles, not the tidy little sails the girls keep, but clumsy pieces with edges that said I used to think I was a promise. I strung them on a string stolen fair and hung them behind the cupboard door facing inwards where only those who opened it would see, and I wrote in felt-tip above them, private as a whisper: Triangles to remind us to do the boring thing first. Beth said it was gauche and then, when she thought I wasn’t looking, stood on the velvet and read the triangles like a fortune teller.

It is not all river and cupboard. Life insists on its schedule. All Saints needed their gate sung back down from the swelling grief, again, because grief is a professional and clockwork can be cruel. The station muttered in a tired dialect, yes, but did not attempt to wear a lung as a hat. The supermarket, unshamed, rolled out display baskets full of paper that performed virtue, and I performed my own small treason by whispering to their handles that they would squeak in an ugly way when lifted by men who shout at customer service assistants, and sometimes pettiness is a spice you put in a stew to make it palatable. The Weather Pocket relapsed and took up gum and I had to concede it made him less dangerous. Martha announced to nobody in particular that hinges and hearts share a philosophy and we told her she was getting sentimental and she told us that if we wanted poetry we should go listen to The Metaphors rehearse, and we did not.

That evening, when the door had earned its eyelashes again with restraint, the boy who had moved away came back with a story he had finally admitted belonged here. He stood in the frame with a letter in his pocket and a music. He went through and the letter did its work in him, which is to say it made a noise he could live with. He came back and caught sight of Rustle, who had been leaning on the cupboard pretending to be somebody’s aunt with a headache. The boy nodded the nod you give to a man who has made the one sound that called your father back from the brink once, long ago, and Rustle, because he is worse and better than you think, did not try to own it. He returned the nod and went on being a person whose itch is the shape of a town. If you require him redeemed, I am sorry. If you require him damned, I am sorrier. He is, like the rest of us, employed.

And because you will be the sort to ask about omens, yes, there was one. A fox came in daylight and napped under the cupboard with the arrogance of a duke. When it woke, it yawned elaborately and made as if to take the dull green’s corner in its mouth, and then it thought better of it, because cupboards are more sacred than kings when they hold things we do not have the language to bless. The fox left by the door, because it is a door, and did not look back. The kerbs hummed a note I had not heard before, or perhaps had not been bored enough to hear. It sounded like a town practising a hymn written entirely in minor repairs and cups of tea. If you do not care for hymns, call it a roadmap. If you do not like roads, sit with us under the elder and learn to fold.

Two mornings later the engineer called Val and Val called nobody and the booms arrived on a lorry that had carried circus ponies once, if the smell told the truth. They were not snakes made of crackle. They were cloth braided onto patience with rings that had been bottle tops, drilled into obedience, and the rattles they made were themselves a history lesson: kitchens, markets, a school play, a dress, an apron, a curtain. Rustle walked past with his hands in his pockets like a man doing the right thing badly to see if it still counts. He did not applaud. He did not spit. He stood with me where the seam runs, and because it was boring, because it was civic, because it was a Tuesday, I allowed our shoulders to touch. The Emm? We stood in a sort of open-air subcommittee and read about cloth booms described as an “innovative interim intervention,” about insurance described as “pragmatic” and a line that said in a tone of surprise that the community response had been “swift without spectacle.” I wanted to underline that and make it into bunting. The door needed oil; oil is love in our business; I did that and told it about Rome because someone needed to, and even a door should get roads now and then. The river sulked less. The fox pretended to learn a trick and then did not because foxes are on the side of trick, not learning. All Saints asked me if I would like to train a new vicar to hang grief on the hinges before opening the gate and I asked them if she made tea and they said yes and we did not interview because she, like the rest of us, is employed.

And so to the part where the seam thickened and I had to decide not to become the moral that goes on tea towels. The kerbs hummed in a note I had not taught them. The station’s glass reflected a boy who was not Clem and was entirely himself and still managed to need a biscuit so badly that the biscuits found him. Val sent me a picture of a spreadsheet with a row of numbers that looked, by chance and no magic of mine, like a body learning to breathe through its nose. Rustle, who has been a problem and a colleague and the occurrent man on the path I both want to avoid and stand next to, sat on the cupboard with his feet tucked as if he had been a child once, and said, delicately and without making a performance of it, “I have a bag I do not want to own. May I leave it here.” He had folded it into a square the size of a confession and it had the smell of storm that has learnt regret.

“Yes,” I said, because I am more custodian than hero, and the cupboard made a space for the square under a shelf that has learned to forgive everything that fits. He put it down with the care you give an animal you love and should not live with. He closed the door as if it could feel draughts. We sat. He did not itch. Or he itched and did not make me watch, which is the same kindness on certain days. The door breathed. The boom took what came. The fox declined to be urban. The cupboard swallowed and said nothing. The paper arrived with a smell that had not been inside our cupboard: toner and urgency. Val brought it in the crook of her arm the way you carry a baby you agree exists but do not yet like. Consultation Draft: Public Realm Rationalisation, it said, with that baritone that thinks it is above verbs. There were maps, and in the maps the Emm was a blue cord pulled too tight; there were grey rectangles that proposed benches where no ankles wanted to be formal; there was a line, mean as a ruler, that said Permitted Temporary Structures (Artistic), and our door had been reduced to a dot with a key letter next to it as if it were a bus stop. The cupboard did not appear at all, which is one way bureaucracy says it has not learned to name the thing that feeds it.

We had seen plans before and we will see them again; there is a rotation to officiousness as reliable as bin day. Still, this one had a particular sentence that put grit in my mouth: All community amenities must demonstrate Spectator Value in line with our town’s refreshed brand. Spectator. Value. Brand. You could see the cylindrical room where those words had shaken hands. I took the paper to the elder and used its bark as a sieve; I let through words that belonged to us—temporary, safe, maintained— and held back the ones that would demand a stage. Rustle read over my shoulder without fidgeting and did not, for once, pretend to be a lawyer. He tapped the Spectator and then the Value and then the Brand, as if counting beads in a rosary for an unhelpful god, and said, carefully, “We are going to have to be very dull at volume.”

The noticeboard did its best impression of a conscience by offering us its blank. We did not fill it with a manifesto; we pinned one page with four lines in the style of an aunt who knows where the broom is kept. Inventory Day, Saturday. Bring one thing that makes enough easier. We will count aloud. Tea if you bring your own cup. There was a temptation to add eyelashes to the word inventory to keep the jokes running, but I resisted; charm is a spice and this stew needed salt. Beth, insulted by restraint, stole two drawing pins and made a swear on a branch and returned them, which is her form of agreement.

On Inventory Day the town arrived shy and then present, like a choir who have been told to sing but only if they can first stand with their hands by their sides. A man brought a wooden spoon and apologised to the spoon for being dramatic about it; a girl brought a length of ribbon unbranded by triumph and said it belonged under the sink in case of parcels; a nurse set down a stack of old flannels and said we have forgotten that softness can be washed; a builder brought a spirit level and did not joke about his spirit, which is how you know he respects you. We counted out loud, not like children but like archivists who have waited their whole lives to be allowed to wear a cardigan in public. One, two, three—on and on—cloth bags with stories sewn under their armpits, boiled sweets for coaxing, a pencil that would not mind the rain. The cupboard swallowed and blinked. The door, plain and kind, watched people pass through on purpose with something that was not pride but looked like it from a distance.

It would have been idyllic if idylls didn’t itch. In the afternoon a boy we did not know, brave with boredom in the way that is its own kind of cunning, pulled at the cupboard’s green and called it stupid, and the green held, because dull paint is another kind of armour. Rustle, two paces away, looked like a man who has practised not making weather and was choosing to pass the exam. He crouched to the boy’s height without melodrama and said, conversationally, “It is boring. That’s the trick. Some things need to be so boring you forget to be watched while you use them.” The boy looked at him, decided he could hate him later if required, and asked, as if checking a timetable, whether the cupboard had biscuits. We did. We are not fools. He took one and, in the transaction of crumbs to mouth, joined a cult less flashy than most.

While we counted our enoughs, the river did sums of her own, and late that week she sent us a memo in the form of a stench. Heat had sat on the town with the entitled gravity of a cat, and the Emm, offended, slowed. The booms of cloth sagged—not failure, but fatigue—and water bloomed that dissuasive green that says algae and grief. “Look,” the Weather Pocket said, with the righteousness of a man who has found a failure in a system he helped build, “sound doesn’t carry in this kind of heat. You can’t hush a pond into manners.” He had gum; it made him laconic in a way I almost liked. “Shade,” Martha said, and she didn’t mean our faces. “We’ll teach the path to be forest for an afternoon and then again. Rustle, if you put so much as a syllable of plastic into this, I will feed it to you alphabetically.”

Shade is cloth’s cousin. We put our hands into old drawers. We stole with permission lace doilies that had not asked to be useful since 1973 and stretched them into a canopy that looked like a grandmother had spun spider silk with a sense of humour. Martha stitched a spine along a line of fishing twine; Ned talked to two posts like errant nephews until they held a tension that made sense to physics and family alike; the Drain Spirals offered hooks with the insouciance of men who have pockets like ironmongers; Val encountered our ladder and took hold of it and I loved her a little for that on a day when love had been rationed. Rustle tuned the shade until it said shh to light without being a sermon. Under it, the path cooled. Children lay down with their faces to the lace and saw constellations named after biscuits. A woman whose headaches are loyal stood still and did not apologise to anybody for doing it. The Emm exhaled. The algae didn't recant, but it conceded the argument about drama.

There was, inevitably, a man with a camera. There are always men with cameras when a town does something unphotogenic well. He asked who was in charge and three of us said No in different dialects and pointed at the hinge. He said we should stand under the lace and look up and laugh. We said we would if he brought his own cup. He laughed like a man who had been told a joke that did not include him as its target and was grateful. Rustle shifted his shoulders and the lace above us murmured, as if remembering how wedding receptions smell when the speeches are kind. The man took his pictures anyway and, to his credit, did not ask the fox to pose.

Because you’ve been trained by committees to expect escalation, here is the small cruelty the town produced to keep its narrative moist. The noticeboard woke with a message in tidy felt-tip that said: HEROES RETIRE, in the same hand as before, but smaller, as if embarrassed to be a slogan. A woman with a pushchair read it and said, without heat, “So do villains,” and ripped it down with the same hand she uses to wipe noses. Behind it was our line about Inventory Day, speckled with rain the way toast can be speckled with optimism. We did not respond. We put up nothing in its place. Blankness as an exertion is a craft you have to learn. I thought of the boy who had been ten and had tuned a gate with a song and had moved away and had become a man who could fold a letter into quiet and I let that thought be my reply.

The council inspection, when it finally arrived, did not wear horns. It wore sandals and a clipboard and the self-consciousness of a public servant who has been asked to be a villain by a memo and is not good at it. The inspector stood in front of the door, read aloud from a sheet about Permitted Temporary Structures, and waited for us to turn into an incident. We were abominably kind. Val produced a folder with laminates that had learned not to shine, full of oiling schedules and hinge notes and signatures in pencils that still had rubbers on the end. Ned showed the man how a spirit level calms a frame, and the frame, tickled, calibrated itself out of politeness. He looked at the door as if it were a poem he had been told would be good for him. He went through it. He came out the other side with a different hold on his pen. He ticked a box. He said, “Leave the eyelashes on the sketch. They make my daughter laugh,” and I made a note to buy a lottery ticket on the day when bland salvation grows in wild settings.

School asked for us again and this time Rustle came to the front because the teacher had a unit to finish and because the worksheets had left the children with a fetish for metrics. He talked about sound without making it perform. He showed them how to make a crackle and then taught them, with the patience of a man trying to train his worst habit to heel, how to quiet one again, and thirty children learned to hush something they had been told to celebrate. Clem held the tray for sails and did not splash even when splashing would have advanced his legend. A boy who bruises easily put his hand up to ask if quiet gets bullied, and I realised I was not equipped to be that sort of teacher and told him the truth anyway: sometimes it does, and then it goes into cupboards and comes out again later when the room has learned courtesy. The boy looked relieved as if I had not told him a fairy tale. The teacher cried at lunchtime in a cupboard of her own and the cupboard enjoyed being useful.

Rustle had been invited to London and not gone and then gone and come back, and it left a worry in him as visible as a stain. He did not say he had stood in a room where praise is a rain that scalds, but he was careful with kettle steam for a week. He brought a ribbon that said Artist and asked me with his eyes to cut it, and I did, and we both listened for the noise that would make as it changed its vocation. Behind the cupboard door there is now a line of triangles that used to declare access-all-areas and now declare only: wipe your shoes, mind the step, bring your own cup. I am not unaware of the little cruelties we perform under the flag of redemption. I am, however, keeping score for the river.

The river and I argued about heat until the weather changed its mind. We had made shade and a boom and an inventory; we had taught the Emm to accept the compliment of cloth; we had become, nauseatingly, a case study in Val’s minutes. Then the rain came with the gladness of a dog that has found something unacceptable to roll in. The booms held. The shade turned into a shy curtain that could be wrung. People forgot umbrellas and remembered to share them. A man who had declared last month that everything was nonsense found himself shepherding toddlers through our door and said thank you aloud to a hinge, and the hinge pretended not to preen.

Calamity tried, as it always does, to arrive in disguise. One night a boy—not Clem; this one wore his sad like a jacket he had paid too much for—came to the cupboard with a knife for the simple reason that knives are a way some boys ask for applause. He tried the latch, found it untempting, and inserted the blade into the green with the deliberation of a man starting a signature. Rustle was there before me, not with hands—his hands can make weather and this was not a weather problem—but with his face. He said, in a tone I have heard him use once, at a funeral, “If you want to cut something that will make you feel like a god for five seconds, there is a bridge three streets over and a poster with a politician’s smile. We did not tell anyone we had saved something that night. There are saves that do not wear medals and should not.

Toward the end of the month, when hawthorn does its white-laced bravado and teenagers let their bare arms be members of the weather, the boy with the lost gloves came to the cupboard with a notebook. He had drawn the door, again, but now the eyelashes were not a joke; they were a technique, a way to show where attention should soften. He had drawn the cupboard as if it were an animal he wanted to be trusted by. He had written on the top page: Inventory of Small Mercies. Under it he had listed in a hand that still hated itself a little—robins on fences, bus that waited, dad that stayed, triangle that sailed. He did not give it to me because he is not sentimental. He slid it behind the velvet where people confess they have hoarded and asked permission to hoard differently, and the cupboard, who is becoming satisfactory company, held it without licking it.

“Do you see,” Rustle said, not to claim the moment but to test whether we could say it without flinching. His jaw clicked once and did not carry on. “Do you see what we are, when we are trying.”

“I see what we can avoid being,” I said, because I am old-fashioned in the church of not jinxing it. “And I see that the Work is not bigger, exactly. It is nearer. That is worse and better for a man who likes to make lists.”

He thought about that. He leaned on the cupboard in a way that would have broken it a month ago and now did not because hinges learn, and he rested the side of his face against the green paint until it took a little of his temperature and decided not to record his fevers as law. That night I did my rounds because habit is the charm I wear in place of power. The Emm argued with a starling and lost, which is not as embarrassing as it sounds; starlings are unions all by themselves. On my way home I met Clem with two friends who have not yet agreed to be named. They carried nothing and that was the point. They walked through the door as if it were a habit, and it is; they came out with nothing and with an expression that meant they had found, for ten steps, the version of themselves that is not narrated by a group chat. “Inventory,” Clem said, by way of hello, and I said, yes, inventory, and he said, satisfied as a man who has put the right spanner back into the right drawer, “We did our three,” and went on. Behind them, at the cupboard, Rustle stood very still. He had his hands empty and visible and his jaw quiet. He had put into the hinge, somewhere earlier when I was busy cataloguing, the tiniest sound, a nearly polite click that says, when the door closes: enough, enough, enough. It is not a slogan, and if anyone says it is I will make the Church Clock bong their names at dawn. It is just a hinge being certain, which, I have been learning, may be the kindest sound a town can grow.
