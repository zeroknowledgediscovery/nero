The asphalt was a black ribbon spooling out under the thin, pre-dawn light. For Sergeant Alexandra Vance, it was the only church she’d ever needed. The rhythmic whir of her bicycle’s freehub, the burn in her thighs as she crested another of Fulton County’s rolling hills, the cool air slicing across her face—it was a catechism of motion and muscle. Fulton was a town that slept in, a place of slow mornings and long memories, but Alex was always up before it, chasing a peace she could never quite catch. The town held its breath in the morning mist, the pines along the road standing like silent, black-robed sentinels. She knew every crack in this pavement, every pothole that threatened to swallow a wheel whole. This was her territory, not just as a cop, but as a rider. The two had become inseparable in her mind.

Her radio, clipped to the strap of her shoulder bag, crackled to life, a jagged tear in the morning’s fabric. “All units, be advised. Report of a juvenile male, non-responsive but conscious, residential backyard at 412 Evergreen Terrace.”

Alex keyed her own mic, her breathing barely disturbed by the climb. “Dispatch, this is 7-Adam. I’m two klicks out. I can take it.”

“Roger that, 7-Adam. Mother is on scene, she sounds… agitated.”

Agitated was Fulton-dispatcher code for hysterical. Alex pressed down on the pedals, the carbon frame of her bike groaning with the effort. Evergreen Terrace. The newer subdivision on the west side of town, where the lawns were too green and the houses looked like they were printed from the same catalog. A place that prided itself on its utter lack of surprises. The thought soured in her stomach. Surprises were her business.

She was the first to arrive, skidding her tires to a stop on the dew-slicked sidewalk. A woman in a silk bathrobe was on the porch, her face a mask of crumpled panic, a cordless phone clutched in her hand like a weapon. She pointed a trembling finger toward the backyard. “He’s… he won’t talk. He just lies there. Leo! My Leo!”

Alex unclipped from her pedals, her cycling cleats clicking awkwardly on the flagstone path. She gave the mother a nod she hoped was reassuring. “I’ve got it, ma’am. Stay here. Let me see.”

The backyard was an oasis of suburban perfection. A pristine lawn, a child’s swing set, a meticulously pruned rose garden. And under the swing set, curled into a fetal position, was a boy of about sixteen. His eyes were wide open, staring at nothing, his breath coming in shallow, hitching gasps. He was shivering, despite the humid morning air. There were no visible injuries, no blood, no signs of a struggle. Just the boy, lost in a sea of manicured grass.

“Leo?” Alex knelt, her voice soft. “My name is Alex. I’m a police officer. Can you tell me what happened?”

The boy’s gaze flickered toward her, but there was no recognition. His lips moved, forming silent words. Then a sound, a dry, rasping whisper. “Spiders… the big spiders… with feathers…” He flinched, a full-body tremor. “He wouldn’t stop. I told him to stop… I couldn’t… breathe…” A choked giggle escaped his lips, a horrifying, joyless sound. It died in his throat, turning into a sob.

By the time Officer Miller arrived, his cruiser sighing to a halt at the curb, Alex was scanning the ground. Miller, a man whose gut strained the buttons of his uniform, ambled into the yard with the weary air of someone whose coffee had been interrupted. “What we got, Alex? Kid on a bad trip?”

“I don’t think so,” she said, not looking at him. Her gaze was fixed on the ground near the swing set. The dew had been disturbed. There were depressions in the soft earth. Footprints. But they were wrong. They were too far apart for a normal stride, and the impressions themselves were enormous, impossibly so. And deep. As if left by someone of immense weight.

“Jesus, what made those?” Miller squinted. “Some kind of prank? Kid in stilts?”

“Maybe.” Alex’s voice was tight. There was something else. A smell. Faint, but cloying. It was sweet, like dried flowers left in a dusty attic, but underneath it was a sharp, chemical tang. It pricked at the inside of her nose. Something sterile and wrong. She walked the perimeter of the yard. The chain-link fence at the back was dented, one of the top bars bent downwards at an unnatural angle, as if something very large had simply stepped over it.

Back at the station, the air was stale with the smell of burnt coffee and paperwork. The fluorescent lights hummed a monotonous tune, a sound Alex associated with dead ends and bureaucratic sludge. Leo had been taken to Fulton General for observation. The preliminary report from the ER doc was useless: no drugs in his system, no signs of assault. Just ‘acute psychological distress.’ The official narrative was already forming in the minds of her colleagues: a teenage prank gone wrong, a cry for attention. They wouldn't look past the easy answer. They never did.

Her desk phone rang, its shrill cry cutting through her thoughts. The caller ID flashed: HOME. She let it ring three times before answering, bracing herself.

“Alexandra.” Her mother’s voice was as crisp and brittle as autumn leaves. “I was just calling to see if you were still alive.”

“I’m alive, Mom.” Alex swiveled in her chair, staring at the scarred plaster of the wall.

“Your father and I were just saying, we never hear from you. We have to see your name in the police blotter in the Fulton Chronicle just to know you’re still in town. Assaulting that poor drunk man behind the liquor store last month. Very dignified.”

“He was assaulting a teenage girl, Mom. It was my job.”

“There are other jobs. Nicer jobs.” A pause, laden with years of unspoken disappointment. “Your father’s dahlias are looking wonderful this year. You should come see them. Mrs. Gable from the book club asked about you. I told her you were very busy with… your work.” The way she said ‘work’ made it sound like a communicable disease.

“I am busy, Mom,” Alex said, her jaw tight. “Something happened this morning.”

“Oh? Nothing too… unpleasant, I hope.”

“A kid is in the hospital.”

“Well, I’m sure you’ll handle it.” The dismissal was as swift as it was complete, a door slamming shut on that part of Alex’s life. “Just try to eat something, dear. You sound thin.” The line clicked, leaving Alex in the humming silence of the station. The chasm between her world and theirs felt wider than ever. It was a canyon she had carved herself, a decade ago, when she’d chosen this uniform over law school, the hard reality of Fulton’s underbelly over the soft comfort of her family’s expectations. They had never forgiven her for the choice, or for the scandal of her first big case, the one that had gone so spectacularly wrong and put her on a first-name basis with the department’s internal affairs division. She had won her career, but lost her family in the process.

She couldn’t shake the boy’s words. *Spiders with feathers.* It sounded like a child’s nightmare. But the footprints, the bent fence, the smell… they were real. She pulled up the crime scene photos on her monitor, zooming in on the images of Leo’s hands. His wrists were red and raw, but not with the clean, sharp lines of rope or zip-ties. The skin was chafed, rubbed raw in a wider band, as if held in a grip that was both immense and unyielding. She stared until her eyes burned. Laughter. He had mentioned laughter. Being tickled until he couldn't breathe.

It was insane. Utterly, certifiably insane. No one gets assaulted by tickling. But Leo wasn’t a liar. She’d seen the terror in his eyes; it was old and genuine. Alex opened a new search on her desktop, typing in keywords. *Fulton. Unexplained psychological trauma. Laughter. Giant.* The search engine returned nothing but links to comedy clubs in bigger cities and articles on the medical effects of hysteria. It was a dead end.

She pushed back from her desk, the wheels of her chair squeaking in protest. The official channels were useless. She needed to go deeper, into the town’s own memory. The station’s archive was in the basement, a damp, forgotten room filled with collapsing cardboard boxes and the ghosts of a thousand forgotten crimes. The air was thick with the scent of decaying paper and mouse droppings. Most cops avoided it. Alex found a strange comfort there. It was a place where things weren't forgotten, just misplaced.

She spent hours sifting through dusty files, her fingers growing grey with grime. She ignored the major crimes, the murders and robberies that everyone remembered. She looked for the oddities, the cases that were quietly closed, the ones labeled ‘unsubstantiated’ or ‘juvenile prank.’ And then she found it. A thin file from 1998. The case of a fifteen-year-old boy named Samuel Bell. Found wandering in the woods near Miller’s Pond, laughing and crying, incoherent for three days. The official cause was listed as a reaction to ingesting wild mushrooms. But a supplemental report, written by a retiring deputy, mentioned something else. The boy’s family had reported a local legend, a folktale parents used to scare their children into coming home before dark. They called him the Giggler. A tall man who lived in the woods, who would catch children and tickle them until they lost their minds. The deputy had dismissed it as rural nonsense. But he’d noted one thing in his report, a detail that had clearly bothered him. The boy, Samuel, had bruises on his ribs. Not from a fall, the coroner had said, but from the inside. His intercostal muscles were torn from the force of his own laughter. And on the back of the file, scrawled in faded ink, was a single, chilling sentence. *"Kid kept saying it was fun, right up until it wasn't."* Alex stood motionless in the silent basement, a cold dread seeping into her bones. This wasn’t a prank. This wasn’t new. This was a story, a nightmare, that had been sleeping in the woods around Fulton, waiting to wake up. And it was awake now.A fine tremor started in her hands, a vibration that had nothing to do with the damp chill of the basement. The file on Samuel Bell felt impossibly heavy. *Intercostal muscles torn from the force of his own laughter.* It was a clinical, detached observation that landed like a body blow. Leo’s choked, joyless giggle echoed in her memory, no longer just a symptom of distress but the sound of the weapon itself. This wasn't a folktale. It was a profile, a method of operation. The Giggler. The name was absurd, childish, which only made the reality of it more grotesque. Alex carefully placed the file back in its box, her movements precise, belying the chaos storming inside her head. The musty air of the archive no longer felt like a comfort, but a tomb, and she was the one unearthing the bodies.

Back upstairs, the squad room felt like a different world. The evening shift was starting to drift in, bringing with them the scent of cheap takeout and a louder, more boisterous energy. Miller was holding court by the coffeemaker, telling a story about a shoplifter who’d tried to hide a frozen turkey down his pants. Laughter bounced off the institutional green walls. It was a normal sound, easy and light, but to Alex, it now sounded sharp and dangerous, each chuckle a tiny, serrated blade. She felt a profound and sudden alienation, a pane of thick, soundproof glass dropping between her and her colleagues. How could she ever explain what she’d found in the basement? How could she bridge the gap between a turkey-stealing drunk and a feathered spider-man who tickled children into catatonia?

She retreated to the bubble of her desk, the noise of the room fading to a dull hum. Protocol demanded she log the old file as related evidence. Common sense told her that would be the fastest way to get laughed out of the room and have the case taken away from her. The Chief would see it as a waste of resources, a wild goose chase born of an overactive imagination—the same criticism she’d faced years ago. She wouldn’t make that mistake again. This one, she would work from the shadows. Her fingers flew across the keyboard, pulling up the state’s integrated records system. She ran a search for Samuel Bell, born 1983. The results were sparse, a life lived in the margins. A few minor charges in his late teens—vandalism, public intoxication. Then nothing for years, until a single, recent entry. He was a resident at the North Fulton Pines Assisted Living Center, a facility on the far edge of the county mostly reserved for long-term psychiatric care.

The knowledge settled like a stone in her gut. Samuel Bell never recovered. He was thirty-seven years old and living in a place where people went to be forgotten. The Giggler hadn’t just scared him for a few days; he had stolen his entire life. The case was no longer just a strange puzzle box; it was a rescue mission, twenty years too late for Samuel, but maybe not for Leo. She looked at the clock. It was almost five. Visiting hours at North Fulton Pines would be ending soon. She grabbed her keys, shrugging on the light jacket that served as her off-duty uniform. “Hey, Miller,” she called out, her voice hard. “I’m heading out. Cover my desk.” He gave her a lazy wave, already halfway through another punchline. He didn’t ask where she was going. No one ever did.

The drive out to the Pines was a journey from Fulton’s manicured present to its decaying past. The road narrowed, the asphalt crumbling at the edges, the tidy lawns of the suburbs giving way to overgrown fields and derelict farmhouses. The facility itself was a grim, brick monolith from the 1960s, set against a backdrop of darkening woods. It had the hopeless, stagnant air of a place where time didn't pass, it just pooled. Alex’s cop instincts screamed that this was a bad place, a place where stories went to die. She flashed her badge at a weary-looking nurse who seemed unsurprised by a police visit. “I need to speak with Samuel Bell,” Alex said, keeping her tone neutral. The nurse sighed, gesturing down a long, linoleum hallway that smelled of bleach and despair. “Room 214. Don’t expect much. Sam hasn’t spoken a complete sentence in a decade. Just be careful. He gets… agitated… by sudden movements. And he hates being touched.”

The door to Room 214 was slightly ajar. Alex pushed it open gently, her heart thumping a heavy, nervous rhythm against her ribs. The room was spartan: a bed, a dresser, and a single chair bolted to the floor. A man sat on the edge of the bed, his back to her, his shoulders hunched. He was methodically, obsessively, folding a piece of paper into smaller and smaller squares. “Samuel?” Alex said softly from the doorway. He didn’t turn, but his hands stilled. “Samuel Bell? My name is Sergeant Vance. I’m with the Fulton Police. I wanted to ask you about something that happened a long time ago. In the woods by Miller’s Pond.” The silence in the room stretched, thick and heavy. She could hear him breathing, a shallow, unsteady sound, eerily similar to Leo’s. When he finally spoke, his voice was a dry, unused whisper, a ghost of a sound that filled the small room with an immense and ancient cold. "He promised it was a game," he rasped, still not turning. "You win if you can stop laughing."The words hung in the sterile air, devoid of irony or jest. They were a statement of fact from a man who had played the game and lost everything. Alex took a slow, deliberate step into the room, letting the door click softly shut behind her. The sound made Samuel flinch, his shoulders drawing up towards his ears. He resumed his obsessive folding, his knuckles white.

“A game,” Alex repeated, her voice barely a whisper. “Who promised you, Samuel? Who was he?”

He shook his head, a tiny, jerky motion. The folded piece of paper in his hands was now no larger than his thumbnail. His breathing grew ragged. “Tall. So tall. His coat smelled like old flowers and rain. Like the attic. He had a hat.” The details came out in a rush, as if a dam had broken, releasing a trickle of stagnant water. He stopped, his focus returning to the scrap of paper. Alex waited, letting the silence stretch, a tactic she’d learned was more effective than any threat. She could feel the weight of twenty-two years of terror pressing down on the small room.

“Leo mentioned spiders,” Alex prompted gently. “Spiders with feathers. Do you know what he meant?”

At the word ‘spiders,’ Samuel’s hands flew to his ribs, clutching his own torso as if he’d been stabbed. A low, guttural sound escaped him, half-whimper, half-chuckle. It was the same horrifying, joyless noise she had heard from Leo. “Fingers,” he gasped, his eyes squeezed shut. “So many long, long fingers. They… they aren't skin. They're like roots. Hard and dry. And they crawl. He puts them on you and they crawl all over.” He shuddered violently, rocking back and forth on the edge of the bed. “The feathers… they were soft. On his face. When he leans in close, you feel the feathers on your cheeks. He whispers… that you’re doing so good… to laugh so hard… that you’re almost winning.” He was talking to the wall, to the past, to the thing that had broken him. Alex felt a cold sweat prickle her hairline. This was a ritual.

“Samuel, where did he take you? Did he come from the woods?”

He didn’t answer. His frantic energy was gone, replaced by a sudden, vacant stillness. He stared at the piece of paper in his hand, which he had now unfolded and begun to smooth out. His brief window of lucidity had closed. He was gone again, retreated back into the fortress of his shattered mind. Alex tried one more time, her voice soft. “Samuel?” But there was no response. He was just a man in a room, smoothing out a piece of paper, a ghost haunted by the memory of a laugh. She knew she would get no more from him. As she turned to leave, her eyes fell on the paper he was so intent on. It was a page torn from a book, an old illustration. It depicted a tall, gangly bird with exaggerated plumage, something like a heron or a crane. Scrawled below it, in Samuel’s spidery, uneven handwriting, was a single word: *Tattler.*

The drive back toward the center of Fulton was a blur of streetlights streaking across her windshield. *Tattler.* Not Giggler. Was it a name? A description? The image of the bird felt important, a piece of a puzzle she couldn’t yet see. Her mind raced, connecting the disparate elements: the bird drawing, the feathered face, the smell of dried flowers, the impossibly long strides. It was a caricature, a nursery-rhyme monster come to life. And it had been hunting in Fulton for decades. She glanced at the clock on her dashboard. 8:47 PM. The library would be closing in thirteen minutes. It was a long shot, but the digital archives had failed her. She needed paper and ink, the town’s real memory, not the sanitized version on a server.

She pulled into the library parking lot with a screech of tires that was obscene in the evening quiet. The Fulton Public Library was her one untainted childhood memory, a sanctuary of polished wood and the intoxicating scent of aging books. It was a holdover from a more civic-minded era, its stone façade carved with the names of philosophers and poets. She pushed through the heavy oak doors just as a librarian with a kind, wrinkled face was about to lock them. It was Evelyn Reed, a woman who had been recommending books to Alex since she was tall enough to see over the circulation desk.

“Alexandra! Cutting it a little fine, aren’t we?” Evelyn’s smile was genuine, a stark contrast to the strained politeness of her own mother. “Don’t tell me you’ve finally decided to read something that isn’t a ballistics report.”

“Evening, Evelyn. I wish. I’m actually here on a case. A historical one.” The lie came easily. “I’m digging into some old local folklore. The name ‘The Giggler’ mean anything to you?”

Evelyn’s brow furrowed in thought as she methodically shut down her computer. “The Giggler? That’s what my mother used to call my Uncle Frank after two glasses of sherry. In the town’s history? No, that doesn’t ring a bell. It sounds a little… silly. Most of our bogles and spooks have more gravitas than that.” She paused, her finger tapping her chin. “What’s the context?”

“A tall man, preys on children, associated with the woods,” Alex said, deliberately leaving out the more disturbing details. “Maybe a folktale parents used to scare their kids.”

“Ah, a bogeyman. We have a few of those. There’s the Woman of the Wash, who drowns wanderers in the Fulton River, and Mad Silas, who was said to haunt the old quarry. But nothing called the Giggler.” She took her keys from a hook, but hesitated, her archivist’s curiosity piqued. “You said a tall man. Anything else? Any other names?”

Alex took the chance. “What about ‘The Tattler’?”

Evelyn’s face changed. The casual curiosity was replaced by a flicker of something else, a deep, instinctual recognition that went beyond simple memory. She put her keys back on the hook. “Now that name I know. It’s not a folktale. Not exactly.” She beckoned Alex to follow her, her footsteps silent on the worn carpet as she led her away from the main floor, towards a heavy door marked ‘Fulton Room - Local History.’ “It’s older than that. Comes from one of the town’s founding families, the Blackwoods. They were strange folk, even by the standards of the 1700s. Kept to themselves in a big estate out in the northern woods. They had their own traditions, their own… stories.”

The Fulton Room was cold, the air perfectly still and dry. It was a vault of a room, lined with glass cases of sepia-toned photographs and shelves of leather-bound town records. Evelyn went to a locked cabinet and retrieved a slim, hand-bound book. The cover was simple, unadorned black leather. She laid it carefully on a reading table, her hands gentle.

“This is the personal journal of Jedidiah Blackwood, from 1823. Most of it is just crop yields and complaints about the weather. But he talks about a family tradition, a ritual to drive ‘melancholy humors’ from their children.” She opened the book to a bookmarked page. The ink was faded brown, the script an elegant, spidery scrawl. “They had a figure they called the Tattler-Crane. On the eve of the harvest moon, a man from the family would dress in a costume. Long stilts to make him tall as a tree, a coat covered in dried herbs and flowers from the attic, and a mask. A mask carved from wood and adorned with the feathers of a Blue Heron, a ‘tattler-crane’ as they called it, a bird known for its rattling cry.”

Alex read the passage over Evelyn’s shoulder, her blood running cold. Jedidiah wrote about the ‘game.’ The Tattler-Crane would hunt the child in the fields. If caught, the child would be submitted to the ‘joyful trial,’ a relentless tickling meant to cast out sadness with forced, hysterical laughter. It was seen as a necessary, if frightening, rite of passage.

“My God,” Alex breathed. “They made a monster to scare their kids into being happy.”

“It gets stranger,” Evelyn said, her voice low. She flipped a few pages. “The last entry in the journal. Jedidiah writes that his youngest son, Ezekiel, disappeared the night after the ritual. He was never found. After that, the Blackwood family fell into decline. The estate was abandoned a generation later. But stories lingered. People claimed to see a tall, feathered man walking the edges of the old Blackwood property on moonless nights. Not playing a game anymore. Just… hunting.” Evelyn looked up at Alex, her eyes wide with a fear that was two centuries old. “The Blackwood estate. It’s all woods now, part of the Fulton State Preserve. But the old house, the root cellars… they’re still out there. Right where Evergreen Terrace backs up against the trees.”The weight of Evelyn’s final words settled in the cold, still air of the Fulton Room, a keystone locking two centuries of scattered, terrifying details into place. Evergreen Terrace. The place that prided itself on its utter lack of surprises, built on the edge of a forgotten nightmare. The meticulous lawns and identical houses were a thin, fragile crust over a deep, dark history. The Tattler-Crane. It wasn’t a monster born of shadows; it was a tradition, a deliberate creation of human cruelty disguised as folk medicine. Alex stared at the spidery script in Jedidiah Blackwood’s journal, the words a direct confession from the grave. A game you win if you can stop laughing.

“Did anyone ever look for the son? Ezekiel?” Alex asked, her voice a low murmur.

Evelyn shook her head, gently closing the leather-bound journal. The soft thud of the cover felt like a coffin lid closing. “Not in any official capacity that I’ve ever found. In the 1820s, a child lost to the woods was just… lost. People assumed it was an animal, or that he simply ran away and perished. The family sealed their records and their gates. They withered away. The story of the Tattler became a whisper, a ghost story to keep kids out of the woods, its real origins forgotten by everyone. Until now, I suppose.” She looked at Alex, her librarian’s curiosity now mingled with genuine fear. “Alexandra, what have you gotten yourself into? This isn’t some historical curiosity, is it? That boy this morning…?”

Alex met her gaze, the unspoken truth passing between them. There was no point in lying to Evelyn, not entirely. “The boy is terrified. He’s talking about things that don’t make sense. And he’s not the first.” She thought of Samuel Bell, folding his life into a tiny square in a sterile room. “Evelyn, this book, these records… can you keep this between us? For now?”

Evelyn Reed, a lifelong purveyor of rules and Dewey Decimal order, didn’t hesitate for a second. “This room and everything in it is under my purview, Sergeant. I decide who has access. Consider it sealed.” She slid the journal back into its place in the locked cabinet, her movements suddenly imbued with a new gravity. She was no longer just a librarian; she was the keeper of a terrible secret. “Be careful, Alex. The things that sleep in the woods around this town… sometimes it’s better to let them lie.”

The warning followed Alex out into the cool night air, but it was useless. You couldn’t tell a homicide detective not to investigate a murder, and you couldn’t tell Alex to walk away from this. It was in her blood, the same stubborn refusal to look away that had alienated her family and defined her career. This was no longer just a case. It was an exhumation. She got into her nondescript sedan, the familiar interior offering no comfort. Her world had tilted on its axis. The rhythmic whir of her bicycle that morning felt like a memory from another lifetime, a hymn to a peace that she now knew was a fiction. The pines along the road were no longer silent sentinels; they were the guardians of the Blackwood estate, their branches intertwined like skeletal fingers, hiding a monster in plain sight.

She didn’t drive back to the station. There was nothing for her there but paperwork and Miller’s oblivious laughter. Instead, she drove west, back toward Evergreen Terrace. She parked her car at the end of a cul-de-sac, the last house before the suburban dream gave way to a solid wall of trees. The Fulton State Preserve. Her territory. She’d ridden the trails in there a hundred times, charting her progress by familiar oaks and rock formations. Now, the familiar woods felt like enemy territory. The chain-link fence Leo’s attacker had stepped over ran along the entire length of the subdivision, a flimsy suggestion of a boundary. Alex found the spot from the crime scene photos, the top bar still bent into a sad, metal frown. This was the door.

She’d come prepared. In the trunk of her car, she kept a go-bag, not for a weekend away, but for the messy realities of her job. She swapped her jacket for a tactical fleece and clipped a high-powered flashlight to her belt, its solid weight a small comfort next to the service weapon on her hip. She pulled on a pair of gloves, not just for the cold, but to keep her own presence here as sterile as possible. She swung a leg over the dented fence, landing silently on a carpet of damp leaves on the other side. The air immediately changed. The smell of cut grass and chlorine from a nearby pool was gone, replaced by the rich, loamy scent of decay and damp earth. And underneath it, faint but unmistakable, was the scent she remembered from Leo’s yard. Dried flowers and a sterile, chemical tang. It was the smell of the attic, the smell of the Tattler’s coat. It was stronger here.

She moved through the undergrowth with a practiced stealth, the beam of her flashlight cutting a clean, white tunnel through the oppressive dark. The woods were unnervingly silent. No crickets, no rustle of small animals. It was a dead quiet, as if the entire forest was holding its breath. Using her phone’s GPS, she set a course for the geographic center of the old Blackwood property, a point deep within the preserve. The terrain grew more difficult, the ground sloping downwards into a shallow ravine choked with thorns and fallen trees. It was clear no one had been through here in years. No official trail, at least. But as she scanned the ground, she started to see signs. A broken branch here, a scuffed patch of moss there. And then, the footprints. The same impossibly long-gaited, deep impressions she had seen in the dew-soaked lawn of Evergreen Terrace. They were old, some nearly erased by rain and time, others fresher. A path, used over and over again. The monster had a commute.

After a twenty-minute hike that felt like an hour, the ground leveled out. The trees thinned, and she stepped into a clearing. In the center, like a mouthful of broken teeth, stood the ruins of a great house. A single, massive chimney stack clawed at the night sky, its stones covered in a thick mantle of ivy. The foundation was a crumbling rectangle of stone, filled with dirt and opportunistic saplings. This was what was left of the Blackwood manor. It felt ancient and malevolent, a wound in the landscape that had never healed. Alex swept her light across the ruins, looking for the one thing Evelyn had mentioned that could survive a fire and two centuries of neglect. A hole in the ground.

She found it near the base of the chimney, almost completely hidden by a sprawling, thorny bush. A pair of heavy, slanted cellar doors, their wood grey and petrified with age, but their huge iron hinges remarkably free of rust. A thick chain and a modern, heavy-duty padlock held them shut. Relief and dread warred within her. The lock meant someone was still using this place. This wasn’t a ghost. It was a man. Her bolt cutters, a tool she’d been ridiculed for keeping in her go-bag, made short work of the lock. The snap of the metal was obscenely loud in the dead quiet of the woods. She paused, listening, every nerve ending alight. Nothing. Just the whisper of the wind through the skeletal ruins.

She heaved one of the heavy doors open. It groaned in protest, the sound like a man groaning in his sleep. A wave of foul air washed over her, a concentrated version of the scent from the yard. It was nauseatingly sweet, the smell of potpourri and rot, of formaldehyde and dust. It was the smell of a museum dedicated to death. Drawing her Glock, she pointed her flashlight into the abyss. A set of steep stone steps led down into perfect, impenetrable blackness. Her heart hammered against her ribs, a frantic drumbeat against the silent dark. Every instinct screamed at her to turn back, to call for backup, to wait for daylight. But she thought of Leo, shivering in a hospital bed, and Samuel, rocking in a room for two decades, and she took the first step down. The world above disappeared, and she was swallowed by the cold, waiting earth.The air that rose from the cellar was a physical thing, a thick, cloying miasma that coated the inside of her throat. It was the smell of a forgotten hope chest, of bridal wreaths and funeral flowers left to crumble into dust, all of it steeped in the sharp, unnatural bite of a preserving agent.
