The wind off the Channel had a particular spite to it in late autumn, a damp, toothy bite that gnawed at the Victorian facades of Hastings and sent shivers through the gulls huddled on the groynes. It was a wind that promised bad weather and worse moods. To Eleanor Bolt, it was just the soundtrack to her Tuesday. The people she looked after at the Seaview Eldercare Residence, they called her Detective Bolt. Not the coppers, of course. To the uniformed and the over-stuffed Hastings constabulary, she was, at best, a recurring annoyance and, at worst, ‘that batty woman from the old folks’ home’. But her residents, they knew. They knew who found Mrs Higgins’s prize-winning petunia pot after it vanished from her windowsill (repurposed as an ashtray by teenagers behind the fish and chip shop). They knew who deduced that Mr Gable’s missing war medals hadn’t been stolen by his estranged son, but had simply been wrapped in a napkin and binned after a particularly messy Sunday roast.

Eleanor was a caretaker. That was her job title. It meant she fixed leaky taps, changed high-up lightbulbs, and listened to endless, looping stories about the war, the decline of the fishing fleet, and the outrageous price of butter. But her real work, the work that hummed in her blood, was noticing things. She noticed the subtle shift in a resident’s gait that foretold a fall, the tremor in a hand that meant a prescription was off. And she noticed the tiny injustices, the small tears in the fabric of their quiet, ordered world. She was their guardian, their fixer, their own private eye. At forty-three, with a face that had settled into a kind of permanent, weary watchfulness and hair the colour of a stormy sky, she had accepted this role. She wore practical shoes and a navy-blue fleece embroidered with the Seaview logo. She was, to all outward appearances, entirely unremarkable. That was her greatest asset.

The case of Preston the Ogre began, as so many things did, with a cup of weak tea and a distressed pensioner. Mr. Abernathy, a retired chartered accountant with a face like a troubled walnut, flagged her down in the hallway, his hand trembling as he clutched a sleek, silver tablet. He was one of the few residents who’d embraced the digital age, mostly to monitor his dwindling stock portfolio and watch videos of steam trains.

“Eleanor, my dear. A word. If you please.” His voice was a reedy whisper. “There is a sickness on that infernal machine. A moral sickness.”

Eleanor, fresh from unplugging a toilet clogged with what appeared to be an entire Dundee cake, followed him into his small, obsessively neat room. The air smelled of lavender polish and old paper. He thrust the tablet into her hands. “I was looking for a local news feed,” he explained, his eyes wide. “And I… I stumbled upon this. This… chronicle of malice.”

The website was called ‘The Crunch’. The banner was a grotesque cartoon of a huge, fleshy foot, clad in an expensive-looking brogue, poised over a tiny, terrified ant. The tagline beneath it read: ‘Preston’s Pavement Press: Stomping Out The Little Guy.’ Eleanor felt a cold knot tighten in her stomach. She scrolled down. It was a blog. The latest post, dated that very morning, was titled ‘Synergy, Schminergy’. It began with a high-resolution photograph of a single black ant, desperately trying to haul a breadcrumb across a paving stone.

Eleanor began to read the text aloud, her voice flat. “‘Meet Bartholomew. A plucky little forager, a provider for his colony. You have to admire the work ethic, the sheer bloody-mindedness of the creature. He thinks his crumb is the key to everything. The promotion. The corner office in the anthill. The respect of his six-legged peers.’” She paused, swallowing. “He gives them names.”

“Keep reading,” Mr. Abernathy urged, his face pale.

“‘But Bartholomew’s business plan has a fatal flaw,’” Eleanor continued. “‘He failed to account for market disruption. For a hostile takeover. For me.’ Then there’s another photo. It’s just… a smear. A dark smear on the stone, next to the crumb.”

She scrolled further. Post after post. Each one the same horrifying formula. A picture of an ant, given a name and a pathetic, human-like backstory. ‘Penelope, a single mother of three hundred.’ ‘Gary, saving up for a bigger mandible.’ ‘Clive, who dreamed of seeing the world beyond the garden wall.’ And then, the inevitable, gloating conclusion, often couched in the language of finance and corporate brutality. ‘Clive’s travel plans were permanently liquidated.’ ‘Penelope’s entire portfolio has been dissolved.’ ‘Gary’s assets have been comprehensively stripped.’ It was a diary of microscopic genocide, narrated by a monster.

And the monster had a face. On the ‘About Me’ page, a man beamed from the screen. He was huge, a mountain of pink flesh squeezed into a pinstripe suit that must have cost more than Eleanor’s car. His face was round and jowly, his eyes small and piggy, glittering with a smug, self-satisfied glee. His name was Preston North. The biography was brief and nauseatingly arrogant. ‘A man of significant stature and even more significant wealth. A titan of industry, a connoisseur of the finer things, and an amateur entomologist with a hands-on approach. Preston believes in the survival of the fittest. And as you can see, he is very, very fit.’ The location tag at the bottom of the page simply said: ‘Hastings, UK’.

He was here. This… ogre. This man who found joy in cataloguing the obliteration of the tiniest, most harmless of creatures, was breathing the same salty air as her. He was walking these streets. His expensive shoes were crushing living things on the same pavements her residents used for their slow, careful constitutional walks.

“It’s the… the glee, Eleanor,” Mr. Abernathy whispered, looking as if he might be sick. “The sheer, unadulterated pleasure he takes in it. It’s evil.”

Eleanor handed the tablet back, her own hands now strangely cold. It wasn’t about the ants. Anyone could step on an ant by accident. This was different. This was ritual. It was the celebration of the squish. The deep, narcissistic satisfaction taken in erasing something smaller than yourself simply because you could. It was the casual cruelty of a bully given a global platform. She had seen that instinct before, in small, subtle ways. In the way a healthy resident might mock a frailer one, in the condescending tone a doctor might use, in the impatience of a driver at a pedestrian crossing. It was the root of a thousand everyday evils. But here, it was distilled, purified, and bottled as a proud philosophy. This wasn’t just a man stepping on ants. This was a man practicing for something bigger. She could feel it, a low, ominous hum beneath the smug prose. The hum of a predator.

Her shift ended in a blur of backed-up drains and misplaced spectacles. The wind seemed to have worked its way inside her, a cold and restless thing. She walked home, her eyes scanning the pavement, wincing at every dark speck on the concrete. Her flat was a small, two-bedroom affair above a bookmaker’s on the Old Town’s High Street, a cosy clutter of books, half-finished knitting projects, and the lingering scent of Earl Grey tea. The main reason she took it was for the second bedroom, which wasn’t a bedroom at all, but a study for her great aunt.

Aunt Agatha, at eighty-nine, was sharper than most people a third of her age. She was the woman who had taught Eleanor to read, not with picture books but with the crime reports in the local paper. She’d taught her to look for what wasn’t said, to listen to the silences, to understand that the biggest truths were often hidden in the smallest details. Agatha sat in her worn armchair, a magnifying glass in one hand and a cryptic crossword in the other, her white hair a fierce halo around her head.

“You’re late,” she said, not looking up. “Problem with the plumbing or a problem with the people?”

“A bit of both,” Eleanor replied, dropping her keys into a ceramic bowl by the door. “Always a bit of both.”

She put the kettle on, the familiar ritual a small comfort. She watched the steam begin to curl from the spout, her mind replaying the grotesque images from Preston’s blog. The smug, fleshy face. The dark smear on the pavement.

“There’s a man,” she said, her back to the room. “He lives in Hastings. He has a website.”

“Good for him,” Agatha said, scribbling a word into the grid. “‘A devious contrivance’. Seven letters. Starts with ‘S’.”

“He posts pictures of ants,” Eleanor continued, her voice low. “Before and after he squashes them.”

Agatha’s pencil stilled. She looked up, her bright, bird-like eyes fixing on Eleanor. “Before and after?”

“He gives them names. Jobs. Dreams. Then he writes about how he… liquidates them. He calls it The Crunch.”

Agatha put the paper down on the table beside her. A long silence filled the small kitchen, broken only by the rising shriek of the kettle. Eleanor poured the boiling water over two teabags, her movements slow and deliberate.

“And this is your new case, is it, Detective Bolt?” Agatha’s tone was gentle, but the question was serious. “The Ant-Squasher of Hastings.”

“Don’t mock,” Eleanor said, bringing the mugs over. “It’s not… It’s not funny. There’s something deeply wrong with him, Aggie. The way he writes. The pride he takes in it. It’s a symptom of something rotten.”

“Most people are rotten in one way or another, dear,” Agatha said, taking a sip of her tea. “It’s just a matter of degrees. So, this man is a bully. He bullies ants because they can’t fight back. He’s a coward with a God complex and a blog. The world is full of them.”

“But he’s here,” Eleanor insisted, sitting opposite her. “In our town. And he’s bragging about his cruelty. What if he gets bored of ants? What if he decides he wants to crush something bigger?”

Agatha looked at her niece, at the fierce, troubled set of her jaw. She had seen that look before. It was the look Eleanor got when she knew, with an unshakeable certainty, that something was profoundly unjust and that she, and perhaps only she, was going to be the one to do something about it. Agatha reached across the table and patted Eleanor’s hand. Her own was a delicate, papery thing, but her grip was surprisingly firm. She thought of the crossword clue. ‘A devious contrivance’. She looked at the worry etched on Eleanor’s face and thought of a man who would celebrate such pointless, petty destruction.

“Scheme,” she said suddenly.

“What?”

“The crossword,” Agatha clarified. “A devious contrivance. It’s ‘scheme’. Show me this website, Eleanor. Show me this Mr. Preston’s nasty little scheme.”Eleanor retrieved her own laptop from the study, a slightly battered machine covered in stickers of puffins and lighthouses. She sat back down at the small kitchen table and typed in the dreadful address. The fleshy foot of the banner image appeared, making the laptop screen seem soiled. Together, she and Agatha scrolled through the digital graveyard. Agatha adjusted her spectacles, leaning in close, her expression one of intense, unblinking concentration. She didn't flinch at the pictures of the dark smears; instead, she read Preston's accompanying prose with the detached air of a literary critic dissecting a badly written novel.

“The vocabulary is telling,” Agatha murmured, pointing a bony finger at the screen. “‘Market disruption.’ ‘Asset stripping.’ ‘Liquidation.’ He’s not just killing them, he’s framing it as a financial transaction. He’s justifying his cruelty with the language of the boardroom. He’s cosplaying as a god of commerce, and the ants are just tiny businesses he’s putting under.” She scrolled back to the ‘About Me’ page, tapping the screen over Preston North’s beaming, porcine face. “Look at that suit. The cut of it. And the watch. Is that a Patek Philippe? This is not a man who has ever worried about the price of butter. This is a man who buys things to prove he can.”

Eleanor had been so caught up in the emotional horror of it all that she hadn't considered these details. To her, it was a monster’s diary. To Agatha, it was a collection of clues. Agatha enlarged one of the ‘before’ photos, the one of ‘Gary, saving up for a bigger mandible.’ The ant was on a large, pale flagstone, textured with fine-grained ripples.

“That’s not a council pavement,” Agatha said, her voice sharp with discovery. “That’s Yorkstone. Beautifully laid, too. You don’t find that on the public rights-of-way, not anymore. That’s a private driveway or a very expensive patio. He’s not just walking down the street, Eleanor. He’s doing this at his home. Or his office. He’s created his own little killing floor.”

The idea made it even worse. This wasn’t random; it was a ritual performed in his own space. He would step outside his grand house, find a victim, and perform his ceremony of crushing it, documenting it, and uploading it for the world to see, all before his morning coffee had cooled. He was building a monument to his own pettiness.

“So we find the house with the Yorkstone patio,” Eleanor said, the hum of the chase beginning to drown out her disgust. It was a familiar feeling, the click of a puzzle piece falling into place.

“It’s a start,” Agatha agreed, leaning back in her chair. “Preston North. An uncommon name. He’s wealthy, arrogant, and lives in or near Hastings. He likes to write and he clearly has a high opinion of his own intellect. He also, and this is his great mistake, has an overwhelming need for an audience. Bullies always do. Their power is worthless without a witness to their cruelty.” Agatha took a thoughtful sip of her now-lukewarm tea. “He wants to be seen. He’s given us his name, his face, and his philosophy. He’s practically begging to be found.”

“But what do we do when we find him?” Eleanor asked. “It’s not a crime, Aggie. The police would laugh me out of the station. They already think I’m the ‘batty woman from the old folks’ home’.”

“The police are for crimes, Eleanor. This is a matter of justice,” Agatha said, a hard glint in her eye. “And justice requires a more creative approach. This man’s ego is the size of a house. His blog, his ‘Pavement Press,’ is the grand front door. We can’t knock it down with force. We have to find the key. We have to find his particular vulnerability and use his own vanity against him.” She smiled, a thin, dangerous little smile that Eleanor knew well. It was the smile she wore when she was three moves from checkmate in chess, or when she’d untangled a particularly knotty bit of plotting in a classic detective novel.

“He bullies the small and the voiceless,” Agatha continued, her voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. “So we will become the voice for the voiceless. He wants to stomp on the little guy? We’ll just have to find a little guy he can’t stomp on.”

A plan began to form between them, passed back and forth across the kitchen table in the quiet language of shared understanding they had perfected over decades. It was audacious, ridiculous, and utterly brilliant. It was an Agatha plan. Eleanor felt the last of the day’s weariness fall away, replaced by a thrum of nervous energy. The case of Preston the Ogre was no longer a source of helpless anger. It was a problem to be solved. A scheme to be enacted. Her real work had begun. Tomorrow, she would not be Eleanor Bolt, the caretaker who fixed leaky taps. She would be Detective Bolt, on the hunt for a monster. Her first task: to walk the wealthy streets of Hastings, looking not for grand houses or sea views, but for the pale, elegant expanse of a Yorkstone patio.The following morning, the wind had dropped, leaving a sullen, grey stillness in its wake. The sea was the colour of slate, flat and exhausted. Eleanor had called in a sick day, a rare indulgence she justified as essential fieldwork. Dressed in her most anonymous clothes – grey trousers, a dark waterproof jacket, and a nondescript woolly hat pulled low over her stormy hair – she was a ghost against the town's muted backdrop. She bypassed the bustling Old Town and the tired glamour of the main promenade, heading inland and uphill, to the secluded avenues where Hastings tucked away its serious money.

Here, the houses stood back from the road, separated by manicured hedges and walls of flint and brick. They had names instead of numbers: ‘The Laurels’, ‘Sea Glimpse’, ‘Trafalgar House’. Eleanor walked with a slow, steady pace, her eyes not on the impressive architecture but fixed firmly on the ground. She scanned driveways, front paths, and the small, gated courtyards that fronted the grander properties. She saw gravel, herringbone brickwork, and oceans of glossy, black tarmac. But no Yorkstone. To anyone watching, she would have looked like a lost tourist or someone searching for a dropped coin, entirely harmless, entirely forgettable. Her superpower was in full effect.

She found it on a street so exclusive it felt like a private estate. The house itself was an architectural affront, a colossal cube of glass and white render jammed between two elegant, ageing Victorian villas. It was a statement of wealth so loud and aggressive it seemed to be screaming at its neighbours. And leading up to its huge, steel-grey front door was a vast, immaculate driveway of pale Yorkstone, each slab perfectly cut and pointed, gleaming faintly in the damp air. It was less a driveway and more a pristine, empty stage. Preston’s Pavement Press had found its physical address.

A low, modern wall offered just enough cover. Eleanor settled onto a damp public bench across the road, partially obscured by a skeletal rowan tree, and began her vigil. She unwrapped a cheese and pickle sandwich she’d made that morning, taking small, methodical bites. Waiting was a skill she had perfected at Seaview. She had waited for doctors to call back, for ambulances to arrive, for the slow, meandering point of a resident's story to finally emerge. She could out-wait anyone.

Just after ten o’clock, the huge front door swung open and Preston North emerged. He was even bigger in person, a fleshy mountain range straining against the confines of a hideously expensive tracksuit made of some shimmering, navy-blue material. He was bellowing into his phone, his voice a gravelly, entitled roar that sliced through the quiet of the morning. “I don’t pay you to have opinions, I pay you to make me money! If the deal is soft, you make it hard! You tell them… you tell them North is coming!” He paused, listening, his piggy eyes scanning his own perfect driveway with an expression of profound ownership. He looked, Eleanor thought, like a man who believed the entire world was a subsidiary of his own ego.

He ended the call with a curt snap and began to pace the Yorkstone, his hands clasped behind his back like a Roman emperor surveying his domain. Then he stopped. His great head tilted, his focus narrowing on a single point on the ground near his gleaming training shoes. Eleanor’s sandwich turned to paste in her mouth. He knelt, a surprisingly fluid movement for a man his size, and pulled his phone from his pocket. He angled it, taking a picture. Then he stood up, a slow, self-satisfied smile spreading across his jowly face. He typed for a moment, no doubt composing some vile little eulogy for his victim. And then he raised his foot. He didn’t just step; it was a deliberate, grinding motion, a transfer of weight designed for maximum obliteration. He twisted his sole for good measure.

He admired his work for a second, then took another photo. The ‘after’ shot. He typed a little more, the smile never leaving his face, and then slipped his phone back into his pocket. He stretched, cracked his neck, and went back inside, leaving a tiny, fresh scar on the face of his perfect world. Eleanor felt a surge of pure, cold fury. It wasn't just about ants. It was about the arrogance, the performance of it all. The way he had created a stage simply to enact his own power, documenting his petty conquests like a conquering hero. Agatha was right. His vanity was everything. And that was the key.

That evening, the scent of Earl Grey and righteous indignation filled the small flat. Agatha listened intently as Eleanor recounted the morning’s events, her bird-bright eyes missing no detail. When Eleanor finished, Agatha nodded slowly, her fingers steepled under her chin. The cryptic crossword lay forgotten on the arm of her chair.

“So, he performs his ritual on his own territory,” Agatha mused. “He feels safe there. Untouchable. The emperor in his palace. He’s created a narrative where he is the only character with any power. A story with only one actor is not a story, it’s a monologue.” She picked up her pencil and tapped it against her teeth. “We need to introduce a new character. An antagonist.”

“And who would that be?” Eleanor asked, topping up their mugs. “Me? I go and stand on his driveway and tell him to stop being a git?”

“Don’t be ridiculous, Eleanor. You are the director, not an actor,” Agatha corrected her. “We don’t confront him in person. We confront him in his own chosen arena: the internet. We fight his narrative with a better one. He’s Preston the Ogre, stomping on the little guy. We need a hero. A David for his Goliath.”

An idea, gleeful and mischievous, lit up Agatha’s face. “His blog is called ‘The Crunch’. It’s aggressive. Final. So ours needs to be the opposite. Something about survival. Endurance. The little guy fighting back.” She grabbed a piece of paper and her pencil. “He has ‘Preston’s Pavement Press’. We will have… ‘The Ant Hill Gazette’. No, too quaint. How about… ‘The Colony’s Communiqué’?”

“What are we going to do, Aggie? Write fan fiction about ants?”

“Precisely!” Agatha declared, her eyes sparkling. “But this won’t be fiction to him. We will use his own methods against him. His own photos. He posts a picture of ‘Bartholomew’ before his untimely demise. We take that picture, and we give Bartholomew a different story. Not a pathetic one, but a heroic one.” She was sketching now, her pencil flying across the paper. She drew a tiny ant, but she gave it a minuscule helmet and a defiant posture.

“Preston North writes about liquidating assets,” Agatha said, her voice filled with a general’s strategic zeal. “We will write about heroic last stands. He talks about market disruption; we talk about valiant scouts reporting enemy movements. He gloats about a smear on the pavement; we eulogise a hero who gave his life to warn the colony of the approaching ‘Fleshy Cataclysm’ or ‘The Great Stomp’.”

Eleanor stared at her great aunt, a slow smile spreading across her own face. It was completely mad. It was also perfect. They wouldn’t just be mocking him; they would be stealing his power, subverting his story, turning his stage into a battlefield where he was no longer the sole, triumphant player. He wanted an audience? They would give him one, but they would be cheering for the other side.

“He’s an amateur entomologist, his biography says,” Eleanor added, the plan taking on a life of its own in her mind. “We’ll be experts. We’ll use the correct Latin names. *Formica rufa*. We’ll talk about pheromone trails and nuptial flights. We’ll make him look like what he is: a brutish, ignorant amateur.”

“Exactly!” Agatha cried, slapping the table with her palm. “We undermine his authority. We turn his chronicle of cruelty into a story of resistance. We create a mythos. We give the ants a champion.” She looked at her drawing of the defiant, helmeted ant. “And every champion needs a name. Not Bartholomew or Gary. Something with strength. Something that sounds like a legend.”

She paused, then wrote a name beneath her sketch with a flourish.

“We’ll call him Spartacus.”The next forty-eight hours were a flurry of quiet, subversive activity. The bookmaker's shop below them closed, the clatter of its shutters a starting gun for their operation. Eleanor, guided by Agatha’s surprisingly specific instructions, purchased a domain name: ‘TheColonyCommunique.org’. She chose a simple, stark website template, using a font that mimicked an old typewriter and a colour scheme of black, white, and a deep, earthy red. For the banner, she scanned Agatha’s drawing of the helmeted ant, Spartacus, standing resolute against an unseen foe. The tagline they settled on was simple and potent: ‘The Smallest Voices Have the Loudest Echoes’.

Their first act was an act of theft. Eleanor navigated back to ‘The Crunch’ and took a high-resolution screenshot of the ‘before’ picture from Preston’s latest post—the ant he had documented just that morning. She cropped it carefully and uploaded it to their new site. Then, she stepped aside and let Agatha work. The old woman sat hunched over the laptop, her expression grimly focused, the keys clicking under her delicate fingers like a coded message being tapped out in the dark. Eleanor brewed more tea, watching her great-aunt craft a legend from a smear of insect.

The post went live just before midnight. It was titled, ‘A Hero Falls: The Scout of Yorkstone Rise’. It began with the picture of the ant, now framed not as a victim but as a protagonist.

‘We knew him as Scout 734, of the *Lasius niger* Western Groyne Colony,’ Agatha had written. ‘A veteran of the Great Wasp Incursion of late summer, he was known for his courage and his unparalleled ability to map pheromone trails through treacherous terrain. Yesterday, he undertook his most dangerous mission yet: to chart the movements of the entity known to us only as The Great Stomp, the Pink Colossus who haunts the pale plains of Yorkstone.’

The post went on to describe, in florid but solemn detail, the scout’s final moments. It spoke of his bravery in holding his ground to gather vital data on the Colossus’s patrol patterns. It theorised that his final, frantic movements were not a panicked scuttling, but a deliberate act of communication, a final report scrawled in pheromones on the cold stone for his comrades to find. The post concluded with a chilling flourish.

‘He did not die in vain. His sacrifice has given us a clearer picture of the mindless, lumbering predator in our midst. To The Great Stomp, he was a momentary inconvenience, a satisfying CRUNCH under a ludicrously expensive shoe. To us, he is a martyr. We will remember him not by the name his killer gave him in his chronicle of sadism, but by the name he earned. We will remember him as Spartacus.’

Eleanor read the finished piece over Agatha’s shoulder, a slow, admiring smile on her face. It was perfect. It was dignified, defiant, and laced with just enough scientific jargon to sound authoritative. It painted Preston North not as a corporate titan, but as a clumsy, brutish monster, a force of nature as mindless and destructive as a landslide. They hadn’t just created a counter-narrative; they had built a whole mythology of resistance.

“Now,” Agatha said, leaning back with a sigh, her work complete. “We post the fly in his ointment.”

Eleanor took the laptop. Her heart hammered against her ribs as she navigated back to ‘The Crunch’. Preston’s post, ‘Synergy, Schminergy,’ was still at the top of the page. It had a few fawning comments already. ‘Brilliant metaphor, Preston!’ ‘Love the hard-nosed philosophy!’ ‘You show those freeloaders who’s boss!’ Eleanor scrolled to the bottom of the comments and, using the anonymous handle ‘FormicaDefender’, she typed out the message Agatha had composed.

It was short and deceptively mild. ‘A fascinating, if somewhat anthropocentric, interpretation of events. For those interested in the entomological perspective and the full story of this brave scout’s sacrifice for his colony, a more detailed account can be found here.’ She pasted the link to ‘The Colony Communiqué’. Then, with a deep breath, she clicked ‘Post Comment’. It appeared instantly, a single note of dissent in a chorus of sycophancy.

“And now we wait,” Agatha said, picking up her cryptic crossword as if she had merely been pausing to stir a sauce. “He has an ego to maintain. He checks his comments. He’ll find it.”

Preston North sat in a vast, white leather armchair that looked like a bloated marshmallow. His living room was a cavern of glass and polished concrete, the only colour coming from an enormous, abstract painting that looked like a fire in a paint factory. He held his tablet in one meaty hand, scrolling through the comments on his blog with a smirk. The validation from his faceless admirers was a pleasant, post-massacre digestif. He was a philosopher king, a digital-age Caesar, and this was his adoring senate.

Then he saw it. ‘FormicaDefender’. The name itself was an irritation. He read the comment, his smirk faltering. ‘Entomological perspective’? ‘Brave scout’s sacrifice’? It was mockery, cloaked in academic language. His thick fingers, accustomed to authoring destruction, fumbled for a moment before clicking the link.

The page loaded. Stark. Black and white. And there, staring back at him, was a cartoon ant in a helmet. His eye twitched. He scrolled down and saw his own photograph, stolen from his site. And then he began to read. His round, pink face, which had been slack with self-satisfaction, began to tighten. The colour deepened to a mottled, furious crimson. *Lasius niger*. *The Pink Colossus*. *A ludicrously expensive shoe*.

It was a violation. Someone had come onto his killing floor, his personal stage, and had rewritten his triumphant play from the perspective of the scenery. They had taken his victim and made it a hero. They had taken him, Preston North, a titan of industry, and turned him into a clumsy, lumbering monster with a silly name. The prose was good, too. Annoyingly good. It was eloquent and intelligent, which made it worse. This wasn’t some spotty teenager in a basement. This was a calculated, intellectual assault.

Worst of all, it undermined his power. His entire blog was predicated on the idea that he had the sole power to define reality, to give names, to write stories, to decide who mattered and who was just a smear on the pavement. And this… this ‘Colony Communiqué’ had stolen that power. It had given the ants a voice. A defiant, articulate, and mocking voice.

A low growl rumbled in his chest. He stabbed a finger at the screen, deleting the comment from his blog. But it was a hollow gesture. The comment was gone, but the website was still out there. A rival press. An insurgency. He slammed the tablet down on the glass table beside him, the sharp crack echoing in the sterile silence of the room. The smug glee of his morning’s work had evaporated, replaced by a hot, boiling rage. Someone in Hastings, someone clever, was laughing at him. They were turning his private sport into a public spectacle where he was the villain. He stood up, pacing the vast, empty room. The Ogre had been challenged. And Ogres do not take challenges well. He would find this ‘FormicaDefender’. He would find them, and he would crush them.Preston North did not sleep. Rage was a poor bedfellow, kicking and writhing through the night. He spent hours prowling his sterile mansion, the vast glass walls reflecting his monstrous silhouette back at him, a constant reminder of the insult. ‘The Pink Colossus’. The name echoed in the cavernous silence, each repetition a fresh sting. His first instinct was to use his wealth as a hammer. He called a tech security firm in London, a number he kept for emergencies, and barked at a sleepy analyst to trace the domain, find the owner, get him a name. The answer, delivered a few hours later, was infuriatingly simple. The domain had been registered through a service that provided anonymity, bouncing the details through a series of offshore servers. It was a digital dead end. The cost of digging deeper, the analyst explained, would be astronomical and likely fruitless. Preston, a man used to buying any answer he wanted, slammed the phone down. He had been outmanoeuvred by a ghost.

Defeated in the shadows, he resolved to fight in the light. His light. His stage. His blog. If they wanted to make his victims into heroes, he would make their deaths so pathetic, so meaningless, that no mythology could take root. He stomped out onto his Yorkstone driveway in the harsh morning light, his face a mask of grim purpose. He didn't wait for a lone ant to appear.
