The steam was a living thing in the kitchens of The Gilded Grox. It coiled and writhed, tasting of grease and roasted marrow and the metallic sweat of overworked men. Kael navigated its clammy embrace with the numb precision of a phantom. Platters, heavy as gravestones, rested on his palms. He didn't feel their weight anymore, not in his hands or arms. He felt it in his bones, a deep, settled ache that was as much a part of him as his own marrow.

Outside the kitchen’s swinging doors, the world changed. The air thinned, chilled, and carried the scent of spiced wine and the faint, cold perfume of polished steel. This was the domain of the Matrons, the Shield-Sisters, the Iron Dames of Vorlag. They sat at heavy oaken tables, their battle-plate gleaming in the soft glow of the lumen-globes. Their laughter was not the lilting melody Kael read about in salvaged Old World texts; it was a percussive, booming sound, like rocks tumbling down a mountainside. They were everything the world said they were: loud, formidable, dangerous. The conquerors. The arbiters. The apex. And men like Kael? They were the waiters. The cooks. The menders and the minders. The soft, pliable things that kept the world running in the quiet spaces between battles that never seemed to happen.

He moved between the tables, a slip of a man in a drab grey tunic, his eyes perpetually downcast. To look a Matron in the eye uninvited was to court a dismissal at best, a casual backhand at worst. He’d seen a fellow server, young Tam, get a tooth knocked loose for stumbling and splashing a drop of water on a Captain’s greave. The Captain hadn't even paused her story, a flick of her gauntleted hand, a clank of metal on bone, and then the laughter resumed. Tam had simply picked up his tooth and scurried away, leaking blood and shame. So Kael kept his eyes on the floor, on the plates, on the task. A ghost with a tray.

Tonight, she was here. Commander Elara. Her armor was a shade darker than the others, the colour of a thunderhead, etched with silver filigree that depicted star-charTs and celestial bodies. She didn’t boom or bellow. Her presence was a vacuum, sucking the noise and nervous energy from the room. Other Matrons deferred to her with a subtle shift in posture, a dip of the head. Kael’s designated tables were on the far side of the hall, but he felt her presence like a change in atmospheric pressure. He kept his back to her, polishing glasses that were already clean, his knuckles white.

His thoughts were a frantic, buzzing hive. A single, desperate mantra. *The Seed is the Star. The Flesh is the Way.* He had found the text-fragment six months ago, wedged inside the casing of a discarded nutrient synthesizer he was stripping for parts. Ancient, brittle, written in a dialect so archaic it had taken him weeks of cross-referencing on the public data-slate to even begin to decipher. It spoke of the Ascendants. Of a time before the Matrons, a time when humanity looked to the void not with fear, but with hunger. It spoke of a latent gene, a ‘Star-Seed’, that allowed a human body to withstand the brutal unmaking of void-travel. More than withstand. To become the vessel itself. But the Seed was dormant in most. It could only be activated, absorbed, through a ritual. A consumption. The text was horribly, viscerally specific.

Kael’s hand trembled, rattling a glass against the bar. His step-brother, Rhys, had the gene. Kael knew it. It wasn’t a guess. The faint, almost invisible shimmer of his eyes in certain light, the strange resonance in his laughter, the way he never seemed to get sick. All minor tells, described in the text with chilling accuracy. Rhys was a carrier. Rhys was the key. Rhys was the meal that would buy Kael a new world. The thought was so monstrous it was almost sublime. It was his secret, a burning coal he held cupped in his soul, and some days it was the only thing that kept him warm.

“You. Boy.”

The voice cut through his fevered thoughts like a shard of ice. It was low, clear, and close. Kael froze. He turned slowly, every joint screaming. Commander Elara was standing right behind him. She wasn't looking at him, but at a smear on the glass he’d just polished. He braced for the blow. His heart hammered against his ribs, a frantic bird trapped in a cage of bone. He thought of Tam’s bloody mouth.

“There is a smudge,” she said, her voice impossibly calm. She pointed with a finger sheathed in black metal. The gauntlet was a masterwork of articulated plates, but it looked clumsy and oversized as she indicated the tiny imperfection. “Here.”

Kael snatched the glass, his breath catching in his throat. “Apologies, Commander. At once.” He grabbed a fresh cloth, his movements jerky and inefficient. He could feel her gaze on him, not with contempt, but with a strange, unnerving… curiosity. He scrubbed at the glass, his mind a total blank. The sheer proximity of her, of the legendary, silent killer of the Outer Rim skirmishes, was undoing him.

Then, the impossible happened. A server, a new boy named Pip, clumsy with nerves, was rushing past with a tureen of steaming Gorok stew. He tripped. It was all slow motion. The boy's feet tangled, the tureen arcing through the air, a comet of scalding brown liquid and chunks of meat headed directly for the Commander. Kael flinched, turning his face away, expecting the roar of fury, the sound of the impact, the boy’s scream.

There was a crash as the tureen shattered on the flagstones. But no roar. No scream. Only a sharp, indrawn breath. Kael risked a look. The stew had missed her, mostly, but a wave of hot broth had splashed up, spattering the pristine, star-etched plate of her breastplate and arm. Pip was on the floor, pale as death, babbling apologies into the spreading mess.

Commander Elara did not move. She simply stood there, looking at the splatter on her armor. Then she looked down at Pip, who was trembling so hard his teeth were chattering. Kael watched, his whole being clenched. This was it. The casual, brutal punishment that enforced the world order.

But it didn't come. Elara let out a small sigh. It wasn’t a sigh of fury. It sounded… tired. She flexed her gauntleted fingers, a motion of frustration. “Get up,” she said. Her voice wasn't a command so much as a weary request. “You’ll stain your tunic.”

Pip stared, uncomprehending.

“Get. Up.” She nudged him, very gently, with the toe of her armored boot. Then she knelt. The sound of her war-plate creaking and groaning as she bent her knees was obscene. Matrons did not kneel. Not for anyone. She reached for a piece of the broken tureen, her metal fingers struggling to get a grip on the slick, curved shard of ceramic. It was an absurdly awkward, human gesture. She fumbled it twice before managing to pick it up, her movements hampered by the very armor that was supposed to make her a god of battle.

“Careful,” she murmured, more to herself than to the petrified boy on the floor. “It’s sharp.”

And Kael saw it. In that moment, the entire, colossal lie of his world developed a hairline crack. It was not the act of a warrior goddess. It was the act of a person trying to clean up a mess. The sheer, unadorned humanity of it. The awkwardness. The soft, concerned tone of her voice. It was all wrong. The stories, the fear, the lifetime of ingrained deference—it all flickered, like a faulty lumen-globe. These women… what if they weren’t what they seemed? What if the armor and the booming laughs were a costume? A performance?

The thought was more terrifying than the threat of violence. If they weren’t monsters, then who was he? What did his desperate, horrific plan make him? He felt a wave of vertigo so profound he had to grip the edge of the bar to stay upright. The world was tilting on its axis, and he was losing his balance. He looked at Elara, now directing two other servers to clean the mess with quiet, efficient instructions, and then his mind flashed to Rhys. To his cheerful, oblivious smile. To the pulse fluttering in his throat.

*The Flesh is the Way.*

The crack in his world didn't change the words of the text. It didn't change the science or the ritual. It only changed the context. He wasn't escaping a world of monsters. He was trying to escape a world of lies. And he would have to become a monster to do it.

The walk home was a journey through a different city. The hulking, brutalist architecture, designed to convey strength and intimidate, now seemed like a hollow stage set. The Matrons striding past him on the wide, windswept avenues no longer looked like predators. They just looked… burdened. Their armor, which he’d always seen as an extension of their ferocity, now seemed cumbersome, like a cage they wore around themselves. The clanking of their greaves on the pavement was not the sound of conquest, but the weary shuffling of prisoners. Had he been blind his whole life? Or was he going mad?

His apartment was a cramped box on the seventh floor of a crumbling habitation block. The air was thick with the smell of stale synth-ale and Rhys’s turpentine. He found his step-brother in the main room, dabbing at a huge canvas with a maniacal energy. The painting was a chaotic swirl of garish pinks and violent yellows, an abstract mess that Rhys called ‘Emotional Expressionism’.

“Kael! You’re back!” Rhys grinned, not turning from his work. His smile was wide and artless. A speck of yellow paint was drying on his cheek. “Tough night wrangling the Iron Bitches?”

Kael flinched at the slur. He used to say it himself, under his breath. Now it sounded jarring, ugly, and most of all, inaccurate. “It was a night,” Kael said, his voice flat. He dropped his worn satchel by the door.

“Guess who sold a piece today?” Rhys said, finally turning around. His eyes, in the dim light of the single lumen-strip, had that faint, otherworldly shimmer. The Star-Seed. Glimmering. “Old Man Hemlock. Bought ‘Sunset Agony’ for twenty credits. Twenty! Said it spoke to the deep, primal rage of the subjugated man.” Rhys let out a hoot of laughter. “I was trying to paint a flower. But hey, rage pays the rent, right?”

Kael looked from Rhys’s vibrant, laughing face to the hideous painting. He saw the easy confidence, the careless talent, the utter lack of any real fear. Rhys lived in the same world as Kael, but he didn't *see* it. He was cushioned by a layer of oblivious optimism. He saw the Matrons as caricatures, figures of fun to be mocked from a safe distance. He’d never felt the cold dread of a Captain’s stare. He’d never had to clean up a mess while a living legend stood over him.

“That’s good, Rhys,” Kael said. The words felt like ash in his mouth.

“Good? It’s brilliant! Here.” Rhys wiped his hands on a rag and tossed a small, heavy pouch to Kael. It clinked. “Your half. For supplies and… whatever it is you do. Brood, mostly.”

Kael caught it. Ten credits. A small fortune. Enough for a week’s worth of high-grade nutrient paste. Or enough to buy the final components he needed. The paralytic agent. The ceremonial blade described in the text, forged from a specific alloy that wouldn’t corrupt the… sample.

He looked at his step-brother. The easy generosity. The casual affection. Rhys was a good person. Annoying, privileged, and blind, but good. This was the part the ancient text never mentioned. It detailed the cutting, the cooking, the consuming. It listed the biological markers and the stellar coordinates. But it never mentioned what it felt like to look at the living, breathing key to your salvation and see the face of the only family you had left.

“Thanks,” Kael managed. He clutched the pouch of coins, the metal cool against his sweaty palm.

“Don’t mention it, little brother,” Rhys said, turning back to his canvas. He started humming, a tuneless, cheerful sound.

Kael retreated to his sleeping alcove, a space barely large enough for his mattress pad. He knelt, pried up the loose floorboard, and pulled out the data-slate. He powered it on, the faint glow illuminating his face. The archaic script filled the screen.

*The vessel must be willing. Unknowing, but willing. The final meal must be a gift. A gesture of trust. Only then is the spirit relinquished without taint. The flesh must be rendered by the star-seeker, and consumed before the next rising of the primary moon.*

*A gift.* Kael thought of the bread he sometimes brought home for Rhys from the restaurant kitchens. He thought of the ten credits currently digging into his palm. A gesture of trust. The ritual was a perversion of love. A corruption of brotherhood.

He looked out the grimy portal at the sliver of the primary moon, a sickly crescent in the polluted sky. He looked at the star-charts he’d painstakingly downloaded, the shimmering destination a place called Kepler-186f, a name that tasted like hope and freedom. Then he listened to the sound of Rhys humming in the next room.

He had the money. He had the means. He had the key, humming happily to himself just a few meters away. The crack in his understanding of the world hadn't presented him with a choice. It had only made the one he already had to make that much more unbearable. He wasn't just killing his brother to escape his jailers. He was killing a kind man to escape a prison that might not even be locked. The thought didn't stop him. It just filled him with a cold, silent, and profound horror. He began to make a list.The list was short and brutal. He wrote it not on the data-slate, but on a scrap of flimsi-plast with a worn stylus, the physical act of scratching the letters an anchor in the swirling chaos of his thoughts. *Paralytic, Vespine-derivative. Non-lethal, fast-acting. Source: The Sump.* Below that: *Blade, non-ferrous ceramic. Min. 15cm length. Must be new. Source: Old Town Scrappers, ask for Silas.* Finally: *Catalyst salts. For the rendering. Source: Apothecary, lower levels.* Each item was a nail being driven into Rhys’s coffin. Each source a place Kael had only ever heard of in hushed, fearful whispers. Places where the Matrons’ authority was a distant rumour and the only law was currency and desperation. He folded the list, the plastic crackling in the silence, and tucked it into the lining of his boot. He had the money. He had the knowledge. Now he had a plan.

The next morning, the smell of frying oil and something vaguely sweet filled their small apartment. Rhys was at the heating element, humming again, flipping a nutrient patty in a pan. He’d mixed in some of the precious ground-spices he’d bought with his art money, a decadent waste that was entirely typical of him. The yellow paint was gone from his cheek, but a smudge of pink now adorned his eyebrow. He looked utterly, maddeningly content. He was a man without a cage, living in the very heart of one, and he didn't even know it.

“Morning, you spectre,” Rhys chirped, sliding a golden-brown patty onto a plate. He pushed it across the small counter toward Kael. “Eat up. A gift from the burgeoning art world to its most sullen critic. A little fuel before you go serve the metal madams.”

A gift. The word struck Kael with the force of a physical blow. He stared at the patty, steam rising from it in delicate wisps. A gesture of trust. He felt the edges of the folded list digging into his ankle. His throat closed up, and for a terrifying moment, he thought he was going to be sick. He had to force the food down, each bite a lump of glue and ash in his mouth. Rhys, oblivious, was already turning back to his canvas, contemplating where to add another violent slash of colour. He didn't notice Kael’s pallor, or the way his hand trembled as he set the plate down. He just saw his brother, eating his breakfast.

Kael left without another word, the lie of his morning routine a heavy cloak on his shoulders. Instead of turning toward the monorail station that would take him to The Gilded Grox, he headed down. Down, into the bowels of the city-spire, where the air grew thicker and the light from the upper levels never quite reached. The Sump was not so much a place as a condition. It was the city's neglected foundation, a labyrinth of massive, groaning sewer conduits and forgotten maintenance tunnels. Here, fungus grew in phosphorescent patches on the walls, providing the only light, and the currency was barter, secrets, and the occasional blood debt.

He found the dealer operating out of a rusted-out water filtration node. The man was wizened and small, with eyes that were cloudy and pale, like fish left too long in a stagnant pool. He didn’t give a name. Kael simply showed him the word on his list. *Vespine-derivative.* The man grunted, his gaze flickering over Kael's drab tunic, his clean hands. He saw a lamb strayed far from the flock.

"For pests?" the dealer rasped, a ghost of a smile playing on his lips. "Or a lover?"

"Pests," Kael said, his voice barely a whisper.

"Big ones, I'd wager." The man shuffled into the shadows of his den and returned with a small, sealed vial containing a clear, viscous fluid. "Two full droppers in the bloodstream, they'll be stiff as a winter corpse in five breaths. Motion returns in three hours. No trace. No pain, they say. Twenty credits."

It was double what it was worth. It was the price for being an outsider. Kael counted out the coins from Rhys’s pouch, his fingers fumbling. The dealer snatched them and dropped the vial into Kael's hand. The glass was cold. It felt heavier than a gravestone. He didn’t ask what Kael was using it for. In The Sump, questions were a luxury no one could afford and a danger no one would risk.

Old Town was next. It was a sprawling market of scavengers and tinkerers built in the shadow of a colossal, ruined pre-Matron era factory. Here, the past was sold off piece by piece. Kael found Silas’s stall, a chaotic mountain of scrap metal, salvaged wiring, and cracked ceramics. Silas was a brute of a man with arms as thick as Kael's waist, his face a mask of grease and burn scars. He specialized in blades. Kael showed him the second line on his list. Silas grunted, his eyes showing a flicker of professional interest.

“Non-ferrous ceramic. Specific,” Silas rumbled. “Not for the kitchen, eh? Can’t be magnetized. Can't be detected by a standard sweep-field. This is quiet work.” He looked Kael up and down. “You don’t look like quiet work. You look like you get others to do the work.”

“It’s for a project,” Kael lied, his heart thumping. “A scientific… display.”

Silas let out a short, barking laugh. “Right. A ‘display’.” He reached under his counter and produced a long, thin box. He opened it. Inside, resting on a bed of worn velvet, was a knife. The blade was matte black, shaped like a shard of obsidian, with an edge that seemed to drink the light. It was utterly devoid of ornament, a thing of pure, brutal function. "Spun-crystal composite. Sharper than steel, holds an edge 'til the sun burns out. Can shave a sleeping man's whiskers without waking him." He named a price that took almost all of what Kael had left. Kael paid it. As he took the box, he felt a strange sense of detachment, as if he were watching someone else’s hands perform these actions. The man in the reflection of Silas’s goggles was a stranger, with hard eyes and a grim set to his jaw.

He bought the catalyst salts from a blind apothecary whose shop smelled of dried herbs and chemical decay. She asked no questions at all, simply measuring the crystalline powder onto a scale with unnerving precision, her head cocked as if listening to the weight. With his pockets empty and his soul heavy with the tools of his salvation, Kael began the long journey back up. Up toward the light. Up toward Rhys. Each step was heavier than the last, the box with the blade in his satchel a cold, hard rectangle against his back, the vial of poison a secret fire in his pocket. He was no longer a phantom with a tray. He was a hunter. And he was heading home to the slaughter.When Kael pushed through the door to his apartment, the change in atmosphere was jarring. He had ascended from the city's fetid depths, through the weary, clanking theatre of the main avenues, and into this small pocket of chaotic life. Rhys had opened the grimy portal, a rare occurrence, and a cross-breeze was cutting through the perpetual stuffiness, carrying the scent of turpentine and the distant, metallic tang of the spire itself. The hideous pink and yellow canvas was finished, propped against a wall to dry. In its place on the easel was a new, smaller canvas, stark white. Rhys was sitting on a stool before it, not painting, but sketching something with a charcoal stick.

“There you are,” Rhys said, without turning. “You missed the grand finale. I’ve decided to call it ‘Post-Prandial Rage’. Has a nice ring to it, don’t you think? Sounds smarter than ‘Flower I Messed Up’.” He turned, and his usual artless grin was softer, more thoughtful. He looked Kael up and down. “Gods, you look like you swam home through The Sump. Rough one?”

“You could say that.” Kael’s voice was a dry rasp. He unslung his satchel, the weight of it a damning accusation. The ceramic blade in its box felt like a slab of dead matter against his back. He needed to hide it. He needed to get it out of sight before its presence became a physical thing in the room, a third entity between him and his brother. He moved towards his sleeping alcove, his steps stiff.

“Hey, before you crawl into your misery hole,” Rhys called out, his voice stopping Kael in his tracks. “I had an idea. For my next piece.” He gestured with the charcoal stick at the blank canvas. “I’m going to paint you.”

Kael froze, his back to Rhys. “Me?”

“Yeah. Not you the surly waiter. You the way I see you. All shadows and sharp angles, but with this… I don’t know. This light inside you that you’re always trying to snuff out. I think it could be my masterpiece. ‘Portrait of a Brother on the Brink’.” Rhys laughed, a soft, warm sound this time, not his usual boisterous hoot. “It’ll make us famous. You’ll see.”

Kael said nothing. He could feel Rhys’s gaze on his back, not the unnerving, curious stare of Commander Elara, but a look of genuine artistic assessment, of affection. It was a thousand times worse. He felt like a specimen under glass, his monstrous intentions laid bare for his brother to unknowingly paint. He ducked into his alcove, his hands shaking so badly he could barely work the loose floorboard. He placed the box and the small, wrapped packets of salt into the dark space. He kept the vial of paralytic in his pocket. It was a cold, smooth comfort against his thigh.

When he emerged, Rhys was in their tiny food-prep area, rinsing two glasses. The primary moon was higher now, a sharp, silver blade in the narrow slice of sky visible through the portal. Time was running out. The text had been clear. *Before the next rising.* This was his only window, this night. The next chance might not come for a month, a year, a lifetime. The thought of another day, another week, another year in the clammy embrace of The Gilded Grox was an impossibility.

“Let’s celebrate,” Rhys said, his back to Kael. “Celebration for ‘Post-Prandial Rage’, and for the twenty credits that are already mostly gone, and for my new project.” He pulled a bottle of dark liquid from a high shelf. It was cheap, fermented grain-spirit, harsh and potent. They saved it for rare occasions. “A toast is in order.”

Kael’s heart hammered. A toast. A drink. *A gift. A gesture of trust.* The words from the text-fragment echoed in his mind, a holy scripture for a profane act. It was too perfect. It was a sign. The universe was not just allowing this; it was conspiring with him, laying the path for him. The crack in his world, the doubt sown by Elara’s tired humanity, sealed over with the tar of grim purpose.

“I’ll pour,” Kael said, his voice coming out surprisingly steady.

Rhys turned, raising an eyebrow. “You? The brooding ghost is playing host? What’s the occasion, really?”

“You said it yourself. A celebration.” Kael took the bottle and the two glasses from his brother. His fingers brushed against Rhys’s, and his brother’s skin was warm, alive. Kael’s own hands were ice-cold. He turned his back, shielding the glasses with his body. His movements were precise, economical. He poured a measure of the spirit into each glass. Then, his right hand dipped into his pocket. The vial was smooth. Uncorking it with his thumb was silent. He tilted it over one of the glasses. One drop. A second drop. The clear, viscous fluid merged with the dark spirit, disappearing without a trace. He palmed the vial, sliding it back into his pocket. It was done.

“You know,” Rhys said conversationally from behind him, “when I’m properly famous, and Old Man Hemlock is begging me for a commission, the first thing I’m buying is a new place. Somewhere high up in the spire. A real studio, with a whole wall made of glass. You could watch the sky-traffic. You wouldn’t have to work in that kitchen anymore. You could just… I don’t know. Read your old texts. Be yourself.”

Kael’s entire body went rigid. He closed his eyes. Rhys was painting a future, a beautiful, impossible future, using Kael as his subject. Every kind word, every hopeful plan, was another twist of the ceremonial blade that Kael now owned. He was killing not just the man, but the dream the man held for both of them. It had to be done. The Star-Seed was the only thing that was real. The freedom it promised was the only truth.

He turned, holding a glass in each hand. He was careful to keep his expression neutral, a blank mask. He extended one of the glasses—the one—to Rhys. “To your new project,” Kael said. The words tasted like poison on his own tongue.

Rhys’s face broke into that familiar, brilliant grin. He took the glass, his eyes shimmering in the dim light. The tell. The sign. The gene. It was right there, looking at him with absolute trust. He raised his glass.

“Not just to the project,” Rhys said, his voice full of a warmth that Kael would never feel again. “To us, little brother. To Pink Dawns and new beginnings.”

He clinked his glass against Kael’s. Then, he tilted his head back and drank.Kael watched, his own glass untouched, his knuckles white around the cold surface. He watched the muscles in Rhys’s throat work as he swallowed. For a second, nothing happened. Rhys lowered his glass, his smile still lingering, a ghost of its recent brilliance. Then he blinked, a slow, deliberate motion, like a faulty camera shutter. He frowned, a slight furrow in his brow.

“That’s… strange,” Rhys murmured, his gaze becoming unfocused. He looked at the glass in his hand as if he’d never seen it before. “My fingers are tingling.” He tried to set the glass down on the counter, but his hand spasmed, his grip loosening abruptly. The glass dropped, not with a loud crash, but with a dull, thick clatter, shattering on the worn floor tiles. The dregs of the spirit pooled like dark blood. Rhys stared at his own hand, a look of profound, childlike confusion on his face. “I can’t… Kael?”

His voice was slurring, the word blurring at the edges. He tried to push himself up from the stool, but his legs wouldn’t obey. He swayed, his balance gone, a puppet with its strings suddenly cut. Kael moved then, a single, fluid motion born of months of frantic imagining. He caught Rhys before he could fall, his arms wrapping around his brother’s chest. Rhys’s body was a dead weight, but a warm one, still thrumming with the last vestiges of life. Rhys’s head lolled back onto Kael’s shoulder. His eyes, wide and shimmering, found Kael’s. There was no accusation in them. Only a vast, terrifying question, a silent plea for an explanation that would never come. Then the light in them dimmed, not extinguishing, but retreating, like a star falling into the deep black of space. His body went completely rigid. The dealer had been telling the truth.

The silence that followed was absolute. The distant hum of the city, the whisper of the breeze through the portal, it all fell away. There was only the weight of his brother in his arms and the frantic, silent scream in Kael’s own head. He stood there for a long moment, locked in a grotesque embrace. Rhys’s last breath ghosted across his cheek, smelling faintly of the cheap spirit. The gift had been given. The trust had been accepted. Now, the ritual had to be completed. With a strength he didn’t know he possessed, Kael half-lifted, half-dragged his brother’s stiffened form into the center of the small room, away from the shards of glass. He laid him gently on the floor, arranging his limbs so that he looked peaceful, as if he were merely sleeping. It was a lie, but it was a kindness Kael could, strangely, still afford.

His movements became automatic, each step dictated by the archaic script on the data-slate. He retrieved the tools from beneath the floorboard in his alcove. The long, thin box. The small, folded flimsi-plast packets of catalyst salts. He knelt beside Rhys, unwrapping a clean rag he used for polishing glassware and laid the items out on it. The black ceramic blade seemed to suck the light from the room, a sliver of pure void. The salts were like crushed diamonds, shimmering faintly. He was no longer Kael the waiter. He was a surgeon performing a cosmic operation. A priest enacting a dark sacrament. He had to separate the man from the monster he was about to become.

Before he picked up the blade, he looked at his brother’s face one last time. The faint, optimistic smile was gone, replaced by a placid neutrality. The smudge of pink paint was still on his eyebrow. He was a piece of art that would never be finished. Kael reached out, his hand trembling, and gently brushed the paint away with his thumb. The skin was still warm. *The vessel must be willing. Unknowing, but willing.* Rhys had toasted to a new beginning. He had offered it as a gift. Kael clung to that thought, twisting it, shaping it into a justification he could bear. This was a new beginning. Just not for both of them. This was the ultimate act of trust: Rhys was giving Kael the stars, and he didn't even know it. The perversity of the thought made Kael’s stomach clench, but it solidified his resolve.

He took the black blade from its velvet bed. It was lighter than he expected, and the handle felt unnaturally cold against his skin, a conduit from the frozen void it promised. He remembered the anatomical charts from the text, the precise location. He unfastened the top of Rhys’s tunic, pulling the rough fabric aside to expose the pale, smooth skin of his chest. Kael could see the faint, steady flutter of the Star-Seed just beneath the surface, a rhythmic, subliminal pulse of light that only he could see, a beacon calling him home. He took a deep, shuddering breath, the air thick with the smell of turpentine and betrayal. He positioned the tip of the blade over his brother’s heart. For an instant, his reflection swam on the matte black surface—a pale, haunted face, a stranger’s eyes. Then he pressed down. The spun-crystal composite edge was impossibly sharp. It parted the skin with no resistance, a clean, bloodless line opening on his brother's chest. The flesh was the way. And the way was now open.There was no turning back. The first cut was a Rubicon crossed, and Kael was now an invader in the land of his brother’s flesh. He worked with a chilling, dispassionate focus, his mind a fortress built of ancient words and desperate hope. He followed the instructions from the data-slate, which had seared themselves into his memory. The text was not a recipe for murder, but a guide to transference. He had to believe that. It was like swallowing a piece of the night sky, filled with the faint, metallic tang of distant constellations and the profound emptiness of the void. For a moment, it was just a strange sensation in his throat. Then it hit his stomach, and his world dissolved into pure, agonizing fire.

It was not a fire of heat, but of transformation, a cellular conflagration that raced through his veins like a shockwave. He collapsed, his body arching in a silent scream, the ceramic bowl shattering beside Rhys's still form. Every nerve ending lit up with excruciating data. He could feel the latticework of his own DNA being unwritten and re-inscribed by a new, stellar code. His bones felt like they were simultaneously freezing and vibrating at an impossible frequency. He pushed himself up, his limbs aching but obedient. He felt… different. The deep, settled ache he had carried in his bones his entire life was gone, replaced by a strange, thrumming vitality. They were the same hands, calloused from work, but they seemed unfamiliar, the tools of a stranger. He stood and saw Rhys. His brother lay exactly as he had left him, a piece of discarded humanity, the empty vessel. The ritualistic trance was shattered, and the cold, stark reality crashed down on Kael with the force of a physical blow. He had done it. He had murdered his brother. The horror and the elation were two sides of the same coin, and it was spinning, impossibly, in the ruin of his heart.The first task was a practical one, an anchor of grim necessity in a roiling sea of the impossible. The Star-Seed hadn’t erased his humanity; it had simply compartmentalized it. With a strange, detached reverence, he rolled his brother’s stiffened form onto the canvas of ‘Post-Prandial Rage’. The violent, joyful slashes of yellow and pink became a bizarre funeral shroud. The canvas was rough and unwieldy, but Kael’s body moved with an unfamiliar grace and power. He lifted his brother, and the weight that should have been a struggle was merely an inconvenience. Rhys, who had been so full of vibrant life, was now just mass and volume, a problem of logistics.

He waited, his brother a grotesque parody of a rolled-up rug in his arms, his own heartbeat a slow, steady drum, no longer the frantic bird-panic of the past. The final, irreversible act of severing. He looked at the garish package that had been his brother, the only person who had ever shown him uncomplicated kindness, and felt the stirrings of the old Kael, a desire to weep, to scream, to take it all back. But the Star-Seed held him fast. It was a cage of cold logic around his heart. Words were useless. Apologies were insults. He had already made his final statement with the blade and the bowl. Returning to the apartment was like stepping into a tomb. The silence was no longer peaceful; it was absolute. It was the sound of a vacuum. He began to clean. He worked with the numb precision he had once reserved for the kitchens of The Gilded Grox, but now it was driven by a powerful, internal engine. He swept up the shards of the shattered glass and the broken ceramic bowl, his new senses able to detect every last glittering speck in the dim light. He scrubbed the floor where Rhys had lain, erasing the faint outline of his presence. He found a half-finished sketch on the easel, a charcoal outline of his own face, captured with a startling, affectionate accuracy. He looked at the confident lines, the nascent portrait of a brother Rhys thought he knew, and his control almost broke. He took the finished canvas, ‘Sunset Agony’, from the wall. He would leave the bag in a public locker. He would sell the painting in the lower-market. An artist who sold a piece, took his earnings and supplies, and vanished seeking inspiration—it was a story that told itself. He was building a lie, brick by painful brick.

When the apartment was sterile, stripped of any sign of the vibrant life that had filled it only hours ago, he went back to his alcove and retrieved the data-slate. The familiar archaic script glowed on the screen, but now the words felt different. They were no longer a theoretical text; they were his own biology. *The vessel must navigate the unseen currents, the stellar streams that flow between worlds. The flesh is the sail, the will is the rudder.* It was still poetry, still frustratingly vague. He had become the ship, but he had no idea how to leave the harbour. The pull towards Kepler-186f was a constant, a low-level hum in his blood, but it was a direction, not a map. How did he bridge the impossible distance? Did he just walk out and leap into the sky? The absurdity of the thought was a flash of the old Kael’s frantic mind, but the new Kael simply filed it away as an unlikely hypothesis. He needed more information. And he suspected, with a cold, sinking feeling, that the rest of the text was not in the fragments he possessed.

He needed to maintain his routine. To disappear now would be to connect his own vanishing with Rhys’s. He had to go to work. The thought of returning to The Gilded Grox, to the steam and the noise and the weight of the platters, was revolting. It was a life that belonged to another person, a ghost he had shed. But he put on the drab grey tunic, the familiar roughness of the fabric a bizarre comfort. He walked out of the apartment, locking the door behind him on a life that was now completely over, and descended into the city. The journey was a sensory assault. He could smell the individual sweat of every person in the monorail car, the faint trace of their morning nutrient paste, the ozone from the train’s electric rails. The cacophony of the station was a symphony of distinct sounds he could isolate and identify. The world was screaming information at him, and he was absorbing all of it.

He pushed through the swinging doors into the kitchen’s clammy embrace. The steam coiled around him, but it no longer felt like a living thing. He could dissect its components: water vapour, atomized grease from the Gorok roast, aerosolized saline from the sweat of the overworked cooks. The platters, when he lifted them, felt as light as flimsi-plast. The ache in his bones was a distant memory. He moved through his tasks with an efficiency that was inhuman, his motions silent and precise. His fellow servers gave him a wide berth, unnerved by his new, silent intensity. He was a ghost with a tray, but a different kind of ghost now. Not one that was ignored, but one that was feared.

That evening, the main hall was full. The booming laughter of the Matrons, which had once sounded like a rockslide, now seemed hollow, a forced performance of strength. He saw them not as conquerors, but as prisoners inside their own gleaming war-plate. Kael moved between the tables, his eyes downcast not from fear, but from habit. Then he felt a gaze on him, and he knew it was hers. He risked a look. She was looking at an imperfection, something that didn’t belong, something that had changed. In that moment, he knew that she, in her own way, could see the Star-Seed in him too.The moment their eyes met, the chaotic noise of the hall collapsed into a single, focused point: her. He smelled the faintest trace of ozone clinging to her plate, a lingering scent of charged atmosphere from a weapon’s discharge or a ship’s corridor far from this world. She was not a goddess or a monster. She was a piece of military hardware, and a tired woman was piloting it.

She didn't beckon him with a flick of a gauntlet. She didn't call out. Then she looked away, returning to her untouched glass of spiced wine, the interview concluded. Kael’s heart, a slow and steady drum, did not quicken. The fear was there, a cold pocket of rational concern in his gut, but the panic was gone. He knew what would happen next.

Now.” The Captain turned and walked away before he could form a reply. There was no choice offered, no room for acknowledgement. It was an execution of an order.

The other servers saw. They scurried away from him, their faces pale, their eyes wide with a mixture of pity and fear. To be singled out by Commander Elara was a death sentence of one kind or another. Kael placed his tray of dirty plates on a stand, the clatter of ceramic unnaturally loud in the sudden pocket of silence around him. He smoothed his tunic, a pointless, reflexive gesture of a man he no longer was, and began the walk across the hall. Every step was a journey across a hostile landscape. He could feel the dozens of eyes on his back. He could hear the whispers starting up behind him, the speculation, the morbid curiosity. He walked past the main kitchens, past the swinging doors he had entered and exited a thousand times, and followed the Captain down a corridor he had never been permitted to use. The air here was different, scrubbed clean, the stone floors polished to a mirror sheen. The lumen-globes glowed with a whiter, colder light. This was the heart of the Matrons’ power, a place men like him were never meant to see.

He spent the long, unblinking hours exploring the prison of his new body. He was a machine to be calibrated, a weapon to be understood. He pulled out the data-slate, the screen’s glow stark in the gloom.
