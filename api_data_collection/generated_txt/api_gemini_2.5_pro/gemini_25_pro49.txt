The heat in Irving didn’t just sit on you; it crawled. It was a physical presence, a million tiny, unseen legs that worked their way under your collar and down your spine, leaving a slick trail of sweat. Leo knew this feeling intimately. He’d spent all sixteen of his years marinating in it, first in a series of sterile foster homes that all smelled of bleach and regret, and now here, at the Haven for Boys, a place whose name was a cruel joke whispered by the universe. The Haven was a long, low brick building that looked like a forgotten elementary school, squatting on a patch of sun-scorched earth where the grass had long ago surrendered.

Life here was a study in beige. Beige walls, beige linoleum floors, beige personalities worn by the staff who drifted through their shifts with the vacant-eyed patience of long-haul truckers. For Leo, the world outside the Haven’s smudged windows was a blur of concrete and strip malls, the constant, whining roar of jets descending into DFW a kind of permanent, droning tinnitus. He was an orphan, a word that felt both too dramatic and not nearly descriptive enough. It didn’t capture the hollowed-out feeling in his chest, the sense of being a ghost haunting someone else’s life. He moved through the crowded hallways and the noisy cafeteria with a practiced invisibility, a skill honed over years of wanting nothing more than to not be noticed. Being noticed led to questions, to expectations, to the possibility of further disappointment. It was safer to be a shadow.

The change, when it came, didn't arrive like a thunderclap. It was quieter, more insidious. It started in the kitchen, late on a Tuesday night. The house was finally still, the cacophony of adolescent boys replaced by the hum of the ancient refrigerator and the ticking of a clock on the wall. Leo had crept down for a glass of water, his bare feet silent on the cool, slightly sticky floor. He was filling his glass at the sink when a flicker of movement by the baseboard caught his eye.

It was a cockroach. Not one of the small, skittering German ones, but a full-blown American roach, a monster of glossy, mahogany armor nearly two inches long. Its antennae twitched, tasting the air. Leo froze, his hand tightening on the glass. He’d seen them before, of course. They were a fact of life in Texas, as common as bluebonnets and pickup trucks. Annoying. Gross. But this was different. The silence of the house amplified its presence. In the dim glow from the stove light, it seemed malevolent, an ancient, alien thing that had scuttled out of the planet’s dark, secret places.

He watched, paralyzed, as it made its move. But it didn’t flee from the light. It didn’t scurry for the safety of the shadows. It ran toward him. With a horrifying, unnatural speed, it charged across the linoleum, a tiny, armored nightmare. Leo let out a strangled gasp and stumbled back, but his reactions were syrupy, slow. The thing was on his foot. A jolt, electric and vile, shot up his leg. He could feel it—or imagined he could feel it—the prickle of its spiny legs, the greasy touch of its body against his skin. A scream tore from his throat, thin and ragged. He kicked out wildly, a frantic, spastic dance of pure revulsion. His foot connected with the wall, and the roach was gone. He didn’t know if he’d flung it or if it had simply vanished. He scrambled backwards, his back hitting the refrigerator with a loud thud, his eyes darting wildly around the empty floor.

He didn't find it. He spent a full minute scanning the baseboards, his heart hammering against his ribs like a trapped bird. Nothing. He backed out of the kitchen, his skin crawling with a phantom itch. In the bathroom, he scrubbed his foot under scalding water, scrubbing until the skin was raw and red, but he couldn't wash the feeling away. The memory of those tiny, gripping legs was branded onto him. That night, he didn’t sleep. He lay stiffly in his narrow bed, listening to the snores of his roommate, Marcus, convinced that with every creak of the old house, a legion of them was emerging from the walls, their antennae twitching, searching for him.

The next day, the world had changed. Every shadow held a potential horror. A piece of lint on the carpet made him jump. The buzz of a housefly was a thunderous roar that sent a spike of adrenaline through him. He saw them everywhere. A dark spot on the sidewalk. The way a dead leaf curled. His food tasted like dust and decay. He couldn't focus in class, his eyes constantly scanning the corners of the room, the space under his desk.

"You're jumpy," Marcus grunted at him during lunch, flicking a pea in his direction. Leo flinched so hard he nearly fell off the bench. A few of the other boys snickered.
"Leave him alone," a voice said.
Leo looked around. No one had spoken. The boys were still laughing. Marcus was stuffing his face with tater tots.
*He’s a troglodyte, Leo. Don’t even give him the satisfaction.*
The voice was in his head. But it wasn't his own thought. His own thoughts were a frantic, screaming mess. This was a different voice. It was calm. Level. Familiar, like a memory of a friend he’d once had.
Leo blinked, shaking his head slightly. The stress was making him crazy.
*No, you’re not crazy. You’re just scared. It’s okay to be scared.*
Leo pressed the heels of his hands into his eyes. This was new. This was weird.
*My name is Finn,* the voice said, patient and clear. *We’ve known each other forever. You just forgot for a little bit.*

His fear didn't subside. It metastasized. A week later, he refused to go into the locker room for P.E. because he’d seen a silverfish disappear into a crack near the showers. The coach, a perpetually annoyed man named Mr. Davison, cornered him.
"What's the problem, Leo?"
"I can't go in there."
"Why not?"
Leo couldn't say it. He couldn't admit that he was terrified of a thing smaller than his fingernail. The shame was a hot lump in his throat. "I just... can't."
Mr. Davison’s jaw tightened. "That's not an answer. You're failing my class. Get in there and change, or you can spend the period running laps in the sun."
So he ran. He ran until his lungs burned and his vision swam, the Texas sun beating down on him without mercy.
*He doesn't get it,* Finn’s voice murmured as Leo gasped for air, his hands on his knees. *They don’t understand. But I do. We’ll figure this out.*
"How?" Leo whispered, his voice hoarse.
*We make a plan. We take control back. They can’t hurt you if we’re prepared.*

The lack of support was a wall he kept running into. He tried to explain his terror to Mrs. Gable, the primary house mother at the Haven. She was a large woman with a kind face and tired eyes, perpetually clutching a mug of lukewarm coffee.
"Leo, honey," she said, patting his hand with a sigh. "I know you had a fright. But it was just a bug. This is Texas. You can’t let a little roach shut you down."
"It's not little," he said, his voice trembling. "And it's not just the one. It's all of them. I feel them... everywhere."
She gave him a sad, pitying look that was worse than any lecture. "You're a sensitive boy. Always have been. Maybe you're just feeling a bit of anxiety. It's a lot, living here. Why don't you try to think about something else?"

She was dismissing him. Just like the coach. Just like the other kids. They saw his fear as a weakness, a character flaw. A thing to be overcome with a bit of grit and positive thinking. They didn't see the monster in the dark. They didn’t feel the phantom crawling on their skin. Only Finn did. Finn never dismissed him. Finn took it seriously.
*Okay,* Finn said that night, as Leo meticulously checked every inch of his bed, pulling the sheets back, shaking the pillow, peering under the frame with the dim light from his phone. *New rule. We do a full sweep every night. No exceptions. We check our shoes before we put them on. We don’t leave clothes on the floor. We control the environment.*
"We?" Leo whispered into the dark.
*Yeah, we. You and me. We're a team.*
The idea was so comforting it almost made him cry. A team. He wasn't alone in his own head anymore. He had backup.
The phobia became their shared project. It gave structure to his days, which were otherwise a long, empty stretch of time to be endured. The rituals grew more complex. Tapping a doorframe before entering a room. Avoiding cracks in the pavement. Sitting only in metal or plastic chairs, never upholstered ones where things could hide. The other boys at the Haven started calling him "Twitch," but the teasing barely registered. It was outside noise. The real battle, the real conversation, was happening inside. Leo's world had shrunk to the size of his terror, but within that small, besieged space, he was no longer alone. He had Finn. And Finn, it seemed, had a plan.The rituals were his armor. Mornings became a military operation conducted in the pre-dawn gray, a time when the shadows were soft and the world was still. *Okay, check the perimeter,* Finn’s voice would prompt, a calm, steady command in the chaos of his mind. Leo would slide from his bed and begin the sweep. With the thin beam of his phone’s flashlight, he’d trace every seam of his mattress, every joint in the metal bedframe. He’d grab his jeans from the back of the chair—never the floor, rule number one—and shake them out with a violence that felt both ridiculous and necessary. Then, the shoes. He’d turn them upside down and bang them hard against the linoleum, ignoring the inevitable groan from Marcus’s bunk, before meticulously inspecting the insides. Only then was it safe. The walk to the bathroom was a tiptoed navigation of a minefield, his eyes locked on the floor, mapping every crack and stain, a soldier moving through hostile territory.

The cafeteria was the heart of the battleground, an ecosystem of filth that gnawed at his composure. Spilled milk, dropped fries, the sticky residue of syrup on the tables—it was an open invitation to the enemy. He learned to sit at the end of a table, his back to a wall, giving himself a clear line of sight and a quick escape route. One morning, a younger boy named Kevin tripped near him, sending a bowl of oatmeal splattering across the floor. Leo didn't even think. He sprang up onto the bench, his body coiling tight, a low whine of terror escaping his lips. The oatmeal was just a slurry of beige, but in his mind's eye, it was a writhing mass, a nest disturbed. Laughter erupted around him, loud and sharp. Even Marcus, who usually treated him like a piece of furniture, howled with glee. A staff monitor, a new guy with a clipboard and a permanently unimpressed expression, stalked over. "Get off the table, Leo. Now." His voice was flat, bored, but held an edge of steel. Leo couldn't move. He was frozen, staring at the spill, his breath coming in ragged, shallow pants.

The monitor had to physically lift him down from the bench, his grip bruisingly firm on Leo's arm. The humiliation was a physical burn, hotter than the Texas sun. He was sent back to his room, his breakfast untouched, the laughter from the cafeteria still echoing in his ears. He sat on the edge of his now-certified-safe bed, shaking. *Ignore them,* Finn said. The voice was closer now, more present than ever. It wasn't just a thought; it felt like it was resonating in the bones of his skull. *Their laughter is just noise. It doesn't mean anything. It’s the sound of the flock.* "They think I'm crazy," Leo whispered to the empty room. *No. They’re blind. That’s not the same thing.* There was a pause, a moment of profound silence in his head, and then the voice added, softer, more conspiratorial, *This fear, Leo. It's not a bug, it's a feature. It's an alarm system. You’re finally noticing the things that have been in the corners all along. The things they can't see.*

The incident in the cafeteria triggered a formal response. A summons. Not to Mrs. Gable's cluttered, coffee-scented office, but to the sterile, intimidating room belonging to Mr. Henderson, the Haven’s director. Henderson was a man who seemed assembled from sharp angles—pointed nose, sharp chin, a suit jacket with shoulders that looked like weapons. He sat behind a vast, empty expanse of a mahogany desk, his fingers steepled. He didn't offer platitudes or pats on the hand. He looked at Leo with an appraiser's cold gaze, as if measuring him for a box. "Your file is… complicated, Leo," he began, his voice dry as dead leaves. "And your recent behavior is becoming a liability. The oatmeal incident was the last straw for some of the staff. We need to find a more… specialized placement for you." He slid a pamphlet across the polished wood. The title on the front, in serene blue letters, read: "Westlake Academy: A Structured Environment for Troubled Youth." Leo stared at it. A new place. New walls, new floors, new cracks and shadows. A whole new, unknown territory to scout and secure. The thought was so overwhelming it made his vision tunnel.

*A new variable,* Finn stated, his tone unreadable. There was no fear in his voice, only a cool, calculating assessment. *A change of scenery.* Leo’s hands, resting in his lap, were trembling so badly he had to clench them into fists to make them stop. A new school. A new bed. New hallways, new bathrooms, new kitchens. A million dark, secret places he hadn't mapped, hadn't secured. It felt less like a new opportunity and more like a death sentence. "I can't," he whispered, so quietly Henderson didn't hear him. *Yes, you can,* Finn countered, his voice a sharp, clear certainty in the roaring static of Leo’s panic. *They call it a structured environment. That’s good. Structure is good. We can work with structure. This isn't a problem, Leo. It's the next level.* Mr. Henderson cleared his throat, pulling Leo’s attention back to the silent, air-conditioned room. "Westlake has a very good record," the director continued, oblivious. "They have specialists. They can help you with this… anxiety." Leo looked from the man's emotionless face to the glossy pamphlet. Help. They didn't want to help him. They wanted to fix him, to sand down his sharp edges of fear until he was beige and smooth like everyone else. They wanted to take away his alarm system. They wanted to make him blind again.Leo’s mind was a siren, a single, high-pitched wail of pure negation. *No. Not a new place. Not a new map.* The thought of it was a physical weight, pressing the air from his lungs. All his work, all the meticulous, exhausting effort of turning his small corner of the Haven into a fortress, would be for nothing. He would be cast out into the wilderness again, where every floorboard was a potential ambush, every dark corner a nest. He felt a desperate urge to explain, to make Henderson understand that this wasn't just "anxiety." It was a war, and he was a soldier who couldn't afford to be redeployed to an un-scouted front. But the words caught in his throat, a tangled mess of shame and fear. He knew how he sounded. He knew the look he would get. It would be the same pitying glance Mrs. Gable had given him, magnified by Henderson’s clinical detachment.

*Stop,* Finn’s voice cut through the panic, not with a shout, but with the cool precision of a scalpel. *Breathe. Analyze the intelligence. He said ‘specialized.’ He said ‘structured.’ He is handing us a blueprint, Leo. The Haven is compromised. The staff is hostile, the other boys are a liability. We’ve outgrown it. Westlake is a promotion. They think they’re sending you to a hospital. We know they’re sending us to a better-equipped base of operations.* The logic was so cold, so alien to the hot terror searing his nerves, that it shocked him into a moment of clarity. A promotion. A better-equipped base. Finn didn’t see a threat; he saw an upgrade. The siren in his head subsided to a low hum. He picked up the glossy pamphlet. The photo on the front showed a modern building of glass and pale stone, surrounded by manicured lawns and neat, orderly trees. It looked nothing like the crumbling, defeated brick of the Haven. It looked clean. Too clean.

He was dismissed with a crisp nod. The walk back to his room felt dreamlike, the familiar, grimy hallway of the Haven already seeming like a place he no longer belonged to. He passed other boys, their shouts and shoves a language from a world he was about to leave. When he got back to his room, Marcus was lying on his bunk, scrolling through his phone. He didn’t look up. The news traveled the way it always did in a place like the Haven, a current of whispers that flowed under doors and through the vents. Later that afternoon, Marcus grunted from his bed, "Heard you're getting shipped out to the loony bin." He said it without malice, a simple statement of fact, but the words still stung. Leo didn’t answer. He was already beginning the process of decommissioning his life.

*Stage one: strategic withdrawal,* Finn dictated. The voice was a constant companion now, the director of his every action. *We will take nothing that isn't essential. We will sanitize every item before it goes into the bag.* Packing became the most intense ritual yet. He laid a clean sheet on the floor—a sterile field. Each t-shirt was shaken out, inspected along its seams, folded, and placed in the duffel bag. He took his two pairs of jeans and turned the pockets inside out, running his fingers along every inch of the denim. The few books he owned were fanned, their pages flicked to dislodge any potential passengers. It was slow, methodical work. It was sanity. Marcus watched him for a while, a flicker of something—maybe curiosity, maybe pity—in his eyes before he rolled over and faced the wall. Leo was already a ghost to him, a weird roommate he would soon forget.

The final night at the Haven was the longest of his life. He performed his security sweep of the room with a sense of finality, the familiar cracks and stains on the linoleum like the features of a departing friend. He knew this territory. He knew that a loose tile by the door sometimes harbored earwigs. He knew that the window frame had a gap where spiders sometimes built their webs. Tomorrow, he would know nothing. He lay in his bed, listening to the groans and sighs of the old building, and felt a bizarre pang of nostalgia for the place he so desperately hated. It was a known quantity. The horrors were familiar. Westlake was a vast, terrifying question mark. *Every soldier feels this before a new posting,* Finn said, his voice a low murmur in the dark. *It’s standard procedure. Fear of the unknown. But we have an advantage they don’t. We know there’s a war on. They think it’s peacetime. We will be ready.* Leo closed his eyes, but sleep wouldn't come. He just lay there, a sentry on his last watch, waiting for the harsh light of morning and the journey to a new battlefield.The journey to Westlake was made in a nondescript county sedan that smelled of stale air freshener and official indifference. The driver was the same monitor who had pulled him off the table in the cafeteria. He hadn't introduced himself then and he didn't now, his eyes hidden behind a pair of reflective sunglasses, his mouth a flat, bored line. He drove with one hand and tapped on his phone with the other, the world outside the tinted windows a smear of sun-bleached highways and identical-looking suburban sprawl. Leo sat rigidly in the back, his duffel bag on the seat beside him like a lone sandbag against a rising tide. Every overpass they went under was a dark shadow full of potential nests. The drainage ditches along the highway were dark, wet veins in the earth. He could feel the vibrations of the road through the seat, and every bump and shudder was the potential scuttling of something unseen in the car's chassis.

*Observe the terrain,* Finn’s voice instructed, cutting through the rising hum of panic. *Note the lack of dense foliage near the target location. Open ground. Clear fields of fire. This is a good thing. Fewer places for ambushes.* As they turned off the main road, the landscape changed. The grubby strip malls and dusty lots gave way to manicured green spaces and expensive-looking office parks. And then they were there. Westlake Academy was not one building, but a small campus of them, all sleek lines, pale stone, and enormous panels of tinted glass that reflected the hard, blue sky. The lawns were a perfect, uniform green, impossibly lush for the Texas heat, and dotted with tastefully arranged, unfamiliar trees. There were no weeds, no bare patches of surrendered earth, no forgotten corners choked with debris. It was antiseptic. It was alien. It was the most terrifying place Leo had ever seen.

The inside was even worse. The air was cool and smelled faintly of lemon and something clinical, like a dentist’s office. The floors were polished gray concrete, and the walls were bare, painted a soft, calming white. There were no shadows. The light was everywhere, diffuse and indirect, coming from recessed fixtures in the high ceilings. It was a space designed to have no secrets, which to Leo, meant that its secrets were just hidden better. He was led down a silent hallway to an office where a woman stood to greet him. She was tall, with dark hair pulled back in a neat twist and a smile that seemed genuine, which immediately put him on high alert. “Leo? I’m Dr. Anya Sharma. It’s a pleasure to finally meet you. Please, come in.” Her office had one glass wall overlooking a serene, empty courtyard with a single stone bench. A trick, Leo realized. Nowhere to hide. *She’s establishing dominance,* Finn noted coolly. *The panopticon principle. Makes the subject feel constantly observed. Standard interrogation tactic. Don’t react.*

Dr. Sharma gestured to a comfortable-looking chair, but it was upholstered. Leo hesitated, his eyes flicking to a simple plastic chair in the corner of the room. "I'd rather sit there, if that's okay." Dr. Sharma’s smile didn’t falter, but her eyes, a warm and intelligent brown, registered the request. She nodded. "Of course. Whatever makes you comfortable." She sat behind her own desk, which was made of light-colored wood and was surprisingly uncluttered. She didn't have a file on him, at least not a visible one. She just watched him, her hands folded. "So," she began, her voice gentle. "Mr. Henderson told me a little bit about what's been going on, but I find it's always better to hear it from the source. Can you tell me what brought you to Westlake?" Leo’s hands were sweating. He stared at a point on the wall just over her shoulder. *Give her the official story. The one they expect,* Finn coached. *Weakness. Confusion. Fear. Do not reveal our strength. Do not reveal me.* "I get... scared," Leo said, letting his voice sound small and thin. "Of bugs."

The interview was a careful dance. Dr. Sharma was good, he had to give her that. She didn't patronize or dismiss him. She asked insightful questions, not about the bugs themselves, but about the feeling. “Does it feel like a loss of control?” she asked. “When you see one, does the world suddenly feel unsafe?” He gave her small nods and one-word answers, a carefully constructed portrait of a frightened, overwhelmed boy. He was a sheep. He was harmless. Inside, Finn was analyzing her every word, her every micro-expression. *She’s building a profile. Looking for the stress points. The cracks in your armor. We will have to be very careful with her.* Finally, it was over. Dr. Sharma stood. "Thank you for being so open with me, Leo. We're going to help you. I promise." She said it with such conviction that for a terrifying, seductive second, Leo almost believed her. *A falsehood,* Finn stated, his voice a cold anchor. *She doesn't want to help *us*. She wants to eliminate me. We will not allow that.*

His new room was on the second floor. It was small, a perfect cube of white walls and gray floor. There was a bed, but it was a solid platform of molded plastic bolted to the floor and the wall. The mattress was covered in a thick, non-porous vinyl. The desk and chair were a single unit of the same material, fused together. There was one window, a thick pane of shatterproof glass that could only be opened a crack, not wide enough for a hand to pass through. It was a cell, but a clean one. A staff member with a kind, vacant face showed him how the magnetically locking door worked and told him dinner was in an hour. Then he was alone. The silence was absolute. For the first time in his life, he didn't have a roommate. He was utterly, completely alone. Except he wasn't. *The operation can begin,* Finn said, his voice filling the quiet room. *Primary sweep. Top to bottom. No assumptions.*

This was harder than the Haven. There were no peeling baseboards, no cracks in the linoleum, no dark voids under a rickety metal frame. Everything was sealed, solid, seamless. It was a minimalist’s dream and a paranoiac's nightmare. The enemy had no obvious points of entry, which meant their methods of infiltration would be more subtle, more sophisticated. He ran his hands over the smooth, cool surfaces, his fingers probing for any flaw, any gap, no matter how small. He checked the vent, a simple grate with a fine mesh screen behind it. Secure. He examined the single light fixture, a sealed dome in the ceiling. Secure. He spent ten minutes on the window, tracing the seal, testing the locking mechanism. It was a fortress. For the first time all day, a tiny sliver of relief pierced his anxiety. Maybe this place was safe. Maybe he could rest.

He was running his finger along the inside track of the window, the narrow channel where the pane of glass rested when it was closed, when he felt it. A tiny irregularity. He tilted his head, bringing his eyes level with the white plastic. There, tucked deep in the corner of the track, almost perfectly camouflaged, was a shape. It wasn't a monster roach or a lurking spider. It was small and impossibly delicate, a lacewing, long dead. Its body was a hollowed-out husk, but its wings were perfect, transparent and intricately veined, like shards of patterned ice. It was beautiful, and it was proof. They were here. They could get past the sealed walls and the locked doors. They could find their way into the most secure fortress. He stared at the tiny, perfect corpse, and a slow, cold dread washed over him, extinguishing the brief flicker of hope. He wasn't safe here. The battlefield had just changed. In the sudden, profound silence of his mind, Finn’s voice was grim, but also energized, a general who has just had his enemy’s presence confirmed. *Confirmed hostile presence within the perimeter. Good. Now we know. Now the real work begins.*He didn't touch it with his skin. The thought was instantly, violently repulsive. Instead, he tore a small, clean corner from the single sheet of paper that had come with his transfer orders, a piece of official refuse he’d kept in his bag. Using the edge of the paper as a tool, he carefully, delicately scooped the lacewing’s body from its plastic coffin in the window track. The corpse was so light it was almost not there, a ghost of a thing. He folded the paper over it once, then twice, creating a tiny, neat packet. This wasn't trash to be disposed of. This was intelligence. This was the enemy's helmet left on the battlefield, proof that the perimeter had been breached. He tucked the small packet into the toe of his spare shoe, which was then placed back into the bottom of his duffel bag. A secret, kept safe. A reminder.

Life at Westlake was governed by chimes, soft, electronic tones that signaled waking, meals, sessions, and sleep. The sound was designed to be soothing, but to Leo, it was the relentless ticking of a clock in a bomb. The first morning, he woke before the chime and performed his sweep. The room was sterile, and the process was faster, but it felt less reassuring. The smooth, seamless surfaces offered no purchase for his fear, no cracks to check and declare clear. It was like trying to find a footing on a sheet of glass. The cafeteria was a cavern of white tables and polished concrete. The light was bright and even, banishing all shadows. Staff members, called “Techs,” moved with a placid efficiency, their faces trained into masks of professional neutrality. The food was served on sectioned blue trays, each item—scrambled eggs, a piece of toast, an orange—occupying its own perfect, isolated square. Leo took a tray and found a table in a corner, his back to the wall, just as he had at the Haven. The habit was so ingrained he didn't even have to think about it.

*They’re watching,* Finn’s voice noted, a low hum beneath the surface of his thoughts. *Camera at ten o’clock, two o’clock. The Tech by the door, his patrol is a three-minute loop. Dr. Sharma is not present. This is the general population. Assess for threats and assets.* Leo’s eyes scanned the other teenagers. They were a quiet, subdued bunch. A boy across the room rocked gently in his seat. A girl with startlingly red hair drew obsessively in a notebook, never looking up. Then his eyes met another’s. A girl with dark, watchful eyes and a shaved head sat two tables away, not eating, just stirring her oatmeal into a gray, listless vortex. She wasn't looking at him with pity or curiosity, but with a flat, analytical stare, as if she were trying to solve a puzzle. She gave him a slow, almost imperceptible nod, then looked away. *She’s an unknown,* Finn concluded. *Potential variable. Avoid for now.*

The first mandatory activity was "Group Dynamics," which was just a fancy name for group therapy. They sat in a circle of plastic chairs in a room with another glass wall. Dr. Sharma led the session, her gentle voice a stark contrast to the palpable tension in the room. She asked a generic opening question about their goals for the week. The answers were mumbled and vague. When she got to Leo, he felt a dozen pairs of eyes on him. *The script, Leo. Give her the script.* "I want to feel… less jumpy," he said, pitching his voice to sound fragile. Dr. Sharma smiled encouragingly. "That's a great goal, Leo. Can you tell us what 'jumpy' feels like for you?" He described the feeling of being startled, the racing heart, the need to check things. He left out the part about the crawling skin, the malevolent intelligence he saw in the creatures, and he certainly left out Finn. As he spoke, he felt the eyes of the girl with the shaved head on him again, her gaze sharp and intelligent. She wasn't buying it.

Lunch was a trial by fire. He sat with his tray, staring at the pre-made tuna sandwich. It looked perfect. Too perfect. The bread was uniformly white, the lettuce crisp, the crusts neatly trimmed. *Contamination vectors are infinite,* Finn stated. *The kitchens are a black box. We have no intel. We cannot trust their sanitation protocols.* A new ritual was born of necessity. Leo picked up the sandwich and, with methodical slowness, began to deconstruct it. He peeled off the top slice of bread and laid it aside. He scraped the tuna salad into a pile, probing it with his fork. He inspected the single leaf of lettuce, holding it up to the light, searching its surface for any imperfection, any speck, any egg. A Tech, a burly man with a thick neck, drifted toward his table. "Everything okay with the food, Leo?" The question was mild, but the implication was not. Leo’s heart hammered. He was being observed, his deviation noted. *Compliance is a form of camouflage,* Finn advised urgently. *Eat. Show them you are normal.* With a hand that trembled slightly, Leo picked up a piece of the bread and put it in his mouth. It tasted like cotton and fear. He chewed and swallowed, the act feeling like a profound betrayal of their mission. He could feel the Tech’s gaze on him until the man was satisfied and moved on. The humiliation was a cold, hard knot in his stomach.

His next one-on-one session with Dr. Sharma was a day later. He sat in the plastic chair, she in her comfortable one. The serene courtyard was empty behind her. "Leo," she began, her tone even more gentle than before. "I've been reviewing your file from the Haven. It mentions that in moments of high stress, you sometimes talk to yourself. Some of the staff there noted you referring to someone named Finn." A jolt went through Leo, cold and electric. His carefully constructed wall of defense had been breached from the outside, by information he couldn't control. He stared at her, his mind a blank wall of static. *Deny. Deny everything.
