The pebble upon which Pipkin sat was a universe. Veined with quartz and secrets, warmed by a sun that, from his perspective, was the size of a dinner plate, it was his throne for the morning. Below him, the shingle stretched out in a mountain range of grey, black, and chalk-white stones, each one worn smooth by a million years of watery arguments with the shore. The sea itself, the great roaring beast of his world, was in a gentle mood today, sighing against the beach with a soft, rhythmic hiss that was the very breath of home. He was a sprite of Hove, after all. Born of the salt-spray that crystallised on the iron railings of the promenade and the stubborn chalk dust that clung to the white cliffs. His skin had the pale translucence of sea-glass and his hair, when the wind caught it, flew out like the frayed end of a fishing net.

His job, his purpose, as far as he had ever been concerned, was small and essential. He was a mender. When a child’s flip-flop strap snapped, a whisper of will from Pipkin would see the rubber fuse back together, unnoticed. When a shard of a whelk’s shell was too sharp, he would tumble it with a flick of his energy until it was smooth enough for a hermit crab’s new home. He was a keeper of the small balances, a polisher of forgotten things. The world of the Big Folk, the lumbering, loud giants who left behind such interesting and catastrophic messes, was simply the landscape he navigated. Their thundering footfalls were earthquakes to be avoided, their booming voices the weather. He lived in the cracks, in the quiet moments between their giant heartbeats.

Today, something was amiss. The air, usually thick with the clean, briny promise of seaweed and salt, carried a sour undertone. A faint, acrid smell, like bitter metal and forgotten regrets. Pipkin tilted his head, his large, iridescent eyes scanning the water. There, just beyond the first gentle break of the waves, was a patch of water that was not right. It wasn't the rainbow shimmer of spilled fuel or the murky brown of churned-up sand. It was a slick of stillness, a patch of glassy, dead-looking grey that seemed to absorb the light around it. The waves broke *around* it, not through it. As he watched, a cormorant, sleek and black, arrowed down from the sky, hit that patch of water to fish, and immediately floundered. The bird didn't dive; it just… stopped. It thrashed for a moment, its movements jerky and panicked, then it seemed to lose all energy, floating listlessly, its head lolling to one side. It was wrong. Deeply, fundamentally wrong.

A tremor of a feeling he’d never known, something colder than the North Sea in winter, slid down his spine. He scrambled down from his pebble-throne, his bare feet making no sound on the stones. He scurried towards the waterline, his small form a mere flicker of movement against the vast beach. The sick cormorant was slowly drifting shoreward on the tide, a dead weight in the water. Pipkin’s instinct, his very nature, screamed at him to *fix* it. He reached the damp sand where the sea-foam fizzed and died, and extended a trembling hand towards the bird. He gathered his energy, the little spark of seaside magic that lived in his chest, ready to push it out, to soothe the bird’s affliction, to mend whatever was broken.

But as the cormorant washed into the shallows, the sour smell intensified a hundredfold. It was a stench of decay, not of the body, but of the spirit. Of hope curdling. Pipkin’s magic sputtered and died in his hand, like a match struck in a gale. He couldn't touch it. He couldn't even get close. The wrongness emanating from the bird was a wall, and it pushed back with a palpable force of utter despair. He saw, in his mind’s eye, a vision of rust, of concrete crumbling to dust, of laughter turning to weeping. He staggered back, falling onto the wet sand, the cold seeping into him. The cormorant lay still, its black eyes, usually so sharp and predatory, now dull and vacant. It wasn't dead. It was worse. It was empty.

He knew, with a certainty that terrified him, who he had to see. The journey was perilous, a mad dash across the shingle, under the looming shadow of the promenade, and towards the skeletal remains of the West Pier. To a sprite, the pier was a fallen cathedral, a bone-yard of a sea-titan. Its legs, thick with rust and draped in cloaks of slimy green weed, plunged into the sea. And on the most barnacle-encrusted leg, the one that groaned the loudest in a high wind, lived the oldest sprite on the coast. He was known only as Old Man Barnacle.

Pipkin found him clinging to a crossbeam just above the high-tide mark, looking for all the world like just another calcified lump among thousands. His skin was the colour and texture of a weathered limpet shell, and his beard was a tangled mess of dried seaweed. Only his eyes, two sharp, black beads, showed any sign of life.
“Knew you’d come,” the old sprite rasped, his voice like stones grinding together. He didn’t turn his head. “Smelt it on the wind. The Great Sourness. The Blight.”

Pipkin, panting from his frantic journey, clung to a sliver of rusted metal nearby. “The cormorant… I saw… I couldn't…”

“Couldn't mend it,” Barnacle finished for him. “Of course you couldn’t. You can’t mend a void. You can’t polish a shadow. This isn’t a broken thing, boy. It’s an unmaking.” He finally turned, his ancient eyes fixing on Pipkin with an unnerving intensity. “It has begun. Just as the Tides-That-Tell predicted.”

Pipkin’s heart, the size of a dried pea, hammered against his ribs. “What has begun? What are you talking about?” He hated the way Barnacle spoke in riddles, as if the world were a collection of old sea-shanties that only he knew the words to.

“The Balance is broken, Mender,” Barnacle said, his voice dropping low. “For every force, an opposite. For every creation, a destruction. For every sprite born of salt and stone, of mending and making… another is born. One born of rust and spite, of oil and apathy. A sprite of the Unmaking.” The old sprite gestured with a claw-like hand towards the sprawling mass of the city that rose up behind the promenade. Towards the distant smoke, the unseen filth, the quiet hum of human discontent. “He is a rot given form. A grievance given a voice. And he hates the Big Folk’s world. He wants to scour it clean with his own brand of filth, to turn it all into a silent, grey monument to his own bitterness.”

Pipkin stared, uncomprehending. A sprite of destruction? It was a concept so alien it barely registered. Sprites were part of the world, tenders of the unseen gears. They didn't break things on purpose. “But… why? What does that have to do with me?”

Old Man Barnacle’s gaze softened for a barest fraction of a second, a flicker of something that might have been pity. “The Tides-That-Tell speak of two. The Unmaker, Malign, who will rise to swallow the world in his despair. And the Mender, born of the Hove-stone, who is destined to face him.” He paused, letting the words hang in the salt-laced air, heavier than any anchor. “The destiny falls to you, Pipkin.”

The world tilted. The sigh of the sea became a mocking laugh. The sun felt cold. Pipkin shook his head, a frantic, tiny motion. “No. Not me. You’re wrong. I’m… I polish pebbles. I fix toys. I’m nobody. I’m a speck!”

“The tide doesn’t ask the pebble if it wants to be moved,” Barnacle croaked, the old saying falling from his lips with the weight of prophecy. “It moves it. The Unmaker will not stop at cormorants. He will poison the water, then the land, then the Big Folk themselves. He will drain the colour and hope from everything, leaving an empire of grey. Your small acts of mending, your love for the forgotten details… that is the only magic that can counter his. The spark of creation against the tide of entropy.”

Pipkin looked from the terrifying old sprite to the vast, indifferent sea. The sour smell seemed to be everywhere now, a taint on the air he breathed. The sick cormorant on the beach. The dead patch of water. It was real. And the weight of it, the impossible, crushing weight of it all, was settling right on his tiny shoulders. He wasn’t a hero. He wasn’t a warrior. He was a sprite from Hove who liked the feel of smooth stones and the smell of the rain on the sea. And he was, quite suddenly, the most terrified creature in the whole, wide, terrifying world.For a long moment, Pipkin remained frozen, clinging to the rust-eaten metal as the waves crashed below. The world had become a fragile, terrible thing, and Old Man Barnacle’s words were the cracks spreading across its surface. His first, overwhelming instinct was to flee. To scurry back across the dangerous expanse of the beach, find his pebble-throne, and sit there until the sun warmed the fear out of him. He would close his eyes and listen only to the sea’s gentle hiss and pretend this conversation had never happened. He would go back to being a polisher of forgotten things, not the saviour of a world he barely understood. He was already turning, his small, nimble body preparing to launch itself back towards the shore, back to the safety of his insignificance.

“And where will you go, Mender?” Barnacle’s voice was as sharp as a broken bottle, cutting through the sound of the surf and rooting Pipkin to the spot. “Back to your warm stone? You think this Blight will respect your tiny kingdom? You think the Unmaker will stop at the tideline? He will sour the very shingle you sit on. He will leech the colour from your favourite piece of sea-glass. He will curdle the salt-spray in the air until you choke on it. There will be nothing left for you to mend, only a grey and silent ruin.” The old sprite’s words were not angry; they were weary, heavy with the certainty of a tide that has turned a thousand thousand times.

Pipkin flinched, but didn't look back. He couldn't. He squeezed his eyes shut. “He can’t,” he whispered, the sound stolen by the wind. “He can’t do that.” It was a plea, not a statement. He was begging the universe to be as it was yesterday morning.

“Look,” Barnacle commanded, his voice suddenly urgent. He pointed a gnarled finger, not out to sea, but up towards the promenade. Pipkin forced his eyes open and followed the old sprite’s aim. Above them, the world of the Big Folk carried on. A woman laughed, her voice carrying down like the cry of a gull. A man was throwing a bright red ball for a dog. And a little girl, no bigger than a giant’s toddler, was skipping, her yellow boots a flash of pure sunshine against the grey paving stones. As Pipkin watched, she stumbled, a minor trip that should have ended in a giggle and a scramble back to her feet. But she didn't get up. She sat on the hard ground and a wail of such profound, desolate misery rose from her that it felt like a physical blow. The yellow of her boots seemed to fade before Pipkin’s eyes, becoming a dull, sickly mustard. The despair that had choked the cormorant was there, in that child’s cry. It wasn’t a cry of pain or frustration; it was a cry of utter hopelessness.

The sight broke something in Pipkin. He could ignore prophecies and old sprites’ ramblings. He could try to forget the sick bird. But he could not ignore this. This was his world, too. The small joys of the Big Folk were the weather that made his life pleasant, the backdrop to his purpose. A child’s sudden, unnatural sorrow was a storm he couldn't hide from. “What… what does he want?” Pipkin asked, his voice trembling, finally turning to face Barnacle fully.

“He wants what he is. An ending,” Barnacle said grimly. “Malign was born of the dregs. The forgotten resentment in a blocked drain, the bitterness of a vandalised bench, the slow, creeping decay of ambition in the burnt-out ballroom at the end of this pier. That is his heartland. He feeds on apathy and despair. The more the Big Folk cease to care, the stronger he becomes. He doesn’t just hate their world; he hates their happiness, their hope, their urge to build and create. He sees it as a lie, and he wants to erase it.”

Pipkin stared past Barnacle, towards the twisted, skeletal dome of the old concert hall at the pier's seaward end, a place even the gulls seemed to avoid. It stood like a hollowed-out skull against the sky. He had never dared go near it. It was a place of deep sadness, of splintered wood and memories of music that no one living could recall. It was a monument to forgetting. “He’s… in there?”

“He is of there,” Barnacle corrected. “That is the epicentre of the Blight, the throne of his grey kingdom. It’s where the Sourness is strongest. The Unmaking begins there, and it spreads outwards on the tides of human misery.” The old sprite finally pushed himself away from the crossbeam, his movements stiff and ancient. He crawled closer to Pipkin, his black bead-like eyes holding the smaller sprite in a gaze as powerful as a whirlpool. “You cannot fight him, not as a warrior would. That is not a Mender’s way. To fight him is to feed his rage. You cannot destroy a void. You must fill it.” He tapped a finger against Pipkin’s chest, right over his frantically beating heart. “Your magic comes from the love of small perfections, from the will to make things whole. His power is the opposite. It is the force that unravels. To face him, you must bring your spark into his shadow. You must mend something in the very heart of his Unmaking.”A Mender must bring his spark into the shadow. Pipkin looked down at his own hands. They were small, calloused in strange places from pushing pebbles and weaving stray threads of fishing line back into nets. They knew the feel of a thousand different surfaces: the surprising smoothness of a wave-worn brick, the brittle lace of a dried sea-urchin shell, the yielding warmth of sun-baked rubber. They were hands made for small, quiet work. He had never imagined they would be asked to hold up the world. He looked back at the old sprite, the question a knot in his throat. “Mend what? What in that place of endings could I possibly make whole?”

Old Man Barnacle’s black eyes seemed to recede further into their wrinkled sockets. “A Mender’s gift is not in the knowing, but in the seeing,” he rasped. “Your eyes do not just see what is. They see what it *could* be. What it *should* be. The Unmaker blinds others to that possibility. He makes them see only the rot, the rust, the hopelessness, until that is all that remains. You must go to the heart of his despair and find the one thing he has overlooked. The one thing that still holds a memory of what it was meant to be.” He gave a low groan and settled back onto his beam, looking once more like a piece of the pier. “Go now. He will feel your purpose like a splinter in his soul. The Blight will rise to meet you. Do not let the grey get inside you, boy. Remember the sun on the stone.”

With Barnacle’s final, ominous words echoing in his ears, Pipkin turned away. The journey back to the shore was no longer an option. His path now led in only one direction: out, over the waves, towards the skeletal dome at the pier’s end. He scrambled up a rusted support pillar, his bare feet finding purchase on barnacles and iron rivets. The world beneath him was a churning confusion of sea and foam. From this height, the beach seemed a world away, a thin strip of safety he had already left behind. The pier, which from the shore looked like a delicate, broken filigree, was a treacherous mountain range up close. Its iron girders were crumbling chasms, its rotting wooden planks a series of traps, and the wind that whistled through its broken frame sounded like a hungry spirit.

He scurried along a main support beam, staying in the shadows cast by the promenade above. The air grew colder, the familiar scent of salt and seaweed completely suffocated by the acrid stench of the Blight. It wasn't just a smell; it was a taste at the back of his throat and a film on his skin. He passed a colony of mussels clinging to a pylon. Normally they would be clamped shut, a dense community of tiny, resilient lives. But these were gaping open, their inner flesh a putrid grey, their life-force seemingly evaporated. Further on, a cormorant’s nest, woven from seaweed and plastic detritus, sat in a precarious nook. The seaweed, which should have been leathery and strong, was brittle as ash, and it crumbled into a fine, sour-smelling dust as a gust of wind passed over it. Pipkin’s mending instinct flared, a painful flicker in his chest. He reached out a hand, wanting to restore the seaweed’s pliancy, to will the mussels shut, but the magic dissolved before it even formed, repelled by the profound wrongness of it all.

The journey was a blur of frantic scurrying and frozen stillness. He was a creature of the cracks, and he used that to his advantage, slipping through gaps in the ironwork that would be invisible to any larger creature. He navigated by instinct, his senses screaming warnings at him. A patch of rust that looked too dark, too angry, he avoided. A section of wood that seemed to groan with a deeper misery than the rest, he tiptoed around. The Blight was a landscape now, with its own malevolent geography. At one point he had to cross a wide, terrifying gap where the pier’s upper deck had long ago collapsed into the sea. The only way across was a single, sagging cable, slick not with sea-spray but with a greasy, colourless slime that seemed to cling with a coldness that had nothing to do with temperature. It felt like despair made tangible. As his fingers touched it, the vision of the weeping child flashed in his mind, and with it, a surge of desperate resolve. He took a deep breath that tasted of regret, and pulled himself across, hand over hand, the abyss of the churning, grey-slicked sea yawning below him.

As he drew nearer to the pier head, a new sound reached him, winding its way through the groans of the dying structure. It was a faint, discordant melody, like a warped and broken music box playing the saddest tune ever composed. It was a sound that promised that nothing would ever be right again. The air grew thick and heavy, pressing down on him, making his limbs feel like lead. Apathy crept into the corners of his mind, whispering that this was all pointless. Why bother? Why not just lie down here, on this rust-eaten girder, and let the grey tide wash over him? It would be so much easier. He squeezed his eyes shut, forcing the thought away. He pictured his pebble-throne, the way the quartz vein caught the morning light. He remembered the satisfying click of a mended strap, the bright, surprised look in a child’s eyes, the vibrant orange of a starfish. These small, perfect things were his shield. He clung to them as he pushed forward, one tiny, terrified step at a time.

Finally, he was there. The skeletal dome of the old ballroom loomed over him, a hollowed-out skull wreathed in a mist of pure misery. The discordant music was louder here, seeming to emanate from the very bones of the building. The entrance was a jagged wound of splintered planks and twisted iron, exhaling a visible, shimmering haze of grey despair. Pipkin crept to the edge, his heart hammering a frantic rhythm against his ribs. He peered inside. The vast, circular room was a spectacle of decay. Moonlight lanced through great holes in the roof, illuminating swirling motes of dust and sorrow. The once-grand dance floor was buckled and rotten, with pools of stagnant, black water in its hollows. In the centre of the room, directly under the largest hole in the roof, sat a ruined grand piano. Its wood was bleached and split, its keys were missing like lost teeth, and a single, rusted string was vibrating in the wind, producing the ceaseless, heartbreaking melody he had been hearing. This was it. The heart of the Unmaking. Pipkin took a shaky breath, the air burning his lungs, and, with the image of a sun-warmed pebble held firmly in his mind, he slipped through the broken doorway and into the shadow.The air inside was a physical presence. It was the damp chill of a tomb mixed with the cloying sweetness of finality, the smell of things that had given up. The weeping note from the single, rusted piano string was the room’s only voice, a nerve-shredding lament that vibrated in Pipkin’s very bones. All other sound was absorbed by the thick carpets of dust and decay. It was a heavy, waiting silence. The moonlight, falling in pitiless white shafts through the ruined ceiling, did not illuminate so much as it exposed. It showed the skeletons of velvet-covered chairs, their stuffing spilling out like entrails. It glinted off the broken glass of a colossal chandelier that lay shattered on the buckled dance floor like a crystal beast slain in battle. And in the centre of the room, perched on the ruined piano like a vulture on a carcass, was the source of the Blight.

He was not a solid thing, not like Pipkin or Barnacle. He was a form amalgamated from the filth, a shape that seemed to have congealed from the shadows and the grime. His body was a flickering, unstable thing made of what looked like rust flakes, congealed oil, and cobwebs thick with a century of dust. He was the colour of a stain, of wet concrete and old bruises. His eyes were two drops of oily water in a vaguely sprite-like face, reflecting the surrounding decay with a dead, listless sheen. A jagged tear in his form served as a mouth, and as he saw Pipkin, it twisted into something that was not a smile, but a sneer of pure, exhausted contempt. He did not move, but the sour note from the piano string seemed to deepen, to become more sorrowful.

“A Mender,” the thing rasped, its voice the sound of dead leaves skittering across concrete. “Come to polish the rust? Come to sweep the dust? How quaint.” The voice was devoid of anger or threat; it was filled with a profound and ancient weariness, the boredom of a being who has already seen the end of all things and is just waiting for the rest of the world to catch up.

Pipkin froze, his entire being screaming at him to flee. The apathy that had lapped at him on the pier was a crushing wave in here. It filled his lungs, his heart, his mind, whispering that this was all there was. This decay was the truth. Everything else—the sun on the stone, the laughter of a child, the mended flip-flop—was a temporary, pointless lie. What was the point of mending something that was destined for this? The thought was so powerful, so seductive in its simplicity, that his knees buckled. But then, Barnacle's words surfaced through the grey fog in his mind: *Find the one thing he has overlooked.* His eyes, the Mender’s eyes that saw not just what is but what *could* be, began to dart around the room, desperately searching for a flicker of potential in this ocean of ruin.

“You see it, don’t you?” Malign’s voice slithered into his thoughts. “The honesty of it all. The Big Folk build their sandcastles of hope and joy, and the tide comes. It always comes. They laugh, they love, they make their music, and it all just adds to the silence when it’s gone. Everything breaks. Everything fades. Everything forgets. I am not a destroyer, little Mender. I am a reminder. I am the truth that has been waiting under the floorboards all along.” He shifted his grimy form, and a cascade of rust flakes fell onto the piano keys with a soft, dead patter. “There is peace in it. The peace of giving up. Why fight so hard to hold together a world that is falling apart at the seams? Let it go. Let it rest.”

Pipkin ignored the whispers. He couldn't fight them with logic, only with purpose. He forced himself to look past the overwhelming decay and to *see*. He saw the dance floor and, beneath the rot, the memory of polished wood and gliding feet. He saw the ruined chairs and, within them, the ghost of an audience rapt by music. These were memories, but they were too big, too faded. He needed something small. Something forgotten. His gaze was drawn back to the piano, the source of the sorrowful sound, the throne of the Unmaker himself. He scanned its broken form, its bleached and splintered wood, its grin of missing keys. And then he saw it. Tucked down between two broken, silent keys, almost completely hidden by dust and grime, was a tiny glint of something that was not rust or rot.

It was a small, heart-shaped locket, its silver tarnished to a dull pewter. The delicate chain had snapped, and its clasp was bent and broken. It was a forgotten trinket, dropped during some long-dead dance, a piece of lost love and memory that had lain in the dark for decades, slowly succumbing to the Blight. But as Pipkin looked at it, his Mender’s sight pierced through the tarnish. He saw not the broken, dull object, but what it was meant to be: a gleaming silver heart, warm from being worn against skin, its clasp clicking shut with a satisfying, perfect snap. Inside, he knew, would be a memory. A photograph, a lock of hair, a tiny promise. It was an object made to hold something precious. It was an anchor against forgetting.

The moment Pipkin’s focus locked onto the locket, Malign flinched as if struck. The oily pools of his eyes narrowed, and for the first time, a flicker of something other than contempt appeared in them: a cold, venomous anger. The sneer on his face deepened into a snarl. “No,” he hissed, the sound now sharp and dangerous. “You will not. You will not bring your pathetic little sparks in here. This is a place of endings.”

The room grew instantly colder. The single weeping note of the piano string escalated into a frantic, agonised shriek. The shadows in the corners of the ballroom seemed to writhe and stretch, deepening from grey to an absolute, light-swallowing black. The weight of despair that Pipkin had been fighting against intensified a hundredfold, becoming a physical, crushing pressure that sought to flatten him to the floor. “It is so much easier to let go,” Malign’s voice echoed all around him, no longer coming from the piano but from the very air, from the dust and the decay. “Just lie down. Just stop. Just… end.”

Pipkin’s legs trembled violently. Every fibre of his being wanted to obey, to curl up into a ball and let the quiet grey wash over him. It was a struggle not of body, but of will. He had to move, to get to the piano. He took one step, and it felt like wading through setting concrete. The image of the sunlit pebble flashed in his mind. He took another step, his breath coming in ragged gasps. He remembered the feel of the sea-spray on his skin. He pushed forward, foot by agonising foot, across the ruined dance floor, his eyes fixed on the tiny, tarnished heart. The Unmaker did not move from his perch, but the force of his will, the sheer power of his apathy, was a storm that threatened to tear Pipkin’s purpose to shreds. He was halfway there when his vision began to grey out at the edges, the colours of his memories fading. He was losing. He was being Unmade. He stumbled, falling to one knee, the weight too much to bear. But as his hand hit the dusty floor, his fingers brushed against the shattered remains of a crystal droplet from the chandelier. Even in its brokenness, it caught a sliver of moonlight and threw a tiny, defiant rainbow onto the rotten wood. It was a spark. It was enough. With a gasp that was half-sob, Pipkin pushed himself up and lurched the final few feet, collapsing against the side of the grand piano. His hand, trembling uncontrollably, reached up towards the broken keys, towards the lost and forgotten locket.His trembling fingertips brushed against the locket. The metal was colder than sea-worn iron, a dead cold that seemed to suck the warmth from his very skin. The moment of contact sent a jolt of pure despair through him, a vision of finality so absolute it threatened to stop his heart. He saw the locket lying here for a thousand years, saw the pier crumble to iron dust around it, saw the sea itself grow slow and grey and tired, until all was silent and still. The Unmaker’s will was in the object, a poison that had seeped into its very atoms. To mend it felt as impossible as trying to reassemble the spray from a crashed wave. But Pipkin did not try to fight the despair. He knew he couldn’t. Instead, he burrowed past it, seeking the memory of the thing itself. He ignored the visions of its end and focused with all his might on the single, perfect idea of its beginning: the satisfying, tiny click of its clasp.

A flicker. A tiny spark of gold, no bigger than a grain of sand, ignited in Pipkin’s chest and travelled down his arm to his fingertips. Where his skin touched the locket, the golden light pooled, a dot of warmth in the suffocating cold. It was a fragile, defiant light, but it was *creation*. Malign, who had been watching with a sneer of utter certainty, let out a sound like grinding glass. He recoiled on his perch as if the tiny spark had been a physical blow, his shadowy form flickering violently. The oppressive atmosphere in the room lessened by a single, crucial degree, allowing Pipkin to draw a shuddering breath that didn't taste entirely of dust and ruin. The fight was not over; it had just been joined.

“Pointless,” the Unmaker’s voice hissed, lashing out like a whip. “A fool’s errand! I have seen its fate. It will be dropped, lost in the shingle, crushed under a thoughtless heel. The girl who wore it is dust, her name forgotten, her laughter silenced. You are polishing a gravestone, Mender. You are tidying a tomb!” The visions intensified, flooding Pipkin’s mind. He saw the locket falling from a chain, a moment of panic on a young face, then resignation. He saw it kicked into a dark corner, swept away with debris, forgotten completely. He felt the slow creep of decades, the ache of loneliness, the slow, inevitable surrender of the object’s purpose to the grip of decay.

But Pipkin’s magic was not of logic, it was of love. A love for the thing itself, for its intended form, for the care that went into its making. As he clung to the locket, pouring his sputtering energy into it, he felt more than just its sad history. He felt the ghost of the silversmith's pride in the delicate hinge. He felt the nervous energy of the boy who had given it as a gift. He felt the warmth of the locket as it lay against the throat of the laughing girl, a silent witness to a moment of pure, unblemished joy on the long-vanished dance floor. These feelings, these echoes of creation and happiness, were the antidote to the Blight. They were the fuel for his spark. The tarnish on the silver began to recede, not merely cleaned, but unmade, as if the years of neglect were being rewound. The dull pewter surface gave way to a soft, luminous gleam.

With a final, desperate push of will, Pipkin focused all his being on the broken clasp. He saw it as it should be, the two tiny, intricate parts aligned and whole. The golden light from his fingertips flared, and the bent metal flowed like water, reforming, strengthening. The two halves of the locket, which had lain agape for nearly a century, swung together on their newly healed hinge. In the vast, groaning ruin of the ballroom, a sound rang out, impossibly clear and sharp. It was the sound of the mended clasp clicking shut. A tiny, perfect, final sound of wholeness.

The effect was instantaneous and absolute. The single, rusted piano string, which had been shrieking its unending lament, snapped with a sharp crack.
