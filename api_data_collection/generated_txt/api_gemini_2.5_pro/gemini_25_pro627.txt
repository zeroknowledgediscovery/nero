The earth beneath Ionna’s fingernails was a dark, crumbly testament. It wasn’t just dirt; it was a composite of histories, of last year’s fallen leaves, of bone meal and time, a rich, fragrant narrative that she could read with her fingertips. Here in her small, walled garden in the sloping streets of Stockport, hemmed in by the damp-stained brick of neighbouring houses, she was a monarch in a kingdom of loam and blossom. The distant, rumbling sigh of a train crossing the viaduct was the only regular intrusion from the world beyond the gate, a sound so constant it had become a part of the garden’s own peculiar silence. She was tending to the foxgloves, their speckled, bell-like throats a haven for bumbling bees, when the front doorbell chimed. The sound, a crisp, electronic two-note tune, was an anomaly, a sharp-edged thing that didn't belong amongst the soft thud of soil and the rustle of rose leaves.

She straightened up, her knees cracking a soft protest. Wiping her hands on the already-stained canvas of her trousers, she felt that familiar sensation of being pulled back into a different kind of reality, one measured by clocks and appointments. Everything in the house felt settled, as if it had found its final resting place and had no intention of moving again. The chime sounded a second time, a little more insistent.

He wore a tweed jacket with leather elbow patches that weren’t just for show; the fabric was genuinely thinning there. He clutched a leather satchel to his chest like a shield.

“Ionna?” he asked, his voice softer than she’d expected. He gave a small, nervous smile that crinkled the corners of his eyes. “Alistair Finch. For the tutoring.”

“Of course,” she said, her own voice feeling rusty, unused. “Come in. Mind the wellies.” She gestured to the row of mud-caked boots by the door, a silent, earthy garrison.

Rain had begun to patter against the roof, a gentle, percussive rhythm that instantly made the small space feel intimate, a world unto itself. “It does what it wants, mostly,” Ionna replied, taking her seat. “I’m just the curator.” She looked at the books. “I’m not very good with theory.”

“That’s what I’m here for,” he said, his smile becoming a little more genuine. He pulled out a copy of John Donne’s poems, the paper thin and almost translucent. Ionna listened, but her eyes were on the garden. She watched a spider meticulously re-stitching a tear in its web, the fine silk glistening with raindrops. Alistair stared at her. The academic certainty in his face softened, replaced by a look of profound curiosity. He had expected to teach, to impart knowledge into a willing but empty vessel. He was falling in love, and it terrified him. It was a slow, creeping vine of a love, wrapping itself around the careful structure of his life. He was her tutor. One afternoon, as a low sun finally broke through the perpetual cloud, casting long shadows across the lawn, he reached for a book at the same time she did. His fingers brushed against hers. Her entire existence seemed to be contained within the walls of this house and its garden. He had once asked her what she did before he arrived each Tuesday. “Ionna,” he began, his throat dry, the word a clumsy weight in his mouth. He didn’t know what he was going to say. That he was falling for her? That she frightened him a little? That her hand felt like death?

“It’s a kind of promise, isn’t it?” she said, her gaze fixed on the bud. “All that effort, all that waiting, for just a few hours of being beautiful.” She turned to face him, the fading light catching the planes of her face, making her look like a statue in a forgotten churchyard. “Some things are worth the wait, Alistair. He looked at her, at the quiet intensity in her eyes, and the professional boundary he had tried so hard to maintain crumbled into dust. The skin was cool, but not cold this time. It was smooth, like polished marble. She leaned into his touch, her eyes fluttering closed, a picture of absolute surrender. And in that moment, under the watchful eye of a flower that lived only for the dark, he knew he was lost.His thumb traced the line of her cheekbone, a slow, deliberate movement. She was real. She was solid. The coolness of her skin was not the absence of heat, but a presence of its own, like the air in a deep forest. He leaned in, and the world narrowed to the space between their faces. The scent of her was not perfume, but something far more elemental: damp moss, crushed petals, the sweet decay of autumn leaves. He hesitated for a second, a final, futile grasp for the logic and reason that had governed his life, and then he closed the gap.

The kiss was not what he had expected. There was no fiery passion, no clumsy fumbling. It was a gentle, solemn meeting, like a pressing of flowers between the pages of a book. Her lips were soft and cool, yielding but strangely passive. It was he who was doing the kissing, he who was pouring his weeks of unspoken longing into the act, while she simply received it, an ancient, patient statue finally being offered a prayer. For a fleeting, terrifying moment, he felt as if he were kissing the garden itself—the earth, the rain, the very idea of a rose. He pulled back, his breath catching in his throat, his heart a frantic drum against the garden’s deep silence.

Ionna’s eyes, when they opened, were dark pools in the gloaming. She didn't look flustered or surprised; she looked as if she had been expecting it, as if it were simply the next logical step in a sequence she had understood long before he did. Her gaze drifted past him, towards the wall. “Look,” she breathed, her voice a mere whisper of sound. Alistair turned. The great, pale bud of the 'Queen of the Night' was trembling. A faint, ethereal fragrance, a combination of vanilla and something far more exotic and unnameable, began to unfurl into the evening air. As they watched, held in a shared trance, the outer petals began to peel back, slowly, majestically, revealing the intricate white filigree of the flower within. It was an act of profound, silent drama, a star being born in the gathering dark.

“It knows,” Ionna said, her voice filled with a quiet reverence. She took a step towards it, her hand outstretched as if to bless it, but not touching. “It’s time.” Alistair watched her, the strange, otherworldly woman who felt more at home with this nocturnal bloom than with the man who had just kissed her. The love he felt for her was tangled with a growing sense of dread, a feeling that he was trespassing in a place governed by rules he could not comprehend. He was a creature of daylight and footnotes, and she was a thing of moonlight and soil. The distance between them felt suddenly immense, unbridgeable.

He followed her gaze back to the flower, which was now almost fully open, a luminous white cup overflowing with scent. It was achingly, impossibly beautiful, and he knew, with a certainty that chilled him, that by morning it would be a shrivelled, brown remnant of its glory. Just as she had said, a few hours of being beautiful. The thought was a dark premonition he couldn't shake. Was that what this was? Was he just a witness to her brief, beautiful season, whatever that meant? The questions multiplied in the cool air, silent and heavy.

“I should go,” he said, the words sounding harsh and clumsy. The spell was broken, or perhaps he had just become too aware of its power. The conservatory light clicked on, activated by the deepening twilight, casting a mundane, yellow rectangle onto the lawn, a stark intrusion of the ordinary. Ionna turned from the flower, her face half in shadow, half in the artificial light. For the first time, he saw a flicker of something in her expression—not sadness, exactly, but a deep, weary resignation. "Yes," she said. "The world outside the gate is waiting." There was no invitation to stay, no acknowledgement of the kiss that still tingled on his lips. She led him back through the garden, the scent of the night-blooming cereus clinging to them both.

At the front door, standing once more amongst the garrison of muddy boots, the tension was thick and strange. He fumbled for his satchel, for his professional persona, but it was gone, left somewhere back in the conservatory with John Donne. "Next week?" he asked, the question hanging between them, laden with unspoken meaning. It was no longer about tutoring. Ionna looked at him, her gaze direct, unwavering. "If you like," she said. He stepped out into the damp Stockport street, the door closing behind him with a soft, final click. The world he re-entered felt thin and grey, a poor imitation of the vibrant, secret kingdom he had just left. He could still feel the impossible coolness of her lips, and he knew, with a sinking feeling, that he would be back. He had to be. The intricate arguments of seventeenth-century theologians, once a source of deep intellectual pleasure, now felt like the sterile chatter of ghosts. The only text that held any meaning for him was the unwritten one of Ionna. He found himself trying to deconstruct her, to apply the same analytical rigour he would to a Donne sonnet. The real world, the one of timetables and peer reviews, had lost its colour and texture; it was a poorly rendered copy of the vivid, breathing reality contained within her garden walls.

Driven by an academic’s desperate need for data, he abandoned his poets and turned his research skills towards her. He found a grainy, digitised photograph from a local newspaper article in 1958, detailing a prize won at the local flower show. A woman stood proudly beside a display of impossibly large roses. She wore a simple dress, her hair in a dark braid, and her face held the same still, placid quality he knew so well. The caption read, *‘Mrs Ionna Petrescu with her prize-winning ‘Félicité et Perpétue’.’* Alistair’s blood ran cold. It wasn’t just a resemblance; the line of the jaw, the calm set of the mouth, the very shape of her stillness—it was her. He stared at the date, his mind refusing to process the fifty-odd years that lay between that photograph and the woman whose cheek he had touched.

When he stood on her doorstep the following Tuesday, his hand trembled as he rang the bell. The familiar two-note chime felt less like an announcement and more like a tolling, marking his passage from one world to another. He felt like a man knowingly stepping into a fairy tale, aware of the dangers but powerless to resist the enchantment. When she opened the door, she was exactly as he had left her. It was as if the kiss, the confession of the night-blooming flower, had been a dream he’d had alone. He felt a dizzying sense of disorientation. Had he imagined it all?

“The dahlias,” she said, her voice pulling him from his spiralling thoughts. She led him through the cool, dark house and into the conservatory. He had been right to dread it; the garden was a riot of impossible colour. They were explosions of life, their perfect, geometric patterns a stark contrast to the wild tangle of the rest of the garden. He decided to press on, emboldened by a strange mix of love and terror. "I looked up the house," he admitted, feeling like a spy. "I found an old photograph. Of a woman named Ionna Petrescu. He left that afternoon with his heart a mess of contradictions. The rational part of his brain clung to her explanation—grandmother, granddaughter, a simple lineage. But the part of him that had come alive in her garden knew a different truth. He was no longer just a man falling in love; he was an archivist stumbling upon a living document, a story that defied chronology. The kiss had not been the beginning of a romance, but an initiation into a mystery far older and stranger than he could ever have imagined. He felt a pang of guilt, as if he were violating a sacred trust, peering into the private history of the woman he was beginning to love. But the need to understand, to anchor her in some recognisable reality, was a current too strong to fight. He began with the name from the newspaper: Ionna Petrescu. He found her easily enough. The fact should have been a comfort, a neat resolution to the puzzle. Grandmother. Granddaughter. A simple, sad story of striking family resemblance and a shared name. It was as if she had simply appeared, fully formed, to take her grandmother's place in the walled garden. He felt a wave of vertigo, the solid floor of the records office seeming to tilt beneath him. He was a man who traded in verifiable sources, in footnotes and citations, and the woman he was falling for had no official beginning.

When he returned the following Tuesday, the air had a new crispness to it, a distinct autumnal chill that promised the coming decay. The vibrant, almost garish, heads of the dahlias were beginning to brown at the edges, their brief, glorious reign coming to an end. Ionna herself seemed changed.
