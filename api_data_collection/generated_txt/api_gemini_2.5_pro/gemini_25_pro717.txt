The smell was the first thing that greeted Salvatore Moretti every morning, a complex bouquet that told the story of the building. It was a foundation of ancient dust and Pine-Sol, layered with the ghosts of yesterday’s dinners—Mrs. Gable’s boiled cabbage from 4B, the garlicky sizzle of the Rosarios’ sofrito in 3A—and underscored by a persistent, damp-earth tang from the basement that no amount of industrial-strength bleach could ever truly vanquish. To an outsider, it might’ve been an olfactory assault. To Sal, it was simply the smell of Tuesday. He ran a calloused thumb over the brass handle of the service entrance, a gesture so ingrained it was more reflex than action, a silent communion with the hundred-year-old brick and mortar he served. The Belvedere Arms wasn't a palace, despite its grandly deceptive name. It was a five-story walk-up (plus one temperamental, cage-like elevator Sal babied like a sickly child) in a corner of Brooklyn that was perpetually ten years away from being the "next big thing." It was a building of settled lives and stubborn plumbing, and Sal was its keeper.

His domain was a kingdom of creaks and groans. He knew the specific lament of the third step on the main staircase, the asthmatic wheeze of the radiator in 2C, the precise jiggle required to coax the lock on the mailroom door into submission. These were his symphonies, the rhythms of his life. And in between the calls for a plunged toilet or a pilot light gone rogue, Sal had his own music. It lived in his chest, a resonant chamber that seemed far too grand for his slightly stooped, five-foot-eight frame. Today, the acoustics were particularly fine in the lobby. The rain outside slicked the world into a muted watercolour, muffling the growl of the BQE and creating a stillness inside the Belvedere that felt almost sacred.
