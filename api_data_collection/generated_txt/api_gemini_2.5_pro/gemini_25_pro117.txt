Pip Quickwit’s world was precisely three feet tall. It smelled of damp earth, night-crawlers, and the faint, sweet perfume of clover. His job, his entire purpose, was to oversee the microscopic wonders that the Big Folk, in their thundering, hurried lives, never noticed. This morning, his task was the dew. Specifically, the dew on the orb-weaver webs strung between the low-hanging branches of the pin oaks that lined the path around Blue Heron Lake. It was a delicate art. With a flick of his wrist, a motion too quick for a human eye to even register as a blur, he’d pull a mote of moisture from the humid morning air and coax it into a perfect, shimmering sphere on a silk thread. Another flick, another sphere. He worked with the focused intensity of a watchmaker, ensuring each web was a masterpiece of transient jewelry by the time the first jogger’s sneakers began to pound the asphalt.

His home was nestled in the gnarled, exposed roots of a particularly stubborn cottonwood, a space no larger than a shoebox, but to Pip, it was a mansion. The main room was wallpapered with the iridescent carapaces of June bugs, and a discarded silver thimble served as a surprisingly comfortable armchair. He drank rainwater from a bottle cap and slept on a bed of milkweed fluff. It was a good life, a quiet life. The life of a sprite in West Des Moines. He had his territory in Raccoon River Park, the other local sprites had theirs, and a quiet, unspoken treaty existed between them and the Big Folk. The sprites kept the small magics of the world turning over—the improbable survival of a frost-nipped flower, the sudden glint of a lost earring in the grass, the gentle breeze that carries the scent of a barbecue for miles—and in return, the Big Folk mostly ignored them, their senses too dull and their minds too cluttered to perceive the hum of enchantment that underpinned their neat, orderly world.

But this morning, the hum was off-key. It was a dissonance he felt in his wings, a faint, staticky buzz at the edge of his hearing. The dew wasn't cooperating. The moisture he pulled from the air felt… thin. Gritty. The spheres he formed were cloudy, lacking their usual prismatic fire. Today, it felt diluted, as if something was filtering it, straining the goodness out. A shadow, darker than any natural shadow ought to be, pooled at the base of the playground slide. It wasn't the shape of the slide. It was amorphous, oily, and it seemed to drink the light around it. As he watched, a dandelion in the shadow’s reach, a fluffy, perfect seed-head he himself had tended to yesterday, seemed to… sigh. The white fluff retracted, shriveling into a blackened knot. The stalk, a vibrant green moments before, turned a putrid brown and sagged to the ground, dissolving into a small puff of greasy dust.

A cold dread, colder than any winter frost, washed over Pip. This wasn't natural. This wasn't death or decay in the way he understood it. That was a cycle, a slow, dignified return to the earth. His fingers tingled with a phantom numbness just from being near it.

A new routine settled over them, built not on the silence of the old treaty, but on the constant, comforting awareness of each other.
