The town of Milton breathed in three-four time, a slow and predictable waltz of seasons. Summer was a long, humid exhalation, autumn a crisp intake of breath that smelled of woodsmoke and decay. Marko, as sheriff, felt he was the town’s metronome, tapping out the beat with the steady clump of his boots on the courthouse steps, the click of his office door, the rhythmic squeak of his cruiser’s suspension over the familiar potholes on Elm Street. His life was a collection of these reliable sounds, a bulwark against the chaos he knew simmered just beyond the county line, and sometimes, within it.

Then Elara moved in next door, and she brought with her a different kind of music.

Her house, the old Hemlock place that had stood vacant and grey for a decade, was painted a shade of blue so audacious it made the sky look timid. From his porch swing, where he’d sit after a shift with a glass of sweating iced tea, Marko watched the transformation. He watched her haul bags of soil from the trunk of a dusty sedan, her arms thin but wiry. He watched her wrestle a trellis against the wall, her frustration a series of small, sharp movements that were somehow graceful. She was a luthier, he’d learned from the postman, a maker of violins and cellos. The word itself sounded ancient and delicate, like something spun from glass. It didn't seem to fit with the sweat on her brow or the determined set of her jaw.

Their first real conversation was about a misplaced package, a box of tuning pegs that had landed on his doorstep. He’d carried it over, feeling oversized and clumsy on her porch, which was already crowded with pots of herbs he couldn’t name.

She opened the door, a smudge of dark varnish on her cheek, and her smile was a sudden, unguarded thing. “Oh, thank goodness. I was about to launch a one-woman investigation.” Her voice was lower than he’d expected, with a gravelly warmth to it.

“Figured it was yours,” Marko had said, holding out the box. It was the longest sentence he’d managed to string together in her presence, having previously limited their interactions to a nod or a half-raised hand.

“You’re the sheriff, right? Marko?” She took the box, her fingers brushing his. A spark, not imagined, a tiny jolt of static and something else. “I’m Elara.”

“I know,” he said, and then felt the heat rise in his own neck. “Town this size.”

She didn't seem to mind his awkwardness. She just leaned against the doorframe, the blue of the house framing her like a portrait. “Well, Sheriff Marko, thank you for protecting my tuning pegs from a life of crime. I owe you one.”

After that, the silence between their houses felt different. It was no longer empty, but filled with a kind of humming anticipation. He found himself timing his departures and arrivals to coincide with hers. He’d linger on the porch, pretending to read the paper, his ears tuned for the sound of her front door opening. He learned the rhythm of her days. The mornings were for sanding and shaping, the air carrying the faint, sweet scent of maple and spruce. The afternoons were for varnishing, a sharper, chemical tang that he came to associate with the intense quiet that would fall over her house. In the evenings, sometimes, a thread of music would escape from an open window—a tentative scale, a fragment of a melody plucked on a violin, raw and full of potential.

He started bringing her things. A bag of tomatoes from his deputy’s garden, because he had “too many.” A bottle of cleaner for her windows, because he’d “bought the wrong kind.” They were flimsy excuses, tissue-thin lies he hoped she saw right through. She always accepted them with that same easy smile, inviting him to sit on her porch for a while, her presence a balm on the rough edges of his day. She spoke of the cities she’d lived in, the orchestras she’d supplied, the persnickety musicians who were her clients. She talked about the wood, the way a certain grain could sing, the way you had to listen to it before you could ever make it speak.

Marko, in turn, found himself talking more than he had in years. He told her about the frustrating predictability of small-town crime—teenagers tipping mailboxes, the perennial domestic dispute down on River Road. He told her about the quiet satisfaction of finding a lost dog. He spoke of things he hadn’t thought about in years, the hollow space his parents had left, the feeling of being anchored to this town by duty and little else. He never spoke of love, or the lack of it. He didn’t have the words, and besides, he felt the concept was too grand for the quiet, creeping thing that was growing in his chest. It wasn’t a grand symphony; it was a single, sustained note, and it was getting louder every day.

One evening in late September, the air holding the first real promise of a chill, he saw her stumble as she brought in her groceries. It was a small thing, a momentary loss of balance, but he saw it. The next day, he noticed she didn't come out to the garden. The day after, the workshop was silent. No smell of sawdust, no snatch of music. The silence was wrong now, heavy and inert. He walked over, his boots unnaturally loud on the pavement. He knocked, waited, knocked again.

Finally, the door opened a crack. It was Elara, but a faded version of her. Her skin was pale and slick with sweat, her eyes glassy. A deep, rattling cough shook her frame.

“Hey,” she rasped, her voice a ghost of itself. “Not a good day for a visit.”

“You’re sick,” he stated, the protector in him overriding the neighbor.

“Just the flu. It’s a monster this year.” She tried to smile, but it turned into a grimace of pain. “I’ll be fine. Just need to sleep it off.”

He didn’t believe her. He saw the tremor in her hands, the blueish tint to her lips. This wasn't just the flu. He’d seen death creep up on people before, slow and then fast. It had a colour, and he was seeing it in her face.

“Marko, no, it’s… I don’t have insurance right now, it’s a whole thing…”

The metronome had stopped.He stood rooted to the pavement long after the siren’s wail had been swallowed by the distance.
