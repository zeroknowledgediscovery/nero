The shingle shifted, a million grey throats clearing at once as the tide pulled back. Above the sound, another rhythm, more insistent: the soft, percussive slap of Miriam Kaufman’s running shoes on the damp concrete of the promenade. Before Hove, before the sun, before the first dog-walkers and their yawning charges, there was just this. The burn in her calves, the North Sea wind a whetstone against her cheeks, and the vast, bruised purple of the pre-dawn sky. It was a ritual of subtraction. Each stride stripped away a layer of sleep, of worry, of the woman who lived in the silent, second-floor flat overlooking the water. Out here, she wasn’t Miriam Kaufman, fifty-three, estranged mother, failed wife. She was just the runner. A lung, a heart, a pair of legs, moving west towards the skeletal remains of the old pier.

She ran not for health, not really, though her body was a testament to the discipline, lean and wired as a racing dog’s. She ran as a form of penance. For what, precisely, was a catalogue too long and too heavy to carry consciously. So she converted it to fuel. The memory of her daughter Rachel’s voice on the phone last week, thin and brittle with disappointment – *'You’re just running away from it again, Mum'* – became a kilometre pushed out at a faster pace. The ghost of her husband, Mark, packing his books into a cardboard box, his silence more damning than any accusation, was the final, agonising push up the slope to the Western Esplanade. She ran until the thoughts became a low, indistinguishable hum, lost beneath the roar of blood in her ears.

This morning, however, the hum wouldn't settle. A new note had inserted itself, a sharp, metallic dissonance. It had started three days ago. A flicker of a face on a lamppost, seen in her peripheral vision. Yesterday, another, stapled to a notice board by the beach huts. Today, she stopped. Her breath plumed in the cold air, her heart thumping a frantic protest against her ribs. The flyer was already peeling, its corners softened by the sea-mist. MISSING. The face was young, barely out of his teens, with a hopeful, slightly lopsided smile that felt achingly familiar. Leo Finch. Last seen in the North Laine, heading towards the station. He wore a dark hoodie, the flyer said. He had dreams of being a graphic designer. Please, call. His mother’s plea was a raw wound on the page.

Miriam touched the damp paper. Leo Finch. The name echoed in the hollow spaces of her memory. He was one of Daniel’s friends. Not a close one, but part of that sprawling, loud, ambitious constellation of boys who had filled her house with the smell of pizza and Lynx Africa before Daniel had… left. Before her house had become a flat. Before the silence. The boys had all been so full of a furious, unfocused energy, a desperate need to make their mark. They talked of start-ups, of clothing brands, of becoming DJs or programmers or, like Leo, designers. They were all going to be rich, famous, important. Daniel had been the most vocal of them all.

She peeled the flyer from the lamppost, folding it carefully, its dampness seeping into her palm. The rhythm of her run home was different. It was no longer an escape. It felt like a search. The familiar landmarks of Hove – the pastel-coloured beach huts, the grand but peeling facades of the seafront avenues, the manicured lawns of Brunswick Square – seemed alien, their placid surfaces hiding something jagged and unseen. Every shadow seemed to stretch a little too long. Every passing car seemed to slow, its occupants unseen behind tinted glass. The town she ran through every day was no longer hers. It belonged to the question of Leo Finch.

Back in her flat, the air was still and sterile. She’d designed it for minimal friction, a life with no sharp corners. White walls, grey sofa, glass coffee table. A space pod for one. But now the silence pressed in. She placed the damp flyer on the kitchen counter, a stark cry against the clean, quartz surface. She made coffee, the machine’s gurgle the only sound, and sat at her laptop. She didn’t search for Leo Finch. Not at first. She searched for her son. Daniel Kaufman. His social media profile was locked down tight, a digital fortress against her, but she scrolled through old tagged photos, a ghost at a feast. There he was, younger, arms slung around a group of boys, Leo Finch among them, all of them mugging for the camera, invincible.

That’s when she started digging. Not through the official news channels, which reported the disappearance with a bland, procedural tone. Miriam descended into the warrens of the internet: local forums, Brighton-centric subreddits, encrypted messaging groups Daniel had once tried to explain to her. Here, the story was different. Here, the whispers had a name. It was a strange name, spoken in jest but laced with a genuine, creeping dread. Zehavit.

It sounded like a joke at first. A modern-day bogeyman for the internet age. They called him the Giant of the Downs, the Hove Horror. The stories were fragmented, contradictory. He was a property developer who’d bought up half the city with dirty money. He was an ex-Mossad agent hiding out in a Sussex mansion. He was a myth, a meme, a cautionary tale about greed. But beneath the wild speculation, a few terrifying threads remained consistent. Zehavit preyed on the young and the ambitious. The boys, it was always boys, who wanted too much, too fast. He’d appear to them like a genie, a patron. He’d find them in pubs, in cafes, through online ads promising investment for brilliant ideas. He’d offer them the world. A start-up funded. A path to fame. A shortcut.

And then, the taunt. The deal would sour. The promises would curdle. Zehavit, the stories said, would enjoy this part most. He’d mock their ambition, their naivety. He’d hold their dreams in front of them and then crush them utterly. The final post on a missing boy’s Instagram, a desperate, rambling message about being cheated. A final text to a friend about meeting a ‘monster’. And then, nothing. Silence. Just like Leo Finch.

Miriam felt a chill that had nothing to do with the sea breeze. It was the other detail, the one that most people dismissed as fantasy, as a grotesque flourish to the urban legend. It was the part that made her stomach clench and her coffee taste like ash. The hands. In a few of the darkest corners of these forums, in hushed, deleted comments, the whisper was there. He doesn't just ruin them. He takes a souvenir. A proof of their failed grasp. He keeps their hands. The idea was so monstrous, so viscerally wrong, that it looped past horror and into absurdity. It couldn’t be real. And yet, it was spoken of. He keeps them, the myth went, perfectly preserved. In a freezer.

She pushed back from the laptop, her heart hammering again, not from exertion, but from a cold, rising terror. This was insane. A ghost story for disaffected millennials. She was a runner, a mother, a resident of Hove. What did this have to do with her? But she looked at the flyer on her counter, at Leo Finch’s hopeful, foolish smile. He was one of Daniel’s friends. And Daniel, her brilliant, reckless, ambitious Daniel, was just the sort of boy a monster like Zehavit would find irresistible. The thought, once formed, was a shard of ice in her gut. She had spent two years running away from the wreckage of her family. But now, the faint, terrifying possibility arose that her son might be running, unknowingly, towards something far, far worse. The silence in her flat was no longer peaceful. It was the sound of a clock ticking. She picked up her phone, her thumb hovering over Rachel's name. She needed to talk to her, to warn her, to ask about Daniel. She pressed the call button, the synthetic ringing tone drilling into her ear, a prelude to a conversation she knew would be another exercise in futility, another lap in her penance.

“Mum? It’s seven in the morning.” Rachel’s voice was thick with sleep and instant irritation.

“I know. I’m sorry, love. I need to ask you something. About Daniel.”

A sigh, long and weary, travelled down the line. “What’s he done now?” The assumption of guilt, immediate and reflexive. A habit Miriam had helped to build.

“Nothing. He’s done nothing. I hope,” Miriam said, her own voice tight. “Have you spoken to him? In the last few days?”

“We texted yesterday. About Gran’s birthday. He’s fine. Why? What’s this about?”

Miriam hesitated, trying to find the words that wouldn’t sound like the ravings of a paranoid obsessive. How could she explain? A flyer, a feeling, a ghost story from the internet? “It’s one of his friends. A boy called Leo Finch. He’s gone missing.”

“Okay…” Rachel’s tone was wary, guarded. She was waiting for the other shoe to drop, for Miriam’s anxiety to metastasise and attach itself to Daniel. “That’s awful, but Daniel has hundreds of ‘friends’. He probably barely knew him.”

“They were in photos together. They… Rachel, I have a bad feeling about it. There are things being said. About why he’s missing.”

“What things? Mum, don’t do this. Don’t build a whole world of catastrophe in your head. It’s what you do.”

The words were a slap. Accurate, but brutal. “It’s not just in my head. There’s a man… people are talking about a man.”

“A man. Right. Leo’s probably just off on a binder, or he’s had a row with his parents. He’ll turn up in a day or two with a hangover. You can’t make every missing kid in Sussex about you and Daniel.”

*About you.* The accusation hung in the air. Miriam’s breath caught in her throat. Yes, maybe that was it. Maybe she was simply so desperate to find a way back into her son’s life that she was willing to invent a monster for her to slay on his behalf. “You’re right,” she said, the words tasting of defeat. “I’m sorry. I didn’t mean to…”

“Just leave it, Mum. Please. And leave Daniel alone. He’s finally getting on his feet. Don’t drag him back into your drama.” The line went dead.

Miriam held the phone to her ear, listening to the dial tone. *Your drama.* She walked to the window and stared out at the sea. The sun was higher now, a pale disc behind a sheet of cloud, and the English Channel was the colour of slate. The first of the day’s ferries was a smudge on the horizon, heading for Dieppe. People going somewhere. She had been running in place for two years. Rachel was right. It was what she did. She created catastrophes to give her own anxieties a shape. But as she looked down at the street, a white van was parked across the road. A refrigerated van. The logo on the side was for a high-end catering company, but all she could see was the stark, clinical box on its chassis. A freezer on wheels. A coincidence. A perfectly normal, everyday sight. But her runner’s heart, the muscle she’d trained to endure, to keep going when everything else screamed stop, began to beat a new and terrifying rhythm. It wasn’t a rhythm of retreat. It was a war drum. She wasn't just building a catastrophe in her head. She was standing at the edge of one, and no one else could see it.She didn’t move from the window until the white van pulled away from the kerb, turned the corner, and disappeared. It was just a van. A delivery. A mundane piece of city life. But the image of it, the cold white box, had imprinted itself on the back of her eyes. Rachel’s dismissal echoed in the quiet flat, a sharp counterpoint to the thrumming in her own chest. *Don’t build a whole world of catastrophe.* But what if the world was already built, and she was just the first to notice the cracks in the foundation? What if this wasn’t her drama, but her son’s, and she was the only one positioned to see the danger? The thought was clarifying. The penance of her morning run was about atonement for the past. This felt different. This felt like a duty to the future.

The sterile comfort of her flat now felt like a self-imposed prison. She had sought a life without friction, and in doing so had sanded away her own agency. She moved with a sudden, uncharacteristic decisiveness. The running clothes, damp with sweat and sea-mist, were peeled off and discarded. She dressed in the anonymous uniform of the city: dark jeans, a grey jumper, a black waterproof jacket. Practical, unmemorable. She was no longer the runner, a fleeting form against the dawn. She was a mother, and she was going to find out what had happened to her son’s friend. She folded the flyer and slid it into her pocket. The paper was still clammy, a damp, persistent reminder of the boy’s hopeful face. She grabbed her purse and keys, the jangle of metal loud in the oppressive silence, and left the flat without a backward glance.

Driving from Hove into the heart of Brighton was like crossing a border into another country. The wide, stately avenues with their cream-coloured Regency townhouses gave way to the narrower, more frenetic streets that fed into the city centre. The air itself seemed to change, losing its salty, open-sea tang and taking on the scent of diesel, fried food, and the sweet fug of incense from a hundred tiny shops. This was the North Laine, a tangled warren of independent traders, vegan cafes, and graffiti-splashed walls. It was Daniel’s territory. A place she associated with panicked phone calls about lost wallets and missed trains, a place she had subconsciously avoided since he had walled himself off from her. Now, she drove directly into its chaotic heart, the slow-moving traffic and swarms of pedestrians a physical representation of the mess she was willingly entering.

She found a parking space a few streets away and walked, her senses on high alert. The North Laine was fully awake, a riot of colour and noise. Music bled from record shops, the aroma of roasting coffee battled with the smell of patchouli, and crowds of young people, vibrant and loud, moved in shoals along the narrow pavements. Miriam felt her age, her irrelevance. She was a ghost here, a grey figure from a different, quieter world. Her mission felt absurd. She was a fifty-three-year-old woman with a bad feeling, hunting for a mythical monster in a district powered by youthful irony and artisanal gin. But she clutched the flyer in her pocket and pushed on, scanning the crowds, the shop windows, the faces, looking for anything that felt out of place.

The flyer said he was last seen in the North Laine, heading towards the station. That was a wide corridor of possibilities. She started on Gardner Street, the Laine’s main artery, her eyes drawn to a small, independent coffee house wedged between a vintage clothing emporium and a shop selling handmade jewellery. It had large windows, and she could see the patrons inside, mostly young people hunched over laptops, their faces illuminated by the blueish light of the screens. It looked exactly like the kind of place a budding graphic designer would haunt. Taking a breath that did little to calm her nerves, she pushed open the door. A small bell chimed her arrival, and a few heads lifted from their work. She felt a flush of self-consciousness, an intruder in their creative sanctuary.

She approached the counter, waiting for the barista – a girl who couldn’t have been much older than Rachel, with bright pink hair and a silver ring in her nose – to finish frothing a jug of milk. Miriam rehearsed the words in her head, trying to sound like a concerned citizen, not a frantic parent on the verge of a breakdown. When the girl looked up, Miriam unfolded the damp flyer and laid it on the counter, next to a jar of vegan cookies.
“I know this is a long shot,” Miriam began, her voice steadier than she expected. “But have you seen this boy? His name is Leo. He’s a regular here, I think.”

The barista’s professionally bored expression softened as she looked at the picture. She frowned, tapping a long, black-nailed finger on Leo’s smiling face. “Oh, man. Leo. Yeah, he’s in here all the time. Or he was.” She looked up at Miriam, her eyes wide with genuine concern. “Is that the flyer? I saw them go up yesterday. I can’t believe he’s actually missing.” She leaned closer, lowering her voice. “He was in here. Three, maybe four days ago. The day he disappeared, I reckon.”

Miriam’s heart gave a hard, painful kick against her ribs. “Did you see him? Did he talk to you? Was he with anyone?”

The girl nodded slowly, her gaze distant as she dredged her memory. “Yeah, he was with someone. That was the weird thing. Leo’s always on his own, laptop out, headphones on. But that day he was with this guy. An older guy. Dressed super smart. Like, a proper expensive suit. Looked like a banker or something. Totally not the usual type for in here.” She paused, her brow furrowed in concentration. “They were arguing. Not shouting, but, you know… intense. Leo looked stressed. Upset. The other guy… he was just smiling. It was weird. A real cold, snakey kind of smile.”A cold, snakey kind of smile. The phrase landed in the centre of Miriam’s chest and spread outwards in a spiderweb of ice. This was it. The abstract horror of the forums given a face, a suit, a seat in a coffee shop. The myth was walking the streets of Brighton. Miriam leaned forward, her hands flat on the counter, forgetting the other customers, the hiss of the espresso machine, everything but the girl’s concerned face.

“Can you describe him?” she asked, her voice a low, urgent hum. “Anything at all. What he looked like, how he spoke. Anything you remember could be important.”

The barista, whose name tag read ‘Chloe’, chewed on her lip, her gaze fixed on the ceiling as if the memory were projected there. “He wasn’t from around here, you could just tell. His suit was dark grey, looked like it cost more than my rent for a year. Not old-old, but… polished. Maybe your age, no offence. He had this really sharp haircut, silver at the sides. And a watch. A serious watch. He didn’t move much, just sat there, perfectly still, while Leo was all fidgety and agitated. It was like watching a cobra with a mouse.” She shuddered, a genuine ripple of distaste. “I didn’t hear much. I was busy. But I heard the man say something like, ‘This is your final opportunity, Leo. Don’t mistake my investment for sentiment.’ And then, just before they left, I heard him laugh, a really quiet, horrible sound, and say, ‘Such ambition. It’s a tragedy to waste it.’”

The words were a direct transcript from the nightmares Miriam had been reading online. The patron, the deal, the taunt. It was the complete, sickening script. He was real. Her son, her brilliant, arrogant, ambitious Daniel, was made of the exact same material as Leo Finch. He was a prime cut of meat for a predator like this. The vague terror she had felt that morning crystallised into a single, sharp point of certainty. This wasn't her drama. This was a hunt, and her son was potential prey.

“Did you see where they went?” Miriam pressed, her knuckles white on the counter’s edge. “Which direction?”

“Towards the station, I think. Leo was almost running to keep up with him. He looked like he was about to cry.” Chloe’s expression turned sympathetic, but also helpless. “Look, I’ve told the police this already. They came in yesterday, asking questions. They took my statement.”

The mention of the police was a bucket of cold water. Of course they knew. But what were they doing? Filing reports, following procedure, looking for a runaway. They weren't looking for a monster from a ghost story. They wouldn’t be looking for a man named Zehavit. “You have cameras in here, don’t you?” Miriam asked, gesturing towards a small black dome in the corner of the ceiling. “His face would be on the footage.”

Chloe’s open expression immediately shuttered. “Yeah, but I can’t… my manager’s not in. It’s all password protected. And we can only give it to the police anyway. Data protection, you know?” She looked genuinely apologetic. “I’m sorry. I wish I could help more.”

Miriam nodded, stepping back from the counter. It was a dead end. She couldn’t force the issue, couldn’t expect this girl to risk her job for a stranger’s paranoid fears. She was on her own. “No, you’ve been a great help. Thank you.” She fumbled in her purse and pulled out a ten-pound note, pushing it into the tip jar. Chloe’s eyes widened, but Miriam was already turning away, the bell on the door chiming her exit.

She stood on the pavement, the chaotic energy of the North Laine washing over her. But it was different now. The crowds of young people, with their fierce fashions and loud laughter, no longer seemed alien and unknowable. They seemed fragile, their furious, unfocused energy a beacon for the thing that moved among them. She saw a dozen Leos, a dozen potential Daniels, in the faces that passed her by. Rachel’s voice echoed in her head—*leave Daniel alone*. But how could she? Leaving him alone felt like abandoning him on a battlefield she was only just beginning to comprehend.

She walked, not aimlessly, but with a new, grim purpose. She wouldn’t go to the police. Not yet. What would she tell them? That the boogeyman from Reddit was real and she’d found a witness who described his expensive watch? They would thank her, placate her, and file her under ‘hysterical mother’. No, the police worked with facts, with evidence. The world Zehavit operated in, the world of ambition, desperation, and secret deals, was a shadow world. To navigate it, she couldn’t be Miriam Kaufman, fifty-three, estranged mother. She had to think like someone who knew its currency. She had to think like Daniel.

She found a bench in the small green space of Pavilion Gardens, the fantastical domes of the Royal Pavilion rising like a mirage behind the trees. She took out her phone, her fingers cold and stiff. She ignored the impulse to call her son, knowing the conversation would be a wall of defensive anger. Instead, she went back to his social media, back to the digital fortress, back to the old tagged photos. She found the one she had looked at that morning. Daniel, arm thrown around a grinning Leo Finch. And on their other sides, three other boys, all of them posturing with the beautiful, heartbreaking arrogance of youth. She zoomed in, her thumb tracing their faces. She didn’t know all their names. But she knew where to start. One of them, a lanky boy with a shock of blond hair, had run a short-lived clothing brand with Daniel a few years back. They had operated out of a small, shared studio space in an old warehouse down near the marina. Miriam remembered because she had paid their first three months’ rent. An investment, Daniel had called it. A plea, she had known it to be. She stood up, the phone clutched in her hand. The hunt had a new direction. She wasn't just looking for a monster anymore. She was looking for her son’s ghosts.The drive east towards the marina was a journey through Brighton’s multiple personalities. She left the heady, incense-laced tangle of the North Laine and emerged onto the wide, windswept artery of the seafront road. To her right, the grey sea churned under a pewter sky; to her left, the grand, crumbling facades of Kemptown. The marina itself appeared abruptly, a modern concrete enclave carved into the base of the white chalk cliffs. It was a place of sterile, functional wealth, a world of gleaming white fibreglass, taut sailing ropes clinking a nervous rhythm against aluminium masts, and chain restaurants promising homogenised comfort. It felt a world away from the desperate, creative energy she was chasing, yet she knew that wealth and desperation were often two sides of the same tarnished coin.

The warehouse was set back from the main marina development, a relic from an older, more industrial time. Its corrugated metal sides were rusted in streaks like dried blood, and a fresh layer of graffiti had been sprayed over the faded logo of a defunct shipping company. She remembered this place. Remembered the pride in Daniel’s voice when he’d shown it to her, the vast, echoing space full of dust motes dancing in the light from the grimy skylights. He and his friend, Toby, had claimed a corner of it for their brand, ‘Apex Threads’. It had felt, for a fleeting moment, like the start of something real. Now, parking her car opposite the graffitied entrance, it felt like a tombstone for youthful dreams.

She pushed open a heavy, groaning side door and stepped inside. The air was cold and smelled of damp concrete and spray paint. The vast open space she remembered had been partitioned into a warren of smaller, crudely constructed units. A labyrinth of plywood and hope. Muffled music, a heavy bassline thudding like a distant heart, leaked from somewhere deep within. She followed the sound down a dim corridor, her footsteps echoing unnervingly. The names of the current occupants were scrawled on the doors in marker pen: ‘Rat King Ceramics’, ‘Gutter Press’, ‘Cyclops Audio’. The music was coming from a unit at the very end of the corridor. The door was ajar. Peering in, she saw him. The blond boy from the photograph, though the years had sharpened his features and etched faint lines of disappointment around his eyes. This was Toby. He was no longer posturing for a camera. Toby glanced up, his eyes taking a moment to focus, and a flicker of recognition turned quickly to guarded surprise.
“Mrs. Kaufman?” His voice was flat. “What are you doing here?”

There was no warmth in his greeting, only the weary apprehension of someone confronting a ghost from a past he’d rather forget. Miriam stepped fully into the room, clutching her jacket. “Hello, Toby. She pulled the folded flyer from her pocket and held it out. “I’m looking for information about Leo Finch.”

Toby wiped his ink-stained hands on a rag, his eyes flicking from Miriam’s face to the flyer and back again. He didn’t take it. “Yeah, I heard. The name ‘Leo’ had been a distant problem; the description of the man was a live wire. “Did you walk away, Toby?” Miriam pressed, her voice soft but relentless. “Did you and Daniel? I remember you two. Apex Threads. Just the kind of boys a man like that would be interested in.”

He finally stopped, slamming the squeegee down onto a workbench with a clatter that made Miriam jump. Except it might not be a story.” He ran a hand through his hair, leaving a faint black smudge of ink. “They call him Zehavit. The Patron. He finds the ones who want it most, the ones who are so hungry for it they’d do anything. He gives them a taste. Funds their project, gets them a meeting, makes them think they’ve made it. And then he pulls the rug out. But it’s not about the money. It’s about the humiliation. He likes to watch them fall.”

It was the name. The name from the forums, spoken aloud in this cold, damp room. It made the myth real, solid, a presence standing between them. Miriam felt a wave of nausea. “He keeps their hands,” she whispered, the monstrous phrase leaving her lips before she could stop it.

Toby stared at her, his face pale. “Jesus. You’ve been on the deep web, haven’t you?” He let out a short, humourless laugh. “I don’t know about that. I don’t want to know. That’s the point. The cold dread in Miriam’s stomach was now a solid block of ice. It had come that close. Daniel had been on the radar. “And Daniel?” she asked, her voice barely audible. “Did he forget? Did he walk away?”

Toby’s gaze fell to the floor. The fear in his eyes was now mixed with a deep, weary sadness. “Daniel thought our brand was small-time. He thought I didn’t have the vision. “The Alembic? What’s that?”

“It’s a club. Not a real club, more like a private society. Super exclusive. For the city’s ‘creators’ and ‘investors’. It’s housed in the old Merchants’ Guild building off Ship Street. Miriam stood frozen for a moment, the name of the club echoing in the silent, ink-scented air. A location. A specific, tangible place. She nodded slowly, turning to leave, her mind already racing. Zehavit didn’t just destroy lives; he feasted on the light that animated them, the brilliant, fleeting incandescence of a dream about to be realised. Miriam walked back through the labyrinth of plywood studios, but she was no longer in the warehouse. The world kept moving, oblivious, while just beneath the surface, a monster was curating a collection of broken boys and shattered futures. And her son, her proud, foolish, beautiful son, was looking for the front door to the abattoir. The Alembic. The name had the ring of arcane knowledge, of alchemical transformation. A place where base ambition was transmuted into golden success. It was a perfect trap, baited with the one thing Daniel could never resist: the promise of a shortcut to greatness.

Ship Street was a narrow canyon of red brick and flint, a stone’s throw from the tourist-clogged arteries of the Lanes but feeling centuries removed. It was a street of discreet law firms, bespoke tailors, and private consulting rooms, their brass plaques polished to a soft gleam. The old Merchants’ Guild building was unmistakable. It seemed to absorb the light, its leaded glass windows revealing nothing of the interior. There was no sign, no name, just a single, heavy oak door and a small, burnished brass intercom panel next to it. It radiated an aura of absolute exclusion.

Miriam parked on a side street and watched from the anonymity of her car. For half an hour, she observed. A sleek black Tesla purred to a stop and a woman in a sharp, cream-coloured suit, her face a mask of composed indifference, stepped out and was buzzed in without a word. A few minutes later, two young men, both brimming with the nervous, over-caffeinated energy Miriam recognised from Daniel’s cohort, approached the door. This was a world she couldn't enter. Her running gear was in a bag on the back seat, a relic from a different life, lived only that morning. The anonymous jeans and jumper that had served her in the North Laine and the warehouse were useless here. Here, she was nothing. An invisible, middle-aged woman in a sensible car. She couldn’t reason her way in. Her anxiety, the familiar, corrosive acid, began to bubble up. This was it, the dead end. The point where her frantic energy dissipated against an immovable wall. She should go to the police, hand over her fragments of rumour and fear, and retreat back to the safety of her white-walled flat. She should go back to running in place.

But the image of Leo Finch’s flyer, of Toby’s terrified face, of the two young men slipping through that dark door, held her fast. The thought of Daniel walking that same path wasn’t a catastrophe she was inventing; it was a future she could see with terrifying clarity. This time, she would run towards it. But she had to change. She had to become someone else, someone who could walk through that door. She started the car, a new, cold resolve settling over her. She knew what she had to do. She had to excavate her own past. She had to resurrect a woman she thought she had buried for good.

Her flat, once a sanctuary of minimalism, now felt like a stage waiting for its set. The stark white walls and grey furniture were a blank canvas. She walked to the heavy oak door of The Alembic without hesitation, her reflection a fleeting, ghostly silhouette in the dark, leaded glass. It was a market, and Miriam understood with a sickening lurch that the currency wasn't just money, it was the soul.

She made her way to the bar, a long, backlit slab of polished obsidian, and ordered a Macallan eighteen, neat. She didn't see Daniel. The relief was a physical blow, making her knees feel weak, but it was immediately replaced by a gnawing dread. If he had been here, and wasn't now, what did that mean? Had he already been vetted, chosen, and moved on to the next stage?

Her blood ran cold. He was real, a physical presence, radiating a chilling aura of power that warped the very air around him. To approach him would be to announce herself as prey. All her carefully constructed armour would melt away under that gaze. She needed another way in, a flanking manoeuvre. Her eyes swept the bar and found a potential opening. A man in his late sixties, with a florid face and a bored, restless energy, was nursing a brandy nearby. He too was watching the floor, but with the jaded eye of a gourmand at a disappointing buffet. Miriam held his gaze for a fraction of a second longer than necessary, then gave a small, conspiratorial nod, a gesture that said, *I know. The quality isn't what it used to be.* He straightened slightly, his interest piqued by this unknown peer. Miriam turned her shoulder to him slightly, a subtle invitation. The hunt had begun, and she had just baited her first trap.The man took the bait. He pushed himself off the bar with a sigh of soft leather and expensive wool and ambled over, his brandy glass cradled in a hand adorned with a heavy gold signet ring. He stopped beside her, leaning his back against the bar with an air of assumed intimacy, the scent of his cologne a cloying, sweet fog.

All I see are lambs bleating for attention, hoping one of the wolves will find them interesting enough to eat. It's frankly depressing." The language was brutal, a dialect she knew he would understand. It was the lexicon of this place, where human potential was just another asset class to be stripped.

A flicker of genuine interest lit his eyes. This was not the conversation he usually had with the few women who ventured into this sanctum. The name landed like a stone in a frozen pond. *Gavriel Zehavit*. The myth had a name. The refrigerated logistics, the cold storage—it all clicked into place with a horrifying, sickening logic. The carefully constructed character of Juliana threatened to dissolve. Miriam gripped her glass, the cold seeping into her fingers. An ice factory. A freezer on an industrial scale. The monstrous whisper from the forums, *He keeps their hands*, screamed in her mind. This was it. The centre of the web. The runner’s heart, the frantic, panicked heart she had tried to suppress, was hammering against her ribs. She had a name, Gavriel Zehavit. And she had a place. An ice factory. The end of the line. The woman she had been inside, the cool and calculating Juliana, had evaporated on the threshold, leaving Miriam Kaufman, fifty-three, shaking in a ridiculously inadequate silk dress. The name, Gavriel Zehavit, was a physical weight in her mind, and the location, the ice factory, was a death sentence she felt she had already received. The cocktail dress, the silk that had felt like armour in The Alembic, was now a ridiculous, flimsy costume. It was Juliana’s skin, and Juliana had no place here. Her circuit took her away from the land and towards the water. The fence ran flush with the edge of a high concrete sea wall that dropped ten feet to the choppy, black water of the harbour entrance. The scale of it, the brutalist efficiency of its security, was designed to crush the very idea of unauthorised entry. She was a fly crawling on the outside of a bank vault.

She almost missed it. Where the new factory extension met the older, original concrete foundations, the brand-new security fence abruptly stopped. The drop to the narrow ledge in front of the pipe was awkward and dangerous. Her running shoes, designed for flat surfaces, slipped on the wet, algae-slicked concrete. His good hand seized her injured shoulder, and a universe of pure, white agony exploded behind her eyes. She screamed, a raw, piercing sound of pure pain, and fell to her knees as he tore the sketchbook from her grasp.

He had it. He stood over her, breathing heavily now, the damp book in his hand. The mask was gone completely, his face a contortion of fury and triumph. He had won. And in that moment of his victory, a new sound intruded. A shout. "Hey! What the hell is going on over there?"

They both looked up. The door of the portacabin was open, and the man in the high-vis jacket was standing on the steps, his face a mask of shocked disbelief. He held a steaming mug in one hand, his newspaper forgotten in the other. He was a creature from another world, the world of tea breaks and morning headlines, a world where men in expensive clothes did not viciously assault filthy, bleeding women in the dawn light. He was the real world, intruding on Zehavit’s private theatre of cruelty.

Zehavit froze, the sketchbook in his hand, his face caught between the triumphant predator and the sudden, unwelcome need for a plausible identity. He looked from the worker to Miriam and back again, his mind racing, calculating. For the first time since she had entered his world, he looked uncertain, wrong-footed. The intrusion of a single, mundane witness had broken his spell, shattered his control. He could not kill them both, not here, not now. It was too public, too messy. His entire world was built on a foundation of secrecy that was now cracking under the gaze of a single, ordinary man.

This was her chance. While Zehavit was pinned by the worker's disbelieving stare, Miriam scrambled to her feet. Ignoring the fire in her body, she turned and ran, not back into the maze, but forward, towards the gate, towards the shouting man, towards the light. She was screaming as she ran, a continuous, ragged, desperate sound, a pure animal cry for help that tore through the quiet morning air. She was no longer hiding. She was making herself the most visible thing in the entire port. She was a catastrophe, a spectacle, a problem that could not be ignored. Behind her, she heard the worker shout again, more urgently this time. She didn't look back. She just ran, her eyes fixed on the solid, reassuring shape of the gatehouse, the final finish line of the longest, most terrible run of her life.The scream was a raw, continuous thread of sound, stitching the grey dawn to her ragged flight. It was not a plea but a weapon, an alarm bell she was ringing with the last of her life’s breath. Each jarring footfall on the hard tarmac was a fresh agony, her ruined ankle sending shards of pain up her leg, but she ran on, a broken puppet animated by a single, ferocious string of will. The solid, brightly-lit shape of the gatehouse was a heaven she no longer believed she deserved but was crawling towards nonetheless. The shouting man, the witness, was a lighthouse in the storm, a fixed point of normalcy in a world that had dissolved into a nightmare of teeth and shadows. She was no longer running from the monster; she was running towards her own world, dragging the reality of him with her, a horrifying spectacle that refused to be unseen.

Zehavit watched her go, a creature of filth and noise fleeing towards the light. He saw the worker on the steps, now fumbling for his phone, his face a cartoon of righteous shock. He looked down at the damp, pulpy sketchbook in his hand. The last piece of Specimen 114. The last tangible evidence of the night’s work. The entire project, the years of meticulous cultivation, the very sanctity of his private theatre, had been irrevocably contaminated. A cold, pragmatic fury settled over him, eclipsing the hot rage of the chase. This was no longer an artistic pursuit; it was a tactical problem. The woman was now a public fact, her testimony soon to be official record. He could not erase her, not here. But he could erase her proof. With a final glance at her receding, screaming form, he made the only logical decision. He was a ghost, and it was time to disappear. He turned, and with a calm, deliberate stride that was a chilling counterpoint to Miriam’s frantic flight, he walked back to his car. There was no hurry. The board was ruined; the only move left was to sweep the pieces from the table.

Miriam did not see him leave. Her entire universe had collapsed to the few yards of open ground between herself and the shouting man. She reached him, her legs finally giving out, and collapsed onto the hard, cold concrete at the foot of the portacabin steps. The scream died in her throat, replaced by a series of harsh, dry, racking sobs that brought no tears. She looked up at the man, a stranger in a high-vis jacket, his face pale and horrified. He was staring at her as if she were an apparition, a thing that had clawed its way out of the earth. His mug lay shattered on the ground, a dark pool of tea spreading across the concrete. She tried to speak, to form the words—*Zehavit, the factory, my son*—but all that came out was a low, animal moan. The world was beginning to tilt, the edges of her vision fraying into black.

From a great distance, she heard the squeal of tires on asphalt, a sharp, angry sound as the Tesla accelerated out of the service road and onto the main thoroughfare, heading away from the port, away from the city. The man was shouting into his phone, his voice high and panicked. "—a woman, bleeding, she’s been attacked—yes, Shoreham Port, the main gate! Send an ambulance!" Running footsteps approached, heavy boots on the tarmac. Two more men in uniforms, security from the gatehouse, their faces grim and professional as they took in the scene. The real world was crashing in, its agents of order and procedure trying to make sense of a situation that had no sense, no place in their daily logs. They were speaking to her, asking questions, but their voices were a meaningless drone, the buzzing of flies in a distant room.

The adrenaline that had carried her through the water, through the pain, through the final, desperate sprint, was gone. It left a vacuum, and into that vacuum rushed a pain so absolute and all-encompassing that it was a mercy. Her body was a map of torment, but beneath it all was a colder, deeper wound. *COMPROMISED*. The word glowed red behind her eyes. The image of Daniel’s beautiful, intricate project dissolving into a waterfall of digital noise. She had escaped, but he had erased her son with a touch. She had lost the sketchbook, her only proof, the last testament of Leo Finch. It was her word against a phantom, a mother’s hysterical ravings against a man who did not exist. The crushing weight of her failure settled over her, heavier than the sea, colder than the concrete. She had won the race for her life, only to lose everything that had given the race its meaning.

She felt gentle hands trying to lift her, the texture of a coarse blanket being draped over her shivering shoulders. The distant, approaching wail of a siren was the saddest, most beautiful sound she had ever heard. It was the sound of a world that was too late, a world that could patch her wounds but could never comprehend the monster that had inflicted them. She looked up at the sky, at the pale, fragile dawn breaking over the tops of the steel mountains. It was the same sky she ran under every morning. But it was not her sky anymore. She had been remade in the darkness, and she knew with a clarity that cut through the pain and the encroaching fog that this was not the end of the run. It was just the end of the beginning. Her eyes closed, and she surrendered to the profound, welcoming blackness, the roar of the siren the last thing she heard before the silence took her.The first sensation was not pain, but the aggressive purity of clean cotton. The sheets were crisp, sterile, and smelled of industrial detergent, a scent so diametrically opposed to the foulness of the pipe that the contrast itself was a form of violence. A rhythmic, electronic beep, steady and implacable, measured out the passage of time beside her ear. Miriam opened her eyes to a world of pale, unforgiving white. A plaster ceiling, a metal arm holding a bag of clear fluid, the starched white uniform of a nurse checking a monitor. She was in a hospital, a sanitised box where the chaos of the world was meant to be held at bay. But the chaos was inside her now, a permanent resident.

She tried to sit up, a movement that sent a blinding, incandescent agony through her left shoulder, and a lower, grinding pain through her ankle. She fell back against the pillows with a sharp, involuntary gasp, her body a litany of broken parts. The nurse turned, her expression a well-practised mixture of kindness and authority. “Easy now, love. You’ve been through the wringer. You’ve got severe hypothermia, a dislocated shoulder which we’ve popped back in, a sprained ankle, and more cuts and bruises than I can count. You’re lucky to be alive.” The words were meant to be reassuring, but they landed like stones. Luck had nothing to do with it.

Memories flooded back, not in a coherent stream, but in a series of visceral, disconnected shocks. The cold smile in the club. The whiteness of the freezer corridor. The sketchbook in her hand. The monstrous boom of the door giving way in the darkness. The brutal grip on her ankle. The final, desperate sprint towards a shouting stranger. She clutched at her chest, her fingers searching for the damp, rectangular shape of the book. It was gone. In its place was the thin, unfamiliar fabric of a hospital gown. The loss was a physical blow, more profound than any of her injuries. Without it, she had nothing. She was just a madwoman with a fantastic story.

Later, after a doctor had prodded and questioned, and she had been given a cup of lukewarm tea that tasted of nothing, they arrived. Two of them, a man and a woman in plain clothes, their expressions a careful blend of professional sympathy and keen-eyed assessment. They introduced themselves as Detective Sergeant Evans and Detective Constable Sharma. Evans was the older of the two, a man with a weary, gravitational pull, as if he had seen every possible permutation of human misery and was no longer capable of being surprised. Sharma was younger, her gaze sharper, more open, but with a similar watchful stillness. They were the agents of the real world, here to file her nightmare into a neat, procedural report.

“Miriam, can we call you Miriam?” Sharma began, her voice soft. “We just need to ask a few questions. The man who saw the assault at the port gave us a statement. He said you were attacked by a man in a dark suit. Can you tell us who he was?”

Miriam took a shaky breath, the antiseptic air searing her lungs. She had to get this right. She had to make them believe. She started at the beginning, her voice low and hoarse. She told them about her run, about the flyer for Leo Finch, the boy with the hopeful smile. She told them about the coffee shop, the North Laine, the fear in Toby’s eyes at the warehouse. She spoke of the whispers online, of the urban legend named Zehavit. As she spoke, she watched their faces. DC Sharma nodded, her pen scratching against her notepad, her expression one of focused concentration. DS Evans, however, remained perfectly still, his hands clasped in his lap, his gaze unwavering, his face a blank mask of professional patience. He was listening not for the truth, but for the inconsistencies, for the cracks in the story that would allow him to label it and file it away.

“And this club,” Evans interjected when she paused for breath, his voice a gravelly monotone. “The Alembic. You gained entry by lying about knowing a Julian Croft?”

“Yes,” Miriam said, her frustration rising. “It was the only way. He was there. The man from the coffee shop. The man they call Zehavit. I heard his name. Gavriel Zehavit.” She pushed on, the words tumbling out now, desperate to paint the full picture before they dismissed it. “He has an ice factory, a cold storage facility at the port. It’s not what it seems. It’s… a morgue. A place where he keeps things. There are corridors of freezers. I found a room, a lab. I found Leo Finch’s sketchbook.”

She saw the shift then, a subtle exchange of glances between the two detectives. Sharma’s pen had stopped moving. Evans leaned forward slightly, his weary expression hardening into one of gentle, clinical inquiry. “You found his sketchbook in a lab inside a cold storage facility run by a man who is the subject of an internet ghost story,” he repeated, not as a question, but as a statement of fact, a confirmation of the narrative’s departure from reality. “And where is this sketchbook now, Miriam?”

“He took it,” she whispered, the defeat a bitter taste in her mouth. “When he attacked me. After I escaped. He took it back. It was the proof.”

“I see,” Evans said, the two words a death sentence for her credibility. He sat back, his role as an investigator complete, his role as a manager of a disturbed victim now taking precedence. “Miriam, we sent a patrol car to the factory, Zehavit Cold Logistics. It’s a legitimate business. State of the art. They handle frozen food and pharmaceuticals. We spoke to the night manager. Nothing out of the ordinary. We’ve run the name Gavriel Zehavit. There are a few, but none with any connection to Brighton. As for the online rumours… we are aware of them. It’s a bogeyman story, ma’am. A meme.”

Every word was a brick in a wall they were building around her, sealing her inside her own unbelievable truth. “The worker saw him,” she insisted, her voice trembling with a desperate, rising panic. “He saw the assault.”

“He saw a man in a suit attacking you, yes,” Sharma said gently, taking over. “His description was good. Tall, silver hair, expensive clothes. We’re treating this as a very serious assault. We’re checking CCTV from all around the port. But the rest of it… the secret society, the factory… it’s a lot to take in.” She was trying to be kind, to separate the ‘real’ crime from the ‘delusion’. But Miriam knew they were one and the same. To discard one was to make the other unsolvable. The monster would remain a phantom, an anonymous man in a suit, and he would walk away clean.The silence they left behind was louder than the siren, more oppressive than the antiseptic smell of the ward. DS Evans’s final, pitying look was a judgment that would haunt her more than any of Zehavit’s physical blows. They had taken her story, broken it into two neat pieces—the credible assault and the fantastic delusion—and discarded the part that mattered. They were searching for a man in a suit who had committed a random act of violence. They were not looking for a philosopher of ambition, a curator of broken dreams. They were not looking for Gavriel Zehavit. Miriam lay back against the stiff pillows, the clean sheets a mockery of the filth she still felt clinging to her soul, and stared at the acoustic tile ceiling, counting the perforations. Each one a missing boy, a stolen future, a story filed away as a runaway or a misadventure. She was just another hole in the pattern.

Her body was a prison of pain, but the pain was a comfort. It was a testament. It was the one piece of evidence they couldn’t dismiss. Every throb of her shoulder, every grinding ache in her ankle, was a grain of truth. She had not imagined the fall down the metal stairs, the crushing grip on her leg, the final, desperate struggle in the cold dawn light. And if that was real, then all of it was real. The white corridor. The frozen doors. The sketchbook. The name on the screen: Daniel Kaufman. *COMPROMISED*. The word echoed not with the finality of an ending, but with the chilling ambiguity of a process begun. What did it mean to be disposed of? She had assumed the worst, a physical erasure to match the digital one. But Zehavit was a creature of psychological, not just physical, cruelty. Perhaps his disposal was something more subtle, more insidious. A slow ruin. A public humiliation. An orchestrated descent into madness that would discredit him forever. She realised with a fresh wave of nausea that she didn't know if her son was dead or alive, and she wasn't sure which was worse.

There was only one person who could bridge the gap between her world and Daniel’s. The thought of the conversation was an exhaustion she felt deep in her bones, another penance to be paid. She pressed the call button for the nurse. “My phone,” she said, her voice a dry rasp. “The police… they had my things.” The nurse was hesitant, but something in Miriam’s eyes—a hard, unyielding desperation that had no room for argument—made her nod and leave to make the inquiry. An hour later, a clear plastic bag was delivered to her bedside. Inside was a damp, grimy smear of a phone. Miriam dried it carefully on a corner of the hospital blanket, plugged in the charger the nurse provided, and willed it to life. When the screen flickered on, she ignored the dozen missed calls from numbers she didn't recognise—the police, the hospital—and found the one name she was looking for. She pressed the call button, the synthetic ringing a familiar prelude to a familiar pain.

“Mum? Oh, thank God. I’ve been calling for hours. The police left me a message. They said you were at the Royal Sussex. What happened? Are you okay?” Rachel’s voice was a torrent of frantic, genuine concern, the irritation and weariness entirely absent. For a moment, Miriam was so overwhelmed by the simple, uncomplicated sound of her daughter’s love that she couldn’t speak.

“I’m… alive,” Miriam managed, the word feeling both inadequate and miraculously true. “I was attacked. I’m okay. Banged up, but okay.”

“Attacked? By who? Where? The police were so vague. They just said there was an incident at the port.” Rachel’s questions were rapid-fire, desperate for facts to anchor her fear.

Miriam closed her eyes, knowing she was at a precipice. The truth, or a palatable version of it? “Rachel, I don’t have time to explain it all now. I need you to do something for me. It’s about Daniel.” The shift in subject was immediate, and Rachel’s tone cooled instantly, the old defences rising. “Mum, whatever this is…”

“No,” Miriam cut her off, her voice gaining a strength she didn’t know she possessed. “You have to listen to me. This isn’t one of my worlds of catastrophe. The world is a catastrophe. The man who did this to me… he knows Daniel. He was targeting him.”

A heavy silence filled the line. Miriam could almost hear her daughter’s internal battle, the lifetime of learned skepticism warring with the raw fear in her mother’s voice. “What do you mean, ‘targeting’ him?” Rachel asked, her voice now a low, cautious whisper.

“His name is Gavriel Zehavit,” Miriam said, putting the name into the world, making it real for her daughter. “He’s the reason Leo Finch is missing. He preys on boys like them. Ambitious boys. He finds them, promises them the world, and then he destroys them. I saw it, Rachel. I saw Daniel’s name on his computer. He is in terrible, terrible danger.”

“Oh my God.” The whisper was barely audible. It was not a dismissal. It was the sound of a carefully constructed reality beginning to crack. “I… I’ve been trying to call Daniel all morning. Since the police called me. His phone is off. It just goes straight to voicemail.”

The words confirmed everything. *Disposed of*. It meant cutting him off, isolating him, making him a ghost. "He's alive," Miriam said, a conviction solidifying in her own mind. Zehavit wouldn't kill him, not straight away. His cruelty was more artistic than that. He would want Daniel to experience his own destruction. "He's alive, but we have to find him before Zehavit does whatever he has planned. Rachel, listen to me. Check your messages, your emails, anything. Look for any mention of a new investor, a patron, a meeting. Anything out of the ordinary in the last few weeks. I need a place to start.”

The next few hours were a blur of enforced stillness and frantic, remote activity. Rachel, now a terrified and efficient ally, became her hands and eyes in the outside world. She arrived at the hospital an hour later, her face pale, her eyes wide with a fear that mirrored Miriam’s own. The sight of her mother—the bruised face, the sling supporting her arm, the raw scrapes on her hands—demolished the last of her skepticism. This was not drama. This was damage. Sitting by the bedside, they worked. Rachel scrolled through her text history with her brother, searching for clues. There was nothing explicit, just a new swagger in Daniel’s messages from the past month. Vague talk of a ‘big fish’ being on the hook, of a ‘game-changing’ meeting. No names. No locations. He had been coached in secrecy. It was a dead end.

Defeated, Rachel slumped in the uncomfortable visitor’s chair. “It’s nothing, Mum. He was careful.”

Miriam stared at the ceiling, her mind racing, replaying every moment, searching for a thread she had missed. The police were a dead end. Daniel’s digital trail was a dead end. The sketchbook was gone. But Zehavit had made a mistake. He had believed himself so far above the world that he had spoken to her, had explained his grotesque philosophy. And in doing so, he had given her a gift. *I am a curator of moments that were too pure to survive this world.* He wasn’t just a murderer; he was a collector. And a collection implies a catalogue. It implies other pieces. Leo Finch was Specimen 114. That meant there were at least one hundred and thirteen others. One hundred and thirteen other boys, other families, other silent, unexplained disappearances and ruined lives. The police were looking for a single crime. She had to show them a pattern. She had to find the other numbers.

“His mother,” Miriam said, her voice quiet but clear.

“Whose mother?” Rachel asked, confused.

“Leo Finch’s mother. Her name was on the flyer. A plea to call. It gave a number.” She turned her head, her eyes locking on her daughter’s, a cold, clear purpose burning in them. “The police won’t listen to me. A hysterical mother is an anomaly. But two? Three? Ten? That’s a conspiracy. That’s a serial predator. You and I can’t find Daniel. But an army of grieving mothers? They can burn this whole city to the ground.”

The weariness, the pain, the defeat—it all receded, replaced by a single, sharp, clarifying thought. She was a runner, trained for endurance. This was not a sprint that was already over. It was a marathon, and she had only just passed the first mile marker. She reached for the phone. "Get me the number from that flyer, Rachel," she said, her voice no longer that of a victim, but of a strategist planning her first campaign. "The hunt starts now."
