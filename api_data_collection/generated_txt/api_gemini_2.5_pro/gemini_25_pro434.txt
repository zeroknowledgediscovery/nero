My internal chronometer, the precise clock that had governed my every action, was broken. There was only before Leo’s arrival, and after. I waited. When I could bear the waiting no longer, I went to the greenhouse. The doors slid open and the smell washed over me. It was weaker now, the aggressive sweetness having given way to a sour, acidic tang, like old vinegar. It was the smell of the end of a process, and the relief it brought was so profound it made my knees weak.

He arrived at 08:30:00. I watched his van approach through the glass, the familiar, sputtering engine a sound that was now woven into the fabric of my own existence. He emerged, clad in the same clean, grey uniform, a walking embodiment of the sterile new world we inhabited. He did not look towards the house. He walked directly to the greenhouse, his purpose clear and unwavering. He entered, and the air seemed to shift, to settle around him. He gave me a brief, almost imperceptible nod, a gesture that was neither greeting nor acknowledgment, but simply a registration of my presence in his workspace. I was an object, a fixture, no different from the potting bench or the coiled hoses. He walked past me, his gaze already scanning the environment, assessing the tasks for the day. He was the system now, and I was just a ghost haunting its periphery.

I stood there, useless, a machine without a function, my hands hanging empty at my sides. He had gone to the central altar, where the great flower was beginning its slow, spectacular collapse. The spathe, yesterday a proud, fleshy trumpet, was now visibly drooping, its edges curling inward like burnt paper. The spadix, the central spire, had lost its turgid, threatening posture; it was beginning to soften, to bend under its own weight. The heat it generated had dissipated, and it was now cool to the touch. Leo placed his palm against it, a diagnostic touch, confirming the death of the furnace. He took a reading with a digital thermometer, noted it in his black book, and then turned. He looked at me, at my uselessness, my paralysis. For a moment, I thought I saw a flicker of something in his eyes—irritation, perhaps—but it was gone as quickly as it appeared. He walked to a rack of tools and took down a soft-bristled brush and a small dustpan. He held them out to me. “The butterflies,” he said. His voice was flat, an instruction delivered to an automaton. “The wings are collecting in the drainage grates. They will cause a blockage.”

I took the tools. The brush was light, its bristles fine and soft. The dustpan was cheap, molded plastic. I walked to the nearest floor drain, a metal grate set into the concrete. As he had said, it was choked with the delicate, papery remains of the Blue Morphos. A month ago, their living presence had been a manufactured miracle, a testament to my power. Now, their corpses were a simple, practical problem of plumbing and hygiene. I knelt, the posture a painful echo of my collapse the day before, and began the task. I swept the fragile, iridescent dust into the pan. The wings crumbled at the slightest touch, their impossible blue turning to a grey, powdery smear. The work was simple, menial, and required a focus that was mercifully narrow. Sweep. Collect. Dump into a refuse sack. Repeat. My world, which had once encompassed global financial markets and the clandestine trade of exotic life, had shrunk to the four-inch diameter of a drainage grate and the brittle, beautiful dust of dead insects.

I worked for over an hour, moving from grate to grate, a silent, methodical janitor in a fallen paradise. Leo worked on the other side of the greenhouse, tending to the orchids, his movements efficient and quiet. He did not check on me. He did not offer praise or correction. He had given me a task I could not fail at, and he had trusted me to complete it. The trust was not personal; it was practical. A simple machine, once reset, should be able to perform its basic function. At one point, I fumbled the dustpan, spilling a small cloud of blue dust onto the clean concrete. My first instinct was a spike of panic, a fear of his disapproval. But when I looked up, he was not even looking in my direction. My failure was my own, a small, insignificant error in a system that had already sustained a catastrophic one. I simply swept it up again, my movements a little more careful, a little more deliberate. I was learning. Not about botany, not about life, but about the simple, physical consequence of my own clumsiness.

When I had finished, I stood with the full refuse sack in my hand, once again at a loss. Leo, as if sensing the cessation of my work, turned. He gestured towards a large bin by the door. I placed the sack inside. I had completed my task. I was once again a machine at idle, waiting for the next command. He looked at the flower, then at me. “It will begin to break down soon,” he said. “The stalk will lose its integrity. It will fall. We need to clear a perimeter. Move the smaller pots from the surrounding beds. We will need the loppers and the saw.” He was already planning the logistics of the dismantling, the dissection of the corpse. There was no sentiment in his voice, no hint of the awe the flower had once inspired. It was now just a problem of mass and volume, a piece of biological waste to be managed and disposed of. He walked towards the tool cabinet, and I, his silent, broken apprentice, followed in his wake, ready to receive my next simple, terrible instruction.The tool cabinet was a small, metal world of grim purpose. Leo unlocked it and the door swung open with a well-oiled sigh, revealing rows of implements hanging in their designated slots, their steel edges gleaming dully under the grow lights. These were not the flimsy, ergonomic tools of a hobbyist; they were the heavy, worn instruments of a man who waged a daily, physical war with the earth. Leo took down a pair of long-handled loppers, their twin blades wickedly curved like the beak of a raptor. He then reached for a pruning saw, its serrated teeth sharp and hungry. He held it for a moment, then turned and offered it to me, handle first. I took it. The weight of it was a shock, a dense, solid reality in my trembling hand. The handle, made of a hard, composite rubber, was textured for grip, a grip I felt entirely unequal to. It was a tool designed for destruction, for the clean, efficient severing of living tissue. I had raised a spade in a fit of theatrical rage; this was an instrument of cold, sober execution.

Our first task was to create a sterile field for the autopsy. Under Leo’s silent direction, we began to move the dozens of smaller pots that surrounded the central altar. I lifted a heavy terracotta pot containing a bird of paradise, its soil damp and dense. The strain shot up my arms and into my back, a chorus of protest from muscles that had spent a lifetime in managed, climate-controlled atrophy. I grunted, an involuntary, animal sound of effort. I set the pot down clumsily, my pristine trousers smudged with wet earth. Leo moved with a fluid, relentless efficiency, lifting and placing two pots for every one of mine, his movements a silent rebuke to my own physical incompetence. He did not speak, directing the operation with a simple nod of his head or a point of his finger, indicating where the next pot should go. The air between us was devoid of anger, but also of any warmth. It was the functional, wordless space between two labourers engaged in a difficult, shared task.

When the perimeter was clear, we stood before the fallen god. The great flower was a pathetic spectacle, its magnificent, frilled spathe now limp and folded, its colour fading to a bruised, mottled grey. The central spadix, once an erect pillar of thermogenic power, now sagged, bent at a sad, defeated angle. It looked like a collapsed lung. Leo circled it slowly, his eyes narrowed, assessing its structural weaknesses, planning his attack. He was a butcher contemplating a carcass, his mind focused entirely on the practical problem of its disassembly. He finally stopped and looked at me, his gaze clear and direct. “We’ll take it down in sections, from the top,” he said. His voice was calm, the voice of a foreman laying out a simple but dangerous job. “When I make the cut, the top section will fall. It’s heavy, mostly water. You just need to guide it down onto the tarpaulin. Don’t try to catch it. Just guide it. Understand?” I nodded, the simple, binary clarity of the instruction a lifeline in the fog of my own uselessness.

He positioned himself, planting his feet wide, and raised the long-handled loppers. He found his purchase high on the stalk, where the flower began to join the spadix. He squeezed the handles. The muscles in his forearms and shoulders bunched, a display of controlled, physical power. There was a sickening, wet crunch, the sound of a thousand tiny fibres tearing at once. It was not a clean cut. A viscous, pale sap, the consistency of thin gruel, began to ooze from the wound, running down the mottled stalk in slow, sticky rivulets. The section sagged, then tore free with a final, ripping sound, and began to topple. I reacted on pure instinct, my hands coming up to guide the falling mass as he had instructed. The weight of it was staggering, a dead, wet heaviness that almost took me off my feet. It landed on the tarpaulin with a thick, percussive slap, the sound of a side of beef being dropped onto a table. A new smell filled the air, supplanting the carrion perfume. It was a green, vegetal smell, like crushed celery and damp, rotting leaves, the smell of the plant’s inner, secret life now exposed to the air.

“Now this part,” he said, pointing with the loppers to a section of the main stalk, a piece as thick as my thigh. “The loppers won’t get through this. You’ll need the saw.” He waited. I moved into position, my heart hammering against my ribs. I placed the teeth of the saw against the fleshy stalk. It felt like pressing a blade against the flank of a great, dead animal. My first few strokes were clumsy, the saw catching and stuttering, my arms weak and uncoordinated. “No,” Leo said, his voice sharp with impatience. He stepped behind me, his body close, his presence an overwhelming field of heat and competence. He placed his hand over mine on the saw’s handle, his grip firm, enveloping. “Let the saw work,” he said, his voice low and close to my ear. “Long, smooth strokes. Don’t force it.” He guided my arm, and the saw bit into the stalk. We fell into a two-man rhythm, a grim, rhythmic sawing that was a grotesque parody of our earlier, happier labours. The blade moved back and forth, back and forth, spewing a wet, green pulp onto our boots. I could feel the steady pulse of his body against my back, the strength in his arm transferring into mine. I was a puppet, an extension of his will, and in that moment of complete surrender, the saw moved freely, and the work became, for a moment, almost easy.

The day bled away in a gruelling marathon of physical labour. We cut and sawed and hauled, reducing the magnificent, towering structure to a heap of obscene, fleshy chunks on the blue tarpaulin. My body screamed in protest. My hands were raw, the beginnings of blisters forming on my soft, uncalloused palms. My back was a single, solid knot of pain. I was drenched in sweat and smeared with the sticky, green-brown fluids of the dissected flower. I was a wreck, a ruin of a man, but as I worked, a strange clarity began to emerge. Each pull of the saw, each lift of a heavy, water-logged piece of stalk, was a simple, physical truth. It was heavy. It was difficult. It smelled. These were not concepts or theories; they were raw, irrefutable data points being fed directly into my exhausted body. I had spent my life in the clean, abstract world of numbers and ideas. This was the world of things, and it was brutal, unforgiving, and utterly, undeniably real.

Finally, it was done. The great altar stood bare, stained and empty, a desecrated throne. The air was thick with the earthy smell of decay, the smell of a compost heap, of life breaking down into its constituent parts. We stood side-by-side, two figures spattered with the gore of our shared work, and looked at the pile of organic debris. It was a pathetic, ignominious end for a god. Leo surveyed the pile, his face grim but satisfied, the look of a man who has completed a hard, unpleasant, but necessary job. “We’ll leave it overnight,” he said, his voice raspy with exhaustion. “Let the rest of the water drain out. It’ll be lighter to bag tomorrow.” He turned and walked to the small sink in the corner to wash his hands, leaving me alone with the dismembered corpse of my ambition. I looked down at my own hands. They were filthy, scratched, and blistered, the nails caked with a black-green grime. I felt the deep, aching exhaustion in my muscles, a profound, physical emptiness. It was the simple, honest emptiness of a body pushed to its limit. And it felt more real, more true, than anything I had ever known.I awoke in the dark, not to a thought, but to a sensation. Pain. It was a novel and extraordinarily detailed signal, a high-resolution map of my own physical inadequacy. Every muscle group, from my shoulders to my calves, was reporting a state of acute distress. To sit up was not a single, fluid motion, but a complex series of negotiated movements, each one accompanied by a groan that seemed to emanate from a stranger’s body. I stood, and the ache in my lower back was a solid, architectural presence, a new and unwelcome pillar supporting my frame. My body, which I had always treated as a transport and life-support system for my brain, was now the loudest thing in my world, its complaints drowning out the silent hum of the house. I had spent a lifetime observing systems from a distance; I had finally, brutally, become one, and its primary output was suffering.

When I entered the greenhouse, the air had changed. The aggressive, cloying perfume of the bloom was gone, replaced by a damp, earthy smell, the honest funk of large-scale decomposition. It was the smell of a forest floor, of a compost heap, of life returning to its constituent elements. It was no longer an accusation; it was simply a fact of organic chemistry. Leo was already there, his back to me. A row of thick, black, industrial-grade sacks lay at his feet. Between us, the blue tarpaulin was a field morgue, piled high with the pale, dismembered chunks of the Titan Arum. He was preparing for the final, ignominious disposal. He turned as I approached, his face unreadable in the morning light. He gestured to the pile with his head. There were no instructions, no clipboard. The task was self-evident.

The work was disgusting. The pieces of the flower, having rested overnight, were now slimy to the touch, their fibrous, water-logged interiors leaking a foul-smelling, brackish fluid. We had no shovels; the mass was too amorphous. We had to lift it with our hands. I bent to hoist a particularly large section of the stalk, using the same clumsy, back-straining technique as the day before. A bolt of white-hot pain shot through my lower back, and my legs trembled. The piece, heavy and slick, slipped from my grasp, falling back onto the pile with a wet, defeated slap. I froze, waiting for the reprimand, the sigh of impatience. Leo simply stopped his own work and watched me. “You’ll break yourself,” he said. His voice was flat, matter-of-fact, not unkind. “Watch.” He bent his own knees, keeping his back ramrod straight, and lifted a piece of comparable size. The strain was visible in his thighs, but his spine remained a solid, straight line. “Legs,” he said, setting the piece down in a sack. “Not your back. It’s a machine for lifting. Use it properly.” He turned back to his work. It was the most profound piece of instruction I had ever received. A simple, mechanical truth, delivered without judgment. I copied his posture, bent my knees, and lifted. The weight was still immense, but the pain was different now—a clean, honest burn in the large muscles of my legs, not the sharp, screaming protest of my spine. I had learned a lesson.

For hours, we worked in a state of shared, focused misery. The world shrank to the tarpaulin, the sacks, and the sickening weight of the next piece. The physical reality of the labour was a kind of brutalist meditation. It left no room for abstract thought. Guilt, regret, longing—these were luxuries my exhausted brain could no longer afford to process. There was only the ache in my thighs, the strain in my shoulders, the slick, slimy texture of the decaying plant matter, and the steady, rhythmic sound of our breathing in the humid air. We filled one bag, then another, then another, the pile of green-brown flesh shrinking, the row of black, bloated sacks growing. It was a grim, funereal production line, and we were its two silent, exhausted workers.

When the last piece was bagged and the tarpaulin lay empty, slick with a patina of green-brown slime, a new phase began. Leo uncoiled a high-pressure hose and handed me the nozzle, a heavy brass and steel implement with a trigger mechanism. “The altar,” he said. “And the tarpaulin. Get it clean.” He showed me how to engage the trigger, how to brace myself against the violent kick of the water jet. The force of it was astonishing, a focused, liquid fury that tore the slime from the slate and sent it streaming towards the drains. The noise was a deafening roar that filled the greenhouse, isolating me in a bubble of violent, cleansing sound. I worked my way across the altar, erasing the stains of our dissection, blasting away the last physical traces of the god we had murdered. The water soaked my clothes and ran down my face, mingling with my own sweat. It was a baptism by fire hose, a forced and brutal purification.

By the time the sun began to cast long shadows through the glass, we were finished. The altar was bare and clean, glistening wetly under the lights. The sacks of the flower’s remains were stacked by the door, a dark, funereal monument awaiting collection. The greenhouse was clean, but it was also empty. The vibrant, chaotic heart of it had been ripped out, leaving a sterile, geometric void. We stood in the centre of the space, two men soaked in water and exhaustion, surveying the scene. Leo began his end-of-day routine, wiping down the few tools we had used. Acting on an impulse I did not recognise as my own, I turned and left the greenhouse, my legs moving with a strange, new purpose. I went into the house, into my sterile kitchen, and took two glasses from a cabinet. I filled them with cold, filtered water from the tap. I returned to the greenhouse and held one out to him.

He stopped what he was doing and looked at the glass, then at my face. His eyes, for a long, unblinking moment, searched mine. He was looking for the angle, the calculation, the hidden clause in this new, simple transaction. He found nothing. I was simply a man, offering another man a glass of water. He took it. His calloused fingers brushed mine, and there was no jolt, only the simple, physical fact of contact. He drank the entire glass without stopping, his throat working, the sound of his swallowing loud in the quiet room. He lowered the empty glass and drew a deep breath. He looked at the glass, then back at me. “Thanks,” he said. The word was a small, smooth stone dropped into the vast, silent lake of everything that had happened between us. He handed the glass back. He said nothing more. He simply finished his tasks and walked out the door, into the evening. I stood alone in the clean, empty space, the cool, empty glass still in my hand, and watched him go. I did not know what would happen tomorrow. For the first time in my life, I had no data, no projections, no plan. I was simply an empty vessel, waiting to be filled. And the thought was not terrifying. It was just… quiet.
