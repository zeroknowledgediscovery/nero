The rain didn't so much fall as it seeped from the sky, a fine, persistent mist that clung to the Gothic spires of Burke Academy and turned the cobblestone paths into glistening, black mirrors. It was a day for ghosts and old books, and the library, with its vaulted ceilings and the hushed scent of decaying paper, was the most haunted place of all. This was where she felt most at home. Ullah wasn't her name, not the one on her birth certificate, but it was the one that fit. It had been given to her in her first year by a boy whose stolen copy of *The Aeneid* she'd located in under an hour, tracing its journey through three dorm rooms and a clandestine sale to a desperate classics major. The name stuck. Detective Ullah. It felt more real than her given name, which she kept folded away like a little-worn piece of formal clothing.

She sat not at a table, but in a recessed alcove on the third-floor mezzanine, a perch that gave her a near-panoramic view of the main reading room below. Her own textbook, a dense tome on post-war political theory, lay open but unread. Her focus was elsewhere, on the patterns of human movement, the subtle tells and twitches that wrote stories no one else bothered to read. She saw the furtive glances between the couple in the history section, the nervous leg-jiggle of the pre-med student cramming for an organic chemistry exam, the slow, almost funereal pace of Professor Abernathy as he browsed the periodicals. Everything was normal. And that, paradoxically, was what first pricked her attention. The normality was too loud, too deliberate. It was the forced calm of a waiting room.

The whispers started near the circulation desk. A low hum that she couldn't quite decipher, but the cadence was familiar. It was the sound of fresh gossip, a currency more valuable than any dollar on Burke's campus. She watched as two girls, their faces pale and earnest, spoke in low, urgent tones to the librarian, a woman whose placid expression rarely wavered. Today, it wavered. A small, almost imperceptible tightening around her eyes. Ullah leaned forward slightly, her own eyes narrowing. The object of their concern became clear when a third student joined them, his voice a stage whisper that carried in the acoustically perfect space. "…didn't show for his nine a.m. seminar. Or the eleven. Just… gone."

Professor Albright. Of course. Dr. Alistair Albright, the head of the Fine Arts department, a man so punctual you could set the campus clock tower by his arrival for morning coffee at the student union. He was a campus institution, known for his vibrant bow ties and his equally vibrant lectures on the Italian Renaissance, a subject he taught with a passion that bordered on religious fervor. For him to miss one class was unthinkable. To miss two was a cataclysm.

Ullah closed her textbook. The faint scent of vanilla and old paper rose from the pages. The quiet hum of the library was now laced with a new frequency, one of anxiety. She didn't move from her perch. She simply watched the ripples spread, cataloging the reactions. Shock. Disbelief. A certain morbid excitement. She felt a cold knot forming in her stomach, a familiar sensation that was equal parts dread and exhilaration. By lunchtime, the official story had been manufactured and distributed.
