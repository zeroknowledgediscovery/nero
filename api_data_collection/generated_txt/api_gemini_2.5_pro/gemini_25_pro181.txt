The air over the Atlantic was a placid, sterile thing. Arthur Dorries liked it that way. Up here, at thirty-seven thousand feet, the world was reduced to predictable metrics: airspeed, altitude, fuel consumption, the gentle hum of the Dreamliner’s twin GEnx engines. It was a world of order, a symphony of engineering he could conduct with minute, precise inputs. Below, the ocean was a wrinkled blue sheet, indifferent. The chaos of the land—the traffic, the noise, the messy, unpredictable lives of eight billion people—was a distant abstraction. He was a pilot. His job was to draw a clean, straight line from Point A to Point B, and for twenty-two years, that’s what he had done. He brought people home, or took them away from it.

Landing in Atlanta was always a descent back into reality. The sterile air gave way to a wet, heavy heat that clung to you the moment you stepped out of the climate-controlled jet bridge. Marietta, his small corner of the sprawling metro area, was even thicker with it. The air felt like warm, wet wool in your lungs. Summer had sunk its teeth into Georgia and refused to let go. Arthur drove his sensible sedan through streets lined with crepe myrtles, the cicadas thrumming a constant, high-pitched drone that was the season’s official soundtrack. His house was a simple brick ranch, identical to a dozen others on his street, a testament to a life built on stability, not flair.

The box was on his porch.

It wasn't an Amazon box, or Zappos, or any of the usual emblems of suburban commerce. It was a plain, brown cardboard box, about the size of a shoebox, sealed with an excessive amount of clear packing tape. There was no shipping label. No return address. Just his name, ARTHUR DORRIES, written in a stark, neat hand with a black marker. He felt a flicker of annoyance. A neighborhood kid’s prank, maybe. He nudged it with his foot. It was heavier than it looked. Not rattling, but a solid, dense weight. He picked it up, the humid air making the key in his hand feel slick as he fumbled with the lock on his front door.

Inside, the air conditioning was a welcome shock. He dropped his flight bag by the door and placed the box on the granite countertop in his kitchen. For a moment, he just stared at it. The neat, precise lettering. The obsessive taping. Something about it felt wrong. It was too deliberate. A pilot’s mind, his mind, was trained to see patterns, to recognize when an instrument reading was just a little off, a subtle prelude to a much larger problem. This box was a needle flickering in the red.

He got a utility knife from the junk drawer. The tape was thick, layered. He had to saw through it, the blade making a sticky, tearing sound. With each slice, a faint, coppery smell began to leak out. Not strong, but cloying and sweet. Like a butcher’s shop left to warm in the sun. He finally cut through the last of it and pulled the flaps open.

Inside, nestled on a bed of what looked like wood shavings, was a human hand.

Arthur didn't scream. The sound he made was a low, guttural gasp, his lungs seizing, expelling air they suddenly didn’t want. He stumbled back, knocking a barstool over with a clatter that sounded deafening in the sudden, roaring silence of his mind. It was a left hand. Pale. The fingers were curled slightly, as if reaching for something. On the third finger was a simple, elegant gold band with a single, small diamond. Eleanor’s ring. He’d helped her husband, Mark, pick it out twenty years ago.

His stomach revolted. He turned and retched into the kitchen sink, a violent, full-body spasm that left him trembling, gripping the cold steel rim. His mind was a frantic, blank static. It wasn't real. It was a prop. A joke. A grotesque, impossible joke. But the smell was real. And the ring. The ring was undeniably, horrifyingly real. It was Eleanor Vance’s hand. Eleanor, who taught AP History at Marietta High. Eleanor, who had come over for a barbecue just two weeks ago, laughing as she argued about the merits of charcoal versus gas grills.

After a timeless minute spent staring at the water swirling down the drain, he forced himself to look back at the box. Tucked beside the severed wrist was a small, folded piece of notepaper. His hand shook so badly he could barely pick it up. He unfolded it. The same neat, black-marker printing.

*A lesson in scarcity. What is most valuable is that which can be taken away. She was a good teacher. But the greedy learn best on an empty stomach.*

*Regards,*
*Guy*

The name was so benign. Guy. Not The Ripper, not The Shadow, not some grandiose moniker a killer might choose. Just Guy. It was the name of a neighbor, a coworker, an everyman. And it was the most terrifying thing Arthur had ever read. He fumbled for his phone, his thumb smearing the screen as he tried to dial 911. His voice, when he finally spoke to the dispatcher, was a broken, unrecognizable croak.

The two days that followed were a blur of flashing lights, solemn-faced detectives, and the quiet, invasive hum of a house no longer his own. They took the box. They took his statement, then took it again. Detective Harding, a man whose weary eyes seemed to have absorbed all the city's misery, asked the questions with a practiced, gentle tone.

“Mr. Dorries, did Mrs. Vance have any enemies? Anyone who might have held a grudge?”

Arthur sat at his own dining room table, a cup of untouched coffee cooling in front of him. “Eleanor? No. Everybody loved Eleanor. She was… she was light. She made everything better.”

“Disgruntled students? An ex-partner?”

“No. Her students adored her. She’s been married to Mark for twenty years. They were happy.” He stopped. The past tense felt like a physical blow. *Were.*

Harding sighed, rubbing the bridge of his nose. “The note mentioned greed. Was she wealthy? Involved in any financial dealings?”

“She was a teacher, detective. Mark’s a CPA. They were comfortable. Not wealthy. The greediest thing Eleanor ever did was take the last brownie from the pan.” The memory, so mundane and warm, brought a fresh wave of nausea.

The police were thorough. They were professional. But Arthur could see it in their eyes. This wasn't a crime of passion. It wasn't a robbery gone wrong. This was something else. This was a monster, and they were hunting in the dark. They had no leads. Eleanor had left school at her usual time. Her car was found in a grocery store parking lot three towns over. No witnesses. Mark was a ghost, a hollowed-out man shuffling through the motions of grief. Arthur sat with him for hours, the silence in Mark’s living room thick with memories. They didn’t talk much. There was nothing to say. How do you comfort a man whose wife was murdered, butchered, and eaten? What words exist for that?

It was on the third night, alone in his silent house, that the pilot in Arthur began to surface through the grief. He sat at his desk, the note—a high-resolution police photograph of it—displayed on his monitor. *A lesson in scarcity.* The words circled in his head. It was a message. Not just a taunt. He looked at the picture of the note again. The handwriting was so neat. Precise. Controlled. Not the scrawl of a madman. It was the writing of someone who believed, utterly, in what they were doing. Someone who saw this horror as a form of instruction. *A lesson.*

The deaths were disparate, unconnected data points. But the rate was increasing. Chicago. Manchester. Now Marietta. The time between them was shrinking. It was exponential.

Arthur Dorries, a pilot from Marietta, who spent his life creating order in the sky, looked at the chaos blooming across his screen. The police were looking for a killer. They were wrong.
