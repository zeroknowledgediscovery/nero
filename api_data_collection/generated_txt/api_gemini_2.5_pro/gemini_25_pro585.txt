The muck of the Trinity River was a perfume Fester had learned to love. It was a complex scent, a rich bouquet of decaying cattails, catfish slime, and the faintest, almost pleasant tang of diesel runoff from the 7th Street bridge above. It was home. His home, a hollowed-out section of concrete abutment, was cool and damp even when the Fort Worth sun was trying its best to bake the world into a brittle cracker. Green light, filtered through the sluggish river water, painted shifting patterns on the mossy walls. This was his kingdom, a kingdom of rust and shadow.

He scratched an itch behind his large, pointed ear with a long, grimy toenail. Mornings were for inventory. He scuttled on calloused, three-toed feet over to the Gleam Pile, his life’s work. A pyramid of treasures scavenged from the river and the careless world above. There was the holy grail: a near-perfectly round hubcap from a '78 Buick, polished to a dull shine. There were bottle caps by the hundreds, sorted by color and brand. A small mountain of iridescent beetle carapaces. His prize, however, was a single, unbroken blue glass insulator from an old telephone pole, which, when the afternoon sun hit it just right, would cast a sliver of heavenly sapphire light across his moss bed.

“Look at it, Barty,” Fester rasped, his voice like gravel in a tin can. He held up a newly acquired Dr. Pepper cap, its red paint only slightly faded. “Classic logo. Pre-swirl. A real beaut.”

From his perch on a specially constructed shelf, Bartholomew the guppy stared out from his pickle jar. A single, large bubble escaped his lips and wobbled to the surface. *Bloop*.

“Yeah, I know. Not as good as the Big Red from last week, but it’s an honest find.” Fester carefully placed the cap in its designated spot. Bartholomew was more than a pet; he was a confidante, a sounding board, an oracle whose bubbly pronouncements Fester had learned to interpret with uncanny accuracy. A single, slow *bloop* was contemplative agreement. A frantic stream of tiny bubbles was a warning. A bubble followed by a flick of the tail was derision. Barty was rarely wrong.

Fester carried the pickle jar, nestled in a custom-made macrame sling, over to the river’s edge. He dipped the jar in, letting a fresh swirl of murky water replace the old. Barty zipped around his glass prison, tasting the new currents. He suddenly stopped, his tiny body rigid. A frantic cascade of bubbles erupted from his mouth. *Blip-blip-blip-blip-bloop-BLIP!*

“Whoa there, fella. What is it?” Fester squinted, his large, yellow eyes scanning the water. The current was wrong. It was slower, more stagnant than it should be after last night's sprinkle. And the smell… the familiar perfume of the river was tainted. An acrid, plasticky note soured the air, a scent as alien and offensive to Fester’s nose as cheap cologne. He sniffed again, wrinkling his flat nose until it nearly touched his upper lip. “That ain’t right. That ain't right a'tall.”

He set Barty’s jar down carefully on a dry patch of concrete and began to shamble upstream, toward the big drainage culvert that was his primary source of fresh flotsam. The culvert was usually a gushing font of possibility, spitting out whatever the city’s storm drains had swallowed. A lost shoe. A half-eaten sandwich. Once, a wallet with three whole dollars in it, which he’d traded to a sewer rat named Scrim for a waterproof watch that only ran backwards. Today, it was silent. The gushing had been reduced to a pathetic trickle.

The mouth of the culvert was choked. A monstrous, multi-colored plug of plastic bags, tens, maybe hundreds of them, were jammed into the opening. They were slick with green-brown algae and swelled with trapped, stagnant water, like grotesque blisters. A wave of the foul, chemical smell washed over him. It was the smell of eternal, unnatural decay.

“For the love of rust,” he muttered. This was more than an inconvenience; this was a blockage of his entire supply line. Annoyance, hot and sharp, pricked at him. He grabbed the nearest bag, a faded white one with cheerful blue letters thanking him for his patronage. He pulled. The plastic stretched, unnervingly strong. With a great, grunting heave, he tore it free. It came away with a wet, sucking sound, followed by a dozen more bags that had been tangled with it. A torrent of foul, trapped water gushed out, soaking his feet and releasing an even more potent wave of the stench.

He stumbled back, gagging. This was no accident. This was not the random accumulation of city litter. He peered closer at the mass still plugging the tunnel. Each bag was connected to another, not just by chance tangles, but by a specific, intricate knot. A series of tight, looping hitches that bound the flimsy plastic into a cohesive, suffocating whole. A net. A wall. Someone had done this. Deliberately.

A low hiss came from a pile of rocks nearby. “Trouble, Fester? Your treasure-hole run dry?”

Fester turned to see the long, scaly head of Silas, a water moccasin whose territory overlapped his own. They maintained a truce born of mutual respect and the understanding that Silas couldn’t swallow him and Fester couldn’t be bothered to try and skin him.

“It’s plugged,” Fester grumbled, kicking at a loose bag. “Someone’s stuffing the drains with this… this garbage.”

Silas slithered closer, his forked tongue tasting the air. “Not just the drains. The rustling blight. I’ve seen it upriver. Nests of it in the reeds. It smothers everything. Scares the frogs. The taste of the water is wrong. My hunting is sparse.” The snake’s black, beady eyes were fixed on the knotted bags. “It whispers in the wind. A sound that doesn’t belong.”

The rustling blight. That’s what it was. A creeping, crinkling sickness spreading through his world. This wasn’t just about his Gleam Pile anymore. This was an invasion. The cheerful blue letters on the bag at his feet seemed to mock him. *HAVE A NICE DAY*.

He looked from the choked culvert to the disturbed water of the river. He heard Barty, even from a distance, let out another series of frantic, warning bubbles. This was bigger than a bit of litter. This was a statement. An act of aggression. And Fester, for the first time in a long while, felt something other than grumpy contentment. He felt a slow, burning anger. He was a goblin who just wanted to be left alone with his trash and his fish, but someone had decided to mess with his trash. And that, that was a declaration of war.

He stomped back to his abutment, his mind racing. Who would do this? Why? What kind of creature finds joy in strangling a river with plastic? He grabbed a tattered leather satchel, a relic from a discarded purse. Inside went the essentials: a spoon sharpened to a wicked point on one edge, a coil of copper wire he’d stripped from a generator, and for sustenance, a piece of beef jerky so old and hard it could double as a club. He gently secured Barty’s jar, the macrame sling creaking under the weight, and settled it over his shoulder. Barty swirled, his fins beating a steady, determined rhythm. *Bloop-bloop*. A bubble of resolve.

“Alright, Barty,” Fester said, adjusting the strap. “Silas called it a blight. Let’s go see what kind of weed needs pulling.”

He didn’t look back at his Gleam Pile, at the comfortable gloom of his home. He turned his face toward the choked culvert, toward the darkness of the storm drain system that lay beyond it. The air that seeped from the tunnel was cold and smelled of filth and manufactured permanence. Fester bared his yellow teeth in a snarl that was half grimace, half grin. He was a hero in the way a grumpy janitor is a hero when he finally decides to unclog the main sewer line—not for glory, not for justice, but because the whole place was starting to stink, and somebody had to do something about it. With a final, disgusted sniff, Fester and his guppy sidekick plunged into the suffocating dark.

The tunnel was a perfect cylinder of concrete, slick with a film of something Fester didn't want to identify. The only light came from the entrance behind him, a rapidly shrinking grey circle. The only sound was the drip-drip-drip of unseen water and the soft, unsettling crinkle of his own feet stepping on stray pieces of plastic that had broken free from the main plug. The air was thick, heavy, and tasted of mildew and polyethylene. Barty’s jar swung rhythmically against his hip, a reassuring weight in the oppressive blackness. The little guppy was still, conserving his energy, his large eye a single, luminous pearl in the gloom.

Fester’s eyes, built for darkness, adjusted quickly. He could see the faint lines in the concrete, the dark streaks of slime, the ghostly shapes of more plastic bags, snagged on imperfections in the tunnel wall. And all of them, every single one, was tied with that same infuriatingly neat knot. It was a signature. A calling card. This wasn't the work of some bored teenager or a random act of pollution. This was organized. Methodical. The thought sent a chill down his spine that had nothing to do with the damp air.

He followed the main pipe for what felt like miles, his goblin-sense of direction guiding him through the subterranean labyrinth. He took a left at a junction where the stench of soured milk was particularly strong, then a right where the dripping sound changed from a 'plink' to a 'plonk'. These were the maps of his world, written in decay and resonance. After an hour of sloshing through ankle-deep, foul-smelling water, he saw a change. A faint, unnatural glow emanated from a smaller tributary pipe ahead. It wasn't the grey light of a sewer grate or the warm light of a human dwelling. It was a flickering, sickly yellow-green, and it was accompanied by a sound.

A rustling.

Silas had called it the rustling blight. It was a sound like a thousand dry leaves skittering across pavement, but without the satisfying crunch of nature. It was a thin, papery, synthetic sound. The sound of a million plastic bags stirred by a faint, fetid breeze. Fester’s hand went to the sharpened spoon in his satchel. He slowed his pace, his soft, calloused feet making no sound in the shallow water. He held his breath.

He peered around the edge of the tributary junction. The pipe opened into a wider chamber, a forgotten cistern from some long-abandoned city works project. And it was full. The floor was a sea of plastic bags, a roiling, shifting landscape of white and blue and red and grey. In the center of the chamber, a pile of the bags rose into a crude throne. And sitting on that throne was another goblin.

He was leaner than Fester, with skin the color of a fresh bruise. He wore a vest stitched together from dozens of colorful plastic bag logos—the red bullseye of Target, the yellow sunburst of Walmart, the stark blue of Best Buy. His fingers, long and nimble, were weaving more bags together, his movements fast and precise, creating another length of the knotted plastic rope. The sickly light came from a collection of old jars filled with captured lightning bugs, their panicked flashes illuminating the ghastly scene. The goblin was humming, a tuneless, nasal drone that was occasionally punctuated by the sharp snap of a plastic bag being stretched taut.

This had to be the source. The architect of the blight. Fester ducked back behind the wall, his heart pounding a nervous rhythm against his ribs. He had found the what, and now he had found the who. The question was why. Why would a goblin, a creature of the earth and the damp, of rust and good, honest decay, surround himself with this sterile, immortal filth?

He risked another peek. The goblin on the throne, Crinkle, Fester named him in his mind, held up a newly finished length of knotted plastic. He admired it, turning it over and over, his eyes gleaming with a kind of adoration Fester had only ever reserved for a particularly fine piece of sea glass or a perfectly rusted cog. Crinkle draped the new rope over his shoulder like a sash and then reached into a massive burlap sack at the foot of his throne. He pulled out not a piece of scavenged metal, not a moldy bit of bread, but a pristine, still-crisp head of lettuce, sealed in a transparent plastic bag from a supermarket.

Crinkle tore the bag open with his teeth and began to eat the lettuce, not with the gusto of a hungry goblin, but with a detached, almost clinical air. When he was done, he didn't toss the crumpled bag onto the floor. Instead, he smoothed it out with reverent care. He folded it into a neat square, his movements filled with a disturbing tenderness. He then tucked the folded square into a pocket on his vest. He valued the container more than the contents.

Fester felt a wave of profound nausea. This was worse than he thought. This wasn’t just malice. This was madness. A deep, fundamental wrongness that twisted the very nature of what it was to be a goblin. Theirs was a world of the discarded, the forgotten, the things that broke down and returned to the muck. This… this was a cult of permanence. An obsession with the things that never rotted, that would outlive them all, choking the world long after their own bones had turned to dust.

In his jar, Barty flicked his tail. A sharp, angry flick. A bubble of pure indignation. *BLOOP*. It was the most aggressive sound Fester had ever heard him make.

“I know,” Fester whispered, his hand tightening on the pickle jar. He looked at the goblin on his throne of trash, at the endless sea of rustling plastic, at the pathetic, flickering light of the captive fireflies. He had come down here expecting to clear a clog, to teach some litterbug a lesson. But this was no mere litterbug. This was a prophet of a new and terrible religion, and his church was spreading. Fester knew, with a certainty that settled in his gut like a cold stone, that he couldn't just walk away. The stink of it was already on him. He had to do something. The question was, what could one grumpy goblin and his guppy do against an entire ocean of plastic?A plan. He needed a plan. Goblins were not fighters; they were scavengers, tinkers, and survivors. They outwitted, they out-waited, they out-rusted their problems. Charging into that rustling sea of wrongness with a sharpened spoon was a good way to get tangled and smothered, to become another lump in the plastic landscape. Fester shrank back into the tributary pipe, the cold concrete pressing against his back. He needed to see the whole picture. He decided to circumnavigate the chamber, using the network of smaller pipes and shadowy ledges as his concealed highway. He moved with a practiced stealth, his three-toed feet finding silent purchase on slick, algae-furred surfaces.

As he crept along the curving wall of the cistern, the true scope of the blight became horribly clear. The chamber wasn't just a dumping ground; it was a workshop. Smaller piles of plastic were sorted by type and color along the edges. He saw mounds of translucent milk jugs, dunes of green soda bottles, and tangled nets of the ubiquitous grocery bags. And there was life here, of a sort. Scurrying between the plastic drifts were rats, but not the sleek, healthy sewer rats he knew. These creatures were mangy and twitchy, their fur matted with filth and melted-on scraps of plastic. They nested not in warm piles of rotted cloth and paper, but in crinkling dens of woven bags. They were Crinkle's acolytes, his congregation, corrupted by the very refuse they lived in.

From a new vantage point, high on a crumbling ledge, Fester could see the back of the throne. It wasn't just a heap of trash; it was an engineering marvel of madness. The knotted plastic ropes were woven around a central, load-bearing pillar of rusted iron, forming a complex, semi-rigid structure. More importantly, he saw that the throne was not just a seat; it was the keystone of a massive dam. The collected water, denied its usual path, was being forced into a single, much smaller channel that led off into a dark, unknown section of the underworks. Crinkle wasn't just stopping the flow; he was concentrating it. He was weaponizing the river’s water, but for what purpose, Fester couldn’t fathom. It was a scheme of terrifying complexity and patience.

He ducked down as Crinkle stood up from his throne and stretched, his long arms reaching for the cavern ceiling. The goblin clapped his hands twice, a sharp, flat sound that echoed in the chamber. In response, a half-dozen of the plastic-pelted rats scurried forward, chittering expectantly. Crinkle reached into his burlap sack again and tossed them a handful of something Fester couldn't make out. The rats fell upon it, squealing and fighting, their feast a frenzy of tiny, synthetic crinkles. They weren't just neighbors; they were his subjects. His army. Fester felt a profound sense of being out of his league. He was just one goblin. A very annoyed goblin, but one goblin nonetheless. He looked down at his meager satchel. The spoon was for prying, the jerky was for chewing, and the wire... the copper wire suddenly felt very important.

As he watched the diverted water surging into the narrow channel, a new, more immediate problem became apparent. The water level in the main cistern was rising. It was slow, a barely perceptible creep up the grimy walls, but it was undeniable. The main drain was so effectively plugged that the entire chamber was beginning to fill like a colossal bathtub with a clogged drain. He glanced at the entrance to his own tributary pipe; the water was already lapping at the bottom edge. Soon, his way out would be submerged. The chamber would become a watery tomb, for him, for the mad goblin, for the rats, for all of it. The ticking clock wasn't a metaphor; it was the inexorable rise of the city's rejected water. Fester suddenly felt a cold knot of panic in his stomach. He had to act, and he had to act now.Panic was a luxury Fester rarely afforded himself. It was inefficient, noisy, and accomplished nothing. But as he watched the grimy waterline swallow another rust stain on the cistern wall, a cold, frantic energy seized him. He was in a bottle, and the water was rising. Crinkle’s madness was about to become his tomb. He scrambled back from the ledge, his mind racing faster than the twitchiest sewer rat. He couldn't fight, he couldn't wait. He had to think. He had to be a goblin. He had to bring things down. He patted his satchel, the lumpy shapes within a sudden comfort. The spoon, the jerky, the wire. Not an arsenal, but an inventory of possibilities.

His eyes darted from the woven plastic dam to the single iron pillar at its heart. That was the weak point. It had to be. The plastic was deathlessly strong, but the iron was old. It was mortal. It was covered in the beautiful, crumbling decay of rust. The plastic ropes were tied to it, using the pillar's strength to anchor the entire suffocating web. If he could sever those tethers, the weight of the trapped water would do the rest. The river would become its own salvation. But the knots were a madman’s masterpiece, too tight and numerous to undo. He would have to cut the ropes themselves.

He pulled the beef jerky from his satchel. It was a dark, petrified slab of meat, hard as a river stone and smelling vaguely of salt and ancient desperation. It was his emergency ration, but this was an emergency of the highest order. He crept to the edge of his tributary pipe, took aim, and with a flick of his wrist, sent the jerky sailing through the air. It landed with a wet smack on the far side of the chamber, skittering across a pile of plastic bottles. The effect was instantaneous. The chittering of Crinkle’s rat acolytes stopped, replaced by a collective, hungry sniff. Then, as one, the mangy horde surged away from the throne, a tide of fur and filth flowing toward the scent of the jerky. A vicious squabble erupted, a symphony of squeals and crinkling plastic that completely masked Fester’s own movements.

“Now’s the time, Barty,” he whispered, securing the guppy’s jar tightly under one arm. He slipped into the water, the foul liquid shockingly cold against his skin. He moved with agonizing slowness, half-wading, half-swimming through the floating debris. The rustling of the bags under the surface was like the whispering of ghosts. He kept his eyes locked on Crinkle, who was still on his throne, distracted by the rat commotion, a look of mild annoyance on his bruised face. He hadn't yet sensed the true threat. Fester reached the rusted iron pillar, his calloused feet finding purchase on its submerged base. It felt solid, yet he could feel the deep pits and flakes of rust under his toes. It was beautifully, blessedly imperfect.

He pulled out the coil of copper wire. He unwound a long section, his grimy fingers working with surprising dexterity. He chose the thickest of the plastic ropes, a monstrous braid of hundreds of bags that ran from the pillar to the very top of the dam. It was the main support, the linchpin of the whole structure. He looped the copper wire around it, pulling it taut and wrapping the ends around his hands. He took a deep breath, the air tasting of plastic and stagnant water, and began to pull, sawing the thin metal wire back and forth against the thick plastic braid.

At first, nothing happened. The plastic was slick and resilient. But Fester was persistent. He put his back into it, his muscles bunching, his teeth gritted. A thin line of smoke, smelling of acrid, burning chemicals, began to rise from the point of friction. The wire grew hot in his hands, but he ignored the searing pain. He sawed faster, a frantic, desperate rhythm. The copper wire bit deeper, melting its way through the layers of fused plastic. It was working. A deep, groaning sound echoed through the chamber, not from Fester, but from the dam itself, as the strain on the severed rope began to shift.

The sound cut through the squealing of the rats. On his throne, Crinkle’s head snapped up. His eyes, glinting in the sickly light of the firefly jars, scanned the chamber and found Fester clinging to the central pillar. For a moment, he just stared, his expression one of pure disbelief, as if a mushroom had suddenly decided to commit an act of architectural sabotage. Then, his face twisted into a mask of pure rage. He let out a piercing shriek, a sound of tearing plastic and fury, and launched himself from his throne. He didn't swim; he ran across the surface of the plastic sea, his light weight and wide feet keeping him aloft on the shifting trash.

Fester sawed with the strength of terror. The wire was more than halfway through. Barty’s jar was a blur of frantic bubbles, a constant stream of pure, liquid panic. *BlipblipblipblipBLIP!* Crinkle was closing in, his long, nimble fingers reaching, his humming drone replaced by a guttural snarl. “You ruin it! You ruin everything!” he screeched. Fester gave one last, monumental heave. With a sound like a pistol shot, the copper wire snapped. But it was enough. The plastic rope, its integrity compromised, tore apart with a deafening ripping sound.

The effect was cataclysmic. A deep, resonant boom shuddered through the cistern as the immense pressure of the water found its release. The plastic dam didn't just break; it exploded. The central pillar, Fester still clinging to it, shuddered as a wall of dark, churning water and plastic debris erupted from the breach. Crinkle, caught mid-stride, was swatted out of the air like a fly, disappearing into the maelstrom of his own filth. The throne of bags was torn asunder, its components scattered and swallowed by the torrent.

Fester held on for his life, the pickle jar clutched to his chest, as the floodwaters raged past him. The roar was deafening. He was submerged, spun, and battered by chunks of his own world being washed away—stray hubcaps, twisted bits of metal, and the endless, suffocating sheets of plastic. Then, as quickly as it began, the roar subsided. The water level dropped precipitously, the cistern draining with a great, gurgling sigh. Fester was left clinging to the pillar, soaked, gasping, and covered in slime, but alive. The chamber was scoured clean. The plastic sea was gone, flushed down into the deeper parts of the sewer system to become someone else's problem. All that remained were a few pathetic, sodden bags clinging to the walls. The sickly yellow-green light was gone; Crinkle's firefly jars had been swept away, their occupants presumably freed.

Fester slid down the pillar, his legs shaking, and landed in the ankle-deep muck of the now-empty cistern. He looked around. The air, while still foul, was clearer, the oppressive chemical tang replaced by the familiar scent of damp concrete and river water. He held up the pickle jar. Barty was floating placidly in the center, his fins barely moving. He let out a single, large bubble that wobbled slowly to the surface and popped with a quiet finality. *Bloop*. Contemplative agreement. The crisis was over. Fester scanned the chamber one last time. There was no sign of Crinkle. The mad goblin had been consumed by his own blight, washed away with the rest of the trash. Fester felt a grim satisfaction. He had come to fix a clog, and he had ended up flushing a cult. He was just a goblin who wanted to be left alone, but the world, it seemed, had other plans. With a weary sigh, he turned and began the long, sloshing walk back toward the light.The walk back was an entirely different journey. The water, which had been a stagnant, ankle-deep soup on the way in, was now a rushing, knee-high torrent of comparatively fresh river water. It pushed at Fester’s legs, eager to reclaim its territory, and carried with it the last vestiges of the plastic blight, which now tumbled and cartwheeled down the drain system like defeated soldiers in a chaotic retreat. The oppressive silence was gone, replaced by the healthy gurgle and splash of a functioning waterway. Fester slogged onward, his body a single, comprehensive ache, the adrenaline of the confrontation replaced by a profound, bone-deep weariness. In his sling, Barty was a silent observer, his tiny fish-brain perhaps contemplating the sudden and violent reordering of his universe, or perhaps just enjoying the ride.

He saw the circle of light long before he reached it. It grew from a pinprick to a coin, then a plate, and finally, the glorious, gaping mouth of the culvert. He stumbled out into the familiar green-tinged afternoon of his kingdom under the bridge, blinking as his eyes readjusted from the subterranean gloom. He took a deep, shuddering breath. The perfume was back. The rich, complex bouquet of decay, slime, and diesel was untainted, pure and perfect. The acrid, chemical ghost of plastic was gone, washed away by the river’s renewed vigor. It was the most beautiful smell in the world. He set Barty’s jar down on the mossy bank and simply stood for a long moment, letting the rightness of his home settle back over him.

His gaze fell upon the Gleam Pile. It had taken a hit. The surge of water from the culvert had plowed into the side of his life’s work, scattering the neat ranks of his bottle cap collection and washing a significant portion of his iridescent beetle carapaces back into the river from whence they came. A pang of his old, familiar annoyance pricked him. He grumbled and began to pick through the disarray, setting his pyramid right. But as he picked up the holy grail, the '78 Buick hubcap, and began to polish its dull shine with his sleeve, he felt a strange sense of detachment. Its roundness, once a source of deep satisfaction, now seemed… just round. He looked over at his prize, the blue glass insulator. The afternoon sun was hitting it, but the sliver of sapphire light it cast across his moss bed didn't feel quite so heavenly anymore. It was just a patch of blue. They were still his things, his treasures, but the battle in the dark had somehow tarnished their gleam.

“The water tastes of itself again.” The voice was a dry hiss from the river’s edge. Silas the water moccasin had emerged, his body a sleek, dark ribbon in the flowing current. His black eyes fixed on Fester. “The frogs are singing again. The blight is gone.” He tasted the air with his forked tongue, a gesture of confirmation. “You were gone a long time, Fester. Did your treasure-hole unclog itself?” Fester grunted, placing a misplaced Pepsi cap back in its proper place. He didn’t feel like recounting the whole sordid affair, the mad goblin, the rat army, the friction-fire and the flood. It sounded ridiculous, even to him. “There was a knot. I untangled it,” he rasped, which was, in its own way, the complete truth. Silas watched him for a long moment, the snake’s unblinking stare holding a kind of ancient wisdom that made Fester’s ears itch. The moccasin seemed to understand that the simplicity of the answer belied the complexity of the event. With a slight dip of his head, a gesture of respect Fester had never seen from him before, Silas slipped back into the water and was gone.

Later, as the sun began to dip below the horizon, painting the murky water in shades of orange and purple, Fester was conducting his final damage assessment. He was consolidating his remaining beetle carapaces into a smaller, sadder pile when he saw it. Wedged in a crack in the concrete, just above the new high-water mark, was a single piece of plastic. It wasn’t a faded, broken scrap like the ones he sometimes tolerated. It was a corner torn from a supermarket vegetable bag, and it was folded. Not crumpled or crushed, but deliberately folded into a neat, tight triangle, the way Crinkle had folded his lettuce bag. It was a signature. A remnant. A tiny, insignificant piece of trash that carried the weight of a terrible gospel. Crinkle may have been washed away, but his ideas, his obsession with permanence, his madness… a piece of it had washed up right on Fester’s doorstep.

Fester stared at the plastic triangle for a long time. The grumpy contentment he had cultivated for years felt like a distant memory, a luxury from a more ignorant time. He was a goblin who had wanted nothing more than to be the curator of his own quiet decay, but he had seen a rot that did not decay. He had faced a blight that promised to outlive rust itself. He picked up the sharpened spoon from his satchel, its edge glinting in the fading light. He looked at his Gleam Pile, no longer a monument to the past, but a resource cache. The copper wire, the broken tools, the sharp edges of tin and glass—they were not just treasures anymore. They were defenses. He was still the king of his small, damp kingdom, but his reign was no longer a peaceful one. He was a janitor, yes, but he now knew the filth was organized, and it would be back. With a grimace that was entirely devoid of any grin, Fester kicked the plastic triangle into the river, watching the current carry it away into the growing darkness. It was a small act of defiance, a promise. He would be ready.The weeks that followed the great flushing were deceptively quiet. The Trinity River flowed with a renewed, if still sluggish, purpose. The perfume of home returned, and the frogs resumed their nightly, throbbing chorus. Yet, a fundamental shift had occurred in Fester’s world, and more profoundly, within Fester himself. The grumpy contentment of the scavenger had been scoured away, leaving behind the grim vigilance of a sentry. Mornings were no longer for polishing hubcaps, but for patrols. He would leave the relative safety of his abutment, Barty’s jar secured in its sling, and follow the water’s edge for miles, his yellow eyes scanning not for treasure, but for sacrilege. Every piece of litter was now an object of suspicion, to be prodded with his sharpened spoon, examined for the tell-tale sign of a neat, tight knot.

He found it on a Tuesday, three miles downstream where the river bent sharply around a forgotten industrial park. It wasn't just a knot.
