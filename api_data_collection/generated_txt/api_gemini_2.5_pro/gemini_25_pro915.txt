The light in Markham had a peculiar quality, a thick, honeyed consistency that seemed to polish the world to an unbearable sheen. It was a benevolent, oppressive light, the kind that promised perfection and demanded it in return. Deirdre felt it on her skin like a weight. Here, in the part of town unironically christened ‘Angel’s Crest’ by its founding families—her family—you were not merely a resident. You were a component. A cog in a flawless, silent machine.

Her last name, Fairchild, was a benediction and a curse. The Fairchilds didn't just live in Markham; they had willed it into existence from farmland and ambition. They were the original angels, the architects of this suffocating paradise. And Deirdre, with her pale hair that caught the sun and a face that could have been sculpted for a cathedral wall, was their crowning achievement. The rebellion started small. A crack in the porcelain. It started with a drive. Caelan Croft. The name itself was a piece of grit in the town’s oyster. His family hadn't built Markham; they’d washed up in it, bringing with them the scent of motor oil and defiance.
