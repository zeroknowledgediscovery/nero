The cold had a way of finding the flaws. It worked its way into the frayed cuff of a glove, the pinhole in a worn boot, the unsealed seam of a window frame. For Sebastian, it was a professional constant, a diagnostic tool. On days like this, with the wind coming off the frozen canal like a slab of thrown ice, he could trace the plumbing of a house just by seeing where the frost bloomed thickest on the clapboard. He understood the city as a network of arteries and veins, some new and efficient copper, others sclerotic with the rust of a century. He was a surgeon of sorts, descending into the dark, damp bellies of Ottawa’s houses with a torch and a wrench to fix the blockages and the bursts.

Today it was a burst. A Victorian in the Glebe, all gables and pride, but its guts were shot. The copper pipe had split wide open in the crawlspace, a frozen geyser that had turned the earthen floor into a miniature glacier. He lay on his back on a sheet of muddy cardboard, the butane torch hissing a blue-white song, the smell of hot metal and flux filling his nostrils. It was a smell that clung to him, embedding itself in the skin of his hands and the fabric of his Carhartts, a smell he no longer noticed but that Elara always did. She’d wrinkle her nose when he came home, a playful, theatrical gesture. “You smell like the city’s secret passages again,” she’d say, and the complaint was a kind of welcome.

His hands, thick-fingered and crosshatched with a hundred tiny scars from pipe threads and sheet metal, moved with an unthinking grace. He sweat the new joint, the silver solder flowing into the seam like water, creating a perfect, permanent bond. He fixed things. It was what he did. He brought order to the chaotic underworld of domestic life. A stopped drain, a silent furnace, a leaking valve—they were all puzzles of pressure and flow, and he was good at solving them. It was a satisfaction that was clean and absolute. Unlike the mess in his own chest, a pressure for which there was no valve, a blockage he couldn't clear.

The drive home was a crawl through the five-o’clock dark. Snow, which had been threatening all day in the bruised-grey sky, began to fall in fat, wet flakes that smeared across his windshield. The wipers struggled to keep up, their rhythmic squeak a counterpoint to the drone of the truck’s heater. He passed the skeletal trees of Dow’s Lake, their branches iced like spun sugar under the sodium lamps. The canal was a long black scar, not yet frozen solid enough for the skaters. It was a city holding its breath, waiting for the real, deep winter to set in and lock everything down. He felt a kinship with it.

Their house in Hintonburg was a narrow semi-detached, identical to its neighbours save for the perpetually dying potted fern on the porch. Inside, the air was warm and smelled of his father’s pipe tobacco and his stepmother Marie’s over-brewed tea. Frank and Marie were fixtures in the living room, their faces lit by the blue flicker of the television, a game show host shouting questions about state capitals. They’d married eight years ago, when Sebastian was fourteen and Elara was thirteen, a late-in-life union of two solitudes that never quite merged. They orbited each other politely, their conversation a well-worn path of logistics and pleasantries. It left a vacuum in the house, a quiet space that he and Elara had rushed to fill, creating a world of their own.

“Seb? That you?” Marie’s voice, distracted, from the couch.

“Yeah, it’s me.” He kicked off his work boots by the door, the melting snow forming a dirty puddle on the mat. The familiar ache in his lower back from crouching in the crawlspace began to assert itself.

He went to the kitchen, the linoleum cold under his socks, and pulled a beer from the fridge. He leaned against the counter, the cold bottle a balm against his palm, and listened to the house. The manic energy of the television, the gentle click of his father’s lighter, the hum of the refrigerator. And then, another sound. The soft scratch of charcoal on paper from the small alcove off the dining room that Elara had claimed as her studio.

He moved toward the sound, drawn by a gravity he had long ago stopped fighting. The alcove was a mess of her own making, a beautiful chaos of stacked canvases, jars bristling with brushes, and the faint, acidic smell of turpentine mixed with something floral that was just her. She was hunched over a large sketchpad on her easel, her back to him. A tangle of dark hair was piled messily on her head, held in place by a pencil. The delicate curve of her neck was exposed, and he found his gaze fixed on it, on the place where her skin met the frayed collar of her oversized sweatshirt.

He didn't speak, just watched. He watched the steady, confident movement of her hand, the way her shoulders tensed with concentration. She was in her own world, the way he was when he was fitting a pipe. They were alike in that way. Both of them makers, one with solder, the other with shadow. He knew without seeing it that her fingers would be smudged with black, her concentration so absolute that she’d probably have a streak of it on her cheek or her forehead where she’d pushed her hair away.

He must have made a sound, a sigh, the creak of a floorboard, because she stilled. She didn’t turn around, not at first.

“Bad one?” she asked, her voice soft.

“Frozen burst. In the Glebe.”

“The fancy houses have the worst guts,” she said, a familiar refrain. She finally turned, not her whole body, just her head, looking at him over her shoulder. Her eyes, a shade of green that always seemed to change with the light, found his. And there it was. The thing he couldn't fix. The jolt that went through him, clean and sharp as an electric shock. It wasn’t just that she was beautiful, though she was, with a face that looked like it was still deciding between the girl she’d been and the woman she was becoming. It was the recognition. In her eyes, he saw the entirety of their shared history: the awkward first dinner, the whispered jokes about their parents, the summer they’d spent painting the fence, the night he’d held her while she cried after her first breakup. She was the repository of his life.

“You’ve got a smudge,” he said, his voice a little rough. He took a step closer, into her space, into the cloud of her scent. He raised his hand, his thumb calloused and rough against the impossible softness of her cheek. He gently wiped away the black crescent of charcoal from just below her eye. His thumb lingered for a second too long. Her skin was warm. Her eyes didn't leave his, and in them he saw a flicker of something that wasn’t sisterly, something that mirrored the chaos in his own chest. Her breath hitched, a tiny, almost inaudible sound. The air in the small space became thick, heavy, charged with all the things they never said. He could feel the frantic, rabbit-quick beat of his own heart, a frantic drumming against his ribs. He could lean in. It would be so easy. Just a few inches. He could close the distance and find out if her lips tasted like the charcoal and the floral scent of her skin. The thought was so powerful, so vivid, it was like a memory of something that hadn't happened yet.

Then the moment broke. She blinked, and the spell was gone. She turned back to her easel, a faint blush on her cheeks. “Thanks,” she murmured, her voice tight. She picked up a piece of charcoal and studied her drawing with a sudden, intense focus, as if he were no longer there.

He let his hand drop. It felt heavy, empty. “No problem,” he said to her back. He retreated to the kitchen, the beer in his hand now slick with condensation. He took a long swallow, the bitter cold of it doing nothing to cool the fire in his gut. It was always like this. These moments that flared up between them, brief and incandescent, threatening to burn down the careful structure of their lives. And then they would retreat, back to their roles, back to the safe, impossible distance of brother and sister. But the pressure was always there, building in the pipes. One day, he knew, something was going to burst.Dinner was a quiet affair, as it often was. Frank, a man who had spent forty years as a civil servant navigating the quiet corridors of federal buildings, seemed to find the noise comforting, a kind of buffer against the silence his own home generated. He ate with a slow, methodical rhythm, his gaze distant.

Sebastian focused on his bowl, chasing the few pieces of carrot and potato with his spoon. He could feel Elara’s presence across the small table more acutely than the heat from the radiator. He didn’t dare look at her, afraid of what he might see in her eyes, or what she might see in his. He kept his own gaze locked on his food, on the chipped rim of his bowl, on the grain of the worn oak table. He was building a wall around himself, brick by mental brick, but he knew it was a shoddy construction. Her quiet was a tangible thing, a force that pressed against his defenses. He could hear the soft sound of her breathing, a rhythm he knew as well as his own.

“So, that pipe in the Glebe,” Frank said suddenly, setting his spoon down. His voice was gravelly from years of pipe smoke. “Old house, you said? Galvanized steel?”

“Copper,” Sebastian answered, grateful for the conversational lifeline, for the solid, understandable territory of his job. “Probably original from a post-war reno. He could explain the tensile strength of copper, the effects of thermal expansion, the precise temperature at which water freezes and expands with enough force to split metal. It was a language of cause and effect, of predictable outcomes.

“They don’t build them like they used to,” Frank said, a statement he made at least once a week. He picked up his spoon and resumed eating, his duty of fatherly conversation fulfilled. Marie glanced from Frank to Sebastian and back again, a faint, tired smile on her face. Then her eyes settled on Elara, who was pushing a piece of potato around her bowl with her spoon.

“Did you get much done today, dear?” Marie asked. Her voice was always softer when she spoke to Elara, laced with a gentle uncertainty, as if she were still trying to figure out the quiet girl who had come with the man she’d married.

Elara looked up, startled, as if pulled from a great depth. “Oh. Um, yes. I was just working on a new series. Some figure studies.” Her eyes flickered toward Sebastian for a fraction of a second before skittering away to the salt shaker in the middle of the table. “Just sketches, really.”

“That’s nice,” Marie said, her smile faltering slightly at the clipped response. The conversation died, leaving the game show to once again fill the void. Sebastian felt a muscle in his jaw tighten. He hated these dinners, the charade of normalcy they all participated in. They were four people sharing a table, but they were each on their own separate island, the space between them a cold and unbridgeable sea. For him and Elara, it was worse; they were on the same island, pretending they weren't, terrified of admitting they were the only two inhabitants.

He reached for the bread at the same time she did. The tips of their fingers brushed against the crust of the loaf—a fleeting, electric contact that sent a shock straight up his arm. It was nothing, a common accident, but his entire body reacted as if he’d touched a live wire. He pulled his hand back too quickly. She did the same, her cheeks flushing that same tell-tale pink he’d seen in the studio. Their eyes met, and for a long, breathless second, the rest of the room fell away. There was no stew, no television, no parents. There was only the charged space between them, the memory of his thumb on her skin, and the terrifying, exhilarating question that hung in the air. He saw it all mirrored in her wide, green eyes: the same shock, the same fear, the same undeniable pull. Then she looked down, her dark hair falling forward to hide her face, and the moment was over, leaving him with a phantom tingling in his fingertips and a heart that was beating far too fast.

After dinner, the house settled back into its usual rhythm. Frank and Marie reclaimed their spots on the couch, the blue light of the television painting their faces in shifting hues of an artificial twilight. Elara disappeared, either back to her studio or up to her room, and the silence she left behind was a hollow ache. Sebastian cleared the table, rinsing the bowls under hot water, the steam rising to fog the window above the sink. He felt caged, the warm, familiar air of the house thick and unbreathable. He needed out. He needed the cold.

He pulled his boots back on without bothering to tie the laces and shrugged on his heavy jacket. He didn’t say where he was going. No one asked. The garage was his true refuge, a detached box of cold concrete and drafts at the end of the driveway. The air inside was frigid, smelling of gasoline, old tires, and sawdust. Here, the problems were mechanical. A faulty spark plug, a clogged fuel line, a worn brake pad. They were things he could diagnose, dismantle, and fix with his own two hands. It was already tight. He was just a man turning a wrench on a bolt that didn't need turning, a pointless gesture against a vast and nameless unease. The cold of the garage seeped through the soles of his socks, a familiar, biting discomfort he welcomed. It was real. It was something to feel other than the hollow thrum of anxiety.

He was so lost in the mindless motion that he didn't hear the side door to the garage creak open. It was the scent that announced her, the same faint floral and turpentine mix that clung to her studio, an aroma that cut through the garage's heavy notes of oil and cold rubber. He stopped turning the wrench but didn't look up, bracing himself.

“I made tea.” Elara’s voice was quiet, tentative, as if she were stepping into a wolf’s den. He could feel the warmth radiating from the two ceramic mugs she held. “Marie’s is undrinkable, so I made a fresh pot.”

He finally straightened up, wiping his hands on an already filthy rag. He turned to face her. In the stark light of the bare bulb, her face was pale, her green eyes dark and enormous. This time, the contact was less of a shock and more of a slow, spreading warmth, a current that flowed from the point of contact all the way to the frantic, cluttered space in his chest. He looked down into the mug. The steam smelled of bergamot. Earl Grey. His favourite. Of course she knew.

“Thanks,” he said, his voice husky. He took a sip. The heat was a welcome shock, chasing some of the chill from his bones. They stood in silence for a moment, the only sounds the hum of the old freezer in the corner and the distant, muffled cadence of the game show host from the house.

“No. Just… tinkering.” He took another sip of tea, avoiding her gaze. He felt her eyes on him, her artist’s eyes, analytical and searching. He knew she wasn’t just looking at him; she was seeing him, seeing right through the flimsy wall he was trying to build.

“You always come out here when you’re like this,” she said softly. It wasn’t an accusation, just a statement of fact, one of the thousand facts she had collected about him over the years. “After a bad day. Or after a bad dinner.”

“It’s quiet out here,” he deflected, gesturing vaguely at the engine. “Things make sense out here. There’s a problem, you find the cause, you fix it.”

She took a small step closer, narrowing the careful distance she had put between them. “And you can’t fix them,” she said, her voice barely a whisper, looking toward the house where their parents sat. “Frank and Marie. The words hit him with the force of a physical blow. *Us*. She had said it. She had given a name to the charged space between them, the shared island they were stranded on. All the air went out of his lungs. He felt exposed, stripped bare under the single glaring bulb, the tools of his trade suddenly feeling like useless toys. He could sweat a perfect joint on a copper pipe buried in the frozen earth, but he couldn't bridge the gap of a few inches that separated his lips from hers. He just stared at her, at the earnest, painful truth in her eyes. He saw the reflection of his own desperation there, his own longing. She knew him. The word was a lit fuse, sizzling in the space between them. For years they had moved around the word, tiptoeing past it as if it were a bomb. And she had just picked it up and handed it to him.

“What are you talking about?” he managed, the words low and rusty, like a valve being forced open after a decade of disuse. He still didn't look at her, focusing instead on a dark stain of old oil near his boot, an irregular shape he could trace with his eyes, something finite and meaningless.

“It’s not… we can’t,” he finally whispered, the words tasting like ash. It was the weakest defense he had, a flimsy shield of impossibility against the undeniable truth.

“Why?” she asked, and the simple question was the most dangerous thing she could have said. I didn’t know how to tell you.” The tears that had been welling in her eyes now spilled over, tracing paths through the faint, residual smudges of charcoal on her cheeks. “I applied last fall. I was miserable. I felt like I was going nowhere. It was just a fantasy, a shot in the dark. I never thought… I never knew that *this*,” she gestured around the small room, at the space that contained their entire world, “was going to happen.”

He ignored the plea in her voice, the desperate explanation. His mind had seized on the one, unchangeable fact. He was a man who understood logistics, cause and effect, the linear progression of events. “So you’ll go,” he stated, the words flat and final. He turned his back on her, a brutal, defensive maneuver, and walked to the window. He braced his hands on the cold sill and stared out at the quiet, snow-covered street, at the sodium lamps casting their lonely orange glow. He saw the city as he always had, a network of systems. But now, he saw only the endings. The roads that led out of town, the bus routes that terminated at the station, the train tracks that disappeared over the horizon. Everything was designed to take people away. He had just discovered her exit route.

“It’s just for six months,” she said from behind him, the words so small and weak they were almost lost in the silence. It was the lie people tell when the truth is unbearable, a flimsy patch on a gaping wound. “I’ll come back.”

He turned from the window, and the raw, unmasked pain on his face was a mirror of her own. “Will you?” he asked, his voice cracking, all the bitterness and sorrow of the last ten minutes compressed into that one question. “The Elara who has a solo show in Montreal? The one who spends her days in a real studio, talking to real artists and critics? Is she going to want to come back here? To hiding in her stepbrother’s bedroom and pretending not to know him at breakfast?” He wasn't trying to be cruel. He was a diagnostician, and he was laying out the fatal, logical progression of their system's failure. He was showing her the schematic of their own destruction.

She had no answer. Her mouth opened, but no words came out. The truth of his question was a punch to the gut, and it left her speechless. He had laid their impossible love against the brilliant, unstoppable force of her future, and in that equation, there was only one possible outcome. Seeing the utter devastation on her face, the last of his cold anger drained away, leaving only an immense, shared sorrow. He crossed the small room and pulled her into his arms, crushing her against his chest as if he could physically hold their world together. She buried her face in the fabric of his shirt, her body shaking with silent, heartbroken sobs. She was not crying for her choice; she was crying because she had no choice at all.

He held her, his hand stroking her hair, his own eyes burning. He breathed in her scent, trying to memorize it, trying to store it up against the coming famine. “You have to go,” he whispered into her hair, and the words were the hardest, most painful thing he had ever constructed. “Of course you have to go. It’s everything. It’s your dream. I would never be the one to stand in the way of that.” It was the only fix. It was the only right thing to do. He was a surgeon of sorts, and he had just performed the amputation of his own heart.

They stumbled to the bed, but there was no passion, no desire. They lay down on top of the comforter, fully clothed, and clung to each other like shipwreck survivors, the letter a piece of white wreckage on the floor. The room, their sanctuary, their secret country, had become a vigil. The ticking of his bedside clock, once an ignored background sound, was now an ominous, deafening countdown. April was no longer a distant month; it was a fixed point on the horizon, a destination they were travelling toward at an unstoppable speed. And beyond it, there was nothing but a vast, empty expanse. The golden age was over. The long, slow work of saying goodbye had just begun.The two months that followed the arrival of the letter were a strange and liminal time, a country of lasts. The nights in Sebastian’s room, once a secret, joyful festival, became a long, quiet wake. The frantic, happy energy of their initial consummation was replaced by a slow, desperate tenderness, an intimacy sharpened to a razor’s edge by the constant, ticking clock of her departure. They were no longer building a world; they were curating a museum of its final moments, committing every detail to memory against the coming emptiness. He would lie awake for hours after she’d fallen asleep, his arm numb beneath her, just to feel the weight of her head on his shoulder, to memorize the scent of her hair on his pillow, stockpiling sensations like a man preparing for a famine. He would watch her sleep, her face peaceful in the slivers of streetlight, and feel a love so vast and painful it felt like a form of grief.

The daytime performances became almost unbearable. Marie, in her oblivious, well-meaning way, embraced Elara’s future with a vicarious excitement that was a constant, smiling torture. Every dinner was a minefield of cheerful planning. She brought home brochures for art supply stores in Montreal, read classified ads for apartments in Le Plateau, and talked endlessly about the prestige of the Leclerc Foundation. “A solo show! Can you imagine, Frank? Our Elara!” she would say, beaming, while Frank would offer gruff, practical advice about shipping costs and winter tires. Sebastian would sit through it all, a hollow man in a skin of polite smiles, murmuring “That’s great, Elara,” his voice a stranger’s in his own ears. Across the table, Elara would meet his gaze, her eyes filled with a shared, silent agony. The secret language that had once been a source of thrilling complicity was now a semaphore of mutual pain, two prisoners communicating between cells, their hands saying what their voices could not.

The strain began to show in the hidden corners of their lives. The easy laughter that had filled their nights was gone, replaced by long silences that were heavy not with unspoken desire, but with unspeakable sorrow. He found her one afternoon in her alcove, surrounded by the beautiful chaos of her work, but she wasn’t drawing. She was just staring at a blank canvas, her hand resting limply on a charcoal pencil, her expression lost and desolate. He knew she was paralyzed by the impossible conflict of her joy and her guilt. He wanted to comfort her, to tell her it was alright, but the words felt like lies. He was drowning, and she was the one who had been thrown the life raft. How could he not want her to take it, even if it meant leaving him behind?

His need to fix things, the core of his very being, became a maddening, useless instinct. He couldn't fix the geography that was about to separate them. He couldn’t fix the clock that was ticking down to April. He felt his helplessness as a physical affliction, a constant, low-grade fever. But one evening, he found a problem he could solve. He found her in the alcove again, this time with a measuring tape and a frustrated sigh, trying to figure out how to transport her larger canvases, especially the portrait of him, which was now nearly finished. It was too big for a standard portfolio, too fragile to be simply wrapped. She looked up at him, her face a mask of weary frustration. “I don’t know how I’m going to get this there in one piece,” she said, gesturing to the drawing of his own haunted face. “I’m terrified it’s going to get ruined.”

Here, finally, was a familiar language. It was a problem of structure, of materials, of protecting something precious from the flaws and failures of the outside world. A current of purpose, the first he had felt in weeks, shot through him. “I’ll build you a crate,” he said, the words coming out with a firm, solid authority that had been absent from his voice for too long. She looked at him, confused. “A crate?” “For your art,” he clarified. “A proper one. Custom-fit. It won’t get damaged. I can build it.” He saw the understanding dawn on her face, and with it, a fresh wave of sorrow. He wasn’t just offering to solve a logistical problem. He was offering to build the very vessel that would carry her life—and a piece of his own—away from him. It was a practical, heartbreaking act of love, the only kind he knew how to perform. “Okay,” she whispered, her voice thick with emotion. “Okay, Seb.”

The garage became his sanctuary again, but this time it was not a refuge from his feelings, but a place to process them. He worked with a grim, focused precision. He bought fine-grained plywood, brass hinges, and foam insulation, sparing no expense. The task absorbed him. The scream of the circular saw cutting through wood was a release, the satisfying bite of a drill a moment of clean, absolute control. He was not just building a box; he was building a final, perfect testament to his love. Every measurement was exact, every joint seamless, every screw driven home with a purpose that was almost holy. This crate would be the best thing he had ever built. It would be strong enough to withstand the careless brutality of shipping companies, insulated enough to protect her work from the shock of temperature changes. It was a piece of his own quiet, ordered world designed to protect her chaotic, beautiful one on its journey into a future he would not share. As he worked late into the nights, the smell of sawdust mingling with the cold metallic air, he was not just a plumber; he was a sarcophagus builder, constructing a beautiful, perfect container for the body of their dead dream. It was the only fix he had left. He was helping her leave, and in doing so, he was bringing order to the chaos of his own breaking heart.

He finished it on a Saturday afternoon in late March. The last of the winter’s snow was melting in dirty rivers down the driveway, and the air held the first, faint, earthy scent of the coming thaw. The crate was a masterpiece of function and form. It was smooth, solid, and perfectly proportioned. He had even routed the edges and sanded the wood to a silky finish. He carried it from the garage and set it down in the middle of her alcove studio. It was a large, unavoidable presence, a wooden monument to their impending separation. Elara was there, packing brushes and tubes of paint into smaller boxes. She stopped what she was doing and simply stared at it. She ran a hand over its smooth, perfect surface, her artist’s fingers appreciating the craftsmanship, the care, the sheer, heartbreaking finality of it. She didn’t thank him, not at first. She just walked over to him, stood on her tiptoes, and kissed him, a slow, sad, grateful kiss that tasted of salt and the dust of her work.

“It’s perfect,” she whispered, her forehead resting against his. “It’s too perfect.” He wrapped his arms around her, holding her there in the middle of her studio, surrounded by the ghosts of the work she had done and the tangible, wooden promise of the work she was about to do. He had done it. He had fixed her path away from him. He had built her a beautiful, sturdy, permanent goodbye. He held her tight, feeling the frailness of her body against his, and knew that all the solder and steel in the world couldn't fix the gaping hole this perfect, empty box was about to leave in the center of his life.The final week of March was a slow, meticulous dismantling. The house, which had once been a stage for their secret life, became a warehouse for its dissolution. Boxes, labeled in Elara’s elegant script—*Books, Brushes, Linens*—began to accumulate in the entryway, a stark, cardboard barricade against the illusion of normalcy. Her alcove studio, once a beautiful chaos, was slowly stripped of its soul. Canvases were wrapped in brown paper, jars of brushes were emptied and cleaned, and the familiar, sharp scent of turpentine was gradually replaced by the dry, papery smell of cardboard and packing tape. At the center of it all, like a solemn, empty sarcophagus, sat the crate he had built. It was a constant, wooden reminder of the impending deadline, a monument to the efficiency of their own heartbreak.

The act of packing it was a silent, solemn ritual they performed on her last Friday night. His parents had gone out to a movie, a rare occurrence that felt like a deliberate, unconscious gift from the universe, leaving them alone in the quiet house. They didn’t use the time for a final, frantic coupling. The energy for that was gone, burned away by the long, slow burn of their sorrow. Instead, they met in the alcove, two custodians of a shuttered museum. The portrait of him, her masterpiece of his own internal wreckage, leaned against the wall, waiting. It was finished now. She had added a flicker of light to the shadowed eyes, a hint of the man who existed only in the dark of his bedroom, a detail that made it almost unbearable to look at.

Together, without speaking, they wrapped the portrait in a soft, clean sheet, their hands brushing as they folded the corners, a fleeting, ghost of a touch. Then, they lifted it. It was heavier than it looked, weighted with the charcoal and the paper and the unspoken truth of their story. They carried it together, a shared burden, and gently lowered it into the foam-lined interior of the crate. It fit perfectly, nestled securely in the space he had designed for it. For a long moment, they both just looked at it, lying there in its soft, white shroud. Then, Elara reached in and placed a small, folded piece of paper on top of the sheet. She didn’t tell him what it was, and he didn’t ask. He just watched her pull her hand back, a final, benedictory gesture. He put the lid in place, the heavy plywood settling with a soft, final *whoosh* of air. He drove the brass screws into their pre-drilled holes, the quiet turn of the screwdriver the only sound in the room, each twist a nail in the coffin of their shared life. When the last screw was flush, the crate was sealed. The story was packed. Her path away was ready.

Their last night together was a country of profound and heavy silence. They lay in his bed, not tangled in passion, but moored side-by-side, his arm under her head, her hand resting flat on his chest. They didn’t sleep. They listened to the house breathe around them, to the distant sigh of traffic on the snowy streets, to the frantic, quiet beating of their own two hearts. They were adrift on a small, dark raft, and the dawn was the waterfall they could hear roaring just ahead. They spoke in whispers, not about their sorrow or the gaping void of the future, but about small, inconsequential things. The memory of a funny-looking dog they’d seen on the canal. The plot of a stupid movie they had once watched together. The taste of the salt and vinegar chips they both loved. They were gathering tiny, smooth stones from the shoreline of their past, tucking them into their pockets to have something, anything, to hold onto when the tide went out.

Just before the first hint of grey light began to dilute the darkness, she shifted, propping herself up on one elbow to look at him. "I have something for you," she whispered. She reached to the nightstand and picked up a small, flat object wrapped in tissue paper. It was a piece of slate, no bigger than his palm, that she used for mixing colours. On its smooth, dark surface, she had etched a drawing with a fine, sharp point. It was a perfect, intricate rendering of his hands, crosshatched with scars, one holding a wrench, the other holding a single, delicate paintbrush. Solder and shadow. Her world and his, held in a permanent, impossible balance. "So you don't forget," she said, her voice catching. "What we were." He took it from her, the slate cool and heavy in his hand, a solid piece of their story. He had no words. He just pulled her down to him and held her, burying his face in her hair, breathing her in, trying to absorb enough of her to last a lifetime.

The morning was a fresh circle of hell, bright, cold, and brutally ordinary. The taxi was scheduled for nine. The house buzzed with a nervous, artificial energy. Frank, awkward and unsure in the face of overt emotion, pressed a handful of twenty-dollar bills into Elara’s hand. “For emergencies,” he mumbled, his gaze fixed somewhere on the wall behind her. Marie, her eyes already red, hugged Elara with a fierce, trembling tightness, pressing a frozen, foil-wrapped brick of her beef stew into her arms. “So you have at least one proper meal before you have to cook for yourself,” she said, her voice choked with tears. Sebastian stood apart from it all, a silent, stone sentinel by the door, watching the familiar, familial goodbyes that had nothing to do with the real, cataclysmic farewell that was taking place.

When it was his turn, the charade reached its agonizing peak. He stepped forward, and the four feet of space between them felt like an impassable canyon. He was supposed to hug her, a brief, brotherly embrace. He moved into her space, his body rigid, and wrapped his arms around her stiff, unresponsive shoulders. For a fraction of a second, in the shelter of that hollow, public hug, she whispered his name into the collar of his shirt, a single, desperate, soundless word. *Seb.* It was a final, secret message, a ghost of their true life passing between them, completely invisible to their tearful, oblivious audience. Then they pulled apart, and she was just his stepsister again, her face a pale, beautiful, heartbreaking mask.

He carried the crate out to the waiting taxi van. It was heavy, but he barely felt the weight. The driver helped him slide it into the back. It looked stark and official in the vehicle’s cavernous interior. He had done his job. The package was ready for transport. He closed the door with a final, echoing slam. Elara got into the back seat, a small, solitary figure swallowed by the space. She looked at him through the glass, her eyes wide and dark. She did not smile or wave. She just looked at him, and in her gaze, he saw the entirety of their shared history, from the first awkward dinner to the last silent night. He stood on the curb, his hands shoved deep in his pockets, the small, heavy piece of slate a secret weight against his thigh. The taxi pulled away from the curb, its tires hissing on the wet pavement. He watched it drive down the street, past the identical houses, until it reached the corner. It paused at the stop sign, its brake lights a final, bloody flare, and then it turned, and she was gone.

He stood on the curb for a long time, the cold, damp spring air doing nothing to numb the gaping, hollow space inside him. Eventually, the sound of his mother closing the front door, a soft click of finality, spurred him into motion. He walked back into the house. It was quieter now, the nervous energy replaced by a thick, mournful silence. Her boxes were gone from the entryway. He walked into the alcove. It was empty. The easel was folded in a corner, the floor swept clean. There was nothing left of her, not a stray brush, not a smudge of charcoal, nothing but the ghost of her scent that he knew would fade by nightfall. He had spent his life fixing things, bringing order to the chaotic underworld. He had built the crate, he had packed the art, he had ensured her safe passage. He had, with his own two hands, engineered the perfect, orderly, and absolute destruction of his own world. He went up the stairs to his room, which was no longer a country, no longer a sanctuary. It was just a room. He closed the door, leaned his back against it, and let the silence wash over him. The show was over. The city's secret passages were empty. The pressure was gone, and the quiet that remained was the most brutal sound he had ever heard.
