The world tilted. Not in the dizzy, post-PT session way, but a slow, tectonic shift that rearranged the landscape of her future. Corporal Elara Vance sat ramrod straight on the edge of her cot, the regulation grey blanket a stiff, unyielding line beneath her thighs. The mobile phone, a sleek, impersonal black slab, felt slick with sweat in her palm. On the other end of the line, a woman with a voice like honey-coated gravel was confirming the impossible.

"So, just to reiterate, Ms. Vance. We'd like to officially welcome you to the live finals of X-Factor."

Elara’s room was a testament to a life lived by the numbers. Bed made, corners hospital-tight. Boots polished to a black mirror, reflecting the stark strip lighting of the barracks ceiling. Her uniform for the next day hung, pressed and perfect, on a hook by the door. The only chaos was the stack of sheet music tucked beneath her pillow, a contraband dream. She stared at her own reflection in the gleaming toe of her boot. The face looking back was all sharp angles and discipline, hair scraped back into a severe bun, eyes trained to assess, not to sparkle. It wasn't the face of a pop star. It was the face of a soldier.

"Ms. Vance? Are you still there?"

"Yes," Elara managed, her own voice a croak. "Yes. Roger that. I'm here."

There was a brief, amused silence. "We usually just get a scream. 'Roger that' is a first."

A bubble of hysterical laughter threatened to burst from Elara’s chest. She swallowed it down, the ingrained discipline holding it in check like a caught breath. Screaming was not authorized. Not here. Not in a room that smelled of floor polish and discipline. "Sorry. Force of habit."

"Not at all. We love it. 'The Singing Soldier.' The press are going to have a field day," the woman, who’d introduced herself as Fiona from production, purred. "A car will be at the main gate for you at 0800 on Friday. That gives you three days to get your affairs in order. Can you make that work?"

Three days. Three days to dismantle one life and step into the blinding glare of another. Three days to explain to her Commanding Officer that she needed indefinite leave to go on a reality television show. She pictured Sergeant Major Davies, a man whose face seemed permanently carved from granite, and felt a fresh wave of absurdity wash over her.

"I'll make it work," Elara said, the words coming out with more confidence than she felt. It was a mission. A task to be executed. She could do that.

"Excellent. Welcome to the family, Elara."

The line went dead. Elara held the phone to her ear for another ten seconds, listening to the empty hiss of the digital void. Then, slowly, she lowered it. She didn't scream. She didn't jump up and down. She stood, walked the three paces to the small window, and looked out over the perfectly manicured parade ground. Empty now, under the deepening twilight, it was a vast expanse of order. Her world. And she was about to leave it for the one thing she’d never been able to drill into submission: her own voice. A slow, wide grin stretched across her face, a truly unregulated expression of pure, unadulterated joy. It felt foreign and wonderful. She pressed her forehead against the cool glass. The Singing Soldier. It sounded like a character. It sounded like someone else. Maybe it was.

Her first call was to the one person who wouldn’t think she’d completely lost her mind. The phone rang twice before he picked up, his voice a familiar, grounding rumble. "Vance. Don't tell me you've forgotten your password for the system again."

"Ben," she breathed, the grin still plastered on her face. "You are not going to believe this."

"Let me guess," Corporal Ben Carter’s voice crackled down the line. "You finally convinced the mess sergeant to put something other than stew on the menu for a Saturday night."

"Bigger."

"You got that promotion to Sergeant?"

"Bigger, you idiot!" she laughed, the sound echoing strangely in the sterile room. "The X-Factor. The live shows. I'm in."

There was a profound silence on the other end, so long she thought the call had dropped. "Say again, Vance? I must have misheard. Sounded like you said you were joining a circus."

"The live finals, Ben. They just called. A car is picking me up on Friday morning. I’m going to the contestants' house. The whole nine yards."

She could practically hear him shaking his head, a gesture she knew was more disbelief than disapproval. "The thing you did on a dare? That audition in Manchester when we were on leave? You were just trying to get a free lunch out of me."

"Well, the lunch was terrible, but the audition apparently wasn't," she shot back. She’d sung an acoustic, stripped-back version of a belting power ballad, her voice quiet and clear in the cavernous audition room. She’d done it for a laugh, egged on by Ben, who’d heard her singing to herself in the back of a transport one too many times. She never for a second thought it would lead anywhere beyond a funny story to tell in the mess. But then came the callback. And another. Each stage had felt like a fluke, a clerical error she was too polite to point out. Now, the error was real.

"Bloody hell, Ellie," Ben said, using her first name, something he only did when he was truly floored. "You're serious."

"As a heart attack."

"What's the CO going to say?"

"I'm choosing to file that under 'Future Ellie's problem,'" she said, sinking back onto the cot. The blanket felt less stiff now, the room less like a cell and more like a launchpad. "Right now, I'm just… I don't know, Ben. I feel like I'm going to vibrate out of my own skin."

"It's what you always wanted," he said softly, and the simple truth of it hit her harder than the initial phone call. She'd never dared to want it. Not really. It was a secret, a hobby, a quiet rebellion against the rigid structure she had willingly chosen for her life. The army was safe. It was predictable. You passed a test, you got a stripe. You followed an order, you completed the mission. Singing… that was anarchy. It was emotion and luck and a thousand other variables she couldn't control.

"I guess," she whispered. "I'm terrified."

"Good," Ben said, his voice firm again. "You do your best work when you're terrified. Remember that training exercise in the Brecon Beacons? When you had to navigate us back to base in a whiteout with a sprained ankle?"

"That's different. That was a map and a compass. This is… glitter and hairspray."

"Same principles apply, Vance. Observe, adapt, overcome. Just try not to salute the judges."

She laughed, a real, genuine laugh this time. "No promises. Okay, I have to go. I have to… I don't know, pack a bag or something. What does a person even pack for this?"

"Something other than camouflage, for a start. Hey, Ellie?"

"Yeah?"

"Knock 'em dead."

The conversation with Sergeant Major Davies was, predictably, excruciating. She stood at ease in his office, the air thick with the scent of old paper and Brasso. He listened, his face impassive, his fingers steepled under his chin, as she explained the situation. It sounded even more ridiculous saying it out loud to him.

"Let me be clear, Corporal," he said when she’d finished, his voice a low gravel. "You wish to request extended, open-ended personal leave… to participate in a televised singing competition."

"Yes, Sergeant Major."

He stared at her for a long, heavy moment. Elara kept her eyes fixed on a point on the wall just over his shoulder, her posture perfect, her breathing even. She was prepared for the lecture, the flat-out refusal, the suggestion that she might be better suited to a career in entertainment after all.

Instead, he sighed. A deep, weary sound. "My wife loves that bloody show. Drives me mad. All that screaming and crying." He leaned forward, picking up a pen and tapping it on his desk. "The Royal Army Service Corps has a proud history of supporting its soldiers' personal development. We have people who climb Everest, who row the Atlantic. I suppose this is no different. Just… louder."

Elara blinked. "So… that's a yes, Sergeant Major?"

"It's a conditional yes. You will be a representative of this regiment, and of the British Army, whether you're wearing a uniform or not. You will conduct yourself with the dignity and professionalism expected of your rank. Do you understand me, Corporal?"

"Crystal clear, Sergeant Major."

"Good. Now get out of my office before I change my mind. And Vance?" He called as she reached the door.

"Sergeant Major?"

A flicker of something—a smile, maybe, though it was hard to be sure—crossed his face. "Try to win the blasted thing. My wife will never let me hear the end of it if you don't."

The next two days passed in a blur of paperwork, packing, and surreal goodbyes. Her squadmates’ reactions ranged from stunned silence to raucous cheering. They slapped her on the back, made her promise to get them tickets, and threatened her with eternal latrine duty if she got voted off in the first week. Packing was a strategic nightmare. Her life fit into one large duffel bag. Most of it was green. She had to do an emergency shopping trip into Ashburn, feeling like a spy as she bought brightly coloured tops and jeans that felt flimsy and unstructured compared to her usual attire. She stood in front of her wardrobe, holding a pair of battered combat trousers in one hand and a new, sequined top in the other. It was her past and her potential future, and she had no idea how to make them coexist. In the end, she packed both. And the sheet music. Always the sheet music.

Friday morning arrived, grey and damp. At 0750, Elara stood by the main gate, her duffel bag at her feet. She wore jeans, a simple black jumper, and her old, comfortable boots. She felt exposed without the familiar weight of her uniform. A sleek black car with tinted windows pulled up precisely at 0800. The driver, a man in a suit, got out and opened the back door without a word, taking her bag and placing it in the boot. It was smooth, efficient. Almost military.

As she slid onto the plush leather seats, she looked back at the red-brick buildings and the Union Jack fluttering from the flagpole. Her home. Her entire adult life had been lived within those gates. The car pulled away, and she didn't look back again. The mission had begun.

The journey was a long, silent passage into another world. They left the familiar MOD signs behind, swapping them for the motorways that snaked towards London. The grey military architecture gave way to suburban sprawl, and then to the dense, glittering chaos of the capital. The car finally turned into a gated driveway in a leafy, North London suburb, pulling up in front of a ridiculously large white mansion that looked like it had been built out of sugar cubes and glass.

This was it. The X-Factor house.

Before the car had even come to a complete stop, the front door flew open and a cacophony of noise spilled out—music, shouting, laughter. People. So many people. They were lounging on the steps, posing for a cameraman, their clothes a riot of colour and carefully constructed nonchalance. Elara felt a knot of anxiety tighten in her stomach. This wasn't a barracks. There was no order, no hierarchy she could recognise. It was a social minefield.

The driver opened her door. "Here we are, miss."

Taking a deep breath, Elara stepped out of the car. Observe. Adapt. Overcome. Her boots crunched on the gravel driveway, a sound that seemed jarringly loud. A few heads turned. She saw the flicker of assessment in their eyes. The quiet girl in the boring jumper. The new arrival. Fresh meat. A boy with a gravity-defying quiff and a leather jacket nudged his friend, whispering something that made them both snigger.

Elara ignored them. She scanned the group, her training kicking in automatically. Threat assessment. There was the boyband, a cluster of five identical haircuts vying for the camera's attention. There was a girl with pink hair and a nose ring who looked like she gargled with gravel, radiating a punk-rock defiance. A young man with an acoustic guitar was sitting by himself on the lawn, already lost in his own world. They were all so… young. So confident. They inhabited this world of performance as if they were born to it.

A producer with a headset and a clipboard hurried over. "Elara! Welcome! So glad you're here. Just leave your bag, we'll get it to your room. Come and meet everyone!"

She was propelled forward, into the heart of the noise. The greetings were a blur of air kisses that missed her cheeks and names that vanished the second she heard them. 'Harmony,' 'Jay,' 'Roxanne,' 'The Fusion Five.' They were all smiles and flashing teeth, but their eyes were sharp, calculating. She was the competition. The soldier. The novelty act. She could feel them slotting her into a box, defining her before she'd even sung a note. She felt the familiar urge to stand at ease, to make herself smaller, but she resisted. She squared her shoulders, just a fraction. Met their gazes directly.

She was being shown the sprawling, open-plan living room, a monument to crushed velvet and bad taste, when a new voice cut through the chatter. A voice she knew. A voice she had hoped she would never have to hear again outside of a nightmare.

"Well, well, well. If it isn't little Ellie Vance from Ashburn."

Elara froze. The blood drained from her face. It was a voice that dripped with condescending sweetness, a sound she associated with stolen solos, whispered lies, and the bitter humiliation of a school talent show fifteen years ago. Slowly, she turned.

Leaning against a grand piano, looking as if she owned it, was Karmen Knight. Her hair was a perfect platinum blonde, her smile a slash of predatory red. She was taller than Elara remembered, sleeker, her entire being radiating an aura of impenetrable, professionally manufactured confidence. Karmen, who had been the star of every school play, the winner of every local competition, the girl who had told a ten-year-old Elara that her voice was "sweet, but too weak for a real stage." Karmen, who had moved away to a prestigious London stage school years ago and vanished into what Elara had assumed was a world of professional success.

Her arch-rival. Her childhood nemesis. Here.

Karmen’s perfectly shaped eyebrow arched. "I almost didn't recognise you without the pigtails. The soldier thing is a cute gimmick, I'll give you that. Very on-brand for our brave little trooper. Did you bring your tin helmet?"The jab landed with pinpoint accuracy, striking a nerve fifteen years dormant. For a split second, she was ten years old again, standing backstage in a scratchy polyester costume, blinking back tears as Karmen swept past with a trophy and a pitying smile. The memory was so vivid it was almost physical, a phantom ache of humiliation. But then, years of training slammed a bulkhead door on the emotion. Her spine straightened, if that were even possible. Her expression, which had been one of cautious observation, went perfectly, professionally blank.

"Karmen," she said, her voice level and devoid of inflection. It was the same tone she used on the firing range. "I see you're still hitting the high notes." She let her eyes drift over Karmen's expensive-looking outfit, a silent appraisal that was far more dismissive than any insult. "It's a bold look. Very... loud."

Karmen’s smile tightened at the edges. She had expected a flustered reaction, a stammer, perhaps even a blush of embarrassment. She hadn’t expected this cool, unreadable soldier to turn her own condescension back on her. Around them, the ambient chatter had died down. The camera, which had been focused on the boyband, smoothly panned to frame the two women. Fiona the producer had a glint in her eye that was pure, predatory television gold. This was conflict. This was a story.

"Well, this is a competition for stars, Ellie. Not for wallflowers. You have to be seen *and* heard," Karmen retorted, recovering quickly and playing to her audience, both in the room and the one she knew was watching through the lens. "But I'm sure you'll do fine. They always love a good sob story. The brave little soldier singing for her Queen and country. It’s very touching."

Before Elara could formulate a response that wouldn't get her court-martialed for conduct unbecoming, Fiona clapped her hands together with practiced cheer. "Alright, you two! Save the fireworks for the stage! Elara, let me show you to your room. You'll be sharing with Roxanne." She gestured towards the pink-haired girl, who had been watching the exchange with a detached, cynical amusement. Roxanne gave a lazy two-fingered salute, a gesture that felt more genuinely military than anything Elara had done since arriving.

Elara gave Karmen a final, unblinking look before turning to follow Fiona up a grand, sweeping staircase. She could feel Karmen’s eyes on her back, along with the gazes of every other contestant. She had been here for less than ten minutes and had already been marked, defined not just as 'The Soldier,' but as Karmen Knight's rival. It was a complication she hadn’t anticipated, an ambush on territory she didn't yet understand.

The room was as jarring as the rest of the house. Where her barracks room was a study in grey and olive drab, this was an explosion of lilac and cream. The bed was a mountain of soft pillows and plush throws, and a vintage-style vanity table with a mirror framed in lightbulbs stood against one wall. On the far side of the room, Roxanne was sprawled on the identical second bed, headphones on, scrolling through her phone. She didn't look up as Elara entered. On the pristine lilac duvet, her drab green duffel bag looked like a piece of unexploded ordnance. She placed it down carefully, the heavy canvas a stark contrast to the delicate fabric.

For a long moment, Elara just stood in the middle of the room, feeling utterly displaced. This was a room for dreaming, for preening, for being a pop star. It felt like a costume she hadn't yet learned how to wear. She ran a hand over her severe bun, the familiar tightness a small comfort. Karmen’s presence had unsettled her more than she wanted to admit. The old insecurities, the ones she had buried under years of drills and discipline, were beginning to surface. The feeling of being too plain, too quiet, not special enough.

She took a slow, deep breath, the kind she took before a marksmanship test. In, hold, out. She closed her eyes. Karmen wasn't just a ghost from her past. In this context, she was an objective. An opponent. This wasn't a school talent show. This was a strategic campaign. Elara’s training had prepared her for hostile environments, for assessing threats and formulating a plan of action. Karmen, with her manufactured confidence and venomous sweetness, was a threat. The difference was, this battlefield was made of television studios and public opinion, and the weapons were key changes and choruses. She opened her eyes, her gaze falling on the contraband sheet music sticking out of her bag. That was her ammunition. Observe, adapt, overcome. The mission parameters had just changed. Winning was no longer just a fantastic dream; it was now a tactical necessity.Roxanne finally pulled her headphones down around her neck, the tinny beat of a drum machine leaking into the room. "You going to stand there all day, or are you going to claim your territory?" she asked, her voice a low, raspy drawl. She gestured with her phone towards the empty half of the room. "This place is a circus. Might as well pick your corner of the cage." Elara, startled out of her stupor, offered a small, tight smile. "Right. Sorry." She began to unpack, her movements economical and precise. She folded her few civilian clothes into perfect, uniform squares in the drawers of the ornate vanity. Her wash-kit was laid out on the surface with geometric precision: toothbrush, razor, soap, all aligned. Roxanne watched her, a flicker of curiosity in her dark-lined eyes. "You for real?" she asked. "That whole soldier thing?" Elara paused, a neatly folded jumper in her hands. "Corporal, yes." Roxanne let out a short, sharp laugh. "Mental. They brought in a ringer." She swung her legs off the bed and stood, stretching like a cat. She was shorter than Elara but took up more space, all sharp angles and restless energy. "Well, Corporal, rule number one in this house: don't eat the last of the good cereal. Rule number two: Karmen Knight is a snake. You seem to have figured that one out already."

An hour later, they were all summoned to the vast living room by Fiona, who now held a champagne flute and exuded an air of manic festivity. Cameras repositioned themselves to capture the group, and a palpable sense of performance settled over the contestants. They arranged themselves on the velvet sofas, a curated tableau of aspiring stars. Elara chose a spot on a stiff-backed armchair slightly apart from the main group, a position from which she could observe everyone. It felt like a pre-mission briefing, except the objective was fame and the CO was a producer high on the scent of potential drama. "Welcome, everyone, to your new home!" Fiona announced, her voice booming. "This is where the magic happens! We have only one rule here: be yourself! Your *best* self, your *star* self! Our team of vocal coaches, stylists, and choreographers are here to unlock the superstar within you. Your first session is tomorrow morning. 0900, sharp!" The room buzzed with excitement. Elara made a mental note. 0900. A civilized hour. She could get a run and a full reconnaissance of the grounds done before then. Fiona’s eyes found Elara in her chair. "And to Elara, our Singing Soldier, a special welcome! We are so honoured to have you. The country is already falling in love with your story." A few of the contestants clapped politely, their eyes sliding towards Karmen, who was sipping her own champagne with a look of supreme boredom. The battle lines, it seemed, were already drawn for the viewers at home.

The next morning, the vocal studio felt like an interrogation room. It was soundproofed and stark, dominated by a black grand piano and a fearsome-looking woman named Cecily, whose severe black bob and piercing gaze made Sergeant Major Davies look like a cuddly grandpa. One by one, the contestants were made to sing a scale, then a verse and chorus of their audition song. The Fusion Five were slick but soulless. Roxanne’s voice was a powerful, raw instrument she wielded like a weapon. Then it was Karmen’s turn. She was technically flawless, her voice soaring through a complex Mariah Carey run with an ease that was both impressive and irritatingly smug. She finished to Cecily's curt nod, a high mark from the severe coach. "Very clean, Karmen. Good breath control," Cecily noted, before her eyes landed on Elara. "The soldier. Let's see if there's a voice to go with the uniform." A few of the others sniggered. Elara stepped forward, her heart thumping a frantic, unregulated rhythm against her ribs. This felt more exposing than standing on a windswept firing range in the pouring rain. She closed her eyes for a second, picturing the parade ground at twilight. Find the centre. Control the breathing. Execute. She sang the same song she had at her audition, the stripped-back ballad. She didn’t look at anyone. She just sang, her voice filling the quiet room not with power, but with a startling, aching clarity. It was pure, controlled, and freighted with an emotion she normally kept locked down. When she finished, the silence in the room was different. It wasn't empty; it was full, ringing with the final, sustained note.

She opened her eyes. Cecily was staring at her, her severe expression replaced by one of intense concentration. "Well," the coach said slowly. "That was… unexpected." Before she could continue, Karmen chimed in, her voice dripping with faux concern. "It was lovely, Ellie, really sweet. But Cecily, don't you think it's a little… small for the live shows? You'll be swallowed up in that huge studio. You need to learn to belt, to really project from the diaphragm." She placed a hand on her own stomach, a picture of helpfulness. "Let me show you." Elara felt a familiar sting of the old criticism—*too weak for a real stage*. But this time, it wasn't a schoolgirl's hurt; it was tactical information. Karmen was trying to get her to sing in a way that wasn't natural to her, to over-extend and fail. She was trying to rattle her cage. Instead of getting flustered, Elara simply turned her attention to the coach. "Is that your assessment, ma'am?" she asked, her tone respectful and direct, as if she were clarifying an order. Cecily’s lips twitched. The "ma'am" clearly amused her. "No, Corporal, it is not," she said, shooting a withering glare at Karmen. "Power is not the same as volume. Your control is phenomenal. It’s what makes the quiet moments land with such impact. We will work on dynamic range, but do not let anyone," she paused, her eyes lingering on Karmen, "convince you to trade your greatest strength for a cheap imitation of someone else's."

That evening, Elara found Roxanne on a small balcony overlooking the manicured garden, smoking a roll-up cigarette. The party was still going on downstairs, the sound a dull throb through the floor. For a few moments, they stood in a comfortable silence, watching the last of the light fade from the sky. "You didn't flinch," Roxanne said, not looking at her. "Down there, with Cecily. When the Ice Queen tried to shank you." Elara shrugged, leaning her forearms on the railing. The pose felt unfamiliar, too relaxed. "She was offering a tactical opinion. It was noted and discarded." Roxanne took a drag from her cigarette, the cherry glowing in the dusk. She blew a smoke ring. "You're a weird one, Corporal Vance. I haven't decided if that's a good thing or a bad thing yet." "I get that a lot," Elara admitted. A genuine smile touched her lips. "But Karmen’s not wrong about one thing. This is a completely different battlefield. I'm not sure my training manual covers sequined outfits and key changes." "Maybe not," Roxanne said, stubbing out her cigarette on the railing and flicking the butt into the garden below. "But it covers dealing with enemies. And in this house, that's ninety percent of the job."

Later, lying in the absurdly soft bed, the chaotic noise of the house a distant murmur, Elara felt the exhaustion of the day settle deep in her bones. It was a different kind of tired from a twenty-mile tab. It was a mental and emotional fatigue, the result of constant assessment, of managing social threats and navigating a world without clear rules. She thought of Ben’s advice: *Observe, adapt, overcome*. Today had been about observation. She had intel on the coach, on Karmen's tactics, on the house dynamics. She had a potential, if prickly, ally in Roxanne. She reached into her duffel bag and pulled out the stack of sheet music, her contraband dream. Spreading the pages out on the lilac duvet, she traced the notes of a song she’d been working on for months, a quiet, intricate melody that was entirely her own. Karmen thought her strength was a gimmick. The producers saw her as a storyline. But here, in the quiet of the night, surrounded by the foreign luxury of her new life, Elara knew her strength was the same as it had always been: discipline, control, and a voice that could find the truth in a quiet melody. The mission was no longer just to win. It was to prove them all wrong.The Styling Suite was a brighter, more chaotic version of hell. Racks of clothing glittered under spotlights, a kaleidoscope of sequins, leather, and neon fabrics. A dozen people buzzed around the room, armed with tape measures, makeup brushes, and cans of hairspray, their chatter a high-frequency hum of industry jargon. It was another tribe with its own language, and Elara was once again the outsider. She stood awkwardly by the door, her simple black jumper and jeans feeling less like civilian clothes and more like a statement of non-compliance.

Karmen was already holding court in the centre of the room, perched on a high stool like a queen on her throne. A stylist with a flamboyant scarf was draping a bolt of shimmering gold fabric over her shoulder. "No, no, Leo, darling," Karmen was saying, catching her own reflection in a full-length mirror. "It needs to be more dramatic. I’m closing the first half of the show. I need to look like a victory, not a consolation prize." She flicked her eyes over to Elara and offered a saccharine smile. "Oh, Ellie. Good, you’re here. Leo, maybe you can find something for our little soldier? Perhaps something in a nice, sensible khaki?"

Leo, the stylist, gave Elara a sweeping, critical glance from head to toe. "Hmm. The bone structure is fantastic. Very severe. We need to soften it. I'm thinking... ethereal. Flowing fabrics, something to counteract the... rigidity." He snapped his fingers at an assistant. "Bring me the silver chiffon gown and the dove grey silk." Elara felt a prickle of annoyance. They weren't seeing her; they were seeing a problem to be solved, a brand to be packaged. 'Rigidity' was her training. It was what kept her alive on exercises and focused on duty. Here, it was a fashion flaw.

She was guided to a chair, and hands immediately descended on her, unpinning her severe bun. The release of tension was followed by a wave of vulnerability as her hair, long and straight, fell around her shoulders. It felt like a part of her armour had been stripped away. A makeup artist began dabbing things onto her face, muttering about "opening up the eyes" and "adding a glow." Elara sat ramrod straight, her hands clasped in her lap, enduring the process with the same stoic patience she'd use for a mandatory briefing on foot hygiene. She was an object being prepared for display.

Roxanne, who was having black glitter expertly applied to her eyelids, caught Elara's eye in the mirror. She winked. "Just pretend it's camouflage face paint, Corporal," she mouthed silently. The corner of Elara's mouth twitched. The shared moment of cynical solidarity was a small anchor in the glittering chaos. An hour later, she was told to stand and look at her reflection. The woman in the mirror was a stranger. Her hair was down, styled into soft waves. Her face was subtly shaded and highlighted, her eyes looking larger and darker, and a hint of colour was on her lips. She was wearing a simple, sleeveless dress of dark, shimmering blue that fell to the floor. It was beautiful, she supposed, but it wasn't her. It was a costume for the character of 'Elara Vance, Pop Star.'

"See? Breathtaking!" Leo declared, clasping his hands together. "We've found the swan under the soldier!" Karmen glided over, her own transformation complete. She was a vision in white, a sharp, angelic counterpoint to Elara's deep blue. "It's a definite improvement," Karmen conceded, her eyes scanning Elara with the critical gaze of a rival commander inspecting troops. "You almost look like you belong here. Just try not to trip over the hem on stage. It's a bit different from marching in boots, I imagine." The barb was expertly delivered, designed to remind Elara of her otherness, her lack of experience in this world of grace and glamour. Elara simply met her gaze in the mirror. "I'm trained to be adaptable," she said, her voice quiet but firm. "I'll manage."

The choreography studio provided a different kind of challenge. A man named Dante, who was all lean muscle and boundless energy, led the session. He bounded around the mirrored room, demonstrating a series of fluid, hip-centric moves that were meant to be the basic choreography for the opening group number. "It's all about the feeling! The passion! Let the music move you!" he enthused. The music was a relentless, thumping pop track, and for Elara, it moved her to do little more than stand perfectly still.

Her body, conditioned by years of marching, drills, and obstacle courses, was an instrument of precision and economy. It did not 'flow.' It moved from point A to point B in the most efficient way possible. When Dante yelled "Feel the rhythm in your hips!" Elara’s hips remained stubbornly, militantly stationary. The Fusion Five boyband moved as one, their synchronised movements practiced to perfection. Roxanne moved with a kind of snarling, punk-rock energy that was uniquely her own.
