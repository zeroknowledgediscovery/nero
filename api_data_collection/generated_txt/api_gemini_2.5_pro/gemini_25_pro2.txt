The heat in the Hogar de Niños Desamparados was a physical presence, a thick, wet blanket that smelled of salt, mildew, and the unwashed bodies of thirty other children. It settled in Elena’s lungs, a constant reminder of a life breathed in common, shared with everyone and belonging to no one. From her perch on the rickety balcony overlooking the courtyard, she could see the cracked concrete sweating in the afternoon sun. The world outside the orphanage walls pulsed with the vibrant, chaotic rhythm of San Juan—the distant thrum of a salsa beat from a corner bar, the angry squawk of a taxi horn, the melodic cry of a piragua vendor pushing his cart down the cobblestone street. Inside, the only rhythm was the metronomic drip of a leaky faucet in the communal washroom and the sharp, bird-like commands of Señora Vargas.

Elena was a ghost long before she knew how to vanish. At twelve, she was made of sharp angles and quiet spaces, a girl who had mastered the art of taking up as little room as possible. Her hair was a tangled black curtain that she often hid behind, and her eyes, the color of burnt coffee, saw everything from the periphery. She saw the way the bigger boys, led by a brute named Hector, would steal the sweet bread from the younger children’s breakfast trays. She saw the fleeting moments of genuine kindness, rare as shade in the noonday sun, when one of the older girls would braid a little one’s hair. She saw the exhaustion etched into the face of Señora Vargas, a map of worries and budgets that never balanced. Elena saw it all, and in turn, learned how to not be seen. It was a survival skill, more valuable than knowing how to scrub a floor or mend a shirt. To be unnoticed was to be safe.

Her only real sanctuary was a small, forgotten corner of the orphanage’s meager library, a dusty room where the books smelled of time and neglect. And even more than the books, her escape was the small, crackling transistor radio she’d found in a trash heap, its antenna bent into a question mark. She’d spent a month secretly trading chores and favors to get the two batteries it required. At night, when the dormitory was a chorus of snores and whispered secrets, Elena would press the cold plastic to her ear, the headphones a flimsy, precious barrier. She would turn the dial with painstaking slowness, sifting through static and sermons until she found it: the music. It poured into her, a secret language that spoke of love and loss and defiance, of worlds far beyond the crumbling walls of the Hogar. The singers were her confidants, their voices the only ones that seemed to speak directly to her, acknowledging the girl hidden beneath the silence.

The one person who seemed to sense the life humming beneath her quiet exterior was her teacher, Mr. Rojas. He was a man out of place, with a soft paunch, a tweed jacket that was always too warm for the Caribbean climate, and a sadness in his eyes that Elena recognized as a kindred spirit. He taught history and music appreciation at the public school the orphanage children attended, and while most teachers’ eyes glazed over when they looked at the uniformed flock from the Hogar, Mr. Rojas’s seemed to search them. His gaze had landed on Elena one afternoon during a lesson on the life of Beethoven. While the other children fidgeted or dozed in the suffocating heat of the classroom, Elena had been leaning forward, her entire being focused on the scratchy recording playing from the school’s ancient phonograph.

“He couldn’t hear, you know,” Mr. Rojas had said, his voice soft, directed at her. “The man who wrote this glorious, world-changing music. He heard it all inside his head. A prisoner of silence, who created a symphony of sound.”

He began giving her little assignments, asking her to write down the lyrics to her favorite songs, to think about why one melody made her feel like dancing and another made her want to cry. A ghost in the machine. The words echoed in Elena’s head. Señora Vargas meant it as an insult, a reduction of her existence to a mere function. But to Elena, it sounded almost like a goal. A ghost was powerful. A ghost could move through walls, unseen, unheard. A ghost was free.
