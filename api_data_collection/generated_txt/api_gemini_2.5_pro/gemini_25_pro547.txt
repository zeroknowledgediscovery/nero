He would make himself poison in their system. A cold smile touched Alon’s lips, a grimace of triumph and exhaustion. He had done it. He had taken the enemy’s diagnostic tool, their sterile probe, and jammed it full of corrupted data. Hiding was no longer an option. Fighting alone was suicide. He had to break his own rules, the unspoken protocols of solitude that kept the city’s few guardians from treading on each other's toes. His act of defiance had not gone unnoticed. A centuries-old acacia tree, once a haven for local spirits, now choked by a concrete wall, its spiritual presence thready and weak. The journey to Bonifacio Global City felt like a willing march into enemy territory. As midnight approached, the lobby of the BGC tower was a vast, echoing cavern of polished marble and cold, recessed lighting. It was a temple to commerce, now empty of its worshippers. A seed of wild, messy, defiant life had just taken root in the most sterile ground in Manila.The coffee shop was a pocket of the past tucked into a forgotten fold of Escolta, a district that was itself a ghost of Manila’s former glory. Ben Cervantes arrived exactly on time, not because he was punctual, but because a lifetime of meeting clandestine sources had taught him that punctuality was a form of professional courtesy. Alon found his new sanctuary in the sprawling, chaotic heart of Divisoria. He rented a small, windowless room above a stall selling plastic toys, paying the landlord in cash. It was a miserable, humid box, but it was invisible.

From this new, anonymous base, he reached out. He couldn't risk a direct call or a traceable message. That night, on his shift in the silent, gleaming lobby of the BGC tower, Rody received the small, anonymous package. In a cramped, cluttered apartment smelling of stale coffee and old newsprint, Ben Cervantes worked under the harsh glare of a single desk lamp.
