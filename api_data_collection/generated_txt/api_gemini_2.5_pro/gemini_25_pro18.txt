The laughter was the worst part. It wasn't the rolling, gut-deep thunder of genuine mirth. No, this was something else. It was the designated sound of approved amusement, a Pavlovian response to the soft green light that pulsed above the stage when a punchline was deemed… acceptable. I stood in that synthetic bath of light, the cheap velvet of the stage curtain smelling of dust and stale perfume, a rictus grin plastered on my face. The Gilded Cage they called this place. It was one of the few sanctioned male-run establishments in the sector. A pressure valve. It was just… wetness. The same with food. I’d chew and swallow, a mechanical process, but the sensation of satiation was a distant, faded memory, like a photograph left out in the sun. The doctors—the male ones, with their perpetually apologetic eyes—called it Chronic Disaffectation Syndrome.
