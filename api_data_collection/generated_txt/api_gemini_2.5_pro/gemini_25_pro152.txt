That was the performance of an artist who will sell millions of records. It was, simply, magic."

It was the final, definitive crack in Wei's composure. The word 'magic' undid him completely. Simon Cowell, the tiger, the ultimate arbiter of fame, had not just praised his voice; he had seen him. He had seen the truth of his life and had not found it to be a pitiful backstory, but a source of strength, of universal truth. The tears now flowed freely, tears of catharsis, of relief, of a lifetime of being invisible made suddenly, blindingly seen. The stage manager, her face now split by a wide, genuine grin, grabbed his arm. "Bloody hell, son. You did it," she breathed, her professional cool utterly shattered. Other backstage crew members, people who had been nothing but stressed-out blurs just hours before, paused their frantic rushing to pat his back, to give him a thumbs-up, their faces reflecting the awe and emotion that had swept through the auditorium. He was no longer an anonymous cog in the machine; he was the heart of it, the reason for the thunderous noise that still echoed from the stage where the next act, a troupe of high-energy street dancers, was now trying to recapture the audience's attention. He felt a profound, almost comical pity for them, having to follow the raw, exposed nerve he had just laid bare.

He was guided, half-walking, half-floating, towards the communal green room, a space that had been thick with anxiety when he’d left it. As he entered, the atmosphere shifted. A few of the contestants who had already performed rushed towards him. The kind-faced comedian wrapped him in a surprisingly strong hug. "Mate, you absolute legend," he said, his voice thick with genuine admiration. "You made me cry. A comedian! Do you know how bad that is for my brand?" Wei laughed, a wet, gasping sound. He saw other faces, too. The family of acrobats were beaming, the father giving him a respectful nod. But he also saw the young girl with the powerhouse voice, sitting quietly in a corner with her parents, her face pale. She didn't look at him, her gaze fixed on the floor. In her eyes, he saw her dream dimming, and the joy of his own triumph was suddenly tempered by a sharp pang of empathy. The room was a roiling sea of hope and despair, and he was the rock upon which all their ships were now in danger of breaking.

He found a quiet corner, sinking into a plush sofa that felt miles deep, the adrenaline beginning to drain from his body, leaving a trembling exhaustion in its wake. He needed to be still. He needed to process the monumental thing that had just happened. Simon's words echoed in his mind, not the praise, but the validation. *You are every person who has ever had a secret dream.* For his entire life, the kopitiam had been something to escape, a mark of his failure, the greasy smell on his clothes a badge of shame. Now, the most powerful man in the industry had held it up to the world and called it a source of honour, of art. He felt a profound shift deep within him, as if tectonic plates in his soul, locked in tension for years, had finally, seismically, settled into a new alignment. He was not a waiter who happened to sing; he was a singer who had been a waiter, and every scraped plate, every shouted order, had been a note in the song he was always meant to sing.

The long wait for the voting to close was a strange, suspended limbo. The twelve acts were a study in tension, some pacing endlessly, others making frantic calls to family, their voices high with hope. Wei remained on the sofa, an island of calm in the swirling currents of ambition. He felt a profound sense of peace. He had already won. He had sung his truth, and he had been heard. The trophy, the money, the contract – they were all secondary to the priceless gift he had been given on that stage: the chance to be truly seen. He reached into his pocket and pulled out his phone. It was the middle of the night in Singapore, too late to call. He opened a message window and typed five simple words to his grandmother: *I sang my song. For you.* He pressed send, knowing that she would understand the universe contained within that short sentence. Then, he unzipped his duffel bag, which a runner had brought to the room, and took out the small plastic container. There was one pineapple tart left. He ate it slowly, the sweet, buttery taste of home a final, grounding ritual in the heart of the glittering chaos.

"Five minutes, everyone! Final line-up! Let's go!" The producer’s voice cut through the room like a blade, and the low hum of anxiety instantly spiked into a frantic buzz. Wei stood up, his legs feeling strangely steady. He put on the jacket of his suit, the fabric now feeling like a familiar friend. He joined the silent, nervous procession of contestants filing out of the green room and back towards the stage. The walk down the dark corridor was the longest of his life. This was it. The final steps of a journey that had begun with a shaky hand on a porcelain saucer. He took his place in the line-up on stage, the lights hotter and brighter than before, the roar of the crowd a deafening, expectant wall of sound. He stood between the comedian and the head of the acrobatic family, all of them smiling brave, brittle smiles for the camera, their linked hands damp with sweat.

Ant and Dec stood centre stage, holding the single, terrifyingly important envelope. "The votes have been counted and verified," Dec announced, his usual cheerful demeanour replaced by a solemn gravity. "I can now reveal that the act finishing in third place is..." He named the acrobatic family. They received the news with grace, smiling and hugging each other as the audience gave them a massive round of applause. And then there were two. "It all comes down to this," Ant said, as the giant screen behind them split, showing Wei's tear-streaked, emotional face on one side, and the young girl’s fiercely determined one on the other. "Our singing waiter from Singapore... or our singing angel from Essex. Your winner of Britain's Got Talent 2024 is..." The pause was an eternity. The entire world seemed to stop. In that silent chasm, Wei’s life flashed before him: the hiss of the kopi machine, the smell of rain on hot Singaporean pavement, Ah Ma’s wrinkled hand patting his knee. He felt a strange, serene detachment. It was okay. Whatever happened now, it was okay.

"...WEI!" The name was a detonation. It took a full second to register, and in that second, a galaxy of silver confetti exploded from the ceiling, showering down on the stage in a silent, glittering storm. The sound of the crowd was a physical blow, a joyous, unrestrained sonic boom. He saw the young singer turn to him, her own disappointment bravely masked as she threw her arms around him in a hug. "Congratulations," she whispered in his ear, her voice genuine. Then the other contestants were upon him, a surging wave of bodies, hugging him, shouting his name, thumping his back. He was lost in the centre of a joyous riot. Ant and Dec reappeared, pushing a large, gleaming trophy into his hands. It was heavy, cold, and unbelievably real. Someone else handed him a giant cheque. He stared at the number written on it, the string of zeroes so long it seemed impossible. His song, his beautiful, heart-wrenching song, began to play over the speakers as the show’s credits started to roll. "Wei! Any final words?" Dec shouted over the beautiful chaos. Wei looked up, finding the main camera, its red light a single, focused eye in the swirling celebration. He knew there was only one person he was talking to. He raised the trophy, his hand shaking, and looked directly into the lens. "Ah Ma," he said, his voice cracking with an emotion too big for words. "We did it."The moment the broadcast feed cut, the magic shattered into a million glittering, messy pieces. The silver confetti that had been a silent, magical storm was now just paper littering the stage, clinging to his suit and hair. The thunderous applause, which had been the unified voice of a nation, fractured into the individual shouts and chatter of a dispersing crowd. The warm, embracing lights snapped off, replaced by the stark, functional white light of the work lamps, revealing the dust motes dancing in the air and the scuffed, weary reality of the stage floor. The dream was over, and the business had begun. Before he could even process the weight of the trophy in his hands, Nigel, the executive producer, was at his side. His face, which had been a mask of tense professionalism, was now alight with a predatory, triumphant gleam. He clapped a firm, proprietary hand on Wei's shoulder, his grip surprisingly strong. "Incredible, son. Absolutely incredible," he said, his voice a low, excited buzz. "We've got a global number one on our hands here. My office, first thing tomorrow, nine a.m. sharp. We'll have the lawyers, the label executives, the whole team. This is just the beginning." The words 'lawyers' and 'label' were alien concepts, heavy and sharp-edged. Wei was swept off the stage in a phalanx of security guards and production assistants, a dazed king being paraded through the frantic backstage corridors, the heavy trophy his sceptre, the giant cheque his shield.

He found a momentary pocket of quiet in a small, deserted dressing room, the air thick with the scent of stale hairspray and nervous sweat. The distant sounds of the wrap party, the popping of champagne corks and forced laughter, felt like they were happening on another planet. He needed to hear one voice, the only one that mattered, the one that could anchor him to the ground before he floated away completely. His hands were still trembling as he dialled, the phone slippery in his grasp. He listened to the international ringtone, a sound that now seemed to stitch the two halves of his world together. Finally, the ringing stopped, and her voice, small and clear and utterly unchanged, came through the line. "Hello?" He couldn't speak, a fresh wave of emotion closing his throat. He could hear the faint, familiar sound of the old fan whirring in the background of the flat. "Wei? Is that you? The television already finished. Very noisy," she said, her tone matter-of-fact, as if commenting on a sudden rainstorm. "Ah Ma," he finally managed, his voice a choked whisper. "I… we won." There was a pause. He waited for excitement, for tears, for something to match the hurricane he was living through. "I know," she said calmly. "I saw. Your shirt was very nice. Did you drink water? Your throat must be very dry."

The beautiful, absurd practicality of her question was the most grounding force in the universe. It cut through the confetti and the contracts, the roar of the crowd and the blinding lights, and brought him right back to himself. "Yes, Ah Ma. I drank water." He sank onto a worn-out chair, the trophy heavy in his lap. "Don't get a big head, now," she continued, her voice gentle but firm. "All those people shouting, it is just wind. Tomorrow you still have to wake up and be a good man. Don't let them make you forget how to wash your own rice." He laughed, a real, unburdened laugh that felt like a release of all the tension he'd held for days, for years. He could picture her perfectly, sitting on her stool, her face illuminated by the glow of the television, her primary concern not the fortune he had won, but the state of his soul. "I won't, Ah Ma," he promised, his voice steady for the first time all night. "I promise." They spoke for a few more minutes, her conversation a comforting litany of neighbourhood news and reminders to eat properly, a soothing balm on his over-stimulated mind. When he hung up, the distant party no longer sounded alien; it was just noise, and he was insulated from it by the profound, unshakeable love of his grandmother.

Leaving the theatre was an entirely new form of trial. The narrow alley behind the stage door, which had been dark and deserted when he arrived, was now a solid wall of people and flashing lights. A roar went up as he emerged, shielded by Sarah and two imposing security guards. Faces, contorted with excitement, pressed in on him. Mobile phones were thrust towards him, their lenses like a hundred unblinking eyes. The pop of paparazzi flashbulbs was no longer part of a controlled press call; it was a chaotic, blinding assault. "Wei! Over here! How does it feel?" a voice shouted. A hand grabbed his sleeve, and he flinched, startled by the sudden, invasive contact. Sarah, her expression a mask of grim determination, pushed the hand away. "Back off! Give him some space!" she yelled, her voice sharp. She hustled him towards the waiting black car, a small, protected island in a sea of clamouring adoration. He fell into the back seat, the door slamming shut and muffling the noise to a dull, frantic roar. He was a phenomenon now, a public commodity, and the city that had once ignored him now wanted to consume him.

The journey back to the hotel was a silent, surreal blur. Sarah, her demeanour now completely transformed, sat beside him, no longer an impatient handler but a fierce, proprietary guardian. "We'll have a proper security detail organised by morning," she said, scrolling rapidly through her phone, which was buzzing incessantly. "Your new manager will want to speak with you tomorrow, he's flying in from LA. We've had offers from every major talk show in the UK and the US. We need to be strategic." The words 'security detail', 'manager', 'strategic' spun around Wei's head, meaningless and heavy. He stared out the window at the rain-slicked streets of London, at the lights of pubs and the glowing windows of flats where ordinary people were living ordinary lives. Just yesterday, he had been an anonymous observer of this world. Now, he was trapped in a moving bubble, staring out at a life he was no longer a part of. The city hadn't changed, but his relationship to it, and its to him, had been irrevocably altered in the space of a single song.

Back in the gilded cage of his hotel room, the silence was a deafening contrast to the chaos of the past few hours. He placed the heavy gold trophy on the polished surface of the desk, where it gleamed under the soft lamplight, a silent, surreal testament to the night's events. He laid the giant cheque beside it, the paper feeling flimsy and ridiculous next to the solid weight of the award. He had won. He was rich. He was famous. He had achieved everything he had ever dreamed of in the humid confines of the kopitiam. He walked to the vast window and looked down at the river of lights that was the city. He was at the pinnacle, the star on top of the tree. But a new kind of loneliness, vast and echoing, began to creep in. It was not the ache of being unseen, but the profound, terrifying isolation of being seen as something he was not yet sure he was. The kopitiam boy was gone, and the global star had not yet been fully born. He was suspended between two worlds, a ghost in a palace, with only the memory of his grandmother's voice and the lingering taste of pineapple tart to remind him of who he truly was.Sleep was a shallow, restless sea. Wei would drift towards its surface, startled awake by phantom sounds – the roar of the crowd, the insistent ring of a hotel telephone, the clatter of a porcelain saucer hitting the floor. Each time, he would open his eyes to the profound, opulent silence of the hotel room, the only light the cold, digital glow of the alarm clock and the distant, unblinking lights of the city outside his window. On the desk, the golden trophy was a solid, gleaming anchor in the darkness, the first and last thing he saw. It was a constant, silent reminder that the world he had woken up in was no longer the one he had known. He felt like a ghost haunting someone else’s magnificent new life, a life that had been tailored and presented to him overnight, and he was yet to find out if it would ever truly fit.

He was awake long before the 8 a.m. alarm he had set. He picked up his phone, his thumb hovering over the news app with a sense of trepidation. His own face stared back at him from a dozen different homepages. He was a headline. 'THE HAWKER HERO: FROM NOODLES TO NUMBER ONE'. 'WEI'S WALTZ TO VICTORY'. They had distilled his life into a catchy, alliterative phrase. They showed pictures of the kopitiam, its grime and clamour looking strangely romantic through the lens of a British tabloid. They had found old school photos, quotes from neighbours he barely knew who claimed they "always knew he had something special." His story, his real, complicated, sometimes painful story, had been polished, packaged, and sold to the world before he had even had his first cup of coffee in his new life. It was a disembodying experience, like reading a biography of a stranger who just happened to look exactly like him.

He chose his own clothes for the 9 a.m. meeting – the same simple trousers and t-shirt he had worn on the plane. It was a small, quiet act of self-preservation, a refusal to wear the costume of the star until he had decided what that meant. When he stepped out of the lift, Sarah was waiting, her demeanour noticeably different. The impatient, brisk efficiency was gone, replaced by a careful, almost deferential respect. A new man stood beside her, broad-shouldered and wearing a discreet earpiece, his presence a silent, imposing buffer between Wei and the world. The sleek black car was idling at the kerb, and as Wei slid into the leather-scented interior, he saw the flash of a camera from across the street. The doorman, his face a mask of practiced indifference, angled his body to block the shot. He was being watched. The city he had walked through invisibly just days before was now a landscape of potential observers, and the car was no longer a transport, but a mobile fortress.

The record label's headquarters was a monument to modern success, a gleaming tower of glass and steel that pierced the grey London sky. The lobby was a minimalist expanse of white marble and expensive art, the walls adorned not with paintings, but with framed platinum discs from artists whose names were global shorthand for fame and fortune. It was a temple built to the gods of music and money, and Wei felt like an unworthy supplicant entering its hallowed halls. He was escorted to a top-floor boardroom, a sterile space with a panoramic view so vast it felt like they were floating above the city. Nigel was there, looking relaxed and triumphant, along with two other men in impeccably tailored suits who radiated an aura of quiet, ruthless power. But all of them deferred to the man who rose to greet him, a man who seemed to have stepped directly out of a magazine. He was tanned, his teeth impossibly white, his suit worn with a casual, confident grace. He extended a hand, his grip firm and his smile disarming. "Wei. Marcus Vance," he said, his voice a smooth, transatlantic baritone. "Your new manager. I flew in from LA this morning. We have a lot to talk about, my friend. You're a star."

Marcus was a master conductor, and the meeting was his symphony. He didn't sit, but paced before the vast window, using the city skyline as his backdrop. He spoke with a captivating, infectious energy, weaving a grand narrative of Wei’s future. He didn't talk about singing; he talked about brand identity, about market penetration, about a multi-platform global launch strategy. "You're not just a guy with a great voice, Wei," Marcus said, his eyes gleaming with genuine excitement. "You're a story. The humble waiter, the underdog, singing for his grandma. It’s authentic. It's relatable. It's a narrative goldmine. We leverage that. Phase one: the single. We strike while the iron is hot. It has to be a Christmas release. That guarantees us the Christmas Number One spot. From there, the album in March. We break the UK, then we break America. I've already got you tentatively booked on Kimmel." He spoke of Wei’s life as if it were a business plan, his dreams a set of quarterly targets, and his very soul a valuable asset to be leveraged for maximum return.

Then came the crescendo of his pitch. He walked to the long, polished table and slid a slim jewel case across its surface, stopping it perfectly in front of Wei. "And this," he announced, his voice dropping to a dramatic, conspiratorial level, "is the rocket fuel. Your debut single." The mock-up cover art showed a heavily stylized photo of Wei on the BGT stage, his face angled towards a shower of digital starlight. The title was printed in a sweeping, elegant font: 'My Starlight Dream'. "Written by the best team in the business," Marcus continued, his smile widening. "These guys have more number ones than I've had hot dinners. It's a huge, soaring ballad about never giving up on your dreams. It's aspirational, it's emotional, it’s everything your new brand represents. It’s a guaranteed, copper-bottomed smash hit." The words, meant to be reassuring, landed like stones in Wei's stomach. He looked at the generic title, the airbrushed photo. This song wasn't his. It was a product, engineered in a lab to appeal to the widest possible demographic. It was a karaoke version of his own life.

He sat in silence, the weight of their collective expectation pressing down on him. The men in the suits were smiling, nodding, their faces alight with the prospect of a massive payday. He remembered the feeling on stage, the raw, terrifying honesty of his performance, the profound connection that had silenced ten thousand people. He remembered Simon Cowell’s words, not about selling records, but about giving a voice to the voiceless. He thought of the kopitiam, the smell of scalded coffee and frying garlic, the chaotic symphony of real life. And most vividly, he heard his grandmother’s voice in his ear, a calm, steady anchor in this swirling vortex of hype and commerce. *Don't let them make you forget how to wash your own rice.* His fear, which had been a cold knot in his gut, slowly began to transform into something else, something harder and more defiant. He was not a brand. He was not a narrative. He was a man who had sung a song for his grandmother.

"Can I hear it?" Wei asked. His voice was quiet, almost a whisper, but in the sterile, soundproofed room, it had the force of a shout. Marcus’s smile broadened, mistaking Wei’s request for eager compliance. "Of course. Prepare to be blown away." He tapped a command into a tablet, and the song flooded the room through a state-of-the-art sound system. The production was immense, a wall of synthesized strings, a booming drum track, and layers of angelic backing vocals. Then the singer, a professional session vocalist, began to sing. The voice was technically perfect, every note hit with flawless precision. But the lyrics were a hollow collection of well-worn platitudes about reaching for the moon and catching a falling star. It was a song written by a committee, scrubbed clean of any grit, any pain, any truth. It was a beautiful, expensive, empty vessel. It was the furthest thing from the sticky soya sauce on a porcelain saucer, from the worn-out sneakers on a grimy floor, from the heart of his own story.

The final, over-produced crescendo faded, leaving a ringing silence in its wake. Marcus leaned back in his chair, his arms spread wide in a gesture of triumph. "See? A monster," he declared, his gaze sweeping the room for confirmation from his colleagues. "The public are going to eat this up. So, Wei. What do you think? It's a hit, right?" He turned his high-wattage smile on Wei, his eyes expectant, waiting for the grateful, compliant nod that would seal the deal and set the machine in motion. Nigel, the show’s producer, shifted uncomfortably, his gaze flicking from Wei to Marcus and back again, his face a mask of anxious disbelief. The panoramic view of London, once a symbol of limitless opportunity, now seemed to mock them, a vast, indifferent backdrop to the sudden, catastrophic failure of their perfectly laid plans.

Marcus Vance, however, was a professional. The cold, hard glint in his eye was quickly masked by a slow, patronising smile, the kind an adult gives a child who has said something charmingly naive but fundamentally wrong. He let out a soft, theatrical chuckle and leaned forward, placing his elbows on the gleaming table. "Wei, my friend. I think perhaps you misunderstand," he said, his voice dropping to a smooth, reasonable tone. "This song, 'My Starlight Dream', this isn't a suggestion. This is the rocket fuel. This is the formula that has worked a hundred times before. It's what the public wants to hear from you. It's the sound of a winner." He gestured around the room, at the platinum discs on the walls. "This is how you get one of those. This is how you stay a star, not just become a fifteen-minute sob story on a reality show."

The condescension in his voice was a sharp pebble in Wei’s shoe, irritating but also grounding. He looked not at Marcus, but at the empty jewel case on the table. He thought of the songs his grandmother loved, the old melodies of Engelbert Humperdinck and Tom Jones, songs that spoke of lost waltzes and green, green grass of home. Those songs had stories, they had soul, they were stained with life. This song was sterile. "It's a very good formula," Wei agreed politely, his quietness a stark contrast to Marcus’s confident patter. "But the song I sang in the semi-final, 'The Last Waltz'... that was for my grandmother. The song I sang in the final… that was my own feeling. That is why people listened. I think… I think that is my formula."

A flicker of genuine annoyance crossed Marcus's face before being suppressed. "Wei, with all due respect," he said, the politeness in his voice now thin and sharp as a razor's edge, "you sell noodles for a living. We sell records for a living. Let us do our job." The blow was delivered with a smile, but it was designed to put Wei firmly in his place, to remind him of the greasy apron and the grimy floor, to remind him that he was an amateur who had gotten lucky, a guest in a world he did not understand. But instead of shame, Wei felt a strange surge of clarity. Marcus was right. He *did* sell noodles. He knew the weight of a hot bowl, the sound of a customer’s grunt of satisfaction, the reality of a long day on his feet. That was his truth. This gleaming boardroom was not.

"Yes," Wei said, meeting his gaze directly. "And that is why I must sing my own story. Not one that is written for me." The two label executives exchanged a look of pure exasperation. One of them finally spoke, his voice gravelly and impatient. "Look, kid. We've invested a lot of money in this. The machine is already in motion. We have producers, writers, marketing teams, all ready to go. You don't just get to walk in here and say no. That's not how it works." Marcus held up a hand, silencing him, reasserting his control. "Okay," he said, his tone now cold and final, the charming mask completely gone. "Let's be very clear. Your prize money is tied to the release of a single with this label. Your contract has clauses about creative cooperation. You can walk out of this room and refuse to play ball. You'll be in breach of contract. We'll sue you, and you'll spend the next five years and every penny you've won fighting us in court. By the time it's over, nobody will remember your name." He stood up, smoothing down his perfect suit. "Or, you can be a professional. You can walk into that studio tomorrow, sing the hit song we have written for you, and become a global superstar. The choice is yours. We'll expect your decision by the end of the day."

The drive back to the hotel was a journey through a frozen landscape. Sarah sat in the front seat, staring rigidly ahead, the air around her thick with disappointment and frustration. She didn't speak a word to him. The silent security guard seemed to take up more space than before, his presence a constant, brooding reminder of the gilded cage that was closing in around him. Wei stared out the window, but he didn't see the red buses or the black cabs. He saw the crossroads Marcus had laid out before him: one path leading to a hollow, manufactured stardom, the other to a protracted, ruinous legal battle and a swift return to obscurity. Both seemed like a kind of death. Back in the hotel room, the magnificent view felt like a taunt. He walked over to the desk. The golden trophy seemed to mock him with its empty victory, and the giant cheque felt like the advance on a deal with the devil. He had stood on that stage and felt a connection so real and powerful it had changed him, and now he was being asked to betray that very feeling, to commodify it into something he didn't recognise.

He sat on the edge of the bed, the silence of the room pressing in on him, amplifying the frantic turmoil in his mind. He couldn't call Ah Ma. He couldn't burden her with this. This was a battle he had to face alone. Or did he? He thought of the judges, of their words. He thought of Simon Cowell. The tiger. The man who had created this whole spectacle. The man who had looked at him and seen not a product, but an artist. The man who had spoken of giving a voice to the voiceless. Was that just part of the show? Was that just another well-crafted line in the television narrative? Or was there a chance, a sliver of a chance, that he had meant it? The idea was audacious, terrifying. It was a leap into the dark. But staying here, waiting for the lawyers to sharpen their knives, was a guaranteed fall. He stood up, a desperate, wild plan forming in his mind. He didn't have a number. He didn't have a contact. But he knew the name of Simon’s company. It was written on half the paperwork he’d been given. Syco Entertainment.

He grabbed his room key and the red packet from his pocket, stuffing it deep inside for courage. He walked out of the room, his heart hammering against his ribs. The security guard, posted impassively in the hallway, straightened as he saw him. "Sir? Do you need something?" Wei’s mind raced. He couldn't just walk out. "I... I need some fresh air," he said, the lie feeling clumsy on his tongue. "I'm just going to the hotel garden." The guard nodded, his eyes betraying nothing. Wei walked to the lift, his back prickling, feeling the guard's stare follow him. Instead of going to the lobby, he pressed the button for the service level in the basement. The lift doors opened onto a concrete corridor smelling of bleach and industrial detergent. He hurried past laundry carts and staff on their breaks, found a fire exit, and pushed his way out into a grimy back alley. The cold, damp London air hit him, a shocking, welcome dose of reality. He was free. For now. He pulled his phone from his pocket, his fingers fumbling as he typed 'Syco Entertainment London office' into the search bar. An address appeared. It was in Kensington, on the other side of the city. He looked up and down the alley, then walked out onto the street, raising a hesitant hand to hail one of London's iconic black cabs, a lone soldier going to face the tiger in his own den.The black cab was a dark, rattling haven, a bubble of anonymity moving through the vast, indifferent city. The journey was a tour of a city he was no longer seeing as a tourist. The grand, historical facades of the buildings were no longer just picturesque scenery; they were monuments to a power and an establishment he was about to confront. He clutched the red packet in his pocket, its crinkly paper a fragile link to a world where value was not measured in platinum discs, but in a well-made meal and a promise kept.

The office was not a gleaming tower as he had imagined, but a discreet, elegant Georgian townhouse on a quiet, tree-lined square in Kensington. It looked less like a corporate headquarters and more like the private residence of an aristocrat, its pristine white facade and black iron railings projecting an aura of old money and impenetrable exclusivity. Taking a deep, shuddering breath, he walked up the three polished stone steps and pushed open the heavy, lacquered black door.

Behind a reception desk that looked like a giant, sculpted block of white marble sat a woman who was a perfect extension of her surroundings. He stood before the desk, feeling clumsy, damp, and utterly out of place. He cleared his throat.

I need to speak with him." The receptionist gave a small, weary sigh, the kind reserved for madmen and lost tourists. He had been categorized, processed, and rejected in under thirty seconds.

He should have turned and walked away. Every instinct for self-preservation screamed at him to retreat, to flee back to the relative safety of his hotel room and face the lawyers. He hadn't planned to say that, the words coming from a place of raw, desperate honesty.

The receptionist stared at him for a long moment, her mask of professional boredom momentarily cracking. He had introduced a piece of information that was not in her script. She had clearly seen the show; everyone had. The name, the face, the story – they were now part of the public consciousness. But rules were rules. "I'll wait," Wei said, and walked over to one of the low, uncomfortable-looking leather sofas that lined the wall. He sat down, his back ramrod straight, and prepared for a siege.

The receptionist pointedly ignored him, taking calls with a hushed, important tone and greeting the occasional, impossibly well-dressed person who came through the door by name. Simon Cowell was an untouchable deity in this corporate pantheon. The man on the television, the judge who had seemed to see into his soul, was just a character, a performance. She made a point of tidying her already immaculate desk, the soft clicks and rustles of paper amplified in the cavernous silence, each sound a tiny judgment. Wei felt his resolve beginning to fray. The hope the intern had ignited was flickering, threatened by the cold, rational reality of the room. He was a fool. A naive, foolish boy from a kopitiam who had mistaken a television persona for a real person and was about to burn his entire future to the ground for nothing. He was just about to stand up, to concede defeat and slink back to the hotel to face the wrath of Marcus Vance, when the frosted glass doors swung open.

He was wearing a simple, plain grey t-shirt and dark jeans, and he looked tired. He ignored Marcus, who was trying to physically block him, and took a step towards Simon. "Mr. Cowell," he began, his voice hoarse but steady. "Last night, you said something to me. You said I gave a voice to people who don't have one. People who work hard and have secret dreams." Simon watched him, his expression unchanging, neither encouraging nor discouraging. A profound, deathly silence descended on the room. Marcus looked as though he might spontaneously combust. The intern, who had been peeking through the crack in the glass doors, held her breath. Wei had laid all his cards on the table. He had challenged the king in his own court, using the king’s own words as his weapon.

Simon was quiet for a long time, his gaze unwavering. He looked from Wei’s earnest, pleading face to Marcus’s apoplectic one, then at the two executives who were practically vibrating with indignation. He was adrift. The receptionist wouldn't meet his eye, already treating him as a non-person. He walked out of the building, the heavy black door closing behind him with an air of finality, and stepped back into the anonymous flow of the London street. The city, which had been a backdrop to his whirlwind of fame, was now a vast, indifferent wilderness, and he was utterly alone within it, stripped of his entourage and his gilded cage. He emerged at Leicester Square, blinking in the unexpectedly bright afternoon light, and found himself instantly swallowed by a human tide. In a small, unassuming eatery, he saw her. An old woman, her back bent into the same gentle, permanent curve as his grandmother's, was methodically wiping down tables with a grey cloth. The song began to arrive, not as a grand idea, but as a series of small, sharp, sensory details. The words were not poetic or grand; they were simple, concrete, and true. He returned to the hotel as evening fell, the keycard surprisingly still active. The magnificent room, which had once felt like a lonely palace, was now his workshop, his sanctuary. He ignored the colossal bed and the panoramic view. He sat at the desk, the golden trophy pushed to one side, his phone propped up against a glass of water. The melody came next, a quiet, simple tune that mirrored the plainness of the lyrics. He hummed it into his phone's voice recorder, his voice soft and raw. It wasn't a soaring power ballad; it was more like a folk song, a lullaby, a secret whispered in the dark. He worked through the night, fueled by the cheap, strong tea from the hotel's complimentary tray and the lingering, comforting taste of the noodle soup. The pressure of the deadline, the fear of failure, the fury of Marcus Vance – it all faded into the background, replaced by the intense, all-consuming focus of creation. He crafted a bridge, a moment where the quiet, observational verses swelled into a chorus of profound, aching longing, not for fame, but for the simple, anchoring reality of home. He was building his song not from a formula, but from the heartwood of his own experience.

As the first, pearly-grey light of dawn began to break over the London skyline, casting long shadows across the plush carpet, he was done. He listened back to the rough voice memo on his phone, his voice, cracked with fatigue, humming the simple, haunting melody over and over. He read the lyrics, a plainspoken poem about a porcelain saucer and a boy with a secret dream. It was a million miles away from the slick, bombastic perfection of 'My Starlight Dream'. It was fragile, it was nakedly honest, and it was entirely his. He was exhausted, his eyes gritty from lack of sleep, his body aching, but he felt a sense of calm, triumphant peace he had never known. He had been given twenty-four hours to write a masterpiece. He didn't know if he had succeeded. But he knew, with an unshakeable certainty, that he had written the truth. He picked up his phone, found a number in the call history from the frantic day before, and dialled. A sleepy, surprised voice answered. It was the intern from Syco. "Hello?" she mumbled. He had thrown his final, desperate firework into the dark, and now there was nothing left to do but wait and see if anyone had noticed the light. The exhaustion of the past forty-eight hours washed over him in a great, leaden wave, but it was a clean exhaustion, the satisfying ache of a hard day’s work, not the gnawing fatigue of anxiety. He walked into the palatial bathroom and stood under a stream of hot water, letting it wash away the grime of the city and the lingering, phantom scent of the recording studio. The journey back to the Syco building was a passage through a dreamscape. The city outside the window was the same, but Wei was seeing it with new eyes. He was no longer an intruder or a supplicant. This was it. The final showdown. This was more terrifying than the Hammersmith Apollo with its ten thousand screaming fans. He sang of the stubborn ghost of dark soya sauce, of the grumpy, impatient voice of Uncle Lim, of the worn-trough sneakers on a greasy floor. He poured all the fear, all the defiance, all the profound, soul-deep homesickness of the last forty-eight hours into his voice. It was a song of quiet dignity, of hidden hopes, and of the unwavering love for a grandmother who was the true north of his entire world. The final note, a soft, questioning hum, hung in the soundproofed air for a heartbeat, then faded, leaving a silence that was more profound than any he had ever known.

Wei opened his eyes. Simon Cowell had not moved. He was staring at Wei, his head tilted slightly to one side, his expression still utterly unreadable. He had chosen the saucer over the starlight. Wei, his heart soaring, his mind reeling, nodded mutely and took his place at the microphone once more, ready to sing his story for the record.The second performance was different. The first had been a desperate, whispered plea in the dark, a final, hopeless gamble. This one was a command performance. Wei stood before the microphone, the fear still present as a low hum beneath his skin, but it was now overlaid with a profound, terrifying sense of purpose. He had been heard. He had been asked to sing again. He was no longer a supplicant; he was an artist being given his first, most important session. He took a breath, not to calm his nerves, but to draw in the memory of the kopitiam, to fill his lungs with the ghosts of steam and grease and cheap coffee. He closed his eyes, and when he sang, the voice that emerged was steadier, imbued with a quiet, bone-deep authority. It was the same simple melody, the same plainspoken words, but this time they were not a confession; they were a declaration.

He sang the song all the way through, his voice the only sound in the acoustically perfect room. The final note faded, and the silence that followed was heavy with judgment. Wei kept his eyes closed, his heart hammering, waiting for the verdict. "Okay," Simon’s voice finally came through the talkback speaker, clipped and devoid of emotion. "That’s the sketch. Now, the work begins. Do it again." Wei’s eyes snapped open. Simon was leaning forward, his focus absolute. "This time," he continued, his voice resonating with the quiet intensity of a master craftsman, "when you sing about the saucer rattling, I want to feel the tremor in your own voice. Don’t just tell me you were nervous; make me feel it. And the line, 'the 7:30 rush'... it's too clean. I want to hear the weariness in it. The sound of someone who has done the same thing six thousand times and is tired to his very soul. Forget I'm here. Forget the microphone. You're back there. Sing me the truth of it."

It was the most insightful, most demanding direction Wei had ever received. He spent the next hour singing his life over and over again, each take a new excavation of his own past. Simon was relentless, a ruthless sculptor chipping away every trace of performance, every hint of artifice. "No. You're anticipating the chorus. Stay in the verse." "Softer on that line. You're telling a secret, not making a speech." "Hold that last note. Let it ache a little before you let it go." It was exhausting, frustrating, and exhilarating. Wei was pushed past the point of conscious thought, beyond his fears and his hopes, until he was nothing but a conduit for the song. He was no longer just reliving the memories; he was controlling them, shaping them, turning the raw, chaotic material of his life into something focused and powerful. He learned to wield his own vulnerability as an instrument, to find the strength in the quietest, most fragile notes.

Then, on the seventh or eighth take, something shifted. As he sang the final verse, a verse about seeing his grandmother's face in his mind and finding the courage to go on, a single, genuine tear escaped and traced a slow path down his cheek. He didn't wipe it away. He sang through it, his voice catching for a fraction of a second, a tiny, unplanned imperfection that was more honest than any perfectly executed note could ever be. He held the final, fading note until his breath gave out, and in the profound silence that followed, he knew. He opened his eyes and saw Simon leaning back in his chair, his expression unreadable, his eyes closed. For a moment, Wei thought he had fallen asleep. Then, Simon opened his eyes, and they were clear and sharp. He looked at Wei for a long, quiet moment, a look of profound, professional respect passing between the powerful mogul and the boy from the kopitiam. He didn't say a word. He simply nodded, a single, slow, definitive gesture, and pressed the talkback button. "We've got it," he said.

With the recording done, the atmosphere in the room changed instantly. The intense, focused artist retreated, and the shrewd, decisive businessman returned. Simon played the final take back through the enormous studio speakers. Hearing his own voice, so naked and raw and amplified to such a scale, was a deeply surreal experience for Wei. It sounded so fragile, yet it filled the entire room, a ghost of his past made tangible and immense. "Right," Simon said, more to himself than to Wei. "This is the master vocal. We build everything around this, and we build it carefully. Nothing that will get in the way of the lyric. A touch of reverb, to give it space. Maybe a single cello line enters on the second verse. Or a piano. Something sparse. Something that sounds like rain." He was already producing the record in his head, protecting its delicate, unconventional core from the very machine he commanded. He understood, with an instinct that had made him millions, that the song’s power was in its quietness, its stark simplicity.

He muted the track and swivelled in his chair to face Wei directly. "Now, for the other business," he said, his tone shifting to one of cool, pragmatic authority. "Marcus Vance will not be your manager. That relationship is over. The contract you signed with the show has been… renegotiated. As of this morning." He paused, letting the weight of his power settle in the room. "My legal team will handle the dissolution of any prior agreements. You don't need to speak to him, or anyone from that label, ever again. Do you understand?" Wei, his mind reeling from the speed and totality of this shift in his fortunes, could only nod mutely. "Good," Simon said. "Because you have a new contract to sign. You're a Syco artist now. You work with me. We'll find you a proper manager, a team that understands what we are doing here. We are not making a pop hit, Wei. We are starting a career."

The walk out of the studio was a journey into a new reality. The building felt different. The air tasted different. The intimidating, chic receptionist looked up as he passed and gave him a small, genuine smile. The intern was waiting by the main entrance, practically vibrating with nervous energy, her face alight with a triumphant, conspiratorial grin. She handed him a small, sealed envelope. "His PA asked me to give you this," she whispered. "It's a car service and a new hotel. A private one. He said you've earned some peace and quiet." She looked at him with wide, admiring eyes. "No one has ever done what you just did. You're a legend." Wei, still dazed, mumbled his thanks. He was no legend; he was just a boy who had been too scared to do anything but tell the truth. He stepped out of the heavy black door and into the cool London air. The sky had cleared, and a pale, watery sun was breaking through the clouds. A discreet, unmarked car was waiting for him at the kerb, the driver holding the door open for him. This was not the flashy, ostentatious transport of a reality show winner; this was the quiet, efficient machinery of a serious artist.

Back in a new room, in a different hotel, one that was quiet and understated and luxurious in a way that didn't shout, Wei finally felt the ground beneath his feet. He walked to the window, which overlooked a quiet garden square, not the sprawling, chaotic city. He placed the red packet his grandmother had given him on the windowsill, its familiar colour a warm, vibrant anchor in the muted elegance of the room. He had walked into the tiger’s den armed with nothing but a song and a desperate hope, and had emerged not just unscathed, but with a future. A real one. He thought of his grandmother’s words, her simple, profound wisdom that had guided him through the fire. He picked up the phone to call her, not with fear or with a desperate need for comfort, but with news. He wanted to tell her that 'The Saucer Song' was real. He wanted to tell her that someone had finally heard the music in the clatter of her life, and his. He wanted to tell her that her boy was finally, truly, on his way home, even though he had never felt further away.The phone rang only twice before she answered, her voice clear and familiar, a lighthouse beacon in the vast, shifting fog of his new life. "Wei?" she asked, a note of quiet question in her tone, as if she had been waiting for his call. He didn't launch into the dramatic tale of his rebellion, of the cancelled contracts and the terrifying confrontation. He simply said, "Ah Ma, they wanted me to sing a song that wasn't true. So I sang them ours instead." He explained, in simple terms, about the song he had written in the night, the song about the kopitiam, about the saucer, about her. He told her how he had sung it for the man she called the tiger. He waited, his breath held, for her reaction. There was a long pause on the other end of the line, a silence filled not with static, but with thoughtful consideration. "Good," she said finally, her voice imbued with an unshakable, common-sense authority. "A song should not be a lie. It is like cooking. If you use bad ingredients, the food will taste bad, no matter how nice the plate is." She paused again, and he could almost hear her nodding to herself. "So, you are working hard. But are you eating? This London is very cold. You must eat soup to keep your body warm."

The next morning, his new life began not with a bang, but with a quiet knock on his hotel room door. A woman stood there, her hair streaked with silver and her eyes kind but shrewd. She was dressed elegantly but simply, projecting an aura of calm competence that was the polar opposite of Marcus Vance’s restless, predatory energy. "Wei? I'm Eleanor Vance," she said, her voice warm and her handshake firm. "I'll be looking after you." She was not related to Marcus, she quickly clarified with a wry smile, as if sensing his momentary alarm. She was his new manager, handpicked by Simon. Over coffee in the quiet hotel lounge, she introduced him to Ben, a young, sharp-witted publicist who spoke about "narrative integrity" and "long-term career building" rather than "market penetration." They didn't present him with a dizzying schedule of press calls and television appearances. Instead, they asked him what he needed. "Simon's directive is clear," Eleanor explained, her gaze direct and serious. "Our first and only job right now is to protect you and to protect the song. The world can wait. We get the art right first."

His first full day as a Syco artist was spent back in the quiet sanctuary of Studio B. Simon was not there, but a producer named Miles was, a quiet, thoughtful man with a beard and a deep love for sound. A young woman with a cello case that seemed bigger than she was sat nervously on a stool in the main recording room. "Simon’s idea," Miles explained. "He wants the cello to be like a second voice, a conversation with yours." Wei watched through the glass as the cellist began to play, her bow drawing a long, melancholic note that seemed to hang in the air like a question. Miles turned to him. "What does that sound make you feel?" he asked. Wei was taken aback. No one had ever asked him a question like that. He thought for a moment, then said, "It sounds… lonely. Like someone waiting for a bus before the sun comes up." Miles nodded slowly, his eyes lighting up with understanding. "Okay. Good. Let's work with that." For hours, they worked, not with a pre-written score, but with feeling. Wei would sing a line, and the cellist would answer. He was not a bystander; he was a collaborator, learning a new language, the language of translating emotion into sound.

The days that followed fell into a new, peaceful rhythm, a quiet, focused routine that was a balm to his frayed nerves. Each morning, a car would take him to the studio, where he would spend the day working with Miles and a small, rotating cast of musicians – a pianist who could make single notes sound like raindrops on a windowpane, a guitarist who added a subtle, finger-picked pattern that felt like the quiet ticking of a clock. The bombast of 'My Starlight Dream' was a distant memory. Here, the philosophy was 'less is more'. Every sound added had to earn its place, had to serve the stark, simple truth of his vocal. He was insulated from the outside world. Ben, his publicist, had erected a silent wall around him. There were no press, no interviews, no frantic demands on his time. His evenings were his own. He would eat simple meals in the hotel restaurant, then retreat to his room, not to stare at the walls in lonely fear, but to listen to the music Eleanor gave him – albums by artists like Nick Drake, Jeff Buckley, and Nina Simone. It was an education, a deep dive into the work of other artists who had woven their own pain and truth into their songs.

One afternoon, in a break between takes, Eleanor, Miles and Wei sat in the studio control room, listening to a rough mix of the song. "We need a title," Eleanor said, tapping a pen on her notepad. "The internal logs just call it 'Wei's Ballad', which isn't going to work. Any thoughts?" Miles suggested a few ideas. "'The Boy from Toa Payoh'? Too literal. 'Porcelain Tremor'? A bit too poetic, maybe?" They looked at Wei. He had only ever called it one thing in his head, the name that had felt right from the very beginning. "In my mind," he said quietly, "it has always been 'The Saucer Song'." Eleanor and Miles looked at each other. There was a pause. Then Miles broke into a slow smile. "Of course it is," he said, as if it were the most obvious thing in the world. "It’s perfect. It's humble, it's strange, and it's real. Just like the song." And so, the song was officially named.

A week after his desperate meeting with Simon, Wei was summoned back to Studio B. The lights were low, and the mood was solemn, almost reverent. Simon was there, sitting in his usual chair behind the vast mixing desk. Eleanor and Miles stood beside him. No one spoke as Miles cued up the final, mastered track. He pressed play, and the song began. The sound that filled the room was so much more than the sum of its parts. It started with just his voice, naked and clear, singing of the rattling saucer. Then, the cello entered, its voice a low, mournful sigh that wrapped around his own, a partner in his loneliness. The sparse piano notes fell like tears, each one perfectly placed. The song built, not to a bombastic chorus, but to a moment of raw, emotional intensity that was all the more powerful for its restraint. Wei listened, his eyes closed, his hands clenched into fists. He was hearing not just a song, but his entire life, given back to him, transformed into something beautiful and true. He heard the ache of his homesickness, the weight of his grandmother's love, the quiet dignity of his past. He had walked into this room a week ago with a desperate, broken whisper of a song, and now, it was a masterpiece. His masterpiece.

The final note, held by the cello, faded into a perfect, resonant silence. No one spoke for a long time. The air in the room was thick with unspoken emotion. Simon sat motionless, staring at the speakers, lost in thought. Finally, he swivelled his chair around and looked at Wei, his expression one of quiet, profound satisfaction. "Well," he said, his voice a low rumble. "There it is. The truth." He stood up, a clear signal that the session was over. "Now," he said, his tone shifting back to business as he looked at Eleanor, "we release it. No big campaign. No hype. We just put it out. Next Friday. We let the song do the work." He walked towards the door, pausing beside Wei. He put a hand on his shoulder, his grip firm and unexpectedly paternal. "You were right," he said quietly, for Wei's ears only. "And I was right about you. Don't ever let anyone tell you who you are again." He then left the room, leaving Wei standing in the quiet, a sense of dizzying, terrifying, and glorious finality washing over him. The song was finished. And now, the world was about to hear it.The week leading up to the release of 'The Saucer Song' was the strangest of Wei’s life. He was a man in limbo, suspended between a past that no longer existed and a future that had not yet arrived. The frantic pace of the competition, the terror of the boardroom showdown, the intense focus of the recording sessions – all of it was replaced by a profound and unnerving stillness. His days were his own, a stretch of unstructured time that felt both like a luxurious gift and an unbearable weight. He was lodged in a quiet, discreet hotel in Marylebone, a world away from the flashy opulence of his previous accommodation. There were no press calls, no frantic schedules, no entourage. Eleanor had been clear: his only job was to rest, to decompress, and to stay invisible. His invisibility, once a source of deep shame, was now a protective cloak, and he wore it gratefully.

He walked for hours through the city's green spaces, his hands buried deep in the pockets of a warm coat Eleanor had insisted on buying for him. In Regent's Park, he watched old men play chess, their faces etched with a concentration that reminded him of the uncles at the kopitiam hunched over their newspapers. In the quiet, leafy squares of Bloomsbury, he would find a bench and just watch the world go by: students rushing to class, mothers pushing strollers, lovers walking hand in hand. He was just another anonymous face in the crowd, and the peaceful anonymity was a balm to his soul. He was not 'The Singing Waiter', not the winner of Britain’s Got Talent, not a headline waiting to happen. He was just Wei, a young man a long way from home, waiting for a song to be born into the world. The anxiety was a constant companion, a low hum beneath the surface of the quiet days, but he learned to live with it. He had done his part. The rest was out of his hands.

On Thursday night, the eve of the release, the stillness became a palpable, living tension. Eleanor had called him that afternoon. "It goes live on all platforms at midnight," she'd said, her voice calm and steady. "Don't look at the numbers. Don't look at the charts. That's my job. Just know that you've made something beautiful. Be proud of that, whatever happens." Wei ate his dinner alone in his room, the food tasteless in his mouth. He tried to read one of the books Eleanor had given him, but the words blurred on the page. He tried to call Ah Ma, but he didn't know what to say. How could he explain the abstract terror of releasing a piece of your soul into the digital ether? So he just sat by the window, watching the London night come alive, the endless stream of headlights a river of other people's lives, all of them oblivious to the quiet drama unfolding in his hotel room.

At five minutes to midnight, he opened the primary music streaming app on his phone. He typed his own name into the search bar, the letters feeling alien under his thumb. Nothing. He refreshed the screen. Nothing. The minutes crawled by, each second stretching into an eternity. His heart was a frantic drum against his ribs. What if it was a mistake? What if the release had been cancelled? What if he had dreamed the whole thing? Then, exactly as the clock on his phone ticked over from 23:59 to 00:00, he refreshed the screen one last time. And there it was. His name, LIM WEI MING, in clean, sans-serif font. And beneath it, the title: 'The Saucer Song'. The cover art was a simple, black and white photograph Ben’s team had taken in the studio. It was just his face, unsmiling, his eyes looking directly into the camera, not with the polished confidence of a star, but with the quiet, weary vulnerability of the man who had written the song. He stared at it for a long moment, this official, packaged version of himself, and felt a dizzying sense of dislocation. He took a deep breath, his finger hovering over the play button, and then he pressed it.

The sound that emerged from the small speakers of his phone was his own voice, exactly as he had heard it in the studio, naked and unadorned. "The saucer rattled in my hand..." He listened, his eyes closed, his body rigid. He wasn't hearing a song; he was hearing his own life, distilled into three minutes and forty-two seconds. He heard the ache of loneliness in the cello's mournful line, the hope in the gentle piano chords, the weariness and the pride and the love in his own voice. When the final, fading note disappeared into silence, he didn't feel triumph or excitement. He just felt empty, hollowed out. He had given it all away. It wasn't his anymore. It belonged to the silence of the night, to anyone with a pair of headphones and an internet connection. He turned off his phone, placed it face down on the bedside table, and tried to sleep, a sailor who had launched his only ship into a vast, dark, and uncharted ocean.

He woke the next morning to a grey, drizzling sky and a profound sense of anticlimax. The world felt exactly the same. He turned on his phone, a knot of dread tightening in his stomach. There were no missed calls. No frantic messages. Just a single, polite good-morning text from Eleanor. He couldn't resist. He opened the music app. The song was still there. It had a play count now, a number that seemed both impossibly large and disappointingly small. He opened a social media app, typed in the song's title, and his heart sank. There were a few posts, mostly from BGT fan accounts announcing the release, but there was no groundswell, no tidal wave of reaction. It was a tiny ripple in an ocean of noise. The fear, cold and sharp, returned with a vengeance. He had been a fool. He had traded a guaranteed, manufactured hit for a quiet, personal song that nobody cared about. He had defied the machine only to be met with the world’s deafening indifference.

He spent the morning in a state of quiet, grey misery, pacing the confines of his hotel room like a caged animal. Every refresh of his screen brought only more silence. By midday, he was convinced it was over. He had failed. He had not only hung himself, as Marcus had so gleefully predicted, but he had done it in front of an empty auditorium. Just as he was spiralling into a full-blown panic, his phone rang. It was Ben, his publicist. "Wei," he said, his voice not triumphant, but filled with a strange, excited energy. "Are you looking at the charts?" "No," Wei lied, his voice thin. "Good. Don't," Ben said. "The charts are irrelevant right now. The charts are for pop songs with massive marketing budgets. That's not what we've released. I need you to listen to me. Something else is happening. Something better." He paused, and Wei could hear the sound of him typing. "It started about two hours ago. It's not a flood. It's a trickle. But the trickle is turning into a stream. People aren't just listening to the song, Wei. They're sharing it. And they're telling their own stories."

"What do you mean?" Wei asked, his heart beginning to beat a little faster. "I mean, it's not the big influencers or the music bloggers who are talking about it," Ben explained, his voice gaining momentum. "It’s ordinary people. "There's one here I want to read to you. It's from a woman who works in a kitchen in Birmingham. She wrote: 'My hands are chapped and they always smell of bleach. Thank you for telling it.' Wei..." Ben's voice cracked slightly. "You haven't just released a song. You've started a conversation." Wei sank onto the edge of his bed, the phone pressed hard against his ear. The numbers on the streaming service didn't matter. The charts were irrelevant. This was the victory. It wasn't loud. It was a quiet, profound, and deeply human connection, a network of shared feeling spreading across the country, from one invisible person to another. He hadn't given a voice to the voiceless; he had simply reminded them that they had a voice of their own. A single tear, not of sadness or relief, but of profound gratitude, rolled down his cheek. He knew who he had to call. He ended the call with Ben and immediately dialled home. When Ah Ma answered, he didn't speak of charts or sales. "Ah Ma," he said, his voice thick with emotion. "Someone who works in a kitchen, just like you... she heard our song. She said she felt seen." There was a soft sound on the other end of the line, a sound of understanding that needed no words. "Of course," his grandmother said simply, as if stating the most obvious fact in the world. "Everyone's hands get tired. Everyone's saucer rattles sometimes."
