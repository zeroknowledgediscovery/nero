Arthur Pumble believed in the substantive. This was, in many ways, his professional and personal failing. As the host, producer, and sole creative force behind “Pumble’s Perambulations” on Paramus Public Access Channel 7, he eschewed the fleeting and the frivolous. While other local personalities were covering ribbon-cuttings at new mattress stores or interviewing teenagers with prodigious cup-stacking abilities, Arthur was dedicating entire thirty-minute episodes to the history of a single, particularly ornate manhole cover on East Ridgewood Avenue. He did a two-part special on the subtle architectural shifts of the Garden State Plaza’s food court between 1985 and 1995. His viewership, according to the cable company’s generously estimated metrics, consisted of three dozen shut-ins, a handful of insomniacs, and his sister Beatrice, who watched out of a pained sense of familial duty.

His apartment, a tidy second-floor walk-up overlooking a parking lot, reflected this same ethos. Everything was solid, everything had a purpose. A heavy oak desk, a cast-iron skillet, a collection of encyclopedias he’d refused to discard in the digital age. He was a man of weight in a world that celebrated the light, the airy, the disposable. And he was, to his quiet despair, utterly invisible.

The idea, when it came, did not arrive as a gentle whisper of inspiration. It landed in the quiet hum of his living room at 2:17 AM with the gentle thud of a dropped anvil. He had been watching a nature documentary, a British production about the Okavango Delta. The narrator spoke in hushed, reverent tones about the river’s inhabitants. And then, it was there. Filling the screen of his modest television was a hippopotamus.

It wasn't just an animal. To Arthur, in that moment, it was an epiphany made flesh. It was the physical embodiment of everything he valued. It was substantive. It had weight, presence, an undeniable and profound reality. A hippo, Arthur thought, his breath catching in his chest, could not be ignored. You could not scroll past a hippo. You could not dismiss a hippo as frivolous. A hippo simply *was*. Its existence was a declarative statement.

The thought, once lodged, refused to be evicted. It grew roots, sprouted limbs, and by morning, it had become a fully-fledged, if utterly mad, plan. Arthur Pumble, a forty-four-year-old presenter from Paramus, New Jersey, was going to adopt a hippo.

His first call was not to a zoo or a wildlife sanctuary. It was to Beatrice.
“A what?” Her voice crackled over the phone, sharp with the static of disbelief.
“A hippopotamus,” Arthur repeated, calmly, as if discussing the weather. “I’m thinking of adopting one.”
There was a long silence on the other end of the line, a silence so dense Arthur could almost feel its gravitational pull. “Arthur,” she finally said, her voice strained, “did you take your allergy medicine? Sometimes the generic brand makes you… loopy.”
“I’m perfectly lucid, Bea. I’ve given it a great deal of thought. It makes perfect sense.”
“In what universe, Arthur? In what possible configuration of reality does a man in a seven-hundred-square-foot apartment in Paramus adopting a three-ton, semi-aquatic, famously homicidal African mammal make *sense*?”
“I wouldn’t keep it in the apartment,” Arthur said, stung by the implication that he hadn’t thought this through. “That’s absurd. I’ve been looking at zoning maps. There’s a parcel of undeveloped wetland near the Saddle River. It would require some engineering, of course. A proper enclosure. A heated pool for the winter months. But it’s feasible.”
“Feasible,” Beatrice repeated, the word dripping with a sarcasm so potent it could have stripped paint. “Arthur, listen to me. People adopt cats. Normal people adopt dogs. Weird, lonely people adopt lizards. No one, and I mean no one, adopts a hippo. They will lock you up.”
“Nonsense. It’s a noble goal. It’s about conservation. It’s about… substance.”
“It’s about you getting flattened into a Pumble-pancake by a creature that can bite a crocodile in half! A goldfish, Arthur. Start with a goldfish. See how that goes.”

The conversation ended, as it often did, with Beatrice exasperated and Arthur serenely resolute. Her objections were predictable, rooted in a world of practicality and convention that Arthur had long since drifted away from. She didn’t see the poetry of it. She didn’t see the magnificent, earth-shaking rightness of the idea.

He spent the next week in a fever of research. He learned that a hippo’s yawn was not a sign of sleepiness but a threat display. He learned they could outrun a human on land with terrifying ease. He learned that their skin secreted a natural, reddish sunscreen. He downloaded blueprints for zoo enclosures, studied dietary requirements (mostly grass, surprisingly manageable), and began a spreadsheet titled “Project Hippopotamus: Budget & Logistics.” The numbers were staggering, but not, he reasoned, impossible. What was a mortgage, after all, but a commitment to a large, unmovable object? This was just a more… animate version of the same principle.

His conviction was only strengthened by his daily drive to the public access studio. On Route 17, a new digital billboard had just been erected. On it, in a twenty-foot-tall loop, was the smirking face of Reginald Thorne. The text beneath the image read: “Thorne’s Take: Cutting Through the Noise. Weeknights at 6 on Channel 4.”

He had clawed his way from public access to a minor regional network, and now to a prime-time slot on a major tri-state news channel. His smile on that billboard, a smile that had been focus-grouped into a weapon, seemed to mock Arthur’s very existence. It was a smile that said, *I am light and airy, and I have won*.

Seeing that billboard every day solidified Arthur’s resolve. The world was drowning in Reginald Thornes, in empty calories and fleeting trends. The world needed a counterbalance. The world, or at least the small corner of it that was Paramus, New Jersey, needed a hippo.

Armed with a binder three inches thick, filled with research, diagrams, and his painstakingly crafted budget, Arthur made an appointment with the Paramus Borough Council.
