The rain fell on Wrexham with a quiet insistence, not the dramatic downpour of films but a steady, grey drizzle that soaked into the ancient brickwork and made the slate roofs gleam like polished obsidian. From the window of my second-floor flat above the old butcher’s shop on Henblas Street, the world looked watercolour-smudged and weary. A perfect night for staying in. A perfect night for Elara.

She was curled on the worn chesterfield, a sketchbook open on her lap, the pencil in her hand moving with a life of its own. Her hair, a chaotic spill of honey-blonde, was pulled into a messy knot that failed to contain the rebellious strands framing her face. The tip of her tongue was caught between her teeth in concentration, a habit she’d had since we were building Lego castles on her living room floor, aged six. I watched the delicate muscles in her hand, the way her knuckles indented, the focused line of her brow. It was a study in intensity, an artist lost in her own creation. My heart did a familiar, painful little lurch. It was an ache I had learned to live with, a phantom limb of feeling for a future I was too cowardly to reach for.

“You’re staring, Chief Inspector,” she murmured, not looking up. The pencil scratched against the thick paper, a sound as comforting as the rain.

“Observing, Elara. There’s a difference.” I turned from the window, the condensation I’d left on the pane already beginning to shrink. My own reflection had been a pale ghost against the darkening street, a brief impression of short, dark hair and the tired set of a mouth that hadn’t laughed properly in a while. At least, not until she was in the room.

“Observing what? The imminent collapse of this sofa? The structural integrity of my terrible top-knot?”

“The way you hold your pencil. It’s aggressive. Like you’re trying to stab the truth out of the paper.”

She finally looked up, her eyes the colour of a summer sky just before a storm. They held a galaxy of mischief. “Maybe I am. The truth is a slippery bastard. You of all people should know that.”

I moved to the small galley kitchen, the worn linoleum cool under my socks. “My truths are usually covered in someone else’s blood and have to be coaxed out with threats of lengthy paperwork. Yours are just charcoal smudges.” I filled the kettle, the click of the switch echoing in the quiet flat. The space was mine, but it only ever felt like a home when she was in it. Her chaotic energy filled the voids my own quiet presence couldn't. A half-finished painting was propped against one wall, a canvas of violent blues and searing yellows that was pure Elara. A stack of her art history books teetered precariously on my neat, alphabetised bookshelf. She was an infestation, a beautiful, welcome one.

“You make it sound so romantic.” She closed the sketchbook with a soft thud and stretched, her spine arching like a cat’s. The jumper she wore, one of mine, rode up to reveal a pale strip of her stomach. I turned away, busying myself with teabags in mugs. Earl Grey for me, a ritual. Peppermint for her, an affectation.

“Police work is the death of romance, El.”

“No,” she said, her voice closer now. She had padded silently into the kitchen behind me. I could feel the warmth radiating from her, could smell the faint, clean scent of her skin underneath the peppermint and charcoal. “A lack of imagination is the death of romance.” She leaned past me to grab the sugar, her arm brushing mine. A jolt, electric and sharp, shot through me. It was always like this. Ten years of being her best friend, five years of being in love with her, and I still hadn't built up an immunity. My body was a traitor to my tightly controlled mind.

The kettle shrieked, a welcome interruption. I poured the boiling water, my hands steady. They had to be. My hands had lifted evidence bags from murder scenes, had comforted sobbing witnesses, had signed off on reports that sealed people’s fates. They would not tremble because Elara Jones was standing too close in a tiny kitchen.

We took our mugs back to the living room. The rain had picked up, drumming a faster rhythm against the glass. Elara tucked her feet under her on the sofa, nursing the hot mug in both hands.

“Had a bad one today?” she asked. Her artist’s eyes saw too much. They saw the new lines around my own eyes, the tension in my shoulders that no amount of stretching could ease.

“Just a tedious one. Domestic dispute out near Acton Park. Ended in a broken patio gnome and a lot of shouting. The paperwork will take longer than the incident.” I gave her the sanitised version, of course. I didn't tell her about the look in the woman’s eyes – a hollowed-out resignation that spoke of years of smaller, un-reported incidents. I didn't tell her about the way the man’s apologies sounded more like threats. I didn't tell her how the whole miserable affair had made me want to drive to her studio, pull her away from her clay and her canvases, and just sit in silence with her, to remind myself that not everything beautiful was destined to be broken.

“No bodies in the library? No clandestine meetings with shadowy informants in the car park at Ty Pawb?”

“Not today. Wrexham’s criminal underworld is apparently taking the evening off.” I sipped my tea. It was scalding, but the burn was grounding. “How was your world? More exciting than mine, I hope.”

“Always.” A slow smile spread across her face, and it was like watching the sun burn through the ever-present Welsh cloud cover. “I have an idea, Alice. A big one.”

I knew that smile. That smile had preceded her deciding to paint a mural on the side of a derelict pub (which got her a caution and a feature in the local paper), her spending her entire student loan on a spontaneous trip to photograph Bolivian salt flats, and her adopting a one-eyed ginger cat she named ‘Sartre’. That smile was the beginning of chaos.

“How big are we talking? Bigger than the Bolivian salt flats?”

“Bigger. More permanent.” She set her mug down on a coaster – a concession to my obsessive tidiness she only sometimes remembered – and leaned forward, her expression earnest, alight with that fire that drew everyone to her. “I want to get a tattoo.”

I felt a ridiculous, disproportionate knot of dread tighten in my stomach. It was illogical. People got tattoos. It wasn't a crime. It wasn't a tragedy. But the thought of her perfect, unblemished skin being marked, changed permanently, felt like a violation.

“A tattoo,” I repeated, my voice flat. I was the Chief Inspector. I could face down a knife-wielding drunk, but I couldn't hide the disapproval from my voice at the thought of Elara getting a tattoo.

“Don’t use your ‘disappointed in the youth of today’ voice on me, Al. It’s not a passing whim. I’ve been thinking about it for months. I’ve been designing it.” She retrieved her sketchbook, flipping through pages of anatomical studies and abstract shapes until she found the one she was looking for. She turned it around and held it out to me.

My breath caught. It wasn't a small, discreet butterfly or a line of sentimental text. The drawing covered the entire page, a swirling, intricate maelstrom of lines. It was a spiral, but a broken, jagged one, coiling in on itself like a dying galaxy. Tendrils like black lightning snaked out from the core, interwoven with shapes that were almost, but not quite, recognisable – a hint of a feather here, a shard of bone there. It was complex and chaotic, and it made my head spin to look at it. It was technically brilliant, the linework was flawless, but it was also… unsettling. Deeply unsettling. It felt like looking at a diagram of a nervous breakdown.

“It’s… a lot, El,” I managed, handing the book back.

“It’s meant to be,” she said, her eyes shining. “It represents… everything. The chaos we come from. The energy. The way everything connects and breaks apart. I want it on my back. From my shoulder down to my hip.”

“Elara, that’s… that’s a massive piece. It will hurt. It will be there forever. What if you change your mind?” My objections sounded weak even to me, the practicalities of a pragmatist against the grand vision of an artist.

“I won’t change my mind.” She looked from the drawing to me, and her gaze was so intense it felt like a physical touch. “It feels necessary. Like something I have to do. To commemorate a turning point.”

“A turning point from what?”

She hesitated, her gaze dropping for a fraction of a second. “From before. To after.”

The words hung in the air, heavy with unspoken meaning. Before what? After what? My mind, my cop’s mind, started racing, searching for a hidden truth. Was she in some kind of trouble? Was this a symbol, a message? But her face was open, excited. I was being paranoid. I was letting my job bleed into my life, seeing conspiracies in charcoal drawings.

“It’s your body, Elara,” I said, finally, forcing a smile that felt like cracking plaster. “But if you get some back-alley scratcher to do it and you get an infection, don’t come crying to me.”

Her answering laugh was bright and loud, chasing the shadows from the corners of the room. “I’d always come crying to you, Alice. You know that.”

She was right. I did know that. I was the one she called when her car broke down, the one who talked her down from her artistic crises, the one who had held her while she sobbed after her father’s funeral. I was her constant, her rock. The thought of that tattoo, that permanent, chaotic sigil being etched into her skin, felt like a premonition. A warning that the ground beneath our feet was about to shift, and that my role as her anchor was about to be tested in a way I couldn't possibly imagine. Outside, the Wrexham rain fell harder, as if trying to wash away a future that was already stained with ink.“I know,” I said, and the words were quieter than I’d intended. The truth of her statement settled in my chest, heavy and warm as a blanket. She would always come to me. It was the foundation upon which our friendship was built, and the very thing that kept me from telling her the truth. I was her safe harbour, and you don’t get to be the harbour and the storm. You had to choose. I had made my choice a long time ago.

I picked up her sketchbook again, my thumb brushing the edge of the page. “Tell me more about the design. The tendrils, what are they?” I was on surer ground here, talking about form and function, analysing the evidence. “They look like lightning, but also like… roots.”

“Both,” she confirmed, pleased by the observation. She leaned her head on my shoulder, a casual gesture that made all the air leave my lungs. Her hair smelled of rain. “They’re conduits. Of energy, of chaos. The spiral is the self, the core. But it’s not contained. It’s constantly sending out feelers, and taking things in. It’s messy. It’s how we’re made.” Her finger traced the jagged lines in the air above the page. “See here? This bit that looks like a feather? And this, like a splinter of bone? They’re fragments. Little pieces of experience that get tangled up in us. It’s not a perfect, clean spiral. It’s a history.”

“A history of what?” I pushed, unable to help myself. The detective in me scented a trail. Her earlier comment echoed in my mind. “You said it was to mark a turning point. From before to after.”

She shifted, lifting her head from my shoulder. The warmth vanished. The small kitchen suddenly felt cavernous. “Just… life, Al. Everything. Before, I was just playing at being an artist. Now, I feel like I’m actually becoming one. It’s about commitment. To the work. To myself.” She wasn’t quite meeting my eyes, focusing instead on a loose thread on the arm of the sofa. It was a tell. A deflection. I saw it every day in interview rooms.

But this wasn't an interview room, and she wasn't a suspect. She was Elara. Forcing the issue would be like trying to nail water to a wall; she’d just slip through my fingers. I let it go, a small, strategic retreat. “It’s a powerful design, El. No one could accuse you of a lack of commitment with that thing etched on you.” I tried for a lighter tone. “Just promise me you’ll go to a reputable place. I know a guy who does health and safety inspections for parlours. I’ll get you a list.”

“Ever the Chief Inspector.” She smiled, but the full, sun-breaking-through-clouds brilliance of it was gone, replaced by a softer, more distant version. “I’ve already found someone. A woman who works out of a private studio in Chester. Her name is Anya. Her linework is incredible. She understood the design instantly.”

The knot of dread in my stomach tightened again. It had a name now. Anya. A stranger who was going to permanently mark Elara’s body. A stranger who apparently ‘understood’ the design in a way I, her best friend, clearly did not. It was a petty, jealous thought, and I was ashamed of it, but it was there all the same. I was the keeper of her stories, the archivist of her scraped knees and teenage heartbreaks. The idea that she would chronicle this mysterious ‘turning point’ with a stranger, in ink on her skin, felt like a betrayal.

“Right,” I said, my voice carefully neutral. I took a long swallow of my now lukewarm tea. “Chester. Well, at least it’s not a garden shed in Gwersyllt.”

She laughed, a real one this time, and the tension eased. “See? This is why I need you. To manage my worst impulses.” She stood up and stretched again, a languid, boneless movement. “Come on, let’s watch something. Something stupid. Something where the only mystery is whether the ridiculously attractive leads will finally kiss in the last five minutes.”

She moved to the television, flicking through the streaming services with the casual air of someone who knew where everything was. She was right, of course. A stupid film was exactly what we needed. An hour and a half of predictable plot points and telegraphed emotions to distract me from the chaotic, unsettling artwork sitting on my coffee table, and the growing fear that the ‘after’ Elara was so eager to step into was a place I wouldn't be able to follow. As the opening credits of some generic romantic comedy filled the room with jaunty music, Elara curled back up on her end of the sofa. I stayed on mine. For the first time in a long time, the distance between us felt less like a few feet of worn fabric and more like a chasm.The film was exactly as advertised: witless, charming, and utterly predictable. On screen, a handsome architect and a quirky bookshop owner navigated a series of contrived misunderstandings, their path to true love signposted with all the subtlety of a motorway pile-up. In any other situation, Elara would have been offering a running commentary, mocking the dialogue, critiquing the cinematography. Tonight, she was quiet, her profile lit by the flickering blue light of the television. She was watching, but she wasn’t *there*. Her focus was turned inward, and I felt like I was sitting beside a stranger. My own mind kept snagging on her words, "a turning point," and the unsettling image of the spiral, a chaotic map to a part of her I didn’t know.

Halfway through the film’s saccharine climax, a soft buzz vibrated from the arm of the sofa. Elara’s phone. She picked it up, her thumb swiping across the screen. A small smile, private and knowing, touched her lips as she typed a reply. It was a minuscule thing, a fleeting expression, but it landed in my gut like a stone. My mind, a traitorous and inventive beast, immediately supplied a name for the person on the other end of that message: Anya. I pictured this faceless artist in Chester, this stranger who ‘understood’, receiving Elara’s late-night thoughts. I imagined them discussing the ink, the needles, the meaning behind the broken galaxy she was so desperate to have scored into her skin. They were building a world together, a secret world of art and commitment, and I was on the outside, peering through a rainy window.

When the credits rolled over the ridiculously attractive leads kissing in a downpour that was far more cinematic than Wrexham’s miserable drizzle, Elara stretched and sighed. “Well, that was terrible. I loved it.” It was the right line, the sort of thing she always said, but it lacked its usual spark. The performance was there, but the feeling was absent. She stood, gathering her things – her phone, her keys, the sketchbook which she left lying on the coffee table. “Right, Chief Inspector. I should let you get your beauty sleep. Criminals to apprehend and all that.”

We stood at the door, enacting the final scene of our familiar play. “Drive safe,” I said. “The roads will be slick.” She pulled me into a hug, her arms wrapping around my neck. It was warm and real, the scent of charcoal and her skin a dizzying comfort. But it felt shorter than usual, a punctuation mark rather than a lingering sentence. “I’ll text you,” she said, pulling back. Her eyes, those summer-storm eyes, seemed to skim past me, already focused on the stairwell, on the street, on the drive to her own flat and the life that was waiting for her there. “Don’t worry so much, Al. It’s just a tattoo.” Then she was gone, her footsteps light on the wooden stairs, the sound swallowed by the drumming of the rain.

I closed the door and leaned my forehead against the cool wood. The silence that rushed in was absolute. My flat, which had felt vibrant and alive just an hour ago, was now a hollow, lonely space. It was just a room again. Her presence had been a tide, filling all the empty corners, and now it had receded, leaving behind the stark evidence of her visit: the lingering scent of peppermint, the indent on the chesterfield, the empty mug on the coaster. I walked back into the living room and my eyes landed on the sketchbook. It was an anchor in the quiet, a tangible piece of the puzzle I couldn't seem to leave alone.

My hands felt steady as I picked it up, a stark contrast to the tremor in my chest. I flipped it open to the drawing. The jagged spiral stared back at me, a vortex of black ink on the page. It wasn’t just a drawing; it was a declaration. A statement of intent. *From before. To after.* What had happened to her? What was this ‘before’ she was so determined to leave behind? My job was to find the hidden narrative, to see the pattern in the chaos. I looked at the drawing, at the splinter of bone and the ragged feather tangled in the chaotic lines, and I knew, with a certainty that chilled me to my core, that this was more than just an artistic whim. This was evidence.

My own phone felt heavy in my hand. What I was about to do was a breach. It was a violation of the trust that was the bedrock of our entire friendship. It was using the tools of my trade, the instincts of a cop, on the one person in my life who was supposed to be exempt from all that. But the fear of the unknown was a claw in my throat, sharper and more insistent than my own conscience. The need to know, to understand, to protect her, overrode everything else. My thumb hovered, then moved with grim purpose. I opened the search browser, the white light of the screen stark in the dim room. My fingers typed the words I already despised. *Anya. Tattoo. Chester.*The search results loaded with an indifferent speed, offering up a digital ghost. There was no casual Facebook profile, no rambling Twitter feed. There was only one hit of substance: an Instagram account. The handle was simple, stark: @Anya.Ink. The profile picture was a close-up of a hand, fingers stained with black ink, holding a single, sharp-beaked raven’s skull. The gallery itself was a monochromatic sea of intricate, unsettling beauty. There were no smiling clients, no shots of the studio. There was only the art. Skin as canvas. Anya’s work was exclusively blackwork, dense and complex. She specialised in anatomical flora, where veins branched into root systems and ribs unfurled into fern fronds. There were geometric patterns that seemed to shift and writhe, and portraits of animals whose eyes held an unnerving, ancient intelligence. And there, three posts back, was a design so familiar it made my stomach clench: a jagged, broken spiral, almost identical to the one in Elara's sketchbook. The caption beneath it read, *Chaos is the form from which order is born. Work in progress.*

A cold tide of confirmation washed over me, carrying with it the bitter sediment of my own trespass. I was no longer just a worried friend; I was an investigator, and Elara was, in some strange way, my case. I scrolled through the comments on the post. They were a litany of awe-struck praise. ‘A masterpiece, Anya.’ ‘Your vision is a gift.’ ‘I need this energy in my life.’ They spoke of her not as a tattooist, but as a shaman, a high priestess of ink and skin. The jealousy I’d felt earlier curdled into something colder and sharper: fear. This Anya wasn’t just an artist Elara admired. This was a figurehead, the leader of a small, devout congregation, and Elara wanted to join. She wanted to be marked as a follower.

The next morning, the grey drizzle had given way to a persistent, heavy rain that drummed against the roof of my Vauxhall and turned the familiar streets of Wrexham into a slick, monochrome reflection of the sky. The police station was an assault of jarring normalcy: the scent of burnt coffee and wet wool coats, the hum of fluorescent lights, the cheerful inanity of DC Davies recounting his weekend’s misadventures in a flat-pack furniture store. I sat through the morning briefing, nodding at the right moments, my gaze fixed on the crime statistics projected on the wall. A spike in commercial burglaries on the industrial estate. A rise in anti-social behaviour near the Racecourse Ground. It was the rhythm of my life, the quantifiable problems of my town, but my mind kept superimposing Anya’s twisting, organic lines over the neat grids of the city map, seeing tendrils of black ink snaking out from Chester, creeping across the border.

Back in the sanctuary of my office, the rain-streaked window behind me, I stared at my computer terminal. The temptation was a physical thing, a pressure building behind my ribs. My training, my ethics, my entire professional code screamed against it. But the image of Elara’s bright, earnest face, so full of a fire I was beginning to suspect she hadn’t lit herself, was a louder siren. *It’s a welfare check,* I told myself, the lie tasting thin and metallic. *I have a legitimate concern for a vulnerable person who may be under the influence of a charismatic unknown.* My fingers, betrayers to the last, moved over the keyboard. I logged into the Police National Computer, the system I used to pry into the lives of criminals and victims, and I typed in the only name I had. It felt like a desecration. *Anya. Chester. Tattoo.* I added what I could glean from the business registration of her studio. *Anya Volkov.*

The system whirred for a moment, searching through the vast, interconnected databases that map the lives of every citizen from birth to death. The result that came back was more alarming than any criminal record. It was a void. According to the PNC, Anya Volkov did not exist. There was no driving license, no tax records beyond the studio's business registration from eighteen months prior, no credit history, no trail of addresses, no national insurance number matching a person of that name and approximate age. It was as if she had been conjured from thin air a year and a half ago, arriving in Chester fully formed with a business licence and an arsenal of tattoo needles. A person couldn't live in the UK, couldn't function, without leaving a trace. Unless they were deliberately and expertly hiding.

I leaned back in my chair, the worn leather creaking in the quiet office. The pieces began to click into place, forming a picture I didn’t want to see. Elara’s sudden secrecy. The talk of a ‘turning point’. Her dismissiveness, her deflection when I pushed. This wasn’t just an artist finding a mentor. This was something else. I thought of the way Elara had described Anya, how she ‘understood the design instantly’. Of course she did. It was her design. It was her philosophy, her brand. Elara wasn't commissioning a piece of art; she was being initiated. The spiral wasn’t a history; it was a pledge of allegiance. The dread was no longer a knot in my stomach; it was a cold, heavy sheet of ice encasing my entire body.

I spent the rest of the day in a fugue state, signing off on reports and authorising patrols with a mechanical efficiency that belied the frantic chaos in my head. The domestic dispute from the day before, the one with the broken gnome, seemed like a relic from a simpler time. At five o’clock, I fled the station, not towards my flat, but out onto the bypass, driving with no destination in mind. The windscreen wipers beat a frantic, hypnotic rhythm, struggling against the deluge. I couldn't call Elara. I couldn’t sit her down in my living room and ask, ‘Who is this woman? Why is she a ghost? What are you getting involved in, El?’ My evidence was illegally obtained. My suspicion was born of jealousy and fear. I would be the paranoid, controlling friend, the Chief Inspector who saw conspiracies everywhere. I would drive her further away, straight into Anya’s waiting, ink-stained hands. There was only one other path. I pulled the car over onto the gravel shoulder of a lay-by, the lights of a passing lorry illuminating the cabin in a brief, stark flash. I took out my phone, my fingers cold and clumsy, and I looked up the address for the studio in Chester. I had to see it. I had to know what kind of place held this much power over the person I loved. I was a detective, after all. And this was a crime scene waiting to happen.The decision, once made, settled in my gut like a cold stone. I pulled back onto the A483, the car cutting through the rain-slicked darkness towards the faint glow of Chester. The journey was short, barely twenty minutes, but each mile felt like a deliberate step away from the person I was supposed to be. Alice the friend would have stayed home, trusted Elara, and worried in silence. Chief Inspector Thorne, however, was now conducting an unauthorised, off-the-books surveillance operation, driven by a gut feeling and a shred of illegal data. The two identities were at war within me, and the cop was winning.

Chester, with its timber-framed Tudor facades and Roman walls, felt like a city from a different country, older and more self-assured than Wrexham. The address I had was for a street tucked behind the main shopping thoroughfare, a narrow cobbled lane where high-end boutiques nestled against ancient stonework. I found a parking space a street over and walked, the collar of my jacket turned up against the persistent rain. The air here smelled different—wetter, cleaner, tinged with the scent of old money and river water.

The studio was not what I expected. There was no garish neon sign, no window filled with flash sheets of skulls and roses. The storefront was a study in severe minimalism. A single sheet of black-painted wood covered the frontage, with a door of the same matte black set into it. The only marking was a small, sandblasted symbol etched into the glass of the door’s narrow window: a raven’s skull, identical to the one on Anya’s Instagram. It was less a place of business and more a declaration of privacy, a fortress disguised as a shop. It was designed to intimidate, to ensure that only those who were meant to be there would dare to knock.

I found a recessed doorway across the lane, a shallow alcove that offered some protection from the rain and a perfect vantage point. From here, I watched. The lane was quiet, deserted in the miserable weather. A single, cold light burned from behind the door's window, just enough to tell me the place wasn’t empty. For a long time, nothing happened. The rain fell. A delivery van rumbled past the end of the alley. I felt the damp seeping into my jeans, a slow, chilling progress up my legs. I was a voyeur, a stalker, and the shame of it was a sour taste in my mouth. But the image of Elara’s face, alight with that dangerous, mesmerised fire, kept me rooted to the spot.

Just as I was about to give up, to dismiss the entire trip as a paranoid folly, the black door opened. My breath hitched. A woman stepped out, pulling the door closed behind her with a solid, definitive click. She was tall, impossibly so, with a severe posture that spoke of discipline. Her hair was a sheet of glossy black, cut into a sharp, angular bob that framed a face of pale skin and high cheekbones. She wore a long, dark coat, but it was her presence that held all the power. It was an aura of absolute, unshakable control. This had to be her. Anya Volkov. She didn’t look like an artist, not the chaotic, paint-splattered kind like Elara. She looked like a surgeon, or a general. She paused on the threshold, turning her head slightly, her gaze sweeping the empty lane. For a terrifying second, I thought she’d seen me, that her dark eyes had pierced my shadowy hiding place. I shrank back into the alcove, my heart hammering against my ribs.

She didn't move towards me. Instead, she looked up into the rain, a flicker of something unreadable on her face, before turning and walking away in the opposite direction, her footsteps sharp and even on the wet cobblestones until she disappeared around the corner. I stayed frozen for a full minute after she was gone, the adrenaline slowly ebbing away, leaving a residue of cold, hard certainty. My gut feeling hadn't been wrong. This woman was formidable. She radiated an intensity that could easily be mistaken for wisdom, a charisma that could pull a searching soul like Elara into its orbit and never let go.

The drive back to Wrexham was a blur of automatic actions. My mind was no longer at war with itself. The line between Alice the friend and Thorne the Chief Inspector had dissolved. They were one and the same, with a single, unified purpose: to find out who Anya Volkov was and what she wanted with Elara. Protecting her was no longer a passive act of providing comfort and tea. It was now an active investigation. The rules of friendship no longer felt sufficient for the threat I was facing.

I let myself into my silent flat, the chill of the evening having settled deep into my bones. The first thing I saw was the sketchbook, still lying on the coffee table where Elara had left it. I picked it up, my fingers tracing the cover. I flipped it open to the jagged spiral. It was no longer just a troubling piece of art. It was a map. It was a symbol of allegiance to a woman who didn't exist, a ghost with a fortress in Chester and an unnerving amount of influence. The drawing was a premonition, Elara's and my own. Before, I was her best friend, her rock, her safe harbour. But now, looking at the chaotic tendrils snaking across the page, I understood. You can’t protect a ship from a storm by staying in the harbour. Sometimes, you have to sail out to meet it.The following morning, the rain had finally tired itself out, leaving behind a sky the colour of a wet slate and a city that glistened under a weak, watery sun. The resolve forged in the darkness of the previous night had not dissolved with the dawn. It had solidified, becoming a cold, hard kernel of purpose in my chest. I made coffee, the familiar ritual a flimsy anchor in a world that had tilted on its axis. My reflection in the dark screen of the television was a stranger’s – a woman with my face, but with eyes that were older, harder. The Chief Inspector had come home, and she was not leaving. My flat was no longer a refuge; it was a forward operating base. And the first order of business was to call in a specialist.

There are rules, and then there is the reality of policing. No one understood that better than Finn. He had been a brilliant detective, a prodigy in the burgeoning field of digital forensics, until his methods became too brilliant, too effective, and too far outside the lines of acceptable procedure. He could find anyone, unearth any secret buried in the digital bedrock of a life. His career had ended not with a bang, but with a quiet, mandatory resignation after he’d legally, but unethically, annihilated a defence lawyer’s case by exposing his entire private life. Now, he ran a small, cluttered electronics repair shop on the edge of the Wrexham industrial estate, a purgatory of broken screens and fried motherboards, and occasionally, for the right price or the right reason, he would dig. The air in the room was not thick with grief, but with the clean, sharp scent of conspiracy, a shared and righteous anger that was more potent than any amount of caffeine.

Our mission began with a digital excavation. Elara was the lead archaeologist, her artist’s eye uniquely equipped to sift through the curated sands of social media for the tell-tale artifacts of a buried life. She hunted for patterns not of criminal behaviour, but of suppressed joy. I became her archivist, my methodical cop’s brain finding a grim satisfaction in creating structured, evidence-based files on each of Anya’s potential victims. We built a gallery of ghosts. There was the musician from Manchester whose social media feed transitioned from raucous live gig photos to silent, sparse videos of a single synth generating a monotonous drone. There was the poet from Bangor whose passionate, chaotic verses about love and landscape had been replaced by short, sterile couplets about form and absence. For each one, Elara found the ‘tell’: a worn guitar pick resting on an empty music stand, a single, faded mountaineering guide on a shelf of otherwise identical white-spined books. They were the footprints of the people who used to live inside these hollowed-out new identities.

Chloe was our Rosetta Stone. She moved through the private forums of the Ordo Corvi, her old login still active, and translated the coded language for us. She explained the subtle hierarchies, the members who were seen as ‘Exemplars’ of the doctrine, and the quiet, almost invisible pressure to conform. When we found the profile of the poet from Bangor, Chloe’s face tightened. “I know him. Rowan,” she whispered. “He wrote a poem for his sister’s wedding last year. It was about the mountains of Snowdonia, how they held the memory of every season. It was magnificent. Anya told him that sentimentality was the enemy of truth, and that his connection to landscape was a form of pathetic fallacy. He took the poem down a week later.” With Chloe’s context, the abstract data points I was collecting became devastating human stories. Each ‘edited’ profile was a fresh crime scene.

It was Elara who crystallised the form of our weapon. Late in the afternoon, after hours of relentless digging, she pushed her chair back from the table, her eyes alight with the fire of creative inspiration. “We can’t just tell their stories,” she declared, pacing the length of my Persian rug. “We have to show them. Side by side. We create our own archive. An anti-Anya. We’ll call it ‘The Unedited Archive.’ An Instagram account. Each post will be a diptych. On the left, the ‘before’ – vibrant, messy, full of life. On the right, the ‘after’ – the sterile, grey void they’ve been sold as peace. No angry captions. No accusations. Just the names, the dates, and the images. We let the pictures do the talking. We let the loss speak for itself.” Her vision was brilliant in its simplicity. It was an attack that mimicked the form of its target, using the cool, detached language of the archive to deliver a devastatingly emotional payload. We would hold up a mirror to the Order of the Raven, but our mirror would show them not their enlightened selves, but everything beautiful they had thrown away.

The first exhibit in our museum of memory had to be Chloe. It was her testimony, her pain, that had galvanized us. But to build it, we needed the raw materials of her past. "My old cloud drive," she said, her voice barely a whisper. "I never deleted it. I just… stopped looking." With Elara sitting beside her, a steadying presence, Chloe typed in a password she hadn’t used in over a year. A moment later, her screen flooded with colour. There they were: hundreds of photographs of her textile work. Intricate weaves of impossible blues and fiery oranges; bold, joyful patterns inspired by wildflowers and bird wings; close-ups of rich textures that were so detailed you could almost feel them through the screen. Chloe stared at the images of her own lost brilliance, and for a moment, I feared she would break again. But instead of sorrow, a slow, burning anger began to smoulder in her eyes. This was what Anya had called ‘visual noise’. This was the creative life she had been convinced to mourn and discard. She began to download the files, each click an act of reclamation, a quiet declaration of war against the woman who had tried to bleach the colour from her soul.

As Elara and Chloe curated the images for the first post, I watched them, a strange mix of awe and a cop’s ingrained unease churning in my gut. This was digital vigilantism. We were about to publicly expose and shame people, using their own lives as our ammunition. It skirted the edges of harassment, of invasion of privacy. But every time the doubt crept in, I thought of Daniel Albright’s empty eyes, of Chloe’s ragged sobs, of Elara’s face as she told me Anya was going to systematically cut me out of her mind. The law had no tools for this kind of psychic warfare. The law could not prosecute a philosophy. Anya Volkov operated in the grey spaces between legality and morality, and to fight her, we had to be willing to step into that same shadowland. This was the only path. My role was no longer to enforce the letter of the law, but to protect my people. This was a new, dangerous, and righteous jurisdiction.

By evening, everything was ready. Elara had designed a simple, elegant template for the post. On the left, a breathtaking photograph of one of Chloe’s early works, a wall hanging that exploded with the colours of a sunset. On the right, a recent photo from Chloe’s own feed: the empty white wall where that hanging had once been. The caption was stark, clinical, and devastating. `Chloe Sterling, Textile Artist. Exhibit A: ‘Midsummer Weave,’ 2022. Exhibit B: ‘The North Wall,’ 2024.` No judgment, no emotion. Just the cold, undeniable evidence of the erasure. The three of us stood clustered around Elara’s laptop, the screen glowing in the dimming light of the flat. "Ready?" Elara asked, her voice quiet. Chloe gave a single, sharp nod. I placed a hand on Elara’s shoulder. With a final, decisive click, she hit ‘Post’. The image uploaded. It was live. The first shot had been fired into the pristine, silent world of the Ordo Corvi. We had no idea what would happen next. We only knew that the war for memory had begun.The quiet that followed the click of the mouse was the loudest sound I had heard in days. It was a vacuum, a digital void where a thousand possible futures were being born and dying every second. The three of us remained clustered around the laptop, staring at the screen as if we could will a response into existence through sheer force of collective will. The post sat there, stark and beautiful and terrible, a diptych of a life bisected. For a full ten minutes, nothing happened. No likes. No comments. No angry messages. The silence was a potent argument against what we had done, suggesting that our grand gesture, our first shot in this righteous war, had been nothing more than a whisper in a hurricane, unheard and unnoticed. The doubt began to creep in, a cold fog at the edges of my resolve. Had we overestimated our impact? Had we simply thrown Chloe’s pain into an indifferent void for nothing?

I watched Elara, who was refreshing the page with a steady, rhythmic tap of her finger, her face a mask of intense concentration. She was the general waiting for the first battlefield report, her faith in her strategy absolute. Chloe was the opposite. She had retreated into the depths of my armchair, clutching a cushion to her chest like a shield, her eyes wide and fixed on her own phone, where she was logged into her personal account. She was braced for the impact, for the wave of condemnation or pity from the community she had just betrayed. I found myself caught between them, my cop’s mind craving a tangible result, an immediate reaction, while my friend’s heart ached with the fear that we had only succeeded in further isolating a woman who had already lost so much. The ticking of the clock on the wall seemed to grow louder, each second a small mockery of our inaction.

Then, a tiny red heart appeared at the bottom of the image. A single like. It was followed a moment later by another. They were insignificant, anonymous, but they were proof that we were not screaming into a void. Then came the first comment. It was from a user whose profile identified her as a curator at a Bristol textile museum. "I remember this piece from your graduate show, Chloe," she wrote. "It was breathtaking. The most vibrant work there. This new direction is… a choice." The deliberate, loaded neutrality of the comment was more damning than any insult. It was the art world equivalent of a raised eyebrow, a quiet, professional judgment that validated our entire premise. Elara let out a breath she seemed to have been holding since we hit ‘post’. “It’s working,” she whispered, her voice filled with a fierce, triumphant awe. “They’re not seeing an attack. They’re seeing the loss.”

Over the next hour, the ripples began to spread. The post was shared by the curator, and then by other artists and designers who had known Chloe’s old work. The comments were a chorus of confusion and remembered beauty. “What happened to your colour, Chloe? It was your superpower.” “This is heartbreaking. Your old work was so full of life.” There was no aggression, only a collective mourning for a talent that seemed to have been extinguished. At the same time, Chloe, monitoring the Ordo Corvi’s private forums, noticed a different kind of reaction. A chilling, coordinated silence. The Unedited Archive account was being blocked, en masse. A directive had clearly gone out from on high: do not engage, do not acknowledge. Starve the fire of oxygen. Anya was not going to be drawn into a public brawl. She was closing ranks, pulling her flock into a tighter circle, reinforcing the walls of their echo chamber against the intrusion of memory.

The coordinated silence was more terrifying, and more confirming, than an open attack. It proved that we had hit a nerve, that Anya considered our archive a genuine threat to her control. The knowledge spurred us on, transforming our fear into fuel. “We can’t let the momentum die,” Elara said, her focus already shifting to the next target. “We need to show this isn’t just about Chloe. We need to show the pattern.” There was no question about who was next. Daniel Albright. The smiling, hollow man who had given away his dog. Chloe, her own confidence growing with every supportive comment on her post, logged back into her cloud drive and found a different kind of archive. She had been part of a shared folder for a project she had pitched to Daniel’s firm years ago. The project had gone nowhere, but the folder remained, a digital ghost ship. In it were not just architectural plans, but informal office photos, pictures from a Christmas party, a team-building day out. And there he was. Daniel Albright, the ‘before’ version. Laughing, a pint in his hand. Arguing passionately over a blueprint, his face alight with creative fervour. And in a dozen shots, there was Pevsner, the scruffy terrier, either asleep under Daniel’s desk or climbing into his lap, his master’s arm wrapped around him in a casual, loving embrace.

We had our images. Elara quickly assembled the second diptych. On the left, a candid shot of Daniel, grinning from ear to ear, with Pevsner’s furry head resting on his shoulder. On the right, a screenshot from Daniel's current, pristine website: a photo of the empty, minimalist kennel he had designed, a beautiful, sterile monument to a love he had discarded. The caption was just as stark as the first. `Daniel Albright, Architect. Exhibit A: With Pevsner, 2021. Exhibit B: ‘Canine Enclosure No. 1,’ 2024.` As Elara’s finger hovered over the post button, a notification pinged on the laptop. It was a direct message to The Unedited Archive account. We had aimed our weapon at the heart of the Ordo Corvi, expecting a cry of pain or a roar of defiance from the command structure. Instead, the first scream had come from one of our own potential allies, and it was a scream of fear directed at us. Rowan’s message was a perfect, chilling reflection of Anya’s insidious power. He did not see an act of liberation in Chloe’s sudden public reappearance; he saw an abduction. To him, we were not a rescue party. We were the shadowy, external threat that Anya had always warned about, the chaotic forces of the ‘old world’ that preyed on the enlightened. His panic was a testament to the strength of the cage we were trying to break.

“He thinks we took you,” I said, the grim reality of our new position settling in my stomach like a block of ice. I looked at Chloe, whose face had gone pale again, the brief, righteous flush of their victory draining away to reveal the raw vulnerability beneath. This was her first test as a defector, and it was a cruel one: to be seen as a victim not of the cult, but of her saviours. She knew exactly the paranoia he was feeling, the carefully instilled terror of the world outside the safety of the doctrine. That last word was the key, the perfect counter-spell to Anya’s mantra of erasure. Remembering was not a weakness or a regression; it was an act of rebellion. I’m remembering.` She hit send, and once again, the three of us were suspended in a void of silent, hopeful tension.

While we waited, staring at the screen for the three small dots that would signal a reply, Elara turned her attention back to the waiting post about Daniel Albright. “We can’t stop,” she said, her voice a low, urgent command. “If we pause now, it looks like we’ve been scared off. The second diptych went live: the laughing man with his beloved dog on the left, the cold, empty kennel on the right. Our war was not just about Chloe; it was about everyone Anya had touched.

Rowan’s reply came minutes later. Elara’s eyes lit up, the artist seeing the composition instantly. As the digital battle for Rowan’s soul played out on the laptop screen, a different kind of anxiety began to gnaw at me. I was a cop, and I was watching my flat—my home—be transformed into the nerve centre of a vigilante justice campaign. I was proud of Elara, of Chloe, of the righteous, brilliant weapon they were building. But Anya Volkov was not a digital phantom. She was a real, dangerous person who had, according to Finn, already walked away from one fatal fire. She had been confronted, her power had been challenged, and her followers were being systematically targeted. She would not remain silent forever. It was a reflex, a habit ingrained by years of thinking about risk and threat, but it felt more urgent now than ever before. This war had an online front, but I knew, with a chilling certainty, that it would eventually have a physical one, too.

I returned to the living room just as a new notification pinged on the laptop. A message from Rowan. Elara opened it. There was no text. There was only an attachment. A single, simple document file. Elara’s breath hitched as she clicked to open it. The document that filled the screen was not a message of surrender or a plea for help. It was a poem.
