The ceiling had a map of a country that didn't exist, a sprawling, sepia-toned continent of water damage. Kira traced its borders with her eyes from the lumpy mattress, naming the mountain ranges of peeling paint and the great deltas of mould. This was her world now: a single room in a Byker terrace that smelled of damp and other people’s fried breakfasts. The net curtains were a nicotine-yellow, filtering the already grey Newcastle light into something even more defeated. She had four hundred and twelve pounds left, tucked into a sock under the bed. Four hundred and twelve pounds and a name that wasn’t hers on a fake ID that wouldn't fool a tired bouncer.

A siren wailed down by the river, its sad, two-tone cry swallowed by the city's hum. It made the muscles in her neck tighten. Everything did, these days. A car door slamming in the street, the heavy tread of the man in the room upstairs, the sudden caw of a gull on the roof. She was a bundle of frayed nerves, a creature of flight living in a shoebox. It had been three months since she’d walked out of her old life, leaving behind a flat in Jesmond, a decent job in software development, and a digital ghost that had apparently siphoned a quarter of a million pounds from her employer into an untraceable account. A ghost in the machine had danced with her login credentials, and the music had stopped with her holding the blame.

They’d called it a sophisticated breach. Kira called it a stitch-up. But proving it meant facing them, and facing them meant a courtroom, and a courtroom meant prison. The evidence was a neat, damning digital chain. Her IP address. Her access codes. Her digital signature. All pointing to her, the quiet coder in the corner. So she ran. She’d packed a bag, drained her savings account, and vanished into the city she was born in, becoming a tourist in her own hometown.

She missed the ridiculous things. The specific heft of her own front door key. The way the light hit her old kitchen in the late afternoon. The taste of coffee from her own mug. Here, everything was transient, cheap, disposable. The mug she drank her instant coffee from had a faded picture of a spaniel on it and a chip on the rim that snagged her lip.

Her phone, a cheap pay-as-you-go burner, vibrated on the rickety bedside table. She flinched. Only one person had this number. Her stomach twisted into a familiar knot of hope and dread. She let it vibrate twice more before answering, clearing her throat.

“Hello?”

“Kira? Is that you, pet?”

Her mother’s voice, thin and stretched with a tension that travelled down the line and coiled in Kira’s gut. “Aye, Mam. It’s me.”

“You shouldn’t be calling me.” The words were a reprimand, not a greeting. “What if they’re listening?”

“They’re not listening to your phone, Mam. I’m not a terrorist, I’m accused of fraud.” She tried to keep her own voice even, but it came out sharp, brittle. “I’m careful.”

“Careful? You’re living… God knows where! Like a criminal. Your father, he can’t even look his mates in the eye down the club.” Her father. Her step-father, Alan. A man whose opinion was measured in pints and pub loyalty.

“I didn’t do it, Mam.” The words tasted like ash. She’d said them so many times they’d lost all meaning, a hollow mantra against a wall of disbelief.

“It doesn’t matter what you did or didn’t do, Kira. It’s the shame of it all. The police coming to the house. The neighbours twitching their curtains. We raised you better than this.”

Kira squeezed her eyes shut, pressing the phone hard against her ear. The map on the ceiling swam. This was the real wound. Not the frame-up, not the running, but this. The casual, unthinking withdrawal of belief. Of love. “I needed to hear your voice,” she whispered, hating the weakness in her own.

There was a sigh on the other end of the line. A put-upon, weary sound. “Well, you’ve heard it. Are you alreet? Do you need anything?” The offer was reluctant, dragged out of duty.

“I’m managing.” A lie. “Just… wanted to check in.”

“Well, there’s some good news in the family, at least,” her mother said, her tone shifting, brightening with a speed that gave Kira vertigo. “Have you seen? Your sister, Eleanor. She’s on the news. On the posters and everything. Standing for the council in the next elections.”

Kira’s blood went cold. Eleanor. Her step-sister. The polished, perfect one. Alan’s eldest daughter. “Right,” Kira said, her voice flat.

“She’s a proper politician now. ‘A New Voice for the City,’ her posters say. She looks so smart. We’re all so proud, Kira. She’s going to go far, that one. A real credit to the family.”

A credit to the family. Unlike you. The words hung in the air, unsaid but deafening. Kira felt a familiar, bitter envy curdle in her stomach. Eleanor Vance. Even her name sounded like a promise of success. While Kira was hiding in a filthy room under a fake name, Eleanor was smiling from lampposts, her professionally-shot photo exuding confidence and integrity. The irony was a physical pain.

“That’s canny,” Kira managed to say, the Geordie term feeling alien in her mouth.

“It is. It really is.” Her mam was oblivious. “She’s the one holding this family together, you know. After everything. After what happened with Chloe… Eleanor’s been a rock.”

Chloe. The name landed like a stone in the pit of Kira’s stomach. The other sister. Eleanor’s sister. Her other step-sister. The one who wasn’t a credit to anyone anymore. The one who had, according to the official report three years ago, jumped from the High Level Bridge on a wet Tuesday in November. A tragedy. A bout of depression nobody saw coming. The family rarely spoke her name now, and when they did, it was in hushed, uncomfortable tones.

But Kira remembered Chloe differently. She hadn’t been polished and perfect like Eleanor. She’d been chaotic and funny, with a laugh that was too loud and a habit of painting her nails clashing, garish colours. She’d been the one who’d snuck Kira her first can of cider, the one who’d listened to her wild, teenage dreams without judgement. And she’d been terrified of heights. Absolutely, profoundly terrified. She wouldn’t even stand near the edge of the viewing platform at the Baltic Centre. The idea of her climbing onto the railing of a bridge… it had never, ever sat right with Kira. It was a discordant note in a song of grief the family had long since stopped singing.

“Kira? Are you still there?”

“Aye. I’m here.” Her voice was a ghost. “Listen, Mam, I have to go.”

“Alright, pet. Just… be sensible. Think about turning yourself in. It’d be for the best.”

For the best. For whom? For them, so the shame could be neatly packed away into a prison sentence. So they could tell the neighbours it was all being ‘handled’. Kira didn’t reply. She just pressed the button, cutting the connection, and tossed the cheap plastic phone onto the bed. The silence it left behind was louder than the conversation.

She stood up and walked to the grimy window, pushing aside the net curtain. Across the street, a bus shuddered past, its side plastered with a political ad. And there was Eleanor’s face, ten feet tall. Smiling. Promising. ‘A New Voice for the City.’ Kira stared at the image, at the perfectly-coiffed hair, the earnest, trustworthy eyes, the string of tasteful pearls. The woman who was a credit to the family. The rock. The one who was succeeding while Kira was failing, who was praised while Kira was shamed. And for the first time in three months, the fear that had been Kira’s constant companion was joined by something else. Something hot and sharp and dangerous. A sliver of pure, undiluted rage. Her fortunes, her mam had said. Her fortunes were improving. And as Kira looked at the smiling face of the politician, a terrible, formless thought began to coalesce in the dark corners of her mind. Perhaps they were.The thought was an ugly, invasive weed, pushing up through the cracked pavement of her despair. It wasn’t just that Eleanor was succeeding while she was failing. It was the neat, almost symmetrical opposition of their fates. Eleanor, rising. Kira, falling. One sister’s name plastered on posters promising a brighter future, the other’s name dragged through the mud of a criminal investigation. Eleanor, the family’s ‘rock’ after a series of tragedies. But what if she wasn’t the rock? What if she was the earthquake? Kira thought back to their childhood, to the board games Alan had insisted they all play on wet Sunday afternoons. Eleanor never just played to win; she played to annihilate. She’d trade properties in Monopoly with a shark’s smile, bankrupting Chloe for Park Lane then offering a paltry, patronising loan. She’d block Kira’s routes in Ticket to Ride with a casual, devastating flick of her wrist. It had been called competitiveness then. Looking at the smiling poster now, Kira wondered if it had been practice.

The rage did not cool. It condensed. It hardened from a diffuse, burning gas into a single, sharp point of purpose. She had been running for three months, shrinking her world down to the size of a water-stained ceiling and the chipped rim of a mug. She had been waiting for the money to run out, for the police to knock, for the end. But the conversation with her mother, the mention of Chloe, the sight of Eleanor's triumphant face—it had cauterised the wound of her fear. A slow death in a bedsit was no longer an option. If she was going to be caught, it would not be here, cowering. It would be out there, hunting for the truth. Her mind, so long mired in anxiety, began to click back into its old, familiar patterns. Problem, analysis, solution. The frame-up was digital. The answer, if it existed, was digital. She needed a weapon. She needed a laptop.

Her hand went to the sock under the mattress, her fingers closing around the thin wad of notes. Four hundred and twelve pounds. It would have to be enough. Not for a new machine, not for anything powerful, but for something. A second-hand, cash-bought ghost of a laptop from a shop that didn’t ask for names or addresses. She pulled on a faded black hoodie she’d bought from a charity shop and a baseball cap that covered half her face. Peering at her reflection in the dark windowpane, she saw a stranger. Thinner, her face sharpened by stress, her eyes wide and haunted. Good. The less she looked like Kira Liddle, the coder from Jesmond, the better. The plan was simple, and its simplicity terrified her. Go into the city. Find a pawn shop or a second-hand electronics store. Pay cash, no questions. Get out. Then, find a place with free, anonymous Wi-Fi where she could sit for hours without drawing attention. The thought of the open streets, of the crowds, of the thousand-eyed city, made her heart hammer against her ribs, but the image of Eleanor’s smile was a spur, driving her forward.

Before she left, she sat on the edge of the lumpy mattress, the room’s stale air thick around her. Chloe. The memory of her step-sister wasn’t just a vague, sad ache anymore; it was a clue, a loose thread she needed to pull. She remembered a conversation, maybe a year before Chloe died. They’d been in a pub in the Ouseburn, the air thick with the smell of craft beer and sawdust. Chloe, three pints in, had been complaining about a row she’d had with Eleanor. “She keeps a ledger, you know,” Chloe had slurred, her brightly painted nails tapping a frantic rhythm on her glass. “Not a real one. Sometimes I think she’d do anything to make sure the books are balanced in her favour.” At the time, Kira had dismissed it as drunken paranoia. Now, the words echoed with a chilling prescience. The books are balanced in her favour. A quarter of a million pounds siphoned away. A rival sister disgraced and removed. A political path cleared of all obstacles. The pieces didn't just fit; they locked together with a sickening, metallic click. Chloe hadn’t seen a bout of depression coming. She had seen Eleanor.With the wad of cash now secured in the front pocket of her jeans, a hard, uncomfortable lump against her thigh, Kira pulled the door of her room shut. The smell of the house, a cloying mix of stale cigarette smoke, damp, and the lingering ghost of someone else’s dinner, seemed to cling to her, a shroud of poverty she feared she’d never wash off. She slipped out the front door into the grey Byker afternoon, pulling her hood further over her face and jamming her hands deep into her pockets, one clutching the precious cash, the other balled into a tight, white-knuckled fist.

A few doors down, a group of men were drinking cans of lager on a garden wall, their laughter harsh and loud. As the train rattled over the QEII bridge, she caught a glimpse of the Tyne, a slash of muddy brown water far below, and the iconic arch of the Millennium Bridge. The noise was a physical assault: the roar of buses, the chime of the street crossing, the cacophony of a thousand conversations. And the faces. So many faces. Kira moved through them like a phantom, her shoulders hunched, her cap pulled low. Eleanor’s face was there too, smiling down from a digital billboard high above, her image flickering between an ad for a new smartphone and a local solicitor’s firm. ‘Vote Vance for a Stronger Newcastle.’ Kira felt a fresh spike of rage. Stronger for who? For people like her, with their stock portfolios and their second homes in Northumberland?

Her destination was a chain of pawn shops and second-hand electronics stores down near Central Station, a grittier part of the city centre where the transactions were quicker and the questions fewer. She found a place tucked between a betting shop and a takeaway, its windows cluttered with dusty games consoles and out-of-date cameras. A bell tinkled weakly as she pushed the door open, stepping into a room that smelled of dust and desperation. Behind a reinforced glass counter, a man with a weary, suspicious face looked up from his newspaper.

“Help you?” he grunted, not bothering to get up.

“Looking for a laptop,” Kira said, her voice barely a whisper. She cleared her throat. “A laptop. Cheap. Just for browsing, writing.”

The man gestured vaguely with his thumb towards a shelf stacked with mismatched machines. “Take your pick. Prices are on the tags. Cash only on those.”

Kira scanned the sad collection of cast-offs. They were old, bulky, covered in scratches and stickers from their previous lives. She found what she was looking for near the back: a ten-year-old Lenovo ThinkPad, a blocky, unlovely thing, but famously durable. Its battery life would be shot, and it would groan under the weight of a modern operating system, but its keyboard was solid and it had the ports she needed. The tag said two hundred and fifty pounds. It was more than she wanted to spend, but it was perfect for her needs – anonymous, functional, and utterly disposable.

“That one,” she said, pointing.

The man heaved himself out of his chair, fetched it from the shelf, and slammed it onto the counter. He plugged it in, and the screen flickered to life, showing a generic Windows login screen. “Works. Two-fifty.”

Kira pulled the crumpled notes from her pocket, smoothing them out on the counter. The man counted them twice, his eyes narrowed, then swept them into a drawer. He handed her a grimy charger and the laptop, not bothering with a bag. The entire transaction took less than two minutes. She clutched the heavy plastic block to her chest, the sharp corners digging into her arms. It felt less like a purchase and more like an arms deal. As she walked out, the bell tinkling behind her, she felt the first real flicker of power she had experienced in months. She had a weapon. Now she needed a battlefield.The battlefield she chose was the city library, a grand Victorian building of sandstone and hushed reverence. It was a place she’d loved as a teenager, a sanctuary of quiet corners and infinite worlds. Now, it was a strategic choice. It offered free, anonymous Wi-Fi, long opening hours, and the perfect camouflage. No one looked twice at a woman with a laptop in a library. But first, she needed to sterilise her new weapon. The ThinkPad was a Trojan horse until she could guarantee it was clean. Her path took her past St. Nicholas's Cathedral, its lantern spire a familiar silhouette against the bruised purple of the evening sky. She walked with a tense, deliberate rhythm, the heavy laptop a solid weight against her chest, a counterpoint to the hollow fear in her gut. She felt the gaze of a thousand CCTV cameras, the unseen eyes of the city watching her every move.

Inside the library, the warmth and the familiar smell of old paper and floor polish settled over her like a comforting blanket, and for a moment, the crushing anxiety eased. She found an empty public-use computer in a row near the history section, the screen glowing with the city council's bland welcome page. She slid into the chair, the hard plastic a world away from the ergonomic mesh of her old office. Her fingers, stiff with cold and tension, flew across the keyboard with an old, forgotten muscle memory. She didn’t browse, she didn’t linger. She navigated with swift, economical clicks to a software repository, downloading a clean, secure operating system—a privacy-focused version of Linux designed to be run from a portable drive, routing all its traffic through layers of encrypted relays. Back in the library's cocoon of silence, she found a secluded desk on the top floor, a single carrel tucked away behind shelves of oversized atlases, facing a blank wall. The old laptop whirred to life with a geriatric groan. She was scouring away the past, creating a clean slate, a single, untraceable point from which to launch her counter-attack.

When it was done, she installed the new operating system onto the now-pristine hard drive. The bulky ThinkPad was reborn, a ghost machine running a ghost OS. Only then did she connect to the library’s public Wi-Fi. The connection established itself with a quiet click. For a moment, she just stared at the blank screen, the blinking cursor a steady, patient heartbeat. The sheer, overwhelming scale of her task threatened to paralyse her. Where did she even begin to unpick a frame-up so perfect, so complete? The answer came, unbidden, with the memory of her mother’s voice, bright with pride. *Your sister, Eleanor. A real credit to the family.*

She didn’t start with her old company’s servers. She didn’t try to trace the stolen money. That was what they would expect, the digital equivalent of returning to the scene of the crime.
