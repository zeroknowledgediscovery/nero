The genoise sponge, for the third time that evening, had the structural integrity of a damp flannel. It slumped with a soft, doughy sigh, exhaling a ghost of vanilla and despair onto the cooling rack. Dr Eleanor Vance, known to a rapidly growing segment of the British public simply as ‘Ellie the GP’, pressed the heels of her hands into her eyes, feeling the fine tremor in her fingers that had nothing to do with fatigue and everything to do with rage. A surgeon’s tremor, she thought with a grimace. Not a baker’s.

Flour dusted every surface of her normally pristine Exeter kitchen, a fine white powder that settled on the stainless steel, the granite countertops, and the framed anatomical charts she’d never gotten around to taking down. It was in her hair, a premature streak of grey, and had formed a pasty residue under her fingernails. This was the reality of the Bake Off final: less bunting and bonhomie, more a state of low-grade, sugar-fuelled warfare against the laws of physics.

She peeled a stray piece of fondant from her elbow. It was supposed to be a decorative corbel for the third tier of her practice showstopper – the ‘Exeter Cathedral in Choux and Chantilly’. At the time, it had seemed like a magnificent, ambitious tribute to her hometown. Now, in the harsh halogen glare of 2 a.m., it felt like a monument to her own hubris.

Mark had found her like this an hour ago, a silent, flour-dusted statue of frustration staring at a bowl of recalcitrant meringue. He’d kissed the top of her head, muttered something about ‘tempering her expectations’ alongside her chocolate, and retreated to bed with the weary air of a man whose partner had been replaced by a high-strung patisserie obsessive. She couldn’t blame him. She barely recognised herself. The calm, methodical GP who could diagnose shingles at twenty paces and gently break bad news was gone, supplanted by a woman who’d had a twenty-minute, one-sided argument with a jar of glucose syrup.

And yet.

The memory of the phone call, three long months ago, still had the power to cut through the exhaustion. She’d been in her surgery, sandwiched between a man with a verruca and a woman convinced her cat had given her phlebitis. Her mobile had buzzed, an unknown number. She’d almost ignored it.

“Is this Dr Eleanor Vance?” a bright, impossibly young voice had chirped.

“It is. I’m with a patient, can I…”

“It’s Chloe from Love Productions. We just wanted to be the first to say… welcome to the tent.”

The world had tilted. The smell of antiseptic from the dispenser, the low hum of the computer, the patient files stacked on her desk – it all faded to a muffled background hum. She’d gripped the edge of her desk. Ten thousand applicants. The gruelling auditions. The technical challenges that felt like culinary school exams set by a sadist. And she had made it. Her, Ellie, the GP from Devon whose secret passion had always been the precise, alchemical magic of turning butter, flour, and sugar into something sublime. The joy had been so pure, so potent, it felt like a physical force, lifting her up. She’d ended the call, walked back into the consulting room, and prescribed a fungal foot cream with a smile so wide the patient had asked if she’d won the lottery. In a way, she had.

Now, that joy was curdled by pressure. The final. It was absurd. The final was for other people, for the preternaturally gifted or the media-savvy wunderkinds. Not for her. But she’d survived Biscuit Week, her jammy dodgers praised by the formidable Prudence for their ‘exquisite snap’. She’d navigated the minefield of Pastry Week, her hot-water crust pie holding its shape like a dream. She’d even conquered the dreaded Vegan Week with an aquafaba pavlova that Julian, the show's silver-haired heartthrob judge, had declared ‘a revelation’.

Week by week, her other life had bled into her real one. Patients would come in for a blood pressure check and leave with a debate about the correct ratio of a Victoria sponge. “Saw you on the telly last night, doc,” Mr. Abernathy, a retired plumber with chronic gout, had said. “That Battenberg looked a bit dry on the cross-section, mind.” The local paper had run a feature: ‘From Prescriptions to Patisserie’. She was a minor celebrity, a curiosity. And she loved it. She loved the escape, the complete and total absorption of it. In the tent, no one was sick, no one was dying. The worst thing that could happen was a soggy bottom.

She scraped the failed sponge into the bin with a vicious swipe of her spatula. No more failures. She had one week. One week until the train to Berkshire, the familiar drive to Welford Park, the final walk up to that glorious white tent. One week to perfect a choux-pastry buttress.

A new sound cut through the quiet hum of the refrigerator. A soft thud from the front of the house, like a heavy parcel being dropped on the doormat. The post didn't come at this hour. She moved through the darkened hallway, the old floorboards groaning a familiar complaint under her feet. She pulled it free. There was no stamp, no address. It had been delivered by hand. Her heart gave a little trip-hammer beat against her ribs. It was probably just a card from a well-wisher, a fan. She’d had a few of those. But it was the middle of the night. The stillness of the house suddenly felt less peaceful, more… watchful. She slit the envelope open with her thumbnail. *Your sugar-glass cathedral is magnificent. But the real prize isn’t a cake stand. We can help you win it.*

The detail was chillingly sharp; she could see the title of a cookbook on the counter behind her, the specific brand of flour she was using, the tense line of her jaw. It wasn't just a picture; it was a violation, an intimate portrait of a private moment, stolen and presented back to her as a threat.

She let the photograph fall to the floor, her fingers numb. Her gaze snapped back to the typewritten words. *The real prize isn’t a cake stand.* What did that mean? For ten weeks, the engraved glass stand had been a holy grail, the symbol of everything she’d striven for. To have it dismissed so casually felt like another, more subtle, violation. And the offer: *We can help you win it.* It wasn’t a threat of sabotage, but something far more unnerving. It was an invitation into a conspiracy. The word ‘we’ hung in the airless hallway, suggesting an organization, a faceless group who had watched her, assessed her, and now sought to recruit her for some unknown purpose.

Her first instinct was to run upstairs and wake Mark, to thrust the note into his hands and let his solid, dependable logic make sense of it. But she stopped herself, her hand on the newel post. She pictured his face, bleary with sleep, his patient sigh as he tried to process her panicked whispers. He’d see the photo, the strange message, and he’d link it to the pressure she was under. He’d call it a cruel prank, the work of an overzealous fan or a bottom-feeding journalist trying to get a rise out of her before the final. He would be reasonable. With a deliberateness that felt alien to her trembling body, she bent and picked up the photograph. The flour on the floor, which minutes ago had been a symbol of her creative frustrations, now seemed like forensic dust, mapping out her movements for an unseen observer. Her failed genoise, her architectural choux, her arguments with glucose syrup – it all felt like a child’s game played in a house with unlocked doors. She had been so focused on the controlled, self-contained world of the competition that she had forgotten about the world outside, and that it could be watching her.

She locked the back door, testing the handle twice. She moved through the ground floor, from the dining room to the study, checking every window, every point of entry. The house, her sanctuary, now felt like a fragile shell. The anatomical charts in the kitchen seemed to mock her, their diagrams of nerves and muscle a stark reminder of her own physical vulnerability. The note and the photograph sat on the granite countertop, an impossible intrusion into her life. Her ambition, the thing that had brought her such pure, unadulterated joy, had apparently put a target on her back. The question was, a target for what? The grand prize of a baking competition seemed a ridiculously low stake for such a sinister, coordinated approach. She stared at the words again, the promise and the threat tangled together. The real prize. What in God’s name was the real prize?Her training took over, a cool wave of clinical detachment washing over the hot tide of panic. The script on the envelope was a flowing, almost archaic cursive, yet the message inside was impersonally typed. A deliberate contrast, designed to unnerve. She forced herself to breathe, the air thick with the lingering scents of vanilla and cooled sugar. The phrase ‘sugar-glass cathedral’ snagged in her mind. She *had* said it. Gushing to Chloe, the young producer, about her grand vision for the showstopper. “I want the windows to look like stained glass, a real sugar-glass cathedral,” she’d boasted, caught up in the fantasy of it all. The kitchen, her sanctuary of controlled chemistry, suddenly felt like an interrogation room. What was the test? The note was a provocation, an intellectual challenge disguised as a threat. *The real prize isn't a cake stand.* The statement was designed to intrigue, to flatter her intelligence, to suggest she was destined for more than a televised baking contest. It appealed to the same part of her that had driven her through medical school, the part that thrived on solving the most complex puzzles. Who were these people who believed they knew her so well? And what prize could possibly be valuable enough to warrant such a high-stakes, elaborate recruitment process? This wasn’t about winning Bake Off. This was about what came after.

Her gaze fell on the bin, where the sad, collapsed remains of her genoise lay. An hour ago, it had been the epicentre of her world. Now it was just a cake. A triviality. She slipped into her small study, the one room Mark rarely entered, crowded with medical journals and textbooks. The secret was hidden, nestled amongst other, more knowable dangers. The act of concealment was a silent acknowledgment. She had entered into a dialogue with them, whether she wanted to or not.

Returning to the kitchen, she began to clean. The activity was automatic, a familiar ritual to soothe a frayed mind. She scraped, wiped, and polished, eradicating every last speck of flour, every sticky trace of fondant. She scrubbed the granite countertops until they gleamed under the halogen lights, erasing the ghostly fingerprints of her earlier panic. But as she worked, the silence of the house pressed in on her. It was no longer the peaceful quiet of a sleeping home, but the loaded silence of a stage, waiting for the next actor to make their entrance. She was acutely aware of the windows, of the darkness that pressed against the glass. The night was no longer empty, but full of watchers. The sun would rise in a few hours, and with it would come the need to perform normality for Mark, to discuss milk and newspapers and the state of the garden, all while this chilling secret coiled in the pit of her stomach. The final walk to the white tent in Welford Park no longer felt like the end of a journey, but the beginning of something else entirely.The shrill beep of the alarm clock at 6:30 a.m. felt like an act of violence. She slapped the snooze button with a hand that felt disconnected from her body.

The lie came easily, a well-practised reflex from a lifetime of delivering difficult truths with a gentle veneer. She was a doctor. The roller blind she’d yanked down now seemed like a flimsy shield, a child's defence against the dark. The joy of this room, the creative chaos she usually revelled in, had been exorcised. It was just a space now, cold and functional, and potentially, a crime scene.

The competition had become a stage, and she was the lead actor in a play she didn't understand, her every move scrutinised by an audience hiding in the shadows.
