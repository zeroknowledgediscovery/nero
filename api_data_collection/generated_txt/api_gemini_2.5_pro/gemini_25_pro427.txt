The rain over Rochdale had a particular quality, a heft to it that felt less like water and more like liquid grit. It fell in grey, determined sheets, slicking the cobblestones of the old town centre until they gleamed like polished obsidian and turning the vast, unforgiving moors that ringed the valley into a treacherous, sucking bog. On days like this, the town seemed to shrink into itself, hunching its stone shoulders against the sky. It was on such a day that they found the boy.

He was laid out by the banks of the River Irk, where the water ran sluggish and brown, thick with the silt of a dozen neglected culverts. Not hidden, but arranged. A deliberate tableau. His youthful limbs, so recently full of swagger and undirected energy, were composed in a parody of repose, his face turned towards the bruised purple of the sky. The raw, red ruin of his throat was a stark, screaming contrast to the pale, almost translucent skin of his chest. But it wasn't the violence that held the town in a grip of cold, suffocating terror. It was the pages.

Tucked into the boy’s cold hand, protected from the relentless drizzle by a carefully folded oilskin wrapper, was a small, hand-stitched booklet. The paper was thick, creamy, expensive. The script within was a thing of obsessive beauty, a calligrapher’s dream of loops and flourishes, the ink a deep, rich sepia. It was a story. His story. A detailed, lyrical, and utterly monstrous account of the boy’s final hours, written from the perspective of his killer. It chronicled the pursuit, the capture, the terrified pleading, and the final, ecstatic moment of extinction. The prose was hypnotic, weaving classical allusions with passages of breathtakingly arrogant philosophy on the nature of beauty, lust, and the divine right of the strong to consume the weak. The press, in a fit of lurid inspiration, had a name for the author of these death-notes. They called him the Ogre.

Lady Beatrice Forster read the account in the Rochdale Observer, her morning tea growing cold in its delicate china cup. Her drawing-room, with its high ceilings, its William Morris wallpaper, and its view of the meticulously tended gardens of her estate, felt like a ship in a storm, a fragile bubble of order and civility in an ocean of barbarism. Three boys. Three in as many months. All of them young, on the cusp of manhood, all of them from the rougher edges of the town.
