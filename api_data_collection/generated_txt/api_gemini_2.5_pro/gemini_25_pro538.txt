The house settled around Dee like a shroud she’d paid too much for. It smelled of lemon polish and old, dry wood, a desperate attempt to cover the deeper scent of decay she felt clinging to the very foundations. From the bay window in the sitting room, the garden was a riot, a battlefield of green where weeds were winning a war of attrition against things that might once have been beautiful. Thorns like rusty wire choked the life from climbing roses, and what was probably a lawn had surrendered to patches of defiant crabgrass and dandelions with their heads held high. It was a mess. A glorious, untamed catastrophe. It was the only part of the property she felt any kinship with.

Chicago was a memory that flickered like a faulty bulb, all sharp angles and the smell of ozone before a storm. The weight of things in her hand. The slickness of certain surfaces. The quiet click of a lock disengaging. These were muscle memories, ghosts in her nerves that had no place here among the chirping birds and the oppressive, verdant quiet. This place, this house, was meant to be an ending. A place to let the dust settle, not on the furniture, but on her. But the dust wouldn't settle. It churned inside her, a fine grit of restlessness that had nothing to do with the city she'd left behind. It was a deeper, more fundamental vibration. A hum beneath the skin.

The sound of tires crunching on the gravel driveway was an intrusion, a tear in the fabric of the afternoon. A truck, older and rust-dusted, parked near the skeletal remains of a birdbath. A man unfolded himself from the driver's seat. He wasn't what the service had described. They’d promised someone ‘experienced.’ She’d pictured a stooped man with a creased face and a permanent squint. This man, he was built of long, lean lines and moved with a lazy grace that spoke of strength held in reserve. He wore a faded t-shirt and jeans that were more dirt than denim. He didn't look at the house, at her, a shadow in the window. His eyes went straight to the garden. And he smiled. It wasn’t a polite smile. It was a genuine, slow-spreading thing, as if the chaos delighted him.

She met him on the porch, the wooden planks warm beneath her bare feet. A splinter, she thought, would be an interesting sensation. Distant. An academic sort of pain. She found she couldn’t quite remember the sharp, immediate sting of a small wound.

"Dee," she said, her voice a little rough from disuse.

"Leo," he replied. His eyes were the color of wet moss. He gestured with a dirt-caked hand toward the tangled mess. "She's a wild one."

"It's a disaster."

"Nah," he said, that smile returning, crinkling the corners of his eyes. "It's just forgotten. It remembers how to be a garden. Just needs a little convincing."

She watched him for the rest of the day. From the window, at first, then from the porch swing, a glass of iced water sweating in her hand, an offering she hadn't yet decided to make. He worked with a rhythmic intensity that was both hypnotic and unsettling. He didn't hack or tear. He knelt in the dirt, his hands moving with surgical precision, coaxing weeds from the soil as if he were helping them leave, not evicting them. He seemed to listen to things she couldn't hear. A language spoken by roots and stems. She knew about listening, too. She listened for the catch in a person’s breath that signaled a lie. The subtle shift of weight that preceded a movement of aggression. The hollow sound of a space that should be occupied. His listening was about creation. Hers was about deconstruction.

A strange scent drifted to her on a sudden breeze, something that came from him. It wasn't sweat, not exactly. It was richer, earthier, with a coppery undertone that made the back of her jaw ache with a peculiar, hollow hunger. It wasn't for food. The thought of food was a gray, uninteresting concept. She hadn’t eaten since morning, maybe yesterday, she couldn't be sure, but the familiar pangs were absent. There was only this other craving. A need for proximity. A desire to be closer to the source of that scent. To understand it.

She finally brought him the water. His fingers brushed hers as he took the glass, and a jolt, cold and sharp, went up her arm. It wasn't unpleasant. It was just… information. His skin was warm, alive. Her own felt like a cool sheath, a container for the humming thing inside her.

"Thank you," he said, his voice a low rumble. He drank the whole glass without stopping, his throat working. She watched the movement, fascinated. The simple, biological mechanics of it. Life.

"You seem to know what you're doing," she observed, her tone flatter than she intended.

He wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. "I just listen to what it wants. This rosebush," he pointed to the thorny monster clinging to the trellis, "it's not angry. It's scared. Been fighting for its life so long it's forgotten how to just grow."

She looked from the rosebush to him. Scared. She understood that. She just never thought of a plant feeling it. Her own fear was a fossil, a thing so old and deeply buried it no longer had a name. It had hardened into something else. Vigilance. Efficiency. The cool, calm knowledge of how to make a threat disappear. She looked at his neck, the way the sun hit the skin just above his collar. The pulse there would be strong. A steady, living rhythm. The thought came unbidden, a clinical assessment from a life she was supposed to be forgetting. It was a disconcerting habit to break. Or maybe, she considered, it wasn't a habit at all. Maybe it was an appetite.

The days fell into a pattern. The crunch of gravel in the morning, the quiet industry of his work, the setting sun painting the sky in bruises of purple and orange, the silence that descended after his truck rattled away. Dee found herself living for the hours he was there. She’d sit on the porch and read, or pretend to, the words on the page turning into meaningless shapes as she watched him resurrect the garden. He was winning the war. Patches of green, actual lawn, began to appear. He pruned the roses, and a few days later, a single, defiant bud appeared, a tightly furled fist of deep crimson.

They spoke, but not much. He would tell her about the soil, how it was too acidic here, how the hydrangeas would probably be blue. She would ask a question, just to keep him talking, to hear the calm, steady cadence of his voice. His presence was a strange anchor in her drifting, timeless existence. Before him, the days bled into one another, a smear of gray. Now, they were marked: the time before Leo, the time with Leo, the time after Leo.

One afternoon, he was wrestling with the root of a stubborn, dead shrub. He grunted, pulling hard, his muscles straining. The root gave way suddenly and he stumbled back, his arm scraping hard against the rough bark of a nearby oak tree. It was a deep, angry gash, from wrist to elbow. Blood welled up instantly, shockingly red against his sun-browned skin.

Dee was on her feet before she even registered the movement. The coppery scent she’d noticed before was now a thunderclap in her senses, overwhelming, intoxicating. The hum beneath her skin intensified into a high-frequency vibration. The hollow ache in her jaw was a physical pain now, a deep, primal need that drowned out all other thoughts. She was moving toward him, her hands outstretched, though she didn’t know what she intended to do. Grab him? Help him?

He just looked at the wound, a frown on his face. "Damn. That's a bleeder." He looked up at her, saw her expression, her unnaturally wide eyes. A flicker of confusion crossed his face. "Hey, you okay? You look like you've seen a ghost."

The word 'ghost' was a splash of cold water. It broke the spell. She stopped, her feet feeling leaden, rooted to the spot. "I... I don't like the sight of blood," she lied. The words tasted like ash in her mouth. She, who had waded through ruin and painted rooms in shades of red, didn't like the sight of it. It was the most ridiculous thing she had ever said. But the smell… the smell was pulling at something deep and dormant inside her, a creature stirring in a long, dark sleep. It wanted. It simply, purely, wanted. He wrapped a dirty bandana around the cut, the fabric quickly soaking through. The scent was slightly muffled, and the roaring in her head subsided to a manageable buzz.

"You should clean that," she said, her voice tight.

"I will," he said, giving her a slightly concerned look. "It's just a scratch."

It wasn't just a scratch. It was a revelation. It was the first clue to the nature of the hunger she had been misinterpreting for weeks. It wasn't a desire for his company, not really. It wasn't a longing for the life he represented. It was something far more simple, and infinitely more terrifying. She turned and walked back into the cold, silent house, and for the first time, she felt the ghost of a real, sharp, and very human fear. It was not a fear of him. It was a fear of the thing inside her that was waking up to his presence. The thing that saw his life not as something to admire, but as something to consume.Back inside, she pressed her shoulders against the cool wood of the front door, as if she could physically hold the outside world at bay. The scent was still there, a ghost on the air, a hook in her senses. She could taste it on the back of her tongue. The lemon polish and dry wood were gone, replaced by the vital, metallic tang of his blood. Her own blood felt sluggish in her veins, a cold, slow river. The hum inside her was a choir of starving mouths. She closed her eyes, trying to force the memory of Chicago to the surface, hoping the hard edges of that life would give her something to hold onto. The precise weight of her preferred tools, the satisfying silence after a job was done. But those memories were clinical, sterile. They offered no defense against this raw, biological imperative.

She’d thought the hunger was a byproduct of her old life, a psychological echo of the violence she’d orchestrated. She had managed it, channeled it, given it a purpose. The people she’d been sent to find had been liabilities, threats, names on a list. Their fear had been a spice, their panicked final moments a grim kind of satisfaction. But she had never felt this. This pure, unadorned, cellular craving. It wasn't about power or control. It was about sustenance. Her previous existence hadn’t been a job; it had been a carefully managed feeding ground, and she hadn’t even known it. This house, this quiet life, wasn't an ending. It was starvation.

Drawn by a morbid curiosity that was stronger than her fear, she returned to the bay window. He was still there, by the oak tree. The bandana around his arm was a dark, saturated red. He had found a small first-aid kit in his truck and was clumsily trying to open a packet of antiseptic wipes with his teeth, his injured arm held stiffly at his side. The sight should have been mundane, even slightly pathetic. To Dee, it was a study in profound vulnerability. Every movement he made, every flex of muscle, every bead of sweat on his brow, screamed of the life that pulsed just beneath his skin. The garden, which had been her only comfort, now looked like a trap. The vibrant green of the new grass, the promise of the crimson rosebud—it was all part of his life-giving energy, an extension of him. He wasn't just tending a garden; he was pouring himself into it. And she was a drought.

He finally managed to clean the wound, wincing as the antiseptic stung. He wrapped it more securely with a proper bandage, his movements slow and deliberate. For a moment, he stopped and looked directly at the house, at her window. His expression wasn't accusatory. It was thoughtful, a little puzzled. He had felt it, she realized. Not her hunger, but the sudden, violent shift in the atmosphere between them. The air had gone from quiet curiosity to something charged and dangerous, and he, a man who listened to the language of roots and stems, had registered the change. He held her gaze for a long moment before turning back to his work, but his movements were different now. Less relaxed. More aware.

The rest of the afternoon was an exercise in torment. She couldn’t look away. He packed his tools, his hands now clean, but she could still see the phantom stain of his blood, smell the ghost of its scent. The sun began its slow descent, and the light slanting through the trees caught him, haloing him in gold. He looked like a minor deity of soil and growing things, utterly out of place in her world of shadows and silent endings. The sound of his truck starting was a physical release of tension in her chest, but the quiet that followed was worse. It was an empty quiet, a hollow space that had, only moments before, been filled with life.

She walked out onto the porch as the dust from his departure settled. The air was cooling, thick with the scent of damp earth and night-blooming jasmine he had uncovered near the foundation. She walked barefoot across the dew-dampened grass to the rosebush he had so carefully liberated. The single bud was a dark promise. She reached out, her fingers tracing the tight spiral of the petals. It was beautiful. A testament to his care, to the life he had coaxed from near-death. The thought made her feel ill. He was creating all this beauty, this life. And the creature inside her, the real Dee, the one she was just beginning to meet, looked at it all and thought only of how to drain it dry. The house was not a shroud. It was a hunting blind. And she had, foolishly, invited life to come and play in her killing field.That night, sleep was a distant country she had no passport for. She paced the house, her bare feet silent on the floorboards, a ghost in her own mausoleum. The hunger was a live thing inside her, a coiled snake in her belly, uncoiling and tightening with a slow, nauseating rhythm. She tried to re-examine her past through this new, horrifying lens. The assignments in Chicago, the men and women who had ended up as statistics in a file she’d personally closed. She had always prided herself on her detachment, her ability to see people as systems of vulnerabilities. She now saw it was a lie she’d told herself. She hadn’t been detached; she had been fixated. She hadn't been targeting vulnerabilities; she had been drawn to the frantic, terrified pulse of life at its most potent. The thrill she’d mistaken for professional satisfaction had been the sating of an appetite.

She found herself in the bathroom, staring into the mirror above the pedestal sink. The face that stared back was hers, but it was a stranger. The shadows under her eyes seemed deeper, her skin holding a pallor that no amount of sun could burn away. Her eyes. They were the same shape, the same color, but they looked… empty. Or rather, they were full of a single, stark purpose that chilled her. She was looking at a predator. A supremely efficient, patient predator that had camouflaged itself so perfectly it had even fooled itself. For years, she had been a wolf who believed she was merely a shepherd’s dog, culling the herd for a distant master. Now she knew. There was no master. There was only the wolf, and its own deep, abiding hunger.

She tried to eat. She opened the refrigerator, its bright, sterile light illuminating a carton of milk, a wilting celery stalk, and a jar of olives. The thought of putting any of it in her mouth was repulsive. She toasted a piece of bread until it was black, hoping the charred, definite taste might ground her. It was like chewing on ash and glue. Her body rejected it, not with sickness, but with a profound and utter disinterest. The complex machinery of human digestion felt alien to her, a function she could remember but no longer access. The only thing that felt real was the hollow ache, the gnawing emptiness that only one specific scent had ever promised to fill. The house was no longer an ending; it was a sensory deprivation chamber, and it was driving her mad.

Morning arrived not as a relief, but as an indictment. The soft, gray light of dawn filtered through the dusty windows, illuminating the churning motes of dust in the air. Each one seemed to represent a moment of her life she had misunderstood. She took up her now-familiar position at the bay window, a sentry at her post. A part of her, the small, flickering ember of the woman who had bought this house for peace, screamed at her to pick up the phone. Call the landscaping service. Tell them the job was done. Tell them he was no longer needed. It was the sensible thing to do. The safe thing to do. But the creature inside her uncoiled at the thought, a wave of cold panic and desperate need washing through her. It vetoed the idea with absolute authority. It wanted him to come. It needed him to come. The hunger, she was beginning to learn, did not negotiate.

When she heard the crunch of tires on the gravel, it was not an intrusion as it had been the first day. It was a summons. A deep, resonant chime that vibrated through the floorboards and up her spine, silencing the frantic chatter in her mind and replacing it with a singular, predatory focus. The hum beneath her skin sharpened, a blade being honed. She watched him unfold himself from the truck, the same lazy grace in his movements. Today he wore a clean grey shirt, and she could see the white of the bandage on his arm, a stark reminder of the revelation from the day before. He paused for a moment, his eyes scanning the front of the house, his gaze lingering on the bay window where he knew she would be. He gave a small, almost hesitant nod, a silent acknowledgment of the strange new territory between them. Dee did not move. She simply watched, her breath held in her chest, as the one thing in the world that could save her, and utterly destroy her, walked into her garden.He didn't start work near the house. He went to the far end of the property, near the crumbling stone wall that bordered the woods, and began the arduous task of clearing a thicket of invasive bittersweet that had strangled a line of old cedar trees. It was a deliberate choice, she knew. He was giving her space, or perhaps, giving himself distance. The air between them was a pane of brittle glass, and he seemed content to work on the other side of it. But distance was an illusion. Her senses, now fully awake and screaming for attention, crossed the yard with effortless ease. She could hear the rhythmic scrape of his shovel against rock, the soft grunt of exertion as he put his weight into the handle, the steady, maddeningly serene cadence of his breathing. The wind, which had once been a neutral carrier of scents, was now a traitor, bringing her whispers of him—earth, sweat, and the faint, lingering echo of iron from the day before.

She could not stay inside. The house had become a cage, the scent of lemon polish an offense. Hiding was a coward’s game, and whatever else she was, she was not a coward. She was a hunter, and a hunter needed to understand its territory. It needed to understand its prey. The thought was cold and clear, and it did not frighten her as it had the night before. The fear was still there, a thin, high-pitched ringing in the back of her mind, but the hunger was louder. It was a roar that demanded action, observation, proximity. She pushed open the porch door and stepped out into the humid air, her bare feet sinking slightly into the new, soft grass he had uncovered. It felt like walking on his skin.

He heard her coming. He stopped his work mid-swing, the shovel held loosely in his hands, and turned. He didn't seem surprised, just watchful. The easy, slow-spreading smile she had seen on the first day was gone, replaced by a neutral mask of professional courtesy that didn’t quite reach his moss-colored eyes. They were assessing her, noting the way she moved, the unnatural stillness of her posture. He was reading the landscape, and she had become the most dangerous and unpredictable feature in it.

"Morning," he said. The word was a simple stone dropped into a deep, silent well.

"The rosebush," she said, forgoing any greeting. Her voice was steady, a carefully calibrated instrument. "The one you pruned. There's a bud." She gestured back toward the porch trellis, not taking her eyes off him.

He nodded, his gaze following her gesture for a fraction of a second before returning to her face. "Told you. She just needed some room to breathe. They're resilient." He leaned the shovel against the stone wall, wiping his hands on his dirt-caked jeans. The gesture was casual, but it also put a tool between them. A barrier. "That whole section will probably bloom in a week or two, now that it's getting some sun."

She began to walk toward him, a slow, deliberate pace. The grass was cool and damp. With every step, the scent of him grew stronger, the background noise of the world fading until all that was left was the thrumming in her own veins and the low, vital hum she could feel emanating from him. It was like approaching a furnace. She stopped a few feet away, close enough to see the fine lines around his eyes, the dark smudge of dirt on his jaw, the faint, rhythmic pulse in his throat. The hollow ache returned, sharp and demanding. She had to clench her jaw to keep her mouth from watering. "You were right. It wasn't angry. It was just forgotten."

"Most things aren't angry," he said, his voice a little quieter now that she was closer. "Just scared, or crowded. You give them a little light, a little space, they remember what they're supposed to be." He looked at her then, a direct and searching look, and she knew he wasn't talking about the garden anymore.

She held his gaze, a silent battle of wills. Her past was a litany of broken men, men who had crumbled under a fraction of this intensity. Leo didn't crumble. He met her stare with a kind of weary, patient curiosity, like a man looking at a storm building on the horizon. He didn't understand the nature of the storm, but he knew it was coming. The moment stretched, taut and shimmering. The hunter and the gardener, standing in a half-tamed patch of green, separated by a chasm of biology and intent. To break the spell, she glanced down at his arm. The bandage was clean and white, a stark contrast to the rest of him. "How's your arm?"

"It's fine. Just a scratch," he said, the same words he'd used yesterday, but they sounded different now. Less dismissive, more like a reassurance meant for her. He flexed his hand, a simple, unconscious movement that sent a tremor of need through her. She could see the muscles shift beneath his skin, imagine the blood flowing through the veins, warm and alive. The urge to close the distance, to place her hand over his, to feel that life against her own cold skin, was so powerful it made her dizzy. She took a half-step back, a small, involuntary retreat that did not go unnoticed. A flicker of something—not fear, but a deep, profound sadness—passed through his eyes. He knew. He didn't know *what*, but he knew that she was the source of the danger, and that she was afraid of it, too. He bent down and picked up his shovel. "This bittersweet's got roots that go halfway to China. It's gonna be a long day." It was a dismissal. A gentle, but firm, redrawing of the line between them. He was turning away, giving her an opportunity to leave. The creature inside her snarled at the denial, but the last vestige of Dee, the woman who had sought out this peace, took the chance. She turned without another word and walked back to the house, the furnace of his life at her back. The test was over. She hadn't attacked him, but she had failed all the same. She now knew that her control was a thread, and proximity was a blade. And he was going to be here all day.Back inside, the thin veneer of civilization she had wrapped around herself shattered. The cool, shaded sitting room was no longer a refuge; it was an echo chamber. Every sound from the garden was magnified, distorted. The rhythmic scrape of his shovel was the grinding of her own teeth. The snap of a severed root was a jolt through her nervous system. She pressed her palms against the bay window, the glass cool against her burning skin. He was a hundred feet away, a figure defined by his labor, yet he might as well have been in the room with her. His presence was an atmospheric pressure that made the air thick and hard to breathe. Her home had become his territory, and she was the trespasser in it, caged by walls of her own choosing.

The fear of the previous night had been a frantic, panicked thing. Now, it settled into something colder and more pragmatic. The hunter in her, sidelined and ignored for so long, was now fully awake and assessing the situation with chilling clarity. It watched Leo not as a man, but as a phenomenon. It cataloged the efficient power in the swing of his arms, the solid plant of his feet on the earth, the way his body radiated a heat she could feel even through the glass. The hunger was no longer a vague, internal hum; it was a specific, directional pull, a form of gravity. It didn't just want sustenance; it wanted *him*. His vitality. The unique, irreplaceable energy that was Leo. It was a connoisseur's appetite, and it was utterly terrifying.

She spent hours at the window, a statue carved from tension. She watched as the sun climbed to its zenith, beating down on the garden, casting sharp, unforgiving shadows. Sweat darkened the grey fabric of his shirt, plastering it to the long, lean lines of his back. He paused occasionally to drink from a canteen, tilting his head back, his throat working. The sight was an agony. It was a visceral reminder of her own profound, unnatural thirst, a dryness that felt ancient and absolute. Her throat was a desert. Her body was a vessel slowly turning to dust. The thought surfaced, unbidden and sharp: a single drop of his blood would be an oasis. It would be more than life; it would be rain after a century of drought.

The idea was so potent, so seductive, that it broke her paralysis. She pushed away from the window, her heart hammering a frantic, useless rhythm. She couldn't just stand here and watch herself lose control. The woman who had orchestrated complex disappearances in the heart of a bustling city, who had outwitted armed and paranoid men, could not be undone by a gardener. She needed a plan. A strategy. She needed to reassert herself, not as a predator, but as a person. The thought was absurd, but it was the only one she had. She would perform a human ritual. She would make him lunch. The sheer, perverse domesticity of the act appealed to her sense of irony. She would feed him, this man whose life force she craved, and in doing so, she would prove to the creature inside her who was in charge.

The kitchen was an alien landscape. She opened the refrigerator and stared at its contents with the detached curiosity of an archaeologist examining artifacts from a lost civilization. A block of pale cheddar. A sweating package of sliced turkey. A loaf of whole wheat bread. It was fuel. Mundane, lifeless fuel. Her hands felt clumsy as she assembled the sandwiches, her movements stiff and unfamiliar. The smell of the mustard was acrid, the scent of the bread like sawdust. She made two sandwiches, placed them on a plate beside a handful of olives, and poured a tall glass of lemonade, the ice cubes clinking like a death knell. The entire tray looked like a prop from a play about a life she had never truly lived. Carrying it, she felt like an imposter. But as she pushed open the porch door and stepped back out into the oppressive heat, she wasn't just an imposter. She was a zookeeper, carrying a bucket of meat into the lion's cage, with the desperate, insane hope that it might prefer the offering to the zookeeper herself.

He had moved back to the center of the lawn and was on his knees, meticulously pulling weeds from around the base of the skeletal birdbath. He didn’t seem to hear her this time, his focus absolute. She crossed the lawn, her shadow falling over him. He looked up, shielding his eyes from the sun with a dirt-smeared forearm. His gaze went from her face to the tray in her hands, and a look of profound surprise, mixed with a healthy dose of suspicion, crossed his features. He slowly got to his feet, brushing dirt from the knees of his jeans.

"You don't have to do that," he said, his voice wary.

"You've been working for hours. You need to eat," she replied. The words felt rehearsed. She held the tray out to him, an offering across a battlefield. "It's just a sandwich."

He hesitated for a long second, his moss-green eyes searching her face for the trap. She met his gaze without flinching, pouring all of her remaining energy into projecting a semblance of normalcy. Finally, with a small, reluctant sigh, he took the plate from her. He gestured toward a low stone bench under the shade of a maple tree. "Alright. Thank you."

They sat on opposite ends of the bench, a careful distance between them. The silence was deafening, broken only by the buzz of insects and the sound of him eating. Dee watched his hands, the way his dirt-caked fingers held the sandwich. She watched his jaw work, the simple, mechanical process of chewing. The sight filled her with a strange, conflicting brew of emotions: the hunter's agonizing frustration, and a bizarre, almost maternal satisfaction at seeing him nourished. She was keeping her prey healthy. The thought made her feel sick.

"This is good," he said, after swallowing the first half of a sandwich in three large bites. "Didn't take you for a cook."

"I'm not," she said flatly. She watched him take a long drink of the lemonade, the glass sweating in his hand, just as hers had the day before. Her eyes fixed on a small, fresh scratch on the back of his hand, a thin red line where he must have caught it on a stone. It wasn't bleeding, but the line of it was like a beacon. The hum inside her, which had receded to a low thrum, began to climb in pitch again. She tore her eyes away, focusing on the gnarled trunk of the maple tree, counting the ridges in the bark. One. Two. Three.

"So, Chicago," he said, startling her. He was looking at her, his sandwich finished, the plate resting on his lap. "What's a woman from Chicago doing out here, in a place like this?"

The question was a test. A normal, human question designed to get a normal, human answer. "I wanted a change," she said, the lie thin and worn. "I wanted quiet."

He nodded slowly, his gaze unwavering. "You find it yet? The quiet?"

She thought of the roaring in her head, the screaming of her cells, the constant, predatory hum beneath her skin. She looked at him, at the source of all the noise, and gave him the most honest answer she could. "No."

His expression softened with something she couldn't name. Pity? Understanding? He picked up an olive from the plate, rolling it between his thumb and forefinger. "Sometimes the quiet isn't about the place. It's about what you're trying to leave behind. It follows you. Gets louder when there's nothing else to hear." He popped the olive into his mouth. "Sometimes you have to turn around and face it, instead of trying to outrun it."

He didn't know how right he was. She was facing it. It was sitting three feet from her on a stone bench, smelling of earth and sweat and life, making her want to tear the world apart. The effort of the last few hours—making the food, bringing it to him, sitting here and pretending to be a person—crashed down on her. A wave of exhaustion so profound it felt like a physical blow. Her control, stretched taut for so long, was beginning to fray. She stood up abruptly. "I'll take that," she said, her voice tight, reaching for his empty plate.

Her hand trembled as she took it. He stood up as well, their faces now only a couple of feet apart. The furnace of him was overwhelming. "Dee," he began, his voice low, "if you're in some kind of trouble..."

"I'm not in trouble," she snapped, her control finally breaking, the words coming out harsher than she intended. She took a step back, clutching the plate to her chest like a shield. "Thank you for the... for the advice. You should get back to work. You're on the clock."

The concern in his eyes vanished, replaced by a cool mask. He gave a single, sharp nod. "Right. The clock." He turned away from her and walked back to the center of the lawn, picking up his tools without a backward glance. The invisible wall between them was back, thicker and higher than before. Dee fled back to the house, the plate rattling in her shaking hands. The experiment had been a catastrophic failure. She hadn't tamed the beast. she had only shown it what the feast looked like. And it was hungrier than ever.Back inside, the plate felt impossibly heavy, a dead weight of failure. She dropped it into the sink with a clatter that echoed the shattering of her composure. The kitchen, with its lingering scent of bread and mustard, was nauseating. It was the smell of a lie, of a life she was only pretending to inhabit. She had marched onto the battlefield under a flag of truce, offering a pathetic parody of human kindness, and had been utterly routed. Not by him, but by the force inside her that had soured the gesture from the start. He hadn’t rejected her; he had seen through her, to the desperate, hollow thing beneath the performance. His parting words, “Right. The clock,” were a verdict. She was a client, he was a contractor. The strange, fragile bridge of curiosity they had been building was gone, replaced by the cold, clear transaction of commerce.

She retreated to the upper floor, as far from the garden as she could get, but the distance was meaningless. The house had become a resonant chamber, and his every movement was the strike of a tuning fork that set her entire being vibrating. She could hear the snip of his shears as he deadheaded withered blossoms, a sound as sharp and precise as a surgeon’s cut. She could hear the whisper of the tall grass as he passed through it. Through the bedroom window, she watched him work, his body a study in contained energy. He was a constant, living rebuke to her own sterile stillness. The hunter in her watched with a cold, analytical fury. It noted his caution, the way he now kept his back to the woods, his senses implicitly turned toward the house, toward her. She had warned him. Her lack of control had been a warning flare, and he had seen it. He was no longer just a gardener. He was a man aware that he was in the presence of something unpredictable and dangerous.

The afternoon sun bled across the sky, and a strange light fell over the garden, thick and golden, making the green of the new lawn seem almost impossibly vibrant. It was in this strange, luminous moment that she saw it. The rosebud. The tightly furled fist of crimson on the trellis by the porch had opened. It wasn't a gentle unfurling. It looked like a wound, a single, perfect drop of arterial blood spilled against the deep green of the leaves. It was exquisitely beautiful, and the sight of it made her stomach clench with a violent, possessive greed. It was his creation. He had coaxed it from the brink, pruned away the dead wood, and given it light. It was a testament to his vitality, a symbol of the life he poured into everything he touched. She wanted to crush it in her fist. She wanted to devour it.

His day ended without ceremony. She watched him pack his tools into the rusted bed of his truck, his movements economical and swift. There was no final glance toward the house, no acknowledgment of her silent vigil. The careful neutrality of his posture was more damning than any accusation. The crunch of the tires on the gravel was a sound of departure, of escape. As the rumble of his engine faded into the oppressive quiet of the countryside, the silence he left behind was not peaceful. It was a vacuum. A void that had, for hours, been filled with the thrum of his life. Now, it rushed in to fill the space, and the hunger, held at bay by his presence, roared into the emptiness with a vengeance.

The house was a tomb. The air was stale, the lemon polish a chemical ghost. She couldn't stay in there. She couldn't bear the dead quiet, the scent of decay that was her own. She pushed open the front door and walked out into the twilight. The air was cooler now, heavy with the scent of cut grass and damp earth. She drifted through the garden, a specter in the gloaming, her bare feet silent on the lawn he had reclaimed. Everything around her was his. The neat edges of the flowerbeds, the liberated hydrangeas, the breathing space around the cedars. His energy was baked into the soil, clinging to every leaf. She felt like an intruder in her own garden, a drought in a land he had promised rain.

Her steps, seemingly aimless, took her to the old oak tree where he had cut his arm. The ground was disturbed where he had knelt to tend the wound. A few discarded antiseptic wipe packets lay like shed skin in the grass. And beside them, half-hidden beneath a clump of clover, was the bandana. It was a dull, dark red, stiff with dried blood. She stopped, her breath catching in her throat. The hunter, the creature, went utterly still. This was a trail. This was a piece of him, left behind. The small, human part of her screamed a silent, desperate *no*. It was a filthy piece of cloth. It was a remnant of a minor injury. It was nothing. But the hunger knew better. It knew that the scent, the essence, the very proof of his life, was woven into those threads.

Slowly, as if in a trance, she bent down. Her fingers, cool and pale in the dimming light, closed around the rough, stiff fabric. It was still faintly warm, not from the sun, but with a residual energy that made her palm tingle. She lifted it. The coppery scent, which had been a phantom on the breeze all day, was now a physical presence. It was the scent of life itself, pure and undiluted. The ache in her jaw became a sharp, stabbing pain. The hum inside her crescendoed into a high, keening siren. The battle was over. The zookeeper had left the enclosure, and the lion was free. With a hand that trembled violently, she lifted the bandana to her face, closed her eyes, and inhaled. The effect was instantaneous and absolute. It was not a scent; it was a current. A jolt of pure, raw life surged through her, lighting up every dormant nerve, every starving cell. It was a flash of lightning in a dark room, illuminating for one blinding second the true, cavernous depth of her emptiness. The moment of ecstasy was searing, and it was gone in a heartbeat, leaving behind a darkness more profound, a hunger more vast and more specific than she had ever imagined possible. It was not enough. It was nowhere near enough. It was just a taste. A promise. And she knew, with a certainty that erased all fear and all doubt, that she would have the meal.The world crashed back in, muted and gray, a photograph bleached of all color. The ecstasy of the scent vanished, leaving a hollow so profound it was a physical presence, a black hole at the core of her being. The taste, the promise, had not sated the hunger; it had given it a name and a face. It had given it a purpose. She was no longer just a starving thing; she was a predator who had found the scent of her designated prey. All the vagaries of her condition, the restless churning she’d mistaken for a psychological remnant of her old life, had snapped into a terrible, singular focus. The hunger didn't want life. It wanted Leo's life.

She clutched the stiff, blood-darkened bandana in her fist, a holy relic. The scent was already fading, absorbed into the fabric of the evening, and a fresh wave of panic, cold and sharp, lanced through her. She couldn't lose it. It was the only real thing in a world of ghosts and shadows. She returned to the house, the silent tomb, and went not to the sitting room or the bedroom, but to a small, hidden compartment built into the back of a linen closet, a secret she had brought with her from Chicago. Inside lay the tools of her former trade, nested in custom-cut foam. They were cold, matte-black steel, instruments of deconstruction, designed for silence and efficiency. She laid the bandana inside, beside a slim stiletto and a garrote wire, as if it belonged there. And in a way, it did. They were all instruments for ending things. The woman who sought peace was dead. The woman who had bought this house as a shroud had finally, truly, grown into it.

Sleep did not come, but neither did the frantic pacing of the night before. She sat in the dark of the sitting room, perfectly still, watching the moon crawl across the sky. The turmoil was gone, replaced by a profound and chilling calm. It was the calm of a decision made, of a path chosen. The hunter no longer fought the hunger; it embraced it. It let the craving hone its senses, sharpen its mind. She thought about Leo, not with the agonized longing of the past days, but with a cool, dispassionate analysis. She cataloged his strengths: his physical power, his quiet awareness, his deep-rooted connection to the living world. And she cataloged his vulnerabilities: his fundamental decency, his instinct to help, his assumption that the world, however strange, operated on a set of shared rules. He was a creature of the sun, of growth and soil. He had no defenses against a creature of the deep, patient dark.

The first hint of dawn was a signal. She rose, her movements fluid and silent, and prepared. She did not prepare food or coffee. She went to the bathroom and looked in the mirror, but this time she was not examining a stranger. She was taking inventory. Her pale skin, her shadowed eyes, the unnatural stillness of her features—they were no longer signs of sickness, but tools of the hunt. Camouflage. She dressed in simple, dark clothing and went out onto the porch, taking a seat on the top step, the cool, damp wood a pleasant shock against her skin. She did not read or pretend to. She simply waited. She was a trap, baited and set. And she had perfect faith that her prey would walk into it.

When the sound of his truck finally broke the morning silence, it was the sound of a key turning in a lock. It was an arrival, an inevitability. He parked in the usual spot, but he didn't unfold himself with his customary lazy grace. He got out of the truck with a stiff, guarded motion, his eyes immediately finding her on the porch. He stopped by the hood of his truck, the engine ticking as it cooled, creating a buffer of space between them. The wariness of yesterday had crystallized into a solid, palpable alarm. He looked at her, at the absolute stillness of her posture, the unnerving calm in her gaze, and he knew, with the certainty of an animal that feels the change in barometric pressure before a storm, that something was fundamentally wrong.

"Dee," he said, and his voice was not a greeting. It was a question. It was a challenge.

She did not answer immediately. She let the silence stretch, let him feel the full weight of her stillness. Then, she smiled. It was not the ghost of a smile from their first meeting. It was a slow, deliberate expression of pure, predatory satisfaction. It did not touch her eyes. "Leo," she said, her voice a low, smooth purr that was more unsettling than any shout. "You came back." She rose to her feet in a single, fluid movement, a panther uncoiling. "I was worried you wouldn't."

She started to walk toward him, her bare feet making no sound on the gravel of the driveway. He didn't retreat, but his body tensed, his weight shifting onto the balls of his feet, his hands clenching into loose fists at his sides. He was preparing for a fight, but he had no idea what kind of fight he was in. She stopped just out of arm's reach, close enough that she could feel the heat of him, close enough to catch the faint, maddening scent of his skin on the morning air. Her eyes dropped to the white bandage on his arm. "You're healing," she observed, her voice soft with a strange, possessive disappointment. "That's good." She looked back up at his face, her smile fading into an expression of intense, unnerving focus. "I was admiring your work this morning." She gestured toward the trellis. "The rose. It’s opened. It’s the color of a fresh wound. It’s perfect."

The words hung in the air between them, obscene in their implications. He was a man accustomed to the straightforward language of soil and rain, and she was speaking a dialect of violence and desire he couldn't possibly comprehend, yet he understood the intent perfectly. It was a claim. She was claiming his work, the life he had nurtured, as her own. "It's just a flower," he said, his voice tight, his jaw set.

"Nothing is ever *just* a flower, Leo," she replied, her gaze unwavering. "Everything is something else, too." She held his eyes for a moment longer, letting him see the vast, cold emptiness there, the single-minded hunger that had consumed everything else. Then she turned and walked back toward the house. "You have a lot to do today," she said over her shoulder, a casual dismissal that was also a terrible promise. "Don't let me keep you." She didn’t go inside. She returned to her seat on the porch step, her back straight, her hands resting calmly in her lap. The game had begun. The garden was no longer his workplace. It was an arena, and she was content to watch, to wait, to let him wear himself out against the suffocating pressure of her presence.The silence that followed her pronouncement was a physical thing, a heavy blanket that smothered the morning songs of the birds. Leo stood frozen for a long moment, a man trying to make sense of a map written in a language he’d never seen before. He was a creature of tangible things—soil composition, root-depth, the angle of the sun. He was being confronted by a creature of pure, predatory intent, and the rules of his world did not apply. He finally broke the standoff, turning his back to her with a deliberate, almost painful slowness. He picked up a pair of shears from the bed of his truck and walked, not to the far end of the property as he had the day before, but to the rosebush on the trellis, the very one she had claimed with her words. It was an act of defiance. He was refusing to cede territory.

Dee watched, a flicker of what might have been respect moving through the cold sea of her hunger. He was not prey that would bolt. He would stand his ground, tend his garden, and dare the storm to break. It made the hunt more interesting. She remained on the porch step, a queen surveying her domain, and let her presence do the work. The rhythmic, hypnotic quality of his labor was gone. His movements were now clipped, efficient, and edged with a tension that radiated across the lawn. Every snip of his shears was a retort. Every weed he pulled was an act of reclaiming ground. He was no longer just gardening; he was fortifying his position, shoring up the world of life and order against the encroaching chaos she represented.

Hours passed in this silent war. The sun climbed higher, beating down on his back. He worked without pausing, refusing to give her the satisfaction of seeing him rest or drink. He was punishing his own body to deny her any perceived victory. Dee felt a strange, detached pity for him. He was fighting with the wrong weapons. He was treating this as a battle of wills, a test of endurance. He could not possibly know that his exertion, the sweat beading on his temples, the strain of his muscles, the life he was pouring into his work, was only making him a more tantalizing meal. He was seasoning himself with his own defiance.

Around midday, she decided to press her advantage. She rose from the step, her body feeling light and humming with energy she hadn't earned, and moved into the garden. She walked a circuitous route, her bare feet tracing the edges of the flowerbeds he had so carefully defined. She paused to brush her fingers against the soft, velvety leaves of a lamb's ear, then ran a single finger along a sharp, serrated blade of crabgrass he had missed. She was inspecting his work, her every gesture a critique, an intrusion. He pretended not to notice, his focus narrowed on the intricate task of tying a stray cane of the rosebush to the trellis. His knuckles were white with tension. She came to a stop directly behind him, so close she could feel the heat radiating from his body, so close she could smell the clean, sharp scent of his sweat and the underlying, vital hum of his being. The creature inside her stirred, its hunger a low growl in her blood.

"It’s a shame to tame it," she said, her voice a low murmur just beside his ear. He flinched, a small, violent jerk that belied his attempt at composure. "It was so beautiful in its chaos." He did not turn. He finished tying the knot with a sharp pull, his breath held tight in his chest. "You're trying so hard to make things live," she continued, her voice laced with a cruel sort of wonder. "What do you do with the things that are meant to die?" He finally turned, his face flushed with heat and anger, his moss-green eyes blazing. He had a thorn in his thumb, a tiny, perfect splinter of wood from the rose cane, and a single, bright bead of blood was welling up from the puncture. The sight of it, that minuscule, perfect drop of life, made the world tilt on its axis. The hum inside her screamed.

"What do you want?" he bit out, his voice a low, furious growl. He ignored the blood on his thumb, his entire being focused on her, the enemy who had breached his final wall. He was cornered, and he was finally baring his teeth.

Her eyes were locked on the drop of blood. It was a ruby. It was a sun. It was the answer to every question she had ever had. The carefully constructed calm, the predatory poise she had worn all morning, evaporated in the face of that tiny, perfect offering. A tremor went through her, a visible shudder of profound, agonizing need. She looked from his thumb to his eyes, and what he saw there finally made him take a step back. It wasn't anger or malice. It was a void. A ravenous, absolute emptiness that wanted to consume him. She took a step forward, closing the distance he had made. "I want," she whispered, her voice rough, her throat tight with a thirst that was a thousand years old, "to feel something other than hungry."

The honesty of it was more shocking than any threat. He stared at her, his anger momentarily forgotten, replaced by a stunned, horrified comprehension. He was not dealing with a person in trouble, or a woman playing mind games. He was dealing with something broken, something wrong, something for which he had no name. The instincts that told him how to read the soil and the sky were screaming at him now, a silent, primal alarm. This was not a storm to be weathered. This was a blight. A drought that saw him as the only spring. He looked from her wide, dark, hungry eyes to the bead of blood on his thumb. He deliberately, slowly, lifted his thumb to his mouth and licked the blood away. The gesture was a sharp, definitive denial. It was a locking of a door. He would give her nothing. Not one more drop.

The act was like a slap. It broke the spell of her hunger with a jolt of pure, unadulterated rage. The creature inside her, denied its prize, snarled. Her face, which had been a mask of desperate need, hardened into something cold and terrible. "A mistake," she said, her voice dropping back into the flat, toneless register of her old life. It was not a threat. It was a clinical assessment. He had shown her he would not be a willing sacrifice. He had just made the hunt more difficult, and therefore, more necessary. Without another word, he turned, grabbed his tool bag from the ground, and strode toward his truck, abandoning the rest of his workday. He didn't run, but his pace was urgent, the walk of a man leaving the site of a disaster. Dee stood alone in the garden, a pillar of stillness amidst the vibrant life he had fostered. She watched him drive away until the sound of his engine was completely gone. The garden was quiet again. But it was not a peaceful quiet. It was the silence of a held breath, the pause before the final, fatal strike. She had lost the battle, but she had just declared the war.The rage was a white-hot flash that seared the edges of her vision, and then it was gone, sublimated into something far more dangerous. It cooled and hardened into resolve, a substance dense and cold as neutron-star matter. She stood in the quiet garden, the air thick with the scent of his panicked sweat and the perfume of the living things he had fought to protect. He had made a choice. He had seen the truth of her, the great and terrible thirst, and he had chosen to defy it. The small, human part of her might have felt a flicker of admiration for his courage, but that part of her was a burnt-out ember, and the creature that remained felt only the cold logic of the hunt. He had turned a negotiation into a conflict, and conflict was a language she understood perfectly.

She walked back into the house, but it was no longer a shroud or a tomb. It was her den. The shadows that had once felt oppressive were now a comfort, the silence a tool. The disused rooms were waiting areas, staging grounds. The ghost of Chicago, the woman of angles and efficiency, walked back into the foreground of her mind, not as a memory, but as an active partner. The primal, cellular hunger now had a voice, a strategist, a professional to direct its raw power. The first step, as always, was information. The psychological games were over. It was time for logistics. She walked to the phone on the hall table, its plastic cool and definite in her hand. Her quarry had a name, but a name was not enough. He had a life, a routine, a place where he slept. A place where he was vulnerable.

Her voice, when she spoke to the woman at the landscaping service, was a masterpiece of concerned sincerity. She was a new homeowner, Dee explained, a little overwhelmed. The young man they’d sent, Leo, was so hardworking, but he’d left so abruptly, she was worried he’d felt unwell. She’d misplaced the invoice, she added, a casual but crucial lie. She just wanted to make sure everything was settled, and could she perhaps have the business address to mail a check directly? The woman on the other end, disarmed by the gentle courtesy, was only too happy to help. She provided the name of his small, one-man operation, "Veridian Landscaping," and a post office box. A dead end. But then, with a little more feigned worry from Dee about his well-being, the woman offered a nugget of gold. “He lives out on Old Mill Road,” she said chattily. “The little blue house just past the covered bridge. Can’t miss it.” Dee thanked her profusely, her heart a cold, steady metronome in her chest, and hung up. The hunt was on.

Getting into her car felt like donning a second skin. The drive into the small, sleepy town was a journey through a world that had become entirely irrelevant. The cheerful storefronts, the people laughing on the sidewalk, the bells of a church marking the hour—it was all background noise, a meaningless diorama inhabited by ghosts. Her senses, so attuned to the vibrant signal of Leo, filtered it all out as static. There was only one point of light in her universe, one source of warmth, and she was moving toward it. She drove through the town and out the other side, following the winding country lane designated Old Mill Road. The trees grew thicker here, their branches arching over the road. Then, as promised, a covered bridge appeared, its dark, tunnel-like passage a symbolic gateway into his territory. She crossed it, the rumbling of her tires on the wooden planks the only sound.

And there it was. A small, blue house, set back from the road, with a gravel driveway and a neatly kept yard that was a testament to his profession. A battered but clean Ford truck, his truck, was parked beside a small, detached garage. It was a simple, unassuming place, but to Dee, it was a sanctuary she was about to violate. Potted herbs lined the front steps. A wind chime made of polished river stones hung from the porch eaves, silent in the still afternoon air. It was a place of peace, a place someone had built to feel safe. The sight of it didn't stir a shred of guilt in her. It only deepened her possessive desire. This was his heartland, the center of his life, and she would stand in the middle of it. She drove past, continuing down the road for a half-mile before turning around and finding a secluded spot on a dirt turn-off, a place where she could watch the entrance to his road, unseen.

She didn't have to wait long. An hour after she had settled in, his truck rumbled past her hiding spot, heading home. She gave him ten minutes, then followed, parking her car on the shoulder several hundred yards from his house, cloaked by a thick stand of pine trees. She got out and moved through the woods that bordered his property, silent as a wraith, her city-honed skills at stealth serving a new, more ancient purpose. She found a position on a small rise, a vantage point shielded by a thicket of mountain laurel. From here, she could see his back porch, his yard, the windows of his kitchen. He was there, on the porch, sitting in a rocking chair, his back to her. He was holding a bottle of beer, but he wasn't drinking it. His shoulders were slumped. He looked exhausted, not just physically, but spiritually. He looked like a man who had seen a ghost and was now trying to convince himself he was still in the real world.

A rangy, golden-haired dog, some kind of retriever mix, nudged its head under his hand. He startled, then relaxed, his fingers stroking the dog’s ears with a weary affection. He murmured something to the animal, his voice too low to carry, but the gesture was clear. It was a simple act of love, of connection to another living thing. Watching it, Dee felt a cold, possessive jealousy so sharp it was like a blade twisting in her gut. That affection, that gentle touch, that life—it belonged to her. He was hers. His dog, his home, the very air he breathed, were all part of the life she would consume. The sun began to set, painting the sky in bruised colors of orange and violet. Lights came on inside his house. He and the dog went inside, leaving the porch empty. Dee remained in the woods as the last of the light faded, the hunter watching the warm, lit square of her prey’s den. She had him. She knew where he lived, where he felt safe. The rage and the frantic need had settled into a deep, patient, and utterly confident hunger. She would not act tonight. A cornered animal was dangerous. She would let him relax. She would let him believe he had escaped. She would let him sleep, and then, she would come for him.She remained on the rise as the last traces of purple faded from the sky and the woods settled into the deep, rustling dark of true night. His house became a ship of light on a black ocean, a beacon of warmth and life that called to her across the darkness. She watched the squares of yellow light move through the house as he did. The kitchen, then a living room, then an upstairs window that she knew, with an instinct that had nothing to do with sight, was his bedroom. When that final light winked out, leaving the house a dark shape against the slightly less dark sky, she did not move. The vigil was not just about waiting for an opportunity; it was an act of possession. She was learning the rhythms of his life, the cadence of his breath, even from a distance. The night was her ally, and she had all the time in the world.

The chill of the night air did not touch her. The damp earth beneath her was just a texture, not a source of discomfort. She felt a part of the darkness, a specialized predator perfectly adapted to this new environment. The frantic, terrified woman who had paced the floors of her sterile house was a distant memory, a shed skin. The conflict that had torn her apart—the human versus the hunger—was over. There had been no victor, only an integration. The human's intelligence and capacity for patience had been repurposed, placed in service of the hunger's singular, absolute will. She was no longer a person afflicted with a craving; she was a craving that had learned to think. The quiet she had so desperately sought in this place, she had finally found it here, in the cold, clear certainty of her purpose.

An hour after his light went out, she retreated. Her movements through the woods were liquid and silent, every step placed with an innate knowledge of the terrain. She was not leaving; she was tactically repositioning. The drive back to her own home was a cool-down lap, a centering exercise. The headlights cut a sterile path through a world that held nothing for her. Her house, when she arrived, was no longer a prison. It was a lair, its silence a comfortable cloak. She did not turn on the lights. She moved through the familiar rooms, her senses alive, tasting the dead air, the lingering scent of lemon polish a monument to a life she had failed to live. She went to the linen closet and opened the hidden panel. She did not touch the tools, but she looked at the dark, stiffened bandana, the relic of her revelation. It was a promise, and she was going to keep it.

Sleep was an unnecessary function, a human habit she had discarded. She sat in the darkness of the sitting room, the bay window framing a starless, overcast sky. The hours ticked by, measured not by a clock, but by the slow, internal shift from anticipation to readiness. The world outside was asleep, vulnerable, its guard down. This was her time. The quiet hum that had been her constant companion had changed its frequency. It was no longer a sign of internal discord; it was the thrum of a perfectly tuned engine, idling before a race. When an internal clock, older than any made by man, told her it was time, she rose. There was no hesitation. She moved with the serene grace of a natural disaster whose moment has arrived.

The drive back to his house was a final pilgrimage. The sleeping town, the dark fields, the tunnel of the covered bridge—they were all waypoints on her journey to him. It was an infiltration. She reached the edge of his property and paused, her senses extending into the space, tasting the air, listening. There was only the sound of the wind in the pines and the distant call of a lone owl. His house was a dark, sleeping heart in the center of the clearing. She moved toward the back porch, her feet making no sound on the damp grass. The lock on his kitchen door was a simple, standard tumbler, a child’s puzzle. She reached into a small pocket and retrieved a thin set of tension wrenches and a pick, the tools of her old trade feeling like extensions of her own fingers. The air inside the house was thick with his life. It was a physical assault on her senses, a warm, rich tapestry of scents: dog, old books, the lingering aroma of coffee, and underneath it all, the clean, vital scent of him. The hunger, which had been a cold, patient thing, flared with a sudden, violent heat. Her jaw ached. Her throat went dry. For a moment, the strategist wavered, and the raw, primal creature threatened to surface and simply tear through the house. But she held it, leashing the urge with an iron will. She could see the curve of his shoulder, the line of his neck. He was so utterly, completely vulnerable. She drifted to the side of the bed, a shadow detaching itself from the deeper shadows of the room. She stood over him, looking down. The hunger was a roaring inferno now, a supernova of need that blotted out everything else. All the days of watching, all the nights of aching, all the patient, calculated waiting, had led to this single, perfect moment. The strategist, the hunter, and the hunger were all in accord.
