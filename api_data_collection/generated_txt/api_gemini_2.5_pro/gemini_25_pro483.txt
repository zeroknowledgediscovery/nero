The Reverend Arthur Penhaligon believed, as an article of both personal and professional faith, that silence had texture. There was the expectant, breath-held silence of the congregation just before the first hymn, a silence like velvet. There was the brittle, post-argument silence between two members of the parish council, which felt like shattered glass. And then there was the silence of the Markham vicarage on a Tuesday afternoon, a silence as thick and as comfortable as a well-worn cardigan, smelling faintly of lemon polish and Earl Grey tea. It was, for the most part, his favourite kind.

Today, however, the cardigan of silence had a thread loose. A persistent, rhythmic tapping emanated from beneath the vast oak desk in his study, a sound utterly at odds with the gentle scratch of his pen as he proof-read the parish newsletter. *Tap-tappa-tap. Tap. Tappa-tap*. It was the syncopated beat of a cha-cha, a ghost of a rhythm that had taken up residence in his left foot and refused to be exorcised. The rhythm was a symptom of a larger malady: a secret, burgeoning joy that felt entirely inappropriate for a man of his calling and vintage.

From his perch on a precarious stack of theological treatises, Mephistopheles watched him. The cat, a ginger tom of astonishing bulk and malevolent intelligence, did not blink. His yellow eyes were twin pools of judgement. Arthur had inherited him, along with a collection of chipped porcelain and a lingering scent of lavender, from the previous incumbent’s widow. She had called him ‘Sunny’. Arthur, after one week of cohabitation, had privately renamed him. Mephistopheles, or Meffy for short, did not approve of the foot-tapping.
