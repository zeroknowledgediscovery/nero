The rain in Pen-y-Cwm had a personality of its own, a character as distinct and familiar as any of the village’s sixty-seven souls. There was the soft, apologetic mist that clung to the slate roofs for days, and the hard, angry downpour that turned the single road into a churning brown stream. Doctor Felix, his hands deep in the damp, black earth of his garden, knew them all. He called it the ‘drizzle-that-soaks’ and the ‘gale-that-stings’. Today, it was a fine, searching rain, the kind that found its way under the collar and down the back of the neck with quiet persistence. It was a weather for introspection, for the careful, meditative work of separating the tender roots of his prize-winning leeks.

His knuckles, scarred and earth-stained, were a testament to a life spent coaxing life from the stubborn Welsh soil. The title ‘Doctor’ was no folksy honorific bestowed by the village; it was a hard-won PhD in Ethnobotany from Aberystwyth, earned two decades ago before he’d retreated from the sterile world of academia to this green, wet sanctuary. He preferred the company of hellebores and hostas to departmental meetings. His work was here, in the whisper of folklore that clung to certain plants, the ancient uses and forgotten stories embedded in root and leaf. He was a curator of growing things and the legends they carried. It was a quiet, ordered existence.

That was why the smell was so unsettling. It caught on the wind, a thin, sharp note that cut through the pleasant scent of wet soil and decaying leaves. Not rot. Not precisely. A piquant sourness that scraped the back of the throat, out of place amongst the peat and petrichor. Felix paused, his fingers still laced around a fragile root system. He tilted his head, his grey-streaked hair plastered to his forehead, and sniffed the air again. It was acrid, chemical. Almost… pickling. He frowned, dismissing it as a vagrant scent from the distant farms, perhaps. Someone burning plastic, though it didn't have that acrid, oily quality. He shook his head and returned to his leeks, the disturbance a small stone dropped into the placid pond of his afternoon.

The click of the garden gate, rusty and complaining, was a much louder intrusion. He looked up to see Mrs. Pryce bustling down the flagstone path, her floral headscarf already darkening with rain, a carrier bag full of what looked like gossip and a tin of Welsh cakes swinging from her arm. She was the village’s central nervous system, every tremor of news vibrating through her first.

“Felix, bach, there you are. Drowning your vegetables again, I see,” she said, her voice carrying easily over the patter of the rain. She stopped at the edge of his meticulously mulched bed, careful not to tread on the emerging shoots of his snowdrops.

“They seem to enjoy it, Beti,” Felix replied, not getting up. “It’s what they know.”

“Well, it’s a terrible business,” she said, skipping any further pleasantries. Her face, usually a rosy map of cheerful wrinkles, was drawn and pale. “A truly terrible business. Little Cerys. You know, from down by the old quarry.”

Felix felt a cold knot tighten in his stomach. The stone in the pond was suddenly a boulder. “What about her, Beti?”

“She’s back,” Mrs. Pryce said, her voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. “Walked right into her own kitchen an hour ago, bold as brass. After being gone all night. The whole village out searching, the police on their way from Brecon… and she just walks in.”

Felix slowly pushed himself to his feet, his knees cracking in protest. He brushed the soil from his hands onto his corduroy trousers. “So she’s alright, then? That’s good news.”

“No,” Beti’s voice was sharp. “No, Felix, that’s the thing. She isn't. Not right at all. Her mum, Elen, she’s in a right state. She said Cerys is just… empty. Staring at the wall. Won’t talk, won’t cry. Just sits there, rocking. And cold. Cold as a stone, Elen said, though she was wearing her thickest coat.” Mrs. Pryce hugged herself, shivering though the day was mild for February. “But that’s not the worst of it. It’s her hand.”

Felix waited. The sour smell seemed to drift on the air again, stronger this time, making his eyes water.

“Elen was trying to get her coat off, you see. And Cerys just had her hand balled up in a fist, wouldn't let go. When Elen finally got her to open it…” Beti took a shaky breath. “Her thumb, Felix. It’s gone.”

The world seemed to tilt. The methodical rows of vegetables, the neat stone wall, the grey sky—it all swam for a moment. “Gone?” he echoed, his own voice sounding distant.

“Gone. Not a scratch on her otherwise. But her left thumb… just gone. And the stump… Elen said it was all clean. Puckered, she said. Like it had been… sealed.” Beti’s gaze was fixed on him, her eyes wide and searching, as if he, the man of strange plants and old books, would have an answer that the police from Brecon would not. “And she had something clutched in her other hand. A single, large dock leaf, wrapped around something small. When the constable made her open it, it was just a little white pebble. A perfect, smooth, white pebble.”

Felix felt the blood drain from his face. The dock leaf. The clean wound. The emptiness in the child’s eyes. And that smell, that vinegary, pickling smell that was now undeniably clinging to the air around his own home. It wasn’t a coincidence. It was a signature. He thought of the old tales, the ones he’d studied not as folklore but as field reports, as behavioural analyses of a predator he had prayed was long extinct, or merely a fantasy. The stories of the *Crebach,* the small ones, the greedy ones. The collectors.

“Felix? Are you alright, bach? You look like you’ve seen a ghost.”

He blinked, forcing the garden back into focus. Beti Pryce was staring at him, her concern a tangible thing. “I’m fine, Beti. Just a chill.” He forced a thin smile. “Thank you for telling me. It’s… shocking news.”

“Go and put the kettle on, there’s a good boy. You’ll catch your death out here.” She bustled past him towards his cottage door, leaving the carrier bag on his bench. But Felix didn't move. He stood in the rain, looking past his garden, past the slate roofs of the village, to the dark, brooding hills that cupped Pen-y-Cwm in their ancient hands. The rain was no longer just rain. It was a shroud. The sour wind was no longer a stray scent. It was a proclamation. The quiet, ordered existence he had so carefully cultivated was over. Something had come back to Pen-y-Cwm. Something that collected things. He finally turned and walked not towards the kettle, but towards the small, cluttered study at the back of his cottage, where a single, iron-banded chest sat beneath a window overlooking the wild fells. The chest had not been opened in twenty years. Tonight, he would open it. The time for gardening was done.The study was Felix’s refuge within a refuge, a small room crammed from floor to ceiling with the collected evidence of his life’s true passion. Orderly chaos reigned. Glass-fronted cabinets displayed carefully labelled soil samples from every corner of Wales. Bookshelves groaned under the weight of modern botanical textbooks pressed against crumbling, leather-bound volumes of local folklore. Framed, pressed specimens of rare mosses and ferns shared wall space with hand-drawn maps of the Black Mountains, annotated with spidery script detailing the locations of ancient yew trees and patches of ghost orchids. The air was thick with the dry, papery scent of old books and dried herbs, a fragrance he had always found profoundly comforting. Today, it offered no solace.

The iron-banded chest sat exactly where he had left it, beneath the window that looked out onto the bleak beauty of the fells. It was made of dark, heavy oak, the iron straps pitted with age but free of rust. He ran a hand over the lid, the wood cool and solid beneath his palm. There was no lock; it was held shut by two heavy hasps that required a specific, forceful twist to open. A test of intent, its maker had once told him. With a grunt of effort that spoke more of mental reluctance than physical weakness, he twisted the hasps. They sprang open with a groan of metal that sounded like a sigh of release. Lifting the heavy lid, he was met not with the smell of dust and decay, but with the sharp, clean scents of cedar, camphor, and something else, something metallic and cold.

Inside, everything was packed with the same meticulous care he afforded his seedlings. Rolled in oilcloth on the top layer were tools. Not for gardening, but for something far more precise and grim. There was a long, thin blade with a handle of polished rowan wood, its steel alloyed with meteoric iron, according to the man who forged it. Next to it, a set of silver probes and forceps, and a heavy, leather-bound gauntlet stitched with iron wire. Beneath this layer lay a series of clay pots sealed with wax. He lifted one, reading the faded label: *Gwrtaith y Tylwyth Teg* – Fairy Manure. A pungent, earthy compound of his own devising, made from powdered iron, dried St. John's Wort, and the pollen of a particular night-flowering campion. It didn't repel the Crebach, but it disrupted their strange, imperceptible senses, making the user effectively invisible to them.

At the very bottom of the chest, nestled in a bed of dried bog myrtle, was a journal. Its leather cover was stained and worn, the pages warped by damp. His own handwriting filled it, tighter and more frantic than the elegant script he used now. He opened it to a random page. *Entry 47: The quarry specimen confirms the 'pickling' agent is a highly concentrated formic acid derivative, secreted from glands in the mandible. Explains the clean cauterisation of tissue. They are not merely taking; they are preserving. Collecting. Why the thumb? Opposable digit, the key to tool use. Is there a hierarchy? Do they value what makes us human?* He slammed the book shut, the memory of the "specimen"—a half-devoured sheep's carcass, its leg bone removed with surgical precision—still vivid and nauseating after two decades.

The gentle, scholarly mask he had worn for twenty years was gone, stripped away by the contents of the chest. His face, reflected dimly in the dark glass of a specimen cabinet, was hard and grim. The man who had faced the Crebach before, the man who had lost a friend and a colleague in these very hills, was looking back at him. He had run from that man, burying him under a mountain of leeks and academic theory. But the Crebach had returned, and in doing so, had resurrected him. The sour, chemical smell that had haunted the air was the scent of their harvest. They were collecting again. Little Cerys was not just a victim; she was a survivor of a biological survey, a specimen tagged and released.

He pulled the heavy gauntlet from the chest, sliding it onto his left hand. The familiar weight was both a burden and a comfort. The fight was not just for the village, or for the little girl with the empty eyes and the missing thumb. It was a continuation of a war he had abandoned. He walked over to the large, annotated map on his wall, his gaze sweeping over the familiar contours of the land. The old quarry. The place where Cerys was found. It was marked on his map with a small, faded red cross, a notation beside it reading: *Hibernation/Nesting Site? High mineral content in rock. Avoid.* They had woken up. Or perhaps, something had woken them. His quiet, ordered existence was a luxury he could no longer afford. He had a debt to pay, and the collectors were back to claim it.He didn't move for a long time, letting the weight of the gauntlet settle on his arm, a grim anchor in the swirling chaos of his thoughts. The map was a lie, a two-dimensional representation of a world that had teeth and appetites. His red cross by the quarry was twenty years old, a relic of a past campaign. The Crebach were creatures of habit, but they were not stupid. They would not return to a site he had previously disturbed. No, they would have found a new place, a new hollow in the bones of the ancient hills. But the quarry was where they had released her. It was a message. A taunt. *We are here. We remember you.*

He peeled the gauntlet off and placed it carefully on his desk. Rushing into the fells now, as evening bled into the grey afternoon, would be suicide. The Crebach were crepuscular, their senses sharpest in the gloaming. He needed to think like a botanist again, not a soldier. He needed to observe the specimen. He had to see the child. He needed to see the wound, to understand the signature of this new incursion.

Shedding his mud-caked gardening clothes, he washed his hands and face in the cold water of the scullery sink, scrubbing away the grime of the garden but not the deeper stain of the day's events. He dressed in the unassuming uniform of his village life: dark trousers, a worn woollen jumper, and a sturdy waterproof coat. From a drawer in his study, he took a small leather satchel. Into it, he placed not the iron-forged knife or the silver probes, but a small magnifying lens, a pair of tweezers, and several glass vials—the tools of a concerned plant expert, a guise he could comfortably inhabit. He also took the tin of Welsh cakes Beti had left. A small offering, a token of normality in a world that had slipped its moorings.

The walk through Pen-y-Cwm was a journey through a village holding its breath. Lights were on in every cottage, casting warm, yellow squares onto the wet slate, but the lanes were empty. Faces peered from behind curtains as he passed, their expressions a mixture of fear and morbid curiosity. News, like the rain, found every crack in Pen-y-Cwm. They knew Cerys was back, and they knew something was wrong. Felix kept his head down, the collar of his coat turned up against the persistent drizzle and the unseen weight of their stares.

Cerys's home was a small, whitewashed cottage at the very end of the village, where the road began its winding ascent towards the abandoned slate quarry. A single police car was parked outside, incongruously modern against the ancient stone. A young, uniformed constable stood on the doorstep, looking overwhelmed and out of his depth. He straightened up as Felix approached.

"Can I help you, sir?" the constable asked, his Valleys accent thick. "Family's asked for a bit of privacy."

"I'm Doctor Felix. From the village," Felix said, his voice calm and even. "Elen, Cerys's mother, she's a friend. I heard what happened. I'm an ethnobotanist. I thought... perhaps the child ingested something up on the fells. A fungus, a root. It can cause... dissociative states. I wanted to see if I could help." It was a plausible, partial truth.

The constable, clearly desperate for any explanation that didn't involve madness or inexplicable violence, seemed to latch onto the idea. "A plant? Right. Yeah. The sergeant's on his way from Brecon, but... look, go on in. But be quick. The mother's in a bad way."

The cottage was small and oppressively warm. A coal fire blazed in the hearth, doing little to dispel the profound chill in the room. Elen, a woman Felix knew from the post office, sat on a low stool, her hands twisting a tea towel in her lap. Her face was a mask of exhausted disbelief. Cerys was in a large armchair by the fire, wrapped in a thick woollen blanket. Mrs. Pryce had been right. The child was a hollowed-out thing, her eyes fixed on a point on the far wall. Her breathing was shallow, and she rocked with a slow, metronomic rhythm that was deeply unnerving. The only sound in the room was the ticking of a mantelpiece clock and the child's ceaseless, gentle motion.

Felix approached slowly, kneeling so he was at her eye level. "Cerys?" he said softly. There was no response. Her eyes, usually a bright, mischievous blue, were flat and depthless, like frosted glass. He reached out and gently touched her cheek. The coldness was shocking, a deep, cellular cold that the fire could not reach. It was the cold of deep earth, of a riverbed in winter. He pulled his hand back as if burned.

"Elen," he said, turning to the mother. "Mrs. Pryce told me about her hand. May I see?"

Elen just nodded, unable to speak. Felix carefully took the child's left arm. It was limp, without tension. He uncurled her small fingers. The stump of her thumb was exactly as Beti had described. It wasn't a wound. It was a conclusion. The skin was pale and puckered into a neat, star-shaped pattern, like the top of a drawstring bag pulled tight. There was no scar tissue, no inflammation, no sign of blood. It looked as though she had been born this way, yet the faint memory of a thumbnail, a phantom shape on the nail bed of the adjacent finger, told him otherwise. His stomach churned. The precision was obscene. It was the work of a collector mounting a butterfly, not a predator attacking its prey.

"The dock leaf," Felix said, his voice barely a whisper. "And the pebble. Where are they?"

"The constable took them," Elen finally choked out. "For evidence, he said. Evidence of what?" She looked at Felix, her eyes pleading for an answer he could not give. "What's happened to my girl, Doctor? What's happened to my Cerys?"

He had no words of comfort that were not a lie. "The coldness... the vacant state... it's consistent with certain neurotoxins found in some rare fungi," he improvised, the botanical terms a flimsy shield against the horrifying truth. "Keep her warm. Keep talking to her. Familiar sounds are important." He stood up, placing the tin of Welsh cakes on the table. "I'm going to look around the area where she was found. See if I can find what she might have touched."

He left the cottage, the image of the child's sealed thumb and empty eyes burned into his mind. The constable gave him a hopeful nod as he passed. Felix ignored him, his gaze fixed on the darkening fells that loomed over the village. The confirmation was absolute. The academic retreat was over, the quiet order shattered beyond repair. He walked back through the rain-slicked streets, not to his cosy fireside, but to the cold study and the waiting iron-banded chest. The time for observation was over. The time for hunting had begun.Back in the study, the comfortable clutter of his life’s work seemed to mock him. The pressed flowers in their frames were fragile ghosts, the scholarly books inert stacks of paper. They were artifacts of a peace that had been a long, deliberate lie. He worked now with a grim efficiency that had lain dormant for two decades. The contents of the iron-banded chest were laid out on the broad oak surface of his desk, a stark and alien collection amidst the gentle chaos of his academic pursuits. The rowan-handled knife gleamed dully beside the silver probes. The iron-wire gauntlet lay like a shed skin, waiting to be reclaimed.

His first task was to prepare the Gwrtaith y Tylwyth Teg. He unsealed one of the clay pots with the edge of a trowel, the wax crumbling away to release a scent of dust and dormant power. Into a large mortar, he scraped the dark, granular contents, then went to a specific cabinet, one labelled only with a series of complex chemical formulas. From it, he took a jar of finely powdered iron filings and another containing the desiccated, almost black petals of St. John's Wort, harvested only on the summer solstice. He added them to the mortar, his movements precise and economical. The final ingredient was the most precious: a tiny phial of pollen, luminescent even in the dim light, collected from a rare, night-flowering campion that bloomed for a single evening in a hidden valley two counties away. He tapped a minuscule amount into the mixture. As he began to grind the components together with the heavy pestle, a low hum, almost too deep to be audible, filled the room, and the air shimmered for a moment, smelling of ozone and crushed nettles. This was the scent of being unseen.

With the compound prepared and decanted into several small leather pouches, he turned to the journal. His hands, now steady, opened the book to the last entry, dated the day he had abandoned this fight. The handwriting was ragged, a testament to his shattered state. *Rhys is gone. They took him. Not his thumb, not a finger. All of him. Dragged him down into the old workings. The iron line held for a while, but there were too many. They learn. They adapt. The sonic lures are useless now, they’ve learned to mimic the frequency. My fault. My theory. They used it against us. I heard him calling. Then I just heard the scraping. The collectors have my friend. God forgive me.* Felix’s jaw tightened until it ached. Rhys. His brilliant, reckless friend, the acoustics engineer who had thought they could drive the Crebach out with sound, had become the centerpiece of their collection. The memory was a fresh wound, a debt that could never be paid, only avenged.

He flipped back through the pages, his eyes scanning past diagrams of their physiology, past chemical analyses of their secretions, to the section on weaknesses. Much of it was crossed out, marked as ‘obsolete’ or ‘ineffective’. But one observation remained stark and uncorrected: *They cannot abide pure, flowing water. Not the stagnant bog or the rain, which is their element, but the living water of a stream or river. It shorts out their bio-electric field, causing intense pain and disorientation. The Afon Ddu is our only true barrier.* The Afon Ddu, the Black River, which cut a deep gorge to the east of the village. It was a line they would be reluctant to cross. This defined the boundaries of their new hunting ground.

He methodically began to gear up. He dusted the inside of his boots and the lining of his coat with the shimmering Gwrtaith powder. The iron knife was slipped into a sheath on his belt, its cold weight a familiar discomfort. The silver probes went into a pocket of his coat. He pulled the heavy gauntlet onto his left arm, flexing his fingers, the iron wire grating softly. It wasn't just for protection; the interwoven strands could be used to parry their paralytic grasp, short-circuiting the charge they delivered on contact. Lastly, he filled a flask not with tea or brandy, but with clean, cold water from his tap, adding a pinch of salt. A simple weapon, but an effective one if thrown correctly.

Dressed for the hunt, he was a different man. The soft-edged, academic air was gone, replaced by a hard, angular readiness. The stoop in his shoulders had vanished, his posture coiled and alert. He stood at the study window, looking not at the comforting lights of the village, but up at the oppressive blackness of the fells. He would not go to the quarry. That was a stage, a place for discarded things. The Crebach were meticulous; their trail would begin where their prize was selected. He needed to find the point of origin, the place where an ordinary walk home from a friend's house had turned into a descent into a living nightmare. He would start where Cerys's footprints vanished from the world. With a final, deep breath that tasted of rain and resolve, Felix unlocked his cottage door and stepped out into the night, melting into the very same searching rain that had begun the day, now a welcome cloak for the hunt ahead.The familiar paths of Pen-y-Cwm were different in the dark, transformed by purpose. Under the shroud of the ceaseless rain, Felix moved with a silence that belied his size. It was the quiet tread of a man who knew where the loose stones were, which patches of grass would squelch and betray him, and how to use the sound of the wind in the ash trees to cover his own passage. He was a creature of the village, but tonight he moved through it like a predator, his senses honed not for the scent of night-stock or the call of a tawny owl, but for the faintest trace of something utterly unnatural. He skirted the last few cottages, their glowing windows like drowsy, unsuspecting eyes, and found the public footpath that cut across the lower slope of the fell, a muddy ribbon connecting the scattered homes of the village’s western edge.

This was Cerys’s route home. A path she would have walked a thousand times, skipping over the roots of the old hawthorn and counting the slate steps built into the rising slope. For a child, it was a familiar adventure; for Felix, it was now a crime scene. He slowed his pace, his eyes, accustomed to spotting the subtle variations in a leaf or the first sign of blight on a stem, now scanning for far more sinister anomalies. The *Gwrtaith* powder in his coat seemed to generate a low-level hum against his skin, a strange, static friction that sharpened the edges of the world. The scent of wet wool and his own faint anxiety was overlaid by the clean, metallic tang of the compound, a personal bubble of un-smell that he hoped would render him nothing more than a ghost to the things he hunted. He knelt, his gloved hand hovering inches above the mud of the path, trying to feel the lingering echoes of what had happened here.

He found it fifty yards past a rickety stile. A small, crumpled piece of silver foil from a caramel wafer, half-trodden into the mud. Cerys’s favourite. He’d seen her with one just last week, sitting on the village green. The sight of it, so mundane and so final, sent a pang of cold anger through him. This was the exact moment. The last normal second of her life, the tearing of the wrapper and the sweet taste of chocolate before the world changed forever. He pulled out his small magnifying lens, ignoring the rain that dripped from his hair onto the glass. The ground around the wrapper was a mess of scuffed mud, but there was something else. A discoloration on a patch of hardy sheep's sorrel growing by the path's edge. The leaves, usually a vibrant green, were tinged with a sickly, bruised yellow, and their edges were unnaturally crisp.

Leaning closer, he caught it again: the sharp, pickling scent, now so concentrated it was almost overpowering. It clung to the blighted plants. He gently touched a leaf with the tip of a silver probe from his satchel. It didn't bend; it shattered like delicate glass, a miniature crystalline explosion. This was the epicentre. He could picture it with horrifying clarity: the child, distracted by her treat, and the thing, or things, emerging from the deep bracken beside the path. The suddenness of it. The cold, paralytic grasp. The searing chemical touch that had left this small, dead patch in the living world. He scanned the verge, his eyes tracing the line of the disturbance back from the path.

The trail was there, but it would have been invisible to anyone else. It was not a set of footprints. The Crebach were too light, too alien in their movements for that. It was a series of barely perceptible drag marks in the moss and leaf litter, thin parallel lines as if a multi-legged garden rake had been pulled through the undergrowth. And everywhere the trail went, there was a faint, almost imperceptible wilting of the vegetation, a shadow of decay left by their proximity. He saw where they had emerged from a thicket of gorse, and he saw the direction they had taken her. The path led away from the village, but it did not lead down towards the quarry. It led up. Up towards the high, bleak spine of the mountains, towards the wind-scoured plateaus where nothing grew but tough grasses and wiry heather.

A grim satisfaction settled over him, cold and heavy as the iron gauntlet on his arm. His theory had been correct. The quarry was a discard pile, a deliberate piece of theatre to send the police on a fool's errand. The real nest, the heart of the collection, was somewhere new, somewhere higher and more remote than before. They were expanding their territory. He stood, the rain plastering his grey-streaked hair to his scalp, and looked back for a moment at the small cluster of lights that was Pen-y-Cwm. It looked so fragile, a tiny, warm island in a vast, cold sea of darkness. The debt to Rhys felt like a physical weight in his chest. He had failed to protect his friend. He would not fail to protect this village. Turning his back on the lights, he took his first step off the human path, following the faint, venomous trail into the deep, waiting blackness of the fells.The higher he climbed, the more the land shed its thin veneer of familiarity. The footpath had long dissolved into a treacherous game of finding purchase on slick, moss-covered stone and deep clumps of waterlogged tussock grass. The rain, which had been a persistent drizzle in the village, became a driven, horizontal assault, hurled at him by a wind that moaned across the barren slopes. This was the ‘gale-that-stings’ in its truest form, and it felt personal. It sought to scour him from the mountainside, to turn him back towards the fragile safety of the lights below. But Felix pushed on, his body angled into the gale, the heavy gauntlet on his arm a counterweight against the wind’s fury. He moved with a hunter’s economy, his focus narrowed to the faint trail only he could see, a ghostly path of bruised vegetation and chemical whispers that was as clear to his trained eye as a line drawn in chalk.

He found the first true testament to their recent work near a weathered outcrop of granite that resembled a molar tooth. It was a sheep, or what was left of it. The animal was not torn or ravaged in the manner of a fox or a stray dog. It lay on its side, its fleece matted with rain, its eyes wide and vacant. Its left foreleg was gone, from the shoulder down, removed with the same horrifying, puckered tidiness as Cerys’s thumb. The exposed joint was a sealed, pale cup of tissue. Felix knelt, the acrid smell of the Crebach’s preservative agent catching in his throat. He brought his face close, noting how the surrounding grass was not trampled or blood-soaked, but simply wilted, as if the life had been leached out of it by mere proximity. This was not a feeding site. It was a harvest. A convenient specimen, taken apart for a single, desirable piece.

It was as he was examining the sheep that he heard it. A faint, chittering scrape from the rocks above him. It was the sound of chitin on wet stone, a sound that did not belong in the Welsh hills. He froze instantly, sinking into the shadow of the granite outcrop, his body becoming just another dark shape in the night. He held his breath, the frantic thumping of his own heart a deafening drum against his ribs. The Gwrtaith powder felt like a cold fire on his skin, his only hope against senses he could not fathom. The scraping grew closer, accompanied by a low, wet clicking. He risked a glance around the edge of the rock. It was there, no more than twenty feet away, its form a nightmarish silhouette against the churning grey sky. It was vaguely humanoid in its bipedal stance, but impossibly thin, its limbs too long and jointed at unnatural angles. Its head, eyeless and smooth, tilted from side to side, sampling the air with a pair of trembling, feathery antennae. It moved with a twitching, insectile grace, its clawed feet barely seeming to touch the ground.

The creature paused, its head swivelling directly towards his hiding place. Felix’s blood ran cold. He clutched the rowan-wood handle of his knife, his knuckles white. The creature took a hesitant step forward, its antennae vibrating furiously. It could sense something, an anomaly in the wind, a disruption in the familiar pattern of rain and rock. But the Gwrtaith held. The scent of iron and sacred herbs was not a smell, but a void, a patch of sensory nothingness that confused it. The Crebach emitted a low hiss of frustration, a sound like steam escaping a cracked pipe, then turned and continued its patrol, scrambling up the rock face with an eerie, weightless agility before disappearing over the crest. Felix remained motionless for a full five minutes, letting the adrenaline drain from him, leaving behind the cold, hard certainty of what he faced. They were patrolling. They were guarding their territory. He was getting close.

The trail grew stronger now, the chemical tang a constant presence on the air. It led him to a part of the fell he had never explored, a narrow, scree-choked valley hidden between two jagged tors. The very geology of the place felt wrong. The rock faces were not weathered and craggy in the normal way; they were unnaturally smooth in places, almost polished, with a faint, greasy sheen that shimmered in the gloom.
