The rumble was a familiar thing, a low and constant friend. It was the sound of the world, the Up-Top world, going about its business. To the humans of Denver, it was the RTD Light Rail, a metal serpent slithering on steel tracks. To Flinch, deep in his burrow under the old Baker neighborhood, it was the dinner bell. Not the bell that said supper was ready, but the one that vibrated through the packed earth and the scavenged brick of his walls, reminding him that the supper hour was approaching, a sacred time that demanded preparation and respect. His ears, long and pointed and sensitive to the slightest shift in barometric pressure or gossip, twitched in contentment.

Flinch was a goblin of particular tastes. While his brethren might be content to gnaw on a sewer rat pulled fresh from a grate or slurp down a handful of undifferentiated sludge, Flinch believed in the alchemy of the kitchen. His home, a dry and surprisingly spacious chamber carved out behind the foundation of a Victorian-era human dwelling, was a testament to this philosophy. It was less a goblin hovel and more a gourmand’s sanctuary. Gleaming on a shelf made from a warped piece of drywall were his tools: a spatula hammered from a flattened ‘YIELD’ sign, whisks fashioned from bicycle spokes, and his prize possession, a heavy-bottomed pot that had once been the hubcap of a 1970s Cadillac. The air, unlike the general damp-sock-and-mildew aroma of the wider tunnels, smelled of dried herbs, earthy mushrooms, and the faint, sharp tang of rust.

Tonight was special. Tonight was Rust-Mite Risotto night. He’d bartered a week ago with Skitterjaw, a notoriously fast runner who braved the grates of the 16th Street Mall, for three dozen of the plumpest, most succulent rust-mites. They weren’t the common pipe-dwelling variety, all spindly legs and acrid flavor. No, these were artisanal. They’d been harvested from the undercarriage of a forgotten sculpture in some forgotten park, feasting for decades on a diet of pure, unadulterated iron oxide and rainwater. Their bodies, when pan-seared, held a flavor that was both deeply mineralic and surprisingly sweet. A pop of umami that flooded the senses.

Flinch laid them out on a clean shard of porcelain, their tiny, reddish-brown bodies glistening under the light of a single, bioluminescent puffball mushroom he cultivated in a corner. He admired their uniformity, the subtle heft of them. This was quality. He ran a long, sharp-nailed finger over his ingredients, assembled like a surgeon’s instruments. A knob of sewer-butter, churned from the fatty deposits skimmed from a specific, ‘clean’ sewer junction. A handful of tunnel rice, a starchy, pale grain that grew only in the perpetual dark and damp. And the secret ingredient, a few drops of ‘surface dew,’ water collected from the underside of a specific manhole cover on a foggy morning, which carried the faint, ghostly flavors of asphalt and exhaust fumes. To an unrefined palate, it was poison. To Flinch, it was terroir.

He began. The hubcap pot went onto his heating element, a complex and highly dangerous contraption of coiled copper wire and stolen electrical cable that hummed with a life of its own. A dollop of sewer-butter melted with a hiss, its pungent aroma blooming in the warm air. Flinch worked with a focused grace, his lanky frame hunched over the pot. He toasted the tunnel rice, each grain turning translucent and fragrant. He added the surface dew, which sizzled and steamed, releasing that strange, urban perfume. The process was a meditation. A stir, a pause. A sniff, a nod. This was what mattered. The reduction of the world into a single, perfect bowl. The troubles of the Under-Denver, the petty squabbles and the endless turf wars over scavenging rights, they all melted away in the face of a well-executed supper.

A heavy scraping sound at his door-flap—a thick sheet of old conveyor belt—pulled him from his reverie. He sighed. There was only one goblin who announced his presence with the subtlety of a rockslide.
