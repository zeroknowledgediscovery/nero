Sarah McKinnon wiped the sawdust from her calloused hands, the familiar scent of pine and oak filling her nostrils as she surveyed the kitchen cabinet she'd just finished. The grain of the wood caught the afternoon light streaming through the workshop window, and she felt that quiet satisfaction that came with creating something beautiful and functional with her own two hands. At thirty-two, she'd been working wood for nearly fifteen years, ever since her father had first put a plane in her hands and shown her how to coax smooth surfaces from rough timber.

The workshop, tucked behind her modest terraced house on Magdalen Street, had become her sanctuary. Here, surrounded by the tools her grandfather had passed down and the newer equipment she'd slowly acquired over the years, Sarah could lose herself in the rhythmic motions of measuring, cutting, and shaping. Her neighbors in this working-class corner of Exeter knew her as the reliable woman who could fix their wobbly tables and craft bespoke furniture that would last generations.

But lately, something had been stirring within her, a restlessness that even the most challenging woodworking project couldn't quell. It had started three months ago when she'd wandered into Murphy's Music Shop on Fore Street, initially looking for a wooden music stand to repair for a client. The shop was cramped and cluttered, instruments hanging from every available surface, but what caught her attention was the acoustic guitar propped in the corner. Its mahogany body gleamed, the wood grain singing to her in a language she understood, yet the instrument itself remained a mystery.

Tom Murphy, the shop's elderly owner, had noticed her lingering gaze. "Beautiful piece of craftsmanship, that one," he'd said, his Irish accent still thick despite decades in Devon. "Made by a luthier in Valencia. The wood's telling a story, isn't it?"

Sarah had nodded, running her fingers along the guitar's smooth curves, recognizing the quality of the construction even if she couldn't play a single note. That evening, she'd found herself researching guitar-making techniques online, marveling at how similar yet different it was from her furniture work. The precision required, the way the wood's properties affected not just durability but tone and resonance—it fascinated her in a way she hadn't expected.

Two weeks later, she'd returned to Murphy's and, in a moment of uncharacteristic impulsiveness, had purchased the guitar along with a beginner's instruction book. Tom had thrown in a set of picks and wished her luck, his eyes twinkling with the kind of encouragement that only came from someone who understood the pull of music.

The first few weeks had been frustrating. Her fingers, so precise with chisel and saw, fumbled awkwardly on the frets. The calluses that served her so well in carpentry seemed to be in all the wrong places for guitar playing. She'd practice in the evenings after work, the thin walls of her terrace house making her acutely aware that Mrs. Patterson next door could probably hear every discordant chord and fumbled note.

Her breakthrough came during a particularly difficult evening when she'd been struggling with a simple G chord progression. Frustrated, she'd set the guitar aside and picked up a piece of cherry wood she'd been planning to use for a jewelry box. As she'd run her hands over its surface, feeling the grain and imagining how it would respond to different tools, she'd realized she was approaching the guitar all wrong. Wood was wood, whether it was becoming a cabinet or making music, and she needed to listen to what it was telling her.

That revelation had transformed her practice sessions. Instead of fighting the instrument, she began to work with it, feeling how her touch affected the strings' vibration, how the pressure of her fingers against the neck changed not just the notes but their quality. Progress came slowly but steadily, and within two months, she could play through several simple songs without too many mistakes.

The real test had come the previous Friday evening at the Red Lion, the local pub where she'd been meeting her friends for years. It was Quiz Night, and their regular team—Sarah, her best friend Emma, Emma's boyfriend Marcus, and a rotating cast of locals—had just finished a particularly triumphant victory when the conversation had turned, as it often did after a few pints, to dreams and aspirations.

"I still say you should start your own design company," Emma had said, gesturing with her half-empty glass of cider. "You're wasting your talent working for Harrison Construction. Those cookie-cutter kitchen fits are beneath you."

Emma Worthington had been Sarah's closest friend since primary school, despite their increasingly different life trajectories. Where Sarah had left school at sixteen to begin her carpentry apprenticeship, Emma had gone on to university, earned a business degree, and now worked for a marketing firm in London, commuting back to Exeter on weekends to maintain her connection to home. She was ambitious in a way that both impressed and sometimes exhausted Sarah, always full of schemes and plans for improvement and advancement.

"The overhead would be murder," Sarah had replied, a conversation they'd had countless times before. "Besides, I like working with my hands, not pushing papers around."

"But you could do both," Marcus had interjected. He was newer to their group, having started dating Emma only six months ago, but he'd integrated himself with the easy confidence that seemed to come naturally to investment bankers. "Scale up, hire other craftspeople, build a brand. It's all about leveraging your core competencies."

Sarah had suppressed a smile at his business-speak, recognizing the good intentions beneath the corporate jargon. But it was what happened next that had really surprised everyone, including herself.

"Actually," she'd heard herself saying, "I've been learning to play guitar."

The table had fallen silent, three pairs of eyes staring at her with expressions ranging from surprise to skepticism. Emma had recovered first, her face breaking into a delighted grin.

"Sarah McKinnon, you dark horse! When did this happen? Why didn't you tell me?"

"It's nothing serious," Sarah had said quickly, already regretting the admission. "Just something I picked up recently. Thought it might be... interesting."

"Can you play anything?" Marcus had asked, and there was something in his tone that made Sarah's defenses rise slightly.

"A few songs. Basic stuff."

"You should play something for us," Emma had said with characteristic enthusiasm. "There's that old piano in the back room, and I bet Murphy would loan you a guitar if you asked."

The suggestion had made Sarah's stomach clench. Playing alone in her workshop was one thing, but performing for others, even close friends, felt impossibly vulnerable. She'd deflected the suggestion with jokes and changing the subject, but the seed had been planted.

Now, three days later, as she cleaned up her workshop and prepared to call it a day, Sarah found herself thinking about that conversation again. Emma's excitement had been genuine, but Marcus's reaction had seemed more complicated—not dismissive exactly, but surprised in a way that suggested he found the idea incongruous. And maybe it was. A thirty-two-year-old carpenter taking up guitar hardly seemed like the stuff of musical legend.

The sound of footsteps on the gravel path outside interrupted her brooding. Through the window, she could see Emma approaching, her London work clothes looking slightly out of place in the workshop setting. Sarah opened the door before her friend could knock.

"Thought I might find you here," Emma said, giving Sarah a quick hug. "Fancy a cup of tea? I've got news."

They walked through to Sarah's small kitchen, where Emma immediately set about making tea with the comfortable familiarity of years of friendship. The house was modest but comfortable, filled with examples of Sarah's craftsmanship—custom shelving, a dining table she'd made from reclaimed oak, window seats with built-in storage. Emma had always said the house felt like a warm hug, every piece of furniture chosen or crafted with care and purpose.

"So," Emma said, settling into one of the chairs at the kitchen table, "I may have just made the most impulsive decision of my life."

Sarah raised an eyebrow. "Please tell me you didn't quit your job again."

"No, though give me time. This is bigger." Emma's eyes were bright with excitement and what might have been nervousness. "Remember how I was telling you about that client we've been working with, the one who owns the small record label?"

"Vaguely," Sarah said, though she had to admit she didn't always pay close attention to Emma's work stories, which tended to involve lots of acronyms and corporate drama.

"Well, he's been struggling financially. The music industry is brutal, especially for independent labels. He's been trying to sell for months, but the offers he's been getting are awful—basically asset stripping operations that would shut down operations and sell off the catalog."

Sarah nodded, starting to see where this might be heading.

"I've been watching how he operates, learning about the business, and honestly? I think he's been making some fundamental marketing mistakes. Good taste in music, terrible at promotion. So yesterday, completely out of the blue, I made him an offer."

"Emma, you didn't."

"I did. I bought Crossroads Records." Emma's grin was part triumph, part terror. "I'm now the proud owner of a small, struggling record label with a catalog of about two hundred songs, three artists currently under contract, and approximately six months of operating capital if I'm very careful."

Sarah stared at her friend, trying to process this information. "Are you completely mad?"

"Probably," Emma admitted cheerfully. "But think about it, Sarah. This could be incredible. I know marketing, I know business development, and I've got enough savings to keep us afloat while I figure out what I'm doing. Plus, the timing is perfect with all the streaming platforms and social media marketing opportunities."

"You don't know anything about music," Sarah pointed out.

"I know what I like. And I'm good at figuring out what other people like and how to connect them with it. Besides, that's what the artists are for, right? I just need to get their music to the right ears."

There was something infectious about Emma's enthusiasm, even as Sarah felt a familiar worry about her friend's tendency toward grand gestures. Emma had always been the dreamer of their pair, full of schemes and ambitions that sometimes worked brilliantly and sometimes crashed spectacularly. Sarah had learned to provide ballast, asking practical questions and offering support when the inevitable challenges arose.

"What does Marcus think about this?" Sarah asked.

Emma's expression flickered slightly. "He thinks I'm having some sort of quarter-life crisis. Says the music industry is a fool's game and I should stick to what I know."

"He might have a point about the industry being difficult."

"Maybe. But Sarah, you should see some of these artists. There's this singer-songwriter from Bristol, Lucy Chen, who has the most incredible voice. She's been playing coffee shops and small venues, but her songs are just... they get inside your head, you know? And there's a folk duo from Cornwall who write these haunting ballads about fishing villages and lost love. Real music, made by real people who have something to say."

Sarah could hear the passion in Emma's voice, and it stirred something in her own chest. "It sounds amazing, Em. Scary, but amazing."

"It is scary," Emma agreed. "I keep waking up in the middle of the night wondering what I've done. But also... I feel more excited about work than I have in years. Like I'm finally doing something that matters, you know?"

They sat in comfortable silence for a moment, both lost in their own thoughts. Outside, the evening light was fading, casting long shadows across Sarah's small garden. Finally, Emma spoke again.

"There's something else. Something I wanted to ask you."

"Oh?"

"You mentioned the other night that you've been learning guitar. I was thinking... would you maybe want to play something for me? Just the two of us, no pressure."

Sarah felt her chest tighten. "Em, I'm really not ready for that. I've only been playing for a couple of months."

"I know, but I'm curious. And after spending the last week listening to demo recordings and trying to understand what makes a song work, I'd love to hear what you're learning."

There was something in Emma's voice—not just curiosity, but a kind of professional interest that Sarah hadn't heard before. It occurred to her that Emma was now, technically, in the music business. The thought was both amusing and slightly intimidating.

"I suppose I could play something simple," Sarah said reluctantly. "But don't expect too much."

She retrieved the guitar from the living room, where it had taken up residence on a stand she'd crafted specifically for it. The instrument felt familiar in her hands now, no longer the alien object it had been three months ago. She settled back into her chair and took a moment to tune the strings, grateful for the small ritual that helped settle her nerves.

"This is 'Blackbird,'" she said finally. "I know it's probably cliché for a beginner, but the fingerpicking pattern is good practice."

She began to play, her fingers finding the melody line while her thumb kept the bass notes steady. It wasn't perfect—there were a few hesitations, a slight buzz on one of the frets—but it was recognizably the song, and there was something in her playing that went beyond mere technical execution. The years of working with her hands, of understanding how pressure and movement could coax different responses from material, translated into a touch that was both gentle and confident.

Emma listened in complete silence, her expression unreadable. When Sarah finished, she set the guitar aside and waited for her friend's reaction.

"Sarah," Emma said finally, "that was beautiful."

"It was sloppy in places."

"No, listen to me. I've spent the last week listening to people play music, and what you just did... there was something there. Something real."

Sarah felt a flush of pleasure mixed with embarrassment. "It's just a hobby, Em."

"Maybe it doesn't have to be."

The words hung in the air between them, loaded with possibility and implication. Sarah felt something shift in her chest, a stirring of ambition she'd never associated with music before.

"What do you mean?"

Emma leaned forward, her business mind clearly engaged. "I mean, I'm looking for artists. Real people with authentic voices and stories to tell. "I've been playing for three months," Sarah protested.

"I know. But there was something in how you approached that song, some quality in your touch that I haven't heard in the demos I've been listening to. Most of them are technically proficient but somehow... empty. What you just played had weight to it."

Sarah picked up the guitar again, running her fingers along the strings without pressing them to the frets, producing only whispers of sound. "I don't know, Em. It's a nice fantasy, but I'm a carpenter. I build things. I'm not a performer."

"Who says you have to be a performer? There are singer-songwriters who barely tour, who make their living from recordings and streaming. And besides, you do perform, in a way. Every piece of furniture you make is a kind of performance, isn't it? You're communicating something about beauty and function and craftsmanship."

The comparison hadn't occurred to Sarah before, but she could see Emma's point. Every dovetail joint she cut, every curve she shaped, was indeed a form of communication—with the wood, with the eventual user, with some broader conversation about what it meant to make something well.

"I wouldn't even know where to start," Sarah said.

"That's what I'm for," Emma replied with a grin. "I may be new to the music business, but I know how to develop talent and build careers. We could start small—get you some proper instruction, maybe find some other musicians to play with. See where it goes."

The offer was tempting and terrifying in equal measure. Sarah had spent her adult life building a reputation for reliability and competence in her trade. The idea of venturing into something completely new, something where she was vulnerable and uncertain, made her stomach flutter with anxiety.

"What would people think?" she asked.

"What people?"

"I don't know. Other carpenters. Clients. People who know me as... well, as what I am."

Emma's expression softened. "Sarah, you're allowed to be more than one thing. You're allowed to grow and change and try new things. The people who matter will support you."

"And the ones who don't?"

"Will reveal themselves to be not worth worrying about."

It was, Sarah reflected, a very Emma answer—confident, optimistic, and probably at least partially naive about how judgmental people could be. But there was also truth in it. She thought about her conversation with Tom Murphy when she'd first bought the guitar, how encouraging he'd been despite knowing nothing about her musical potential. She thought about the satisfaction she'd felt mastering that first chord progression, different from but somehow related to the satisfaction of completing a particularly challenging piece of joinery.

"I need to think about it," she said finally.

"Of course. Take all the time you need. I'm not going anywhere, and neither is Crossroads Records. Well, hopefully not, anyway."

Emma stayed for another hour, and they talked about everything except music—Emma's concerns about her new business, Sarah's current projects, mutual friends and local gossip. But underneath the familiar rhythm of their conversation, Sarah could feel something new taking shape, a possibility she'd never considered before beginning to crystallize into something that might, eventually, become real.

She ran her hands over the surface of her current project, a cherry wood bookshelf commissioned by a professor at the university. The wood was responding beautifully to her tools, the grain revealing itself in subtle patterns that would make each shelf unique while maintaining the overall harmony of the piece.

Music, she thought, might be something like that—individual notes and phrases that were beautiful on their own but gained meaning through their relationship to the whole. The guitar in her hands was itself a piece of craftsmanship, wood and metal and string combined to create something that was greater than the sum of its parts.

She thought about Emma's observation that her playing had felt honest. It was an interesting word choice, one that resonated with Sarah's approach to her carpentry work. She'd never been interested in flashy techniques or trendy designs that sacrificed function for appearance.
