The morning sun cast long shadows across the eighteenth green at Millbrook Country Club when the sky first began to shimmer. Marcus Thornfield adjusted his grip on the putter, oblivious to the celestial disturbance brewing overhead. He'd been chasing par for three hours, and his handicap seemed to mock him with every swing. The tournament meant everything – sponsors, endorsements, a shot at the professional circuit he'd dreamed about since childhood.

"Focus, Marcus," whispered his caddie, a weathered man named Pete who'd seen more golf courses than most people see sunrises. "Just sink this one and you're in the lead."

But Marcus couldn't concentrate. Something felt different about the world today, though he couldn't pinpoint exactly what. The air seemed thicker somehow, charged with an energy that made his skin prickle. Even the other players appeared restless, glancing skyward between shots with expressions of barely contained unease.

The putt rolled wide.

"Dammit!" Marcus slammed his club against the pristine grass, leaving a small divot that the groundskeeper would surely notice. His playing partner, a silver-haired veteran named Richardson, shot him a disapproving look.

"Language, son. There are ladies present."

Marcus glanced toward the clubhouse where indeed several women in pastel cardigans watched from the terrace, their afternoon cocktails forgotten as they stared not at the golfers but at something beyond them. Following their gaze, Marcus finally looked up.

The sky was wrong. Where blue should have been, ribbons of gold and silver light danced like aurora borealis in broad daylight. The phenomenon stretched across the entire horizon, pulsing with a rhythm that seemed almost musical. Other golfers had abandoned their games entirely, standing transfixed by the supernatural display.

"Pete, you seeing this?"

His caddie nodded slowly, his usual composure cracking. "Forty years I've been on these courses, never seen nothing like it."

The light show intensified, and suddenly the temperature dropped by at least twenty degrees. Marcus's breath came out in visible puffs as he reached for his jacket. Around them, the carefully manicured landscape began to frost over despite the season being late spring.

Then came the screaming.

It started from the direction of the parking lot, a sound so filled with terror that every person on the golf course turned as one. Through the trees, Marcus could see figures running, stumbling, fleeing from something he couldn't yet identify. The screams multiplied, echoing across the fairways like a chorus of the damned.

"We need to get inside," Richardson said, his earlier pomposity replaced by genuine fear. He was already moving toward the clubhouse, his golf bag abandoned on the green.

But Marcus found himself walking toward the commotion instead. Something compelled him forward, a curiosity stronger than common sense. Pete grabbed his arm.

"Don't be stupid, kid. Whatever's happening over there, we don't want any part of it."

Marcus shook him off. "Someone might need help."

He jogged through the rough, dodging sand traps and water hazards, following the sounds of chaos. As he crested a small hill, the parking lot came into view, and what he saw there defied every rational thought he'd ever held.

Bodies littered the asphalt, but they weren't corpses. They were transforming. Pale figures writhed on the ground, their backs arching as enormous wings erupted from their shoulder blades. Wings that shimmered with the same golden light that painted the sky. Their faces, twisted in what Marcus had initially mistaken for agony, now showed expressions of rapture, of transcendence.

"Holy shit," he breathed.

"Indeed it is."

Marcus spun around to find a woman standing behind him, though he was certain she hadn't been there moments before. She was tall and elegant, with platinum hair and eyes the color of winter storms. Most remarkably, she had wings – not the feathered variety he might have expected, but structures of pure light that seemed to exist just slightly outside normal reality.

"Who are you?" he managed.

"I am what your friend there just became," she said, gesturing toward the parking lot where more figures were sprouting luminous appendages. "Though I completed my transformation some time ago. My name is Seraphina, and I bring news that will change everything you understand about existence."

Marcus backed away instinctively. The rational part of his mind insisted he was having some kind of breakdown, probably from the pressure of the tournament. People didn't grow wings. Angels didn't exist outside of Sunday school stories and Renaissance paintings.

"This isn't real," he said.

Seraphina smiled with genuine compassion. "Reality has just expanded, Marcus Thornfield. What you're witnessing is the Great Awakening. Every vampire on Earth is being transformed into what you might call an angel, though we prefer the term 'Elevated.' The age of predator and prey is ending. A new era of enlightenment begins today."

"Vampires?" Marcus laughed, a sound edged with hysteria. "Lady, I don't know what kind of medication you're on, but vampires aren't real. This is all some kind of mass hallucination or gas leak or something."

"Is it?" She gestured again, and Marcus followed her indication to see Pete approaching from the golf course. His caddie's weathered face had taken on an otherworldly pallor, and gossamer wings were slowly unfurling from his back.

"Marcus," Pete called out, his voice carrying new harmonics that seemed to resonate in the air itself. "I understand now. We've been living in shadow, all of us. But the shadow is lifting."

"Pete, what the hell is happening to you?"

His caddie – former caddie, apparently – touched down gently beside them, his new wings allowing for graceful flight. "I've been Changed, son. All of us who fed in darkness have been brought into the light. No more hunting, no more hiding. We're something better now."

Marcus felt the world tilt beneath his feet. Pete had been a vampire? Pete, who complained about his arthritis and told stories about his grandchildren?"How long?" Marcus asked, his voice barely above a whisper.

Pete's expression softened with something that might have been regret. "Since 1987. I was seventy-three when it happened, figured I was dying anyway. Some young vampire took pity on an old man, gave me a choice between the grave and eternity. Seemed like a good deal at the time." He flexed his luminous wings experimentally, as if testing their functionality. "Spent thirty-six years learning to control the hunger, finding ways to feed without killing. Golf courses were perfect hunting grounds, actually. Lots of wealthy folks with more blood than sense, easy to take just enough and make them think they'd had too much scotch at the nineteenth hole."

The revelation hit Marcus like a physical blow. All those times Pete had insisted on carrying the golf bags himself, staying close to the players, his uncanny ability to read people's moods and weaknesses on the course. Marcus had attributed it to experience, to decades of observing human nature. He'd never questioned why Pete seemed so energetic for his supposed age, or why he always wore long sleeves even on the hottest days.

Seraphina moved closer, her presence somehow both comforting and terrifying. "The transformation is not random, Marcus. It affects only those who had been turned, who lived with the curse of vampirism. For centuries, they existed as parasites, necessary evils in the grand design. But evolution has come at last. The universe has decided that predation is no longer required. We are being elevated to our true purpose."

"Which is what, exactly?" Marcus found himself asking, though part of him didn't want to know the answer.

"Guardianship. Protection. The same beings who once hunted humanity are now meant to shepherd it. We retain our enhanced abilities, our longevity, our strength, but the bloodlust has been replaced with something far more powerful – compassion coupled with the ability to act upon it." Seraphina's wings shimmered as she spoke, casting rainbow patterns across the ground. "We are becoming what humans have always imagined angels to be."

Around them, more figures were taking flight. Marcus watched in fascination and horror as people he'd never suspected of being anything other than human revealed their transformed nature. The elderly woman who ran the pro shop soared overhead, her sensible cardigan fluttering as gossamer wings carried her toward the clubhouse. A groundskeeper Marcus had seen every week for two years landed gracefully on the fairway, his work clothes somehow seeming more dignified now that he bore wings of silver light.

"How many of you are there?" Marcus asked.

Pete answered, his voice carrying that new musical quality that seemed to harmonize with itself. "More than you'd think, fewer than the movies would have you believe. We lived quietly, most of us. Found ways to coexist without drawing attention. The smart ones learned to take just enough to survive without leaving a trail of bodies. Some worked in hospitals, taking small amounts from blood banks. Others found willing donors among the lonely, the desperate, people who craved any kind of connection even if they couldn't quite remember it afterward."

"And now you're all just... changing? Everywhere on Earth?"

Seraphina nodded. "Simultaneously. The signal came at dawn in Jerusalem, propagated instantly across all time zones. Every vampire, regardless of age or strength or moral standing, is experiencing the same transformation. Those who fought against their nature for centuries are finding peace at last. Those who embraced darkness are being forced into the light whether they wish it or not."

The implications began to sink in. If Pete had been a vampire, how many others had been living double lives? Marcus thought about his regular foursome – Richardson with his old-money connections, always seemed to know everyone at every course they played. Jennifer Walsh from the women's league, who never seemed to age despite claiming to be in her sixties. The mysterious benefactor who'd sponsored several junior tournaments, always made his donations at night, never attended the award ceremonies.

"The tournament," Marcus said suddenly, looking back toward the eighteenth green where his abandoned putter still lay beside the hole. "What happens to normal life now? Do we just pretend this never happened? Go back to work on Monday like the entire supernatural world didn't just reveal itself?"

Pete chuckled, a sound that now carried harmonic overtones that seemed to resonate in Marcus's chest. "Son, there's no going back to normal after this. The world just learned that vampires were real and now they're turning into angels. Every government, every military, every religious organization on the planet is about to lose their collective minds. We're looking at the biggest paradigm shift in human history."

As if to emphasize his point, the sound of sirens began echoing across the landscape. Emergency vehicles, Marcus realized, responding to reports that probably sounded like mass hysteria. He tried to imagine the 911 calls: "Yes, hello, I'd like to report that my neighbor is growing wings in his backyard." The authorities would think it was some kind of coordinated prank or terrorist attack until they saw the evidence for themselves.

Seraphina was watching him with those storm-colored eyes, and Marcus had the uncomfortable feeling she was reading his thoughts. "You're wondering what this means for you, aren't you? Whether your life will ever make sense again."

He nodded, not trusting his voice.

"The answer is both yes and no. Your old life, the one where vampires were fiction and the biggest concern was sinking a putt, that's over. But a new life is beginning, one where you'll play a crucial role in helping humanity adjust to this new reality. Some humans will be chosen as liaisons, bridges between the Elevated and those who remain unchanged. You, Marcus Thornfield, have just become very important indeed."

"What do you mean, chosen? I didn't volunteer for anything."

Pete moved closer, his presence now carrying an otherworldly calm that seemed to seep into Marcus's bones despite his rising panic. "The universe doesn't always ask permission before it changes your destiny, kid. Trust me on that one. But it rarely makes mistakes about who can handle the responsibility.""I don't want responsibility," Marcus said, backing away from both of them. The words came out louder than he intended, echoing across the golf course where other transformed beings were still discovering their new abilities. Several of them turned to look at him with expressions of serene understanding that made his skin crawl. "I just want to play golf. I want to make the professional circuit and pay off my student loans and maybe buy a decent car that doesn't break down every other month. I don't want to be part of some cosmic plan or whatever this is."

Seraphina's expression didn't change, but something in her posture suggested she'd heard similar protests before. The way she folded her luminous wings behind her was almost casual, as if she'd been doing it for decades rather than hours. "The universe rarely consults our preferences when it comes to destiny, Marcus. I once owned a small bakery in Prague. My greatest concern was whether the sourdough would rise properly each morning. Then I was turned, and for ninety years I struggled with an existence I never asked for. Now I've been transformed again, given a purpose I'm still learning to understand. The pattern of our lives is rarely the pattern we would choose for ourselves."

The sirens were getting closer now, and Marcus could hear helicopters in the distance. The media would be next, he realized. News crews and government officials and probably military personnel, all converging on what would undoubtedly be the biggest story in human history. The golf course would become a circus, and he'd be trapped in the middle of it simply because he'd been in the wrong place at the wrong time. The tournament he'd worked so hard to qualify for, the sponsors who might have noticed his performance, the carefully constructed plans for his future – all of it seemed impossibly trivial now.

Pete was watching him with those enhanced senses that Marcus was beginning to understand had always been there, hidden behind the facade of an ordinary caddie. "I know it's overwhelming, son. Believe me, I remember what it was like to have my worldview shattered overnight. But running away isn't going to change what's happened. The world has shifted, and we all have to figure out how to live in it now."

"Why me, though? Why am I supposedly important? I'm nobody special. I'm a mediocre golfer with a business degree who can barely make rent most months. There have to be better candidates for whatever cosmic liaison position you're talking about." Marcus gestured toward the clubhouse where he could see people on their phones, probably trying to call loved ones or emergency services or anyone who might be able to explain what they'd witnessed. "What about Richardson? He's got connections, influence, money. Or Dr. Morrison from the club board – she's brilliant, speaks six languages, has degrees from Harvard and Oxford."

Seraphina moved with that fluid grace that seemed to be common among the transformed, positioning herself so that Marcus would have to look at her when he spoke. Her presence carried a weight that had nothing to do with physical mass, as if she existed more fully in reality than ordinary humans did. "Intelligence and influence are valuable, but they're not the primary qualifications for what's coming. The bridge between human and Elevated needs to be someone who can be trusted by both sides, someone whose motivations are transparent and whose reactions are genuine. You've spent the last few minutes arguing against your own importance while showing concern for people you barely know. That combination of humility and empathy is rarer than you might think."

From the parking lot, the sounds of confusion were growing louder. Marcus could hear car engines starting and stalling, people shouting questions at each other, the general chaos that accompanies any situation where the fundamental rules of reality have suddenly changed. Some of the newly transformed were trying to comfort confused humans, their enhanced abilities allowing them to project calm and reassurance. Others seemed as bewildered by their new nature as the unchanged observers were.

A black sedan with government plates pulled into the parking lot, followed by what looked like unmarked FBI vehicles. Marcus watched as men and women in dark suits emerged, their hands near their weapons despite the obvious futility of conventional arms against beings who could fly. The lead agent, a woman with graying hair and the bearing of someone accustomed to command, surveyed the scene with the kind of professional composure that comes from years of dealing with situations that don't make sense.

"Agent Sarah Chen," Seraphina said, apparently recognizing the woman. "Department of Extranormal Events. I'm not surprised they responded so quickly. They've been tracking our kind for decades, though they never understood what they were really dealing with."

"There's a Department of Extranormal Events?" Marcus asked, then immediately felt stupid for being surprised. Of course there would be government agencies dedicated to investigating supernatural phenomena, especially if vampires had been real all along. The world he'd thought he understood was revealing itself to be far more complex and dangerous than he'd ever imagined.

Pete nodded toward the agents who were now setting up some kind of perimeter around the transformed beings. "Black budget stuff, mostly. They investigate reports of unusual activity, cover up incidents that might cause public panic, occasionally try to make contact with non-human entities. Sarah's actually one of the good ones – more interested in understanding than containing. We've had dealings with her before."

"We?" Marcus turned to stare at his former caddie. "You've been in contact with government agents? For how long? What else haven't you told me?"

The older man's expression carried a mixture of regret and something that might have been relief. The weight of decades of secrecy was apparently lifting along with his transformation, allowing him to speak freely for the first time in years. "The golf course network was useful for more than just feeding, Marcus. We moved information, provided safe passage for vampires traveling between territories, helped maintain the balance that kept our existence hidden from mainstream humanity. Some of us worked as informal consultants for government agencies that needed to understand supernatural threats."

Marcus felt another piece of his understanding of the world crumble away. The golf courses he'd played on for years, the familiar environment where he'd felt most at home, had apparently been part of some vast supernatural infrastructure he'd never suspected existed. "So what, you were all spies or something? The entire Professional Golfers Association is actually a front for vampire intelligence operations?"

"Not all of it," Pete said with a slight smile that revealed teeth that were noticeably sharper than they'd been an hour ago. "Most golfers are exactly what they appear to be – humans obsessed with hitting a small ball into a distant hole. But the sport provided perfect cover for those of us who needed to travel frequently, interact with wealthy and powerful people, and maintain a respectable public facade. Country clubs are ideal hunting grounds and meeting places. Private, exclusive, full of people who value discretion and don't ask too many questions about their companions' personal lives."

Agent Chen was approaching now, her team maintaining a respectful distance while she walked toward the small group on the hill. Her expression was professionally neutral, but Marcus could see the tension in her shoulders, the way her eyes tracked the movement of wings and the play of supernatural light across the landscape. This was clearly a scenario she'd prepared for but never quite believed would actually occur.

"Seraphina," she called out when she was close enough to be heard without shouting. "I assume you're coordinating the local response to whatever's happening here."

"Hello, Sarah. Yes, I'm helping newly transformed individuals understand their situation and assisting unchanged humans in processing what they've witnessed." Seraphina's tone was cordial but carried an undercurrent of authority that suggested their previous interactions had established a working relationship. "This is Marcus Thornfield, whom I believe will be serving as a primary liaison between your people and ours in the coming weeks."

Agent Chen turned to study Marcus with the kind of professional assessment that made him feel like a specimen under a microscope. Her eyes were intelligent and calculating, taking in his golf attire, his obvious confusion, the way he kept glancing between the supernatural beings beside him and the chaos in the parking lot below. "Mr. Thornfield. Can you tell me what you observed here today? When did the transformations begin?"

Marcus found himself explaining the morning's events, starting with the strange quality of the light during his final putts and progressing through his first sight of the changing figures in the parking lot. Agent Chen listened without interrupting, occasionally making notes on a device that looked more sophisticated than any smartphone he'd ever seen. Her questions were precise and professional, focusing on timelines and specific details rather than his emotional reactions to the impossible things he'd witnessed.

"The simultaneous global nature of the event suggests coordination beyond anything we've previously encountered," she said when he finished his account. "Our field teams are reporting similar transformations at vampire gathering places worldwide. London, Tokyo, São Paulo, Cairo – everywhere our intelligence indicated significant supernatural populations, we're seeing the same phenomenon."

"So you knew about them," Marcus said. It wasn't really a question. "The government has known vampires were real this whole time."

Agent Chen's expression didn't change, but something in her posture acknowledged the accusation inherent in his statement. "Certain agencies have been aware of non-human entities for approximately seventy years. The knowledge was classified at the highest levels and shared only with personnel who had demonstrated both exceptional competence and psychological stability. The existence of vampires, werewolves, and various other supernatural beings has been considered a matter of national security, with disclosure limited to those with an absolute need to know."

"And now?" Seraphina asked, her wings catching the afternoon light in a way that created brief rainbow patterns on the ground around her. "The secret is rather dramatically out, I'm afraid. How does your government plan to handle the revelation that everything humanity thought it knew about the nature of reality was incomplete?"

The agent was quiet for a moment, her gaze moving across the landscape where more transformed beings were testing their abilities while confused humans tried to process what they were seeing. Several news helicopters were visible on the horizon, and Marcus could see satellite trucks beginning to gather on the roads leading to the country club. "Honestly, we're in uncharted territory. The protocols we developed assumed gradual disclosure under controlled circumstances, not a simultaneous global event that makes concealment impossible. The President is being briefed as we speak, and emergency sessions are being called at the UN Security Council and NATO headquarters."

Pete flexed his wings experimentally, achieving a brief hover before settling back to the ground. The movement was graceful despite his obvious unfamiliarity with the new appendages, suggesting that whatever force had transformed him had also provided instinctive knowledge of how to use his enhanced abilities. "The political implications are going to be staggering. Every government on Earth is going to have to acknowledge that supernatural beings have been living among their citizens for centuries. Treaties will need to be renegotiated, laws rewritten, entire legal frameworks reconceptualized to account for non-human persons with enhanced abilities."

Marcus felt overwhelmed by the scope of what they were discussing. Hours ago, his biggest concern had been whether he could afford new golf cleats before the next tournament. Now he was listening to conversations about international law and global political restructuring as if they were routine topics. The transformation of vampires into angels was apparently just the beginning of changes that would ripple through every aspect of human civilization.**Chapter 2: The New Normal**

Three weeks had passed since what the media had dubbed "Ascension Day," though Marcus was beginning to suspect that nothing about his life would ever qualify as normal again. The Millbrook Country Club had been transformed from an exclusive golf retreat into what amounted to an international diplomatic facility, complete with security checkpoints, media staging areas, and a temporary command center that the Department of Extranormal Events had erected in what used to be the pro shop. The eighteenth green where Marcus had been putting when the world changed was now cordoned off with yellow tape, not because it was a crime scene, but because it had become something like a pilgrimage site for people trying to understand what had happened to their reality.

Marcus sat in his apartment, nursing his third cup of coffee and staring at the stack of documents Agent Chen had delivered the previous evening. Official forms with seals and signatures he didn't recognize, all bearing his name and identifying him as a "Class One Liaison" between human and Elevated populations. The title came with a government salary that was roughly four times what he'd been making as a struggling professional golfer, along with benefits that included medical coverage for "supernatural exposure incidents" and life insurance policies that contained clauses he was afraid to read too carefully. The job description was simultaneously vague and terrifying: facilitate communication, provide cultural interpretation, assist in the development of integration protocols, and serve as a trusted intermediary during the "adjustment period" that experts were estimating might last anywhere from months to decades.

The apartment itself had become a strange hub of activity that Marcus still couldn't quite believe was his life. Pete stopped by most evenings, still adjusting to his transformation but adapting with the pragmatic acceptance of someone who had already lived through one supernatural transition. The former caddie had retained his weathered appearance and dry sense of humor, though both were now enhanced by an otherworldly presence that made even mundane conversations feel weighted with significance. Seraphina visited less frequently but with greater impact, her appearances usually coinciding with developments that required Marcus to make decisions he felt entirely unqualified to handle. Other Elevated individuals had begun treating his apartment as a sort of informal embassy, stopping by to discuss everything from practical concerns about their new abilities to philosophical questions about their purpose in a world that was still coming to terms with their existence.

The transformation itself continued to fascinate and disturb Marcus in equal measure. The Elevated retained their personalities and memories from their vampire years, but the fundamental nature of their existence had shifted in ways that were still being catalogued by government scientists and academic researchers. The bloodlust that had defined their previous existence had been replaced by what Seraphina described as "purpose resonance" – an instinctive drive to protect and guide humanity that operated below the level of conscious thought. They no longer needed to feed on blood, their sustenance now derived from what appeared to be ambient energy that was somehow connected to their protective activities. The more good they accomplished, the stronger and more capable they became, creating a feedback loop that encouraged benevolent behavior in beings who had once been predators.

Marcus had witnessed this phenomenon firsthand during an incident at downtown hospital two days after Ascension Day. A multi-car accident had overwhelmed the emergency room, and several Elevated individuals had arrived uninvited to assist with triage and treatment. They seemed to intuitively understand which patients were most critically injured, could work for hours without fatigue, and demonstrated an uncanny ability to calm panicked family members and medical staff. The hospital administration had initially been resistant to accepting help from beings they didn't understand, but the results were undeniable. Patient survival rates improved dramatically, wait times decreased, and the overall atmosphere in the emergency department shifted from barely controlled chaos to something approaching serene efficiency.

Similar reports were flooding in from around the globe. Elevated individuals were appearing at disaster sites, crime scenes, and anywhere human suffering was concentrated, offering assistance that was both remarkably effective and completely voluntary. They asked for no payment, sought no recognition, and seemed to possess an intuitive understanding of exactly what kind of help was needed in each situation. Governments and aid organizations were struggling to develop protocols for coordinating with these supernatural volunteers while also trying to maintain some semblance of institutional control over emergency response operations.

The public reaction had been as varied and complex as Marcus had expected. Religious groups were split between those who saw the transformations as evidence of divine intervention and those who proclaimed it the work of demonic forces seeking to deceive humanity. Scientists were frantically attempting to study the Elevated while also grappling with the revelation that their understanding of biology, physics, and evolutionary theory required fundamental revision. Military leaders were simultaneously relieved that former vampires were no longer potential security threats and concerned about how to protect populations from supernatural beings whose capabilities far exceeded conventional defensive measures.

Marcus's phone rang, interrupting his contemplation of a document titled "Preliminary Guidelines for Civilian-Elevated Interaction Protocols." The caller ID showed Agent Chen's number, which had become as familiar as his mother's over the past few weeks. Sarah Chen had essentially become his supervisor, though the exact nature of their working relationship remained somewhat unclear to both of them. The Department of Extranormal Events was itself adapting to circumstances it had never anticipated, expanding from a small classified operation into something approaching a new branch of government.

"Good morning, Sarah," Marcus answered, already reaching for his jacket. Experience had taught him that calls from Agent Chen usually resulted in immediate travel to locations where something unprecedented was occurring.

"We have a situation in downtown that requires your attention," she said without preamble. Agent Chen had never been one for small talk, but the events of recent weeks had made her even more direct in her communications. "Three Elevated individuals have taken up residence at the old Cathedral of Saint Mary, and the Archbishop is requesting immediate intervention. Apparently, they're performing what he describes as 'unauthorized miracles,' and the crowds gathering outside are becoming unmanageable."

Marcus sighed, already envisioning the complications that would inevitably arise from the intersection of supernatural beings and organized religion. The Catholic Church's official position on the Elevated remained deliberately ambiguous, with Vatican statements referring to them as "a new manifestation of divine will that requires careful theological study." Individual clergy members had expressed opinions ranging from complete acceptance to vehement condemnation, creating confusion among congregations worldwide. Agent Chen's tone carried the weary patience of someone who had been dealing with impossible situations for weeks and was beginning to develop a professional acceptance of the impossible. Marcus grabbed his keys and headed for the door, already mentally preparing for another day of navigating between competing interests that had no historical precedent for cooperation. The drive downtown had become routine, though the city itself continued to show signs of the massive changes rippling through society. News vans were permanently stationed at locations where Elevated individuals were known to gather. Emergency services had developed new protocols for coordinating with supernatural volunteers. Several businesses had begun advertising themselves as "Elevated-friendly," though exactly what that meant was still being determined through trial and error.

Others appeared to be protesters, carrying placards denouncing the Elevated as false prophets or demons masquerading as angels. Police barriers had been erected to maintain some semblance of order, but the crowd was largely self-regulating in a way that surprised Marcus. People waited patiently in lines that had formed organically, sharing food and water with strangers, offering comfort to individuals who were obviously suffering from serious illnesses. Several other individuals in religious garb stood nearby, their faces showing mixtures of wonder, concern, and professional uncertainty as they observed whatever was happening in the main sanctuary.

Three Elevated individuals arrived here four days ago and requested permission to offer healing services to anyone who sought them. Archbishop Morrison initially agreed, thinking it would be a small, private ministry. Instead, word spread through social media and religious networks, and we're now dealing with thousands of people traveling here from across the country."

"Mr. Thornfield, I've heard excellent reports about your work facilitating cooperation between our communities. I hope you can help us find a solution that honors both the genuine good being accomplished here and the need for proper order in sacred spaces."

"They've identified themselves as Michael, Gabriel, and Raphael, though whether those are their original names or adopted designations is unclear. They claim to be following what they describe as 'resonant imperatives' that guide them toward locations where they can alleviate suffering.
