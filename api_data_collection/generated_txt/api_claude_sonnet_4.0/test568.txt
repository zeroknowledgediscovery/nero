The morning sun cast long shadows across the gleaming towers of New Matriarca, where glass and steel reached toward a sky that had forgotten the old world. Marcus Thorne adjusted his burgundy tie—regulation color for male banking executives—and stared out the window of his corner office on the forty-seventh floor. Below, the streets hummed with the ordered chaos of a society rebuilt from the ashes of patriarchal collapse.

It had been thirty-seven years since the Great Reversal, when women across the globe had simultaneously seized power in what historians now called the most peaceful revolution in human history. No blood had been spilled, no governments had fallen violently. Instead, women had simply... taken over. Corporate boardrooms, legislative chambers, military commands—overnight, the world had transformed into something unrecognizable to Marcus's generation.

He touched the small silver bracelet around his wrist, the one that monitored his productivity, his emotional state, his compliance with the New Order. Every man wore one. Every man had to.

"Mr. Thorne?" The voice belonged to his assistant, James, whose nervous disposition had only worsened since their department had been placed under review by the Ministry of Financial Harmony. "Director Castellano is here to see you."

Marcus turned from the window, straightening his shoulders. Director Elena Castellano was a woman who commanded respect through presence alone—tall, silver-haired, with eyes that seemed to calculate the worth of everything they surveyed. She entered his office wearing the deep purple robes that marked her as a high-ranking official in the new government.

"Marcus." She didn't offer her hand, didn't smile. "We need to discuss your sister."

The words hit him like a physical blow. Marcus felt his knees weaken slightly, though he remained standing. "Luna? What about Luna?"

"She's been classified as a Level Seven Creative Anomaly," Director Castellano said, settling into the chair across from his desk without invitation. "Her songs... they're having an unprecedented effect on the population. Both male and female citizens are reporting emotional instabilities after listening to her music. The Council of Maternal Wisdom considers her a threat to social order."

Marcus knew his sister possessed something extraordinary. Luna had always been different—even as a child, her voice could make people weep or laugh with an intensity that seemed almost supernatural. In the old world, she might have become a celebrated artist. In this new one, her gift had marked her for elimination.

"What do you want from me?" Marcus asked, though he suspected he already knew.

Director Castellano reached into her robes and withdrew a small crystalline device that pulsed with an otherworldly blue light. "This is a Harmonic Synthesizer. It can absorb and replicate the unique frequency patterns that make your sister's voice so... influential. But it requires a genetic link to function properly."

"You want me to betray my own sister."

"I want you to save the world from chaos," she corrected. "Luna's influence is spreading beyond our borders. Other nations are beginning to question the stability of our system. If her music continues to propagate, it could undo everything we've built."

Marcus stared at the device, its light seeming to pulse in rhythm with his heartbeat. "And if I refuse?"

"Then both of you will be terminated, and we'll find another way." Director Castellano's voice carried no malice, only the cold efficiency of necessity. "This way, at least, part of her lives on. Her voice, her essence—it will exist forever in the digital realm, where it can be properly regulated and controlled."

The weight of the choice pressed down on Marcus like a physical thing. Outside his window, the city continued its daily rhythm, unaware that its fate might hinge on a decision made in a banker's office high above the streets.

"How does it work?" he heard himself asking.

"The process is... intimate. You must consume a part of her while she sings. The device will record not just the sound, but the biological resonance that makes her voice unique. It's the only way to create a perfect replica."

Marcus's stomach turned. "Consume?"

"A small portion of tissue. A finger, perhaps. Something with sufficient genetic material." Director Castellano's clinical tone made the horror of it seem almost reasonable. "She won't survive the process, but her death will be quick. Merciful."

The silence stretched between them like a chasm. Marcus found himself thinking about their childhood, before the Great Reversal, when he and Luna would sneak onto the roof of their apartment building and she would sing to the stars. Her voice had always seemed to reach something beyond the ordinary world, as if she were calling to forces that existed in the spaces between notes.

After she left, Marcus sat alone in his office, staring at the device. Its blue light continued to pulse, hypnotic and alien. He thought about the world outside—ordered, peaceful, but built on the suppression of voices like Luna's. He thought about his sister, probably composing another song in her small apartment across the city, unaware that her brother held her life in his hands.

The bracelet around his wrist grew warm, responding to his elevated stress levels. Somewhere in the Ministry's monitoring station, an algorithm was noting his distress, filing it away in a database that would determine his future value to society.

Marcus reached for his phone to call Luna, then stopped. What could he possibly say? How could he explain that loving her might require destroying her?

Instead, he walked to his window again and looked out at New Matriarca. In the distance, he could see the great amphitheater where public performances were held under strict government supervision.
