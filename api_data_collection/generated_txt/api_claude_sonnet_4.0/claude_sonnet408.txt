She stood before her full-length mirror, one hand pressed against her stomach to calm the butterflies that had taken residence there since receiving the phone call three days ago. The phone call that changed everything.

"You've made it to the finals, Sarah," the competition director had said, her voice crackling through the old landline. "The Northern England Dance Championship finals. Ten years you've been trying, and finally, here you are."

Ten years. A decade of early morning practices, blistered feet, sequined costumes that cost more than her monthly grocery budget, and dreams that felt simultaneously within reach and impossibly distant. At thirty-four, Sarah Henley was considered past her prime in the competitive dancing world, but she refused to let age define her limitations. Her auburn hair, though now requiring regular touch-ups to hide the emerging silver threads, still caught the light beautifully when she spun. Her green eyes still sparkled with the same determination that had driven her to her first dance lesson at age seven.

The irony wasn't lost on her that she lived above Henderson's Bakery, where the sweet aroma of fresh bread and pastries provided a constant temptation to someone who needed to maintain the lithe figure of a dancer. Mrs. Henderson, the elderly proprietor, had become something of a surrogate mother to Sarah after her own parents passed away in a car accident five years earlier. The old woman would often leave a small bag of day-old croissants outside Sarah's door with a note that read, "Dancers need fuel too, love."

Sarah's reflection stared back at her, revealing the worry lines that had deepened around her eyes over the past seventy-two hours. The competition was in four days. Four days to prepare for the performance that could potentially change her life, open doors to teaching positions at prestigious dance academies, maybe even lead to choreography work with touring companies. But there was one rather significant problem that threatened to derail everything she had worked toward.

Marcus was missing.

Her ex-husband had vanished without a trace on Tuesday morning, leaving behind only a hastily scrawled note on their kitchen table that read, "I'm sorry, Sarah. I can't do this anymore. Don't look for me." The police had been less than helpful, explaining that adults had the right to disappear if they chose to do so, and that unless there were signs of foul play or mental instability, there was little they could do beyond filing a missing person report.

The cruel twist of fate was that Marcus held the key to Sarah's participation in the competition, quite literally. In their divorce settlement two years ago, he had been awarded their joint safety deposit box, which contained, among other things, Sarah's grandmother's diamond earrings. These weren't just any earrings; they were her lucky charms, worn at every significant dance performance since she was sixteen. More importantly, they were worth enough money to cover her competition fees, costume costs, and travel expenses to Manchester, where the finals would be held.

Sarah had been counting on Marcus to cooperate, to meet her at the bank and retrieve the earrings so she could sell them. They had arranged to meet on Tuesday afternoon. Instead, she had arrived at his flat to find the door unlocked and that cryptic note waiting for her.

She turned away from the mirror and walked to her small kitchen, where a cup of tea had grown cold while she wrestled with her thoughts. The flat was modest but cozy, decorated with photographs from various dance competitions and performances. Ribbons and small trophies adorned a shelf above her television, creating a timeline of her dancing journey. Each award represented hours of practice, moments of triumph, and yes, instances of disappointment when she fell short of her goals.

"Any word from the missing husband?" Jamie asked without preamble. He had a talent for cutting straight to the heart of matters, a quality that made him an excellent dance partner but sometimes a challenging friend.

"Jamie, I don't know if there's any point. Without those earrings, I can't afford the competition fees, let alone travel and accommodation."
