The salt-tinged breeze carried the scent of sawdust and roses through the open workshop windows as Kassandra Davies ran her weathered hands along the grain of English oak. Each plank told its own story—rings marking seasons of drought and abundance, knots where branches once reached toward sunlight. She understood wood the way others understood people, reading its moods and weaknesses with an intuition inherited from her grandfather.

Southampton's harbor district had changed dramatically since her childhood, but her workshop remained a constant, tucked between modernized flats and trendy cafés. The rhythmic sound of her plane smoothing rough timber provided a meditative backdrop to her thoughts, which lately seemed to drift toward the woman working in her small garden behind the shop.

Kassandra had hired Elena Moreau three months prior, responding to a handwritten note posted at the local market. The penmanship was careful, almost artistic, and something about its measured loops suggested patience—a quality essential for coaxing life from Southampton's challenging soil. When Elena appeared for the interview, Kassandra found herself momentarily speechless. The woman possessed an ethereal quality, with dark hair that caught sunlight like spun silk and eyes the color of deep forest pools.

"I studied horticulture in France," Elena had explained, her accent lending music to ordinary words. "But I prefer working with my hands rather than theories from textbooks." She gestured toward Kassandra's struggling herb garden. "Your rosemary is drowning. Too much water, not enough drainage."

That simple observation sealed Elena's employment, though Kassandra suspected her decision had less to do with horticultural expertise and more to do with the way Elena's smile seemed to illuminate the entire courtyard.

Now, watching through her window as Elena knelt among the newly planted lavender, Kassandra felt an unfamiliar flutter in her chest. At thirty-four, she had convinced herself that romance was a luxury she couldn't afford. Building her carpentry business had required every ounce of energy and focus, leaving little room for relationships that inevitably complicated everything.

Yet Elena's presence had transformed more than just the garden. The workspace felt brighter somehow, infused with possibility. Even her most challenging clients seemed less demanding when she could glimpse Elena's graceful movements through the glass.

"Kassie!" Her assistant Marcus burst through the workshop door, nearly colliding with a partially completed dining table. "Mrs. Henderson called again about her kitchen cabinets. She wants to know if you can match the grain pattern exactly."

Kassandra sighed, reluctantly turning away from the window. Mrs. Henderson possessed the unfortunate combination of unlimited budget and impossible expectations. "Tell her I'm a carpenter, not a magician. Wood grows naturally—no two pieces are identical."

Marcus grinned, his unruly red hair escaping from beneath a paint-splattered cap. "Should I phrase it more diplomatically?"

"Use your judgment. But don't promise anything I can't deliver."

As Marcus reached for the phone, Elena appeared in the doorway, soil still clinging to her gardening gloves. "Forgive the interruption, but I wanted to show you something."

Kassandra's pulse quickened unexpectedly. Elena had never entered the workshop before, maintaining professional boundaries that Kassandra simultaneously respected and regretted. Now, seeing her surrounded by the tools and materials of Kassandra's craft, something shifted in the dynamic between them.

"The herb spiral is nearly finished," Elena continued, seemingly unaware of Kassandra's internal turmoil. "But I need your opinion about adding a small bench. Somewhere peaceful for morning tea."

The request was practical, even sensible. But the way Elena's eyes lingered on Kassandra's face suggested deeper implications. "I could build something simple," Kassandra managed. "Cedar, perhaps. It weathers beautifully."

"I was hoping you'd say that." Elena's smile held secrets. "I have very specific ideas about what would work best."

Marcus cleared his throat pointedly. "Mrs. Henderson is holding."

Kassandra nodded absently, her attention still focused on Elena. "We could discuss the bench over dinner. Tonight, if you're free."

The words escaped before caution could intervene. Elena's expression flickered with surprise, then something warmer. "I'd like that very much."

The observation struck uncomfortably close to truth. Kassandra had indeed built her life around predictable routines and achievable goals. Relationships introduced variables she couldn't control, outcomes she couldn't guarantee with skill and determination alone.
