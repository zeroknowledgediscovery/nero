Mario Benedetti had always thought his life was perfectly ordinary, if you could call fixing toilets for corporate monkeys ordinary. Every morning at seven-fifteen sharp, he'd load his van with wrenches, plungers, and an industrial-strength sense of resignation, then drive through the gleaming financial district where baboons in three-piece suits discussed quarterly earnings and chimpanzees made hostile takeover bids from corner offices that smelled faintly of bananas.

The Great Simian Revolution of 2019 had changed everything, though nobody talked about it much anymore. One day humans ran the world, the next day they were taking orders from orangutans with MBA degrees. Mario remembered the transition being surprisingly smooth, all things considered. The monkeys had simply walked into boardrooms, presented superior business plans, and somehow convinced everyone this was the natural order of things. Within six months, every Fortune 500 company had a primate CEO, and Mario found himself the go-to plumber for beings who could swing from chandelier to conference table without missing a beat in their PowerPoint presentations.

"Another clogged executive washroom," Mario muttered, reading the work order that had been faxed to his cramped apartment above Giuseppe's Pizza. The letterhead read "Silverback Industries" in bold, imposing font. He'd been there before – their head of operations was a particularly demanding gorilla named Mr. Kongo who had strong opinions about water pressure and an unfortunate habit of flushing important documents when he got excited about hostile acquisitions.

The drive downtown took forty-five minutes through traffic that seemed specifically designed to test human patience. Mario watched a group of spider monkeys directing traffic from an overpass, their tiny hands waving cars through intersections with an efficiency that put the old human traffic control systems to shame. A billboard advertised "Banana Republic Bank – Where Your Money Grows on Trees," featuring a well-dressed macaque shaking hands with a human customer who looked suspiciously happy about his investment portfolio.

Mario parked his van in the Silverback Industries loading dock and gathered his tools. The security guard, a stern-looking mandrill with a clipboard, barely glanced up as Mario signed in. This was routine now – humans doing maintenance work while their simian employers ran global empires from climate-controlled offices designed to accommodate both boardroom meetings and the occasional urge to swing from the ceiling fixtures.

The elevator music was still terrible, even under monkey management. Mario hummed along to a muzak version of "Monkey Business" as he rode up to the thirty-seventh floor, where Mr. Kongo's personal bathroom had apparently suffered what the work order described as "a catastrophic plumbing failure involving approximately fourteen pounds of imported tropical fruit."

The massive gorilla was wearing a pinstriped suit that had clearly been tailored by someone who understood the unique challenges of dressing a four-hundred-pound primate with a chest the size of a compact car. Mr. Kongo shifted uncomfortably in his custom-built chair, which creaked ominously under his weight. "Well, you see, I was celebrating the successful acquisition of Lemur Logic Corporation, and perhaps I got a bit enthusiastic about disposing of the celebration refreshments. The toilet seemed like a convenient waste disposal system at the time."

Mario sighed and rolled up his sleeves. This wasn't the first time he'd dealt with a monkey executive who had trouble distinguishing between toilets and garbage disposals. He'd learned not to ask too many questions – the pay was good, and in a world where baboons controlled the stock market, job security meant keeping the corporate bathrooms functional.

Five years ago, he'd been a struggling plumber trying to make ends meet in a human-dominated world.
