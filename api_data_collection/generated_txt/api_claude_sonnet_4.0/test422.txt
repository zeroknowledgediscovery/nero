The morning fog rolled across Brighton's coastline like a gray shroud, but Mr. Telep barely noticed as he adjusted his pilot's cap and walked toward the small airfield hangar. His leather boots clicked against wet concrete, each step echoing his growing unease about the reports filtering through aviation channels. Something was terribly wrong in the city, and it wasn't just the unusual weather patterns he'd been tracking from his cockpit.

Inside the cramped office that doubled as his operations center, newspaper clippings covered every available surface. Headlines screamed warnings about a disturbing pattern of attacks targeting families, particularly those with children. The descriptions made his stomach turn—a woman who seemed to take perverse pleasure in terrorizing kids before subjecting them to humiliating acts that stripped away their dignity and sense of security.

Telep had spent twenty-three years flying commercial routes between London and various European destinations, but nothing in his experience had prepared him for this kind of evil. The woman they were calling Janis had emerged like some twisted specter, driven by hatred so pure it seemed to burn through her actions. Witnesses described her as someone who delighted in cruelty, particularly when her targets belonged to families that didn't fit her narrow worldview.

The phone rang sharply, cutting through his dark thoughts. Detective Inspector Walsh's voice crackled through the receiver, heavy with exhaustion and frustration. "Telep, I know this sounds mad, but we need your help. She's moved beyond the ground now. Three separate sightings have her accessing rooftops, fire escapes, places where our units can't reach quickly enough."

"What makes you think I can do anything the police cannot?" Telep asked, though something inside him already knew the answer. His military background, combined with years navigating complex flight patterns and emergency situations, had given him skills that might prove useful in tracking someone who seemed to move through the city like smoke.

"Because you see things from above that we miss down here," Walsh replied. "And because frankly, we're running out of options. She struck again last night—a family in Kemptown. Same pattern, same sick ritual with the children. We found her latest calling card, something about 'purifying' them through humiliation."

Telep closed his eyes, remembering his own children who lived with his ex-wife in Manchester. The thought of someone like Janis approaching them filled him with cold rage. "Send me the locations of every attack. I'll start aerial reconnaissance this afternoon."

That evening, as amber streetlights began flickering to life across Hove, Telep sat in his small flat reviewing everything they knew about Janis. The pattern was becoming clearer—she targeted families in progressive neighborhoods, always striking when children were present. Her methods were designed to maximize psychological damage, using bizarre humiliation tactics that seemed calculated to destroy innocence.

The most disturbing detail was how she seemed to enjoy making those awful sounds—crude raspberries blown mockingly at terrified children before she began her ritualistic shaving. It was as if she wanted to reduce them to objects of her contempt, stripping away not just hair but dignity and security.

His neighbor Mrs. Henderson knocked gently on the door, carrying a cup of tea and wearing the worried expression that had become common throughout the area. "Any word from the police today, love?" she asked, settling her considerable bulk into his threadbare armchair.

"They're doing everything possible," Telep assured her, though his tone betrayed his own doubts. "Just keep your grandchildren close and don't let them play outside after dark."

"It's not right, what she's doing to those poor families," Mrs. Henderson said, shaking her head. "My Harold fought in the war so people could live freely, love who they choose. This Janis woman, she's like something from the worst parts of history come back to haunt us."

After she left, Telep spread maps across his kitchen table and began plotting flight patterns that would allow him to monitor the areas where Janis had struck. If she was using rooftops and elevated positions, his aircraft might be the key to finally spotting her before she could attack another family.
