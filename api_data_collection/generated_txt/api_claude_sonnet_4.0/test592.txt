Harold Pemberton had always been the kind of man who blended into wallpaper, which made his recent discovery both terrifying and oddly appropriate. For forty-three years, he'd lived in Greenville, South Carolina, keeping meticulous books for a dozen small businesses downtown. His khaki pants were always pressed, his shirts eternally beige, and his black-rimmed glasses perpetually sliding down his nose. Nobody paid Harold much attention, which was exactly how he preferred it.

The transformation began on a Tuesday morning in March while Harold sat hunched over spreadsheets at Meredith's Flower Shop, reconciling their quarterly taxes. Mrs. Meredith bustled around arranging daffodils, humming an off-key tune that made Harold's teeth ache. He rubbed his temples, wishing desperately that he could simply disappear from the overwhelming scent of jasmine and the woman's incessant chatter about her grandson's baseball team.

That's when it happened. Harold glanced down at his hands and watched in stunned silence as his fingers began to fade like watercolors in rain. The pencil he'd been gripping clattered to the desk, seemingly floating in midair before tumbling to the floor. Mrs. Meredith continued her monologue, completely oblivious to the miracle occurring three feet away.

Panic seized Harold's chest as he examined his vanishing limbs. His forearms had become translucent, revealing the floral wallpaper behind him. Within moments, his entire body had dissolved into nothingness. He could see himself—or rather, couldn't see himself—but felt perfectly solid when he pressed his invisible palms together. The sensation was utterly bizarre, like being erased from reality while remaining completely present.

"Harold?" Mrs. Meredith called out, finally noticing the empty chair. "Where'd that man go? I swear he was just here." She wandered around the cramped office, peering behind filing cabinets and even checking under her desk. "Harold? This isn't funny!"

But Harold wasn't laughing. He sat frozen, invisible and terrified, watching the elderly woman search for him with increasing agitation. What had happened to him? Was this some kind of medical emergency? Should he speak up? The questions tumbled through his mind like clothes in a dryer.

After twenty minutes of frantic searching, Mrs. Meredith concluded that Harold had stepped out for coffee without telling her. She returned to her flowers, muttering about the rudeness of younger generations. Meanwhile, Harold discovered that by concentrating intensely on becoming visible again, he could gradually materialize. His fingers appeared first, followed by his hands, then arms, until his complete form had returned.

"There you are!" Mrs. Meredith exclaimed when she spotted him. "Where on earth did you go?"

"Restroom," Harold lied, his voice barely above a whisper. He gathered his papers with trembling hands, desperate to escape and process what had just occurred. "I'll finish these at home."

The drive back to his modest ranch house on Elm Street passed in a blur. Harold's mind raced with possibilities, each more outlandish than the last. Had he imagined the entire episode? Was he suffering from some rare neurological condition? Had someone slipped hallucinogenic drugs into his morning coffee?

Once safely inside his living room, Harold decided to test his theory. He settled into his worn leather recliner, closed his eyes, and concentrated on disappearing. Nothing happened. He tried visualizing himself fading away, imagining his molecules dispersing into the atmosphere. Still nothing. Perhaps he'd suffered a brief psychotic break brought on by tax season stress.

Then Harold remembered the intensity of his desire to escape Mrs. Meredith's overwhelming presence. He thought about her grandson's batting averages, her piercing voice, the cloying flower scents that made his sinuses burn. He wished desperately to be anywhere else, to vanish completely from that suffocating environment.

His reflection in the television screen began to waver and blur. Within seconds, Harold had completely disappeared again. He laughed out loud—a sound that seemed to come from nowhere—then immediately clapped his invisible hand over his mouth. This was real. Somehow, impossibly, he had developed the ability to turn invisible.

For the next several hours, Harold experimented with his newfound power. He discovered that strong emotions, particularly the desire to escape or hide, triggered the transformation. Becoming visible again required conscious effort and concentration. The process was exhausting, leaving him feeling drained and slightly nauseous.

By evening, Harold had managed to maintain visibility for several consecutive hours, though he noticed an odd tingling sensation beneath his skin, as if his cells were vibrating at a different frequency than before. He prepared his usual dinner of microwave lasagna and canned green beans, trying to convince himself that life could continue normally despite this extraordinary development.

The phone rang while Harold was washing dishes. His neighbor, Janet Morrison, sounded frantic.

"Harold, thank goodness you're home! Did you see anything suspicious around your house today? Someone's been prowling through backyards, and Mrs. Patterson swears she saw a floating coffee cup in her kitchen this morning."

Harold nearly dropped the phone. During his afternoon experiments, he'd wandered outside while invisible, testing the limits of his ability. He'd been careless, picking up objects and moving through neighbors' yards without considering that others might notice floating items or unexplained sounds.

"I haven't seen anything unusual," Harold replied, technically telling the truth. "Maybe it was the wind?"

"Coffee cups don't float in the wind, Harold. I'm calling the police. There's something very strange happening in this neighborhood."

After hanging up, Harold realized his life had become infinitely more complicated. How could he explain his absences? How could he control when the invisibility occurred? And most importantly, who could he tell? The answer to that last question was obvious: absolutely nobody.

Harold spent the following weeks learning to master his ability while maintaining his mundane routine. He continued handling books for his clients, attended his monthly Rotary Club meetings, and played golf every Saturday at Greenville Country Club. To outside observers, nothing had changed about boring, predictable Harold Pemberton.

But Harold was changing in ways that went beyond the invisible transformations. For the first time in his adult life, he felt powerful. He could observe people when they thought they were alone, listen to conversations he was never meant to hear, and move through the world without being seen or judged. The sensation was intoxicating.

However, not everyone remained oblivious to Harold's transformation. His golf caddy, a nineteen-year-old kid named Marcus Williams, began noticing odd things during their Saturday rounds. Golf balls would change direction mid-flight, Harold's shots improved dramatically despite his terrible form, and sometimes Marcus could swear he heard Harold's voice coming from empty spaces on the course.

Marcus was a music student at Furman University who caddied weekends to pay for his education. He possessed the keen observational skills of a songwriter, always watching for details that others missed. Harold's increasingly strange behavior fascinated rather than alarmed him.

"Mr. Pemberton," Marcus said after Harold's ball had mysteriously avoided a water hazard, "you been taking lessons or something? Your game's gotten real interesting lately."

Marcus grinned. "Must be some kind of practice."The following Saturday, Harold decided to skip golf entirely.
