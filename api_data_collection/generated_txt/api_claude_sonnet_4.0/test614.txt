The rain hammered against the windows of Wrexham Police Station with the persistence of an unwelcome visitor. Alice Morgan pressed her palm against the cold glass, watching rivulets trace erratic paths downward like tears on a grieving face. Her reflection stared back—sharp blue eyes, auburn hair pulled into a practical ponytail, the weight of command evident in the set of her shoulders. At thirty-four, she had climbed the ranks faster than anyone expected, earning respect through determination rather than politics.

"Another late night, Chief?" Detective Sergeant Tom Williams appeared beside her, steam rising from two mugs of coffee. The familiar ritual had become their unspoken tradition during the long winter months.

"Crime doesn't keep office hours, Tom." Alice accepted the coffee gratefully, inhaling the bitter aroma. "Besides, someone needs to review these budget reports before the morning briefing."

Tom nodded, understanding the burden she carried. The station had seen better days—paint peeling from the walls, outdated equipment that barely functioned, and a team stretched thin by budget cuts. Yet somehow, Alice held it all together with a combination of fierce determination and genuine care for her officers.

The door chimed as someone entered from the storm outside. Alice turned, and her heart performed that familiar flutter it had been doing for the past fifteen years whenever Emma Davies walked into a room. Emma shook droplets from her dark hair, her photographer's bag slung over one shoulder, green eyes bright with the excitement that always accompanied her latest assignment.

"Bloody hell, it's vicious out there," Emma announced, peeling off her soaked jacket. "The whole of Wales seems to be floating away."

"What brings you here at this ungodly hour?" Alice asked, though she already felt lighter just seeing Emma's smile.

"Finished editing the photos from that community outreach event last week. Thought you might want to see them before I submit them to the Chronicle tomorrow." Emma produced a manila envelope, her fingers briefly brushing Alice's as she handed it over.

Tom excused himself diplomatically, mumbling something about paperwork. He had witnessed countless interactions between the two women over the years, and while he never commented, Alice sometimes caught him watching them with an expression that suggested he saw more than he let on.

Emma settled into the chair across from Alice's desk, curling her legs beneath her in that unconsciously graceful way she had. They had been inseparable since university, where Alice studied criminology while Emma pursued her passion for visual storytelling. Their friendship had weathered career changes, failed relationships, and the general chaos of adult life with remarkable resilience.

"These are brilliant, Em," Alice said, spreading the photographs across her desk. Emma had captured moments of genuine connection—officers playing football with local children, elderly residents sharing stories with community support officers, teenagers laughing despite their usual wariness around police.

"You know what you've done here? You've shown them who we really are, not just the uniforms and authority. This is exactly what we need right now."

Emma beamed at the praise. "Mrs. Patterson from Grosvenor Road said it was the first time in years she felt safe walking through the estate. That's down to the work you're doing, Alice. The community trusts you in a way they haven't trusted police leadership in decades."

Their conversation flowed as naturally as breathing, touching on everything from local politics to Emma's latest freelance assignments. Alice found herself studying Emma's animated expressions, the way she gestured when passionate about a subject, the small scar above her left eyebrow from a childhood accident that Alice remembered happening.

"I should let you get back to those reports," Emma said eventually, glancing at the stack of papers on Alice's desk. But she made no move to leave, and Alice felt no urgency to encourage her departure.

"They'll still be boring in the morning," Alice replied. "Tell me about the exhibition you mentioned last week. The one in Cardiff?"

Emma's eyes lit up. "It's a group show focusing on contemporary Welsh identity. They want to include my series on post-industrial communities. I'm terrified and thrilled simultaneously."

"You should be thrilled. Your work deserves recognition beyond Wrexham."

"This is home, though. These streets, these people—they're part of me. I can't imagine living anywhere else."

Alice felt a surge of relief she had no right to experience. Emma was her best friend, nothing more, despite the way her pulse quickened during moments like these. She had built walls around those feelings years ago, convinced that preserving their friendship mattered more than risking everything on romantic possibilities.

The phone rang, shattering the intimate atmosphere. Alice answered with professional efficiency, her expression growing serious as she listened to the report. A break-in at the old textile factory, possible injuries, officers requested on scene immediately.

"I have to go," she told Emma, already reaching for her jacket.

"Be careful out there," Emma said softly, concern evident in her voice.

Their eyes met for a moment longer than necessary, and Alice felt that familiar tension—the space between what was said and what remained unspoken. Then she was moving, duty calling her into the storm-swept streets of Wrexham, leaving Emma alone in the fluorescent-lit office.

The factory loomed against the night sky like a monument to abandoned dreams. The break-in appeared straightforward enough—teenagers seeking shelter, no malicious intent, minor damage to a window already scheduled for repair.
