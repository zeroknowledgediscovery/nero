The rain hammered against the windows of the Jacksonville Police Department with an intensity that seemed almost personal. Detective Chief Inspector Sarah Shelbrook stood at her office window, watching the water cascade down the glass in erratic patterns. At thirty-eight, she had seen enough darkness in this world to know that storms often brought more than just wet weather. Tonight felt different somehow, charged with an electricity that had nothing to do with the lightning flickering across the sky.

Her phone buzzed insistently on the desk behind her. The third call in ten minutes. Sarah didn't need to look at the caller ID to know it would be another frantic parent, another missing teenager report. She'd been tracking the pattern for weeks now, though the official investigation had only begun three days ago when the Hendricks family finally convinced the department that their seventeen-year-old son Marcus hadn't simply run away to join some punk rock band in Seattle.

"DCI Shelbrook," she answered, her voice carrying the weight of too many sleepless nights.

The woman's voice cracked. "You think something's happened to her, don't you? Like those other kids?"

Before Sarah could respond, the line went dead. She stared at the phone for a moment, then set it down with deliberate care. The other kids. That's what everyone was calling them now, as if giving the disappearances a collective name might somehow make them less terrifying, more manageable.

Detective James Morrison knocked on her door frame, his usually neat appearance disheveled. Jim had been her partner for four years, ever since she'd transferred from the cold case unit. He was steady, reliable, and one of the few people who knew about her past at the children's home on Riverside Drive.

"I know. I just spoke to her." Sarah grabbed her jacket from the back of her chair. "That makes seven in two months, Jim. Seven teenagers who just vanished without a trace."

"Actually," Jim's expression darkened, "it makes eight. Tommy Reeves was reported missing an hour ago. Sixteen years old, last seen leaving the mall with friends around four o'clock yesterday afternoon."

Sarah felt something cold settle in her stomach. The disappearances were accelerating. Whatever was happening in Jacksonville, it was getting worse.

They drove through the rain-soaked streets in relative silence, both lost in their own thoughts. Sarah found herself thinking about her own teenage years, the uncertainty and fear that had defined her time at Riverside Children's Home. She'd been one of the lucky ones, aging out of the system at eighteen with a scholarship to study criminology. Many of her fellow orphans hadn't been so fortunate.

"Please, come in. I have everything ready." She led them into a living room that was almost obsessively clean, as if maintaining perfect order might somehow protect her family from chaos.

Amy Chen smiled out from a dozen framed photographs scattered around the room. A pretty girl with long dark hair and her mother's intelligent eyes. Honor roll student, volunteer at the animal shelter, captain of the debate team. The kind of teenager who had her whole life mapped out ahead of her.

Jim was taking notes while Sarah asked questions, but she could see the same frustration in his eyes that she felt. These families all told similar stories. Good kids, stable homes, no reason to run away or put themselves in danger. Yet somehow, eight teenagers had simply vanished from the face of the earth.

They spent another hour with Mrs. Chen, gathering details about Amy's friends, her routine, her favorite places to go. By the time they left, the rain had intensified to a torrential downpour that turned the streets into rivers.
