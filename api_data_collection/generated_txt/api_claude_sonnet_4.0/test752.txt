The morning mist clung to the cobblestone streets of Burke like a shroud, obscuring the ancient lampposts that lined the narrow pathways between centuries-old buildings. Dr. Sarah Blake pulled her coat tighter against the October chill as she hurried toward St. Margaret's Hospital, her breath forming small clouds in the crisp air. She had worked the night shift for three years now, but something about this particular morning felt different—charged with an electricity that made the hair on her arms stand on end.

The emergency department buzzed with its usual chaos when she arrived. Nurses scurried between rooms, their soft-soled shoes squeaking against the polished linoleum floors. The familiar sounds of beeping monitors and hushed conversations created a symphony she had grown accustomed to, yet today even these routine noises seemed somehow amplified, more urgent than usual.

"Doctor Blake!" called Margaret Chen, the head nurse, her voice cutting through the ambient noise. "We've got something strange in trauma bay three. You need to see this."

Sarah's stomach tightened as she followed Margaret down the corridor. Strange cases weren't uncommon in their line of work, but Margaret's tone suggested this was beyond their typical unusual. The fluorescent lights overhead flickered intermittently, casting dancing shadows on the pale green walls that had seen decades of medical emergencies.

Inside trauma bay three, Dr. James Morrison lay unconscious on the examination table, his usually pristine white coat torn and stained with something that looked like dark mud. His colleagues clustered around him, their faces etched with confusion and concern. What struck Sarah immediately wasn't the obvious injuries—though there were plenty—but the peculiar indentations covering his torso, as if something small but incredibly strong had grabbed him repeatedly.

"Found him outside the pediatric wing around five this morning," explained Dr. Rebecca Torres, her hands trembling slightly as she gestured toward Morrison's prone form. "Maintenance discovered him near the dumpsters, completely unconscious. His injuries... they're unlike anything I've ever encountered in fifteen years of practice."

Sarah approached the table, her medical training overriding the unease that crept up her spine. Morrison's breathing was shallow but steady, and his vital signs, while concerning, weren't immediately life-threatening. What puzzled her most were those strange marks—perfectly formed handprints no larger than a child's, yet pressed deep into his flesh with inhuman strength.

"Has anyone reviewed the security footage?" Sarah asked, snapping on latex gloves with practiced efficiency. The familiar ritual helped calm her nerves as she began her examination.

"That's the thing," Margaret replied, lowering her voice. "The cameras malfunctioned around midnight. All we have is static until six in the morning. IT says they've never seen anything like it—the equipment just... stopped working."

As Sarah carefully examined Morrison's injuries, she noticed something else peculiar. Scattered around his clothing were small, muddy footprints that seemed to lead from nowhere and disappear into thin air. The prints were roughly the size of a ten-year-old child's, but the depth suggested whatever made them weighed considerably more than any human child possibly could.

"Doctor Morrison's starting to come around," announced Nurse Patricia Williams from beside the monitor. Indeed, Morrison's eyelids fluttered, and soft groans escaped his lips as consciousness slowly returned.

Sarah leaned closer, her voice gentle but urgent. "James, can you hear me? You're in the hospital. You're safe now."

Morrison's eyes opened gradually, unfocused at first, then widening with what appeared to be terror as memory returned. His body tensed, and he tried to sit up despite Sarah's restraining hand on his shoulder.

"No, no, no," he mumbled, his voice hoarse and barely audible. "It was laughing. The thing was actually laughing while it..." He trailed off, his eyes darting around the room as if expecting something to materialize from the shadows.

"What thing, James?" Sarah asked softly, though part of her wasn't certain she wanted to hear the answer. "What do you remember about last night?"

Morrison's breathing became more labored, and sweat beaded on his forehead despite the cool temperature of the trauma bay. "Green," he whispered, his voice gaining strength as the memories apparently became clearer. "Bright green skin, about three feet tall. But so strong, impossibly strong. Its eyes... they glowed in the darkness like some kind of predator."

The medical staff exchanged glances, each wondering if Morrison might be suffering from a head injury that was causing hallucinations. However, the physical evidence suggested something had indeed attacked him—something with small hands but tremendous strength.

"It spoke to me," Morrison continued, his voice growing steadier but more frightened. "Not in English, but I understood somehow. It said something about love, about doing this all for love. Then it started squeezing, and laughing, that horrible cackling laugh that seemed to echo from everywhere at once."

Sarah felt a chill run down her spine that had nothing to do with the hospital's air conditioning. She had always prided herself on being rational, scientific in her approach to medicine and life. But Morrison's account, combined with the inexplicable physical evidence, was challenging everything she thought she knew about the world.

"Did you get a good look at its face?" she asked, trying to keep her voice professional despite the growing unease.

Morrison nodded weakly. "Pointed ears, sharp teeth, but the expression... it wasn't angry or hungry like you'd expect from a monster. It looked almost... joyful? Like it was playing some kind of game that only it understood. And it kept repeating a name—Idan, I think. Over and over, like it was introducing itself."

Margaret Chen had been quietly taking notes throughout Morrison's account, but now she looked up with a frown. "Doctor Blake, we should probably contact the authorities. If there's someone—or something—attacking hospital staff, we need to increase security immediately."

Sarah nodded, though she doubted the local police would know how to handle a case involving what sounded like a creature from folklore. Burke was a quiet town where the most excitement usually involved teenagers vandalizing park benches or the occasional domestic dispute. They weren't equipped to deal with whatever had attacked Morrison.

"Let's get James admitted for observation," Sarah decided. "I want to run a full neurological workup and keep him under watch for at least forty-eight hours. Margaret, can you contact Dr. Williams in psychiatry? We should have someone evaluate James for trauma-related symptoms."

As the team began preparing Morrison for transfer to a regular room, Sarah couldn't shake the image his description had painted. A small, green creature with glowing eyes and superhuman strength, attacking people while laughing and claiming to act out of love. It sounded like something from a children's fairy tale, except the injuries were real, the fear in Morrison's eyes was genuine, and somewhere in Burke, whatever had done this was still at large.

The rest of her shift passed uneventfully, but Sarah found herself glancing over her shoulder more frequently than usual, paying attention to shadows that hadn't seemed significant before. When she finally left the hospital that evening, the October sun was already setting, painting the sky in shades of orange and red that somehow seemed ominous rather than beautiful.

Walking to her car, Sarah noticed something that made her blood run cold. There, in the soft dirt beside the parking lot, were more of those small, deep footprints she had seen around Morrison. They seemed to lead from the hospital's main entrance toward the wooded area that bordered the property, disappearing into the underbrush where the fading light couldn't penetrate.

Against her better judgment, Sarah found herself following the trail. Her rational mind screamed that this was foolish, that she should call security or the police, but something deeper compelled her forward. Perhaps it was medical curiosity, or maybe just the need to understand what had happened to her colleague.

The footprints led about fifty yards into the woods before stopping abruptly at the base of an enormous oak tree. Sarah looked up into its branches, half-expecting to see glowing eyes staring back at her, but found only the rustle of leaves in the evening breeze.

It was then that she heard it—a sound that would haunt her dreams for weeks to come. Laughter, high-pitched and gleeful, echoing through the trees from no discernible direction. The sound seemed to bounce off the trunks and branches, making it impossible to locate its source, but Sarah was certain it was close, very close.

"Who's there?" she called out, surprised by the steadiness of her own voice. "If someone's hurt, I'm a doctor. I can help."

The laughter stopped abruptly, replaced by a silence so complete that even the usual forest sounds seemed to have been swallowed. Sarah waited, her heart pounding in her chest, but heard nothing more. Finally, as full darkness began to settle over the woods, she turned and hurried back to her car, certain that something was watching her retreat.

That night, Sarah sat in her small apartment above the bakery on Main Street, researching everything she could find about creatures matching Morrison's description. Most of what she found was folklore and fairy tales, stories of goblins and sprites that seemed too fantastical to take seriously. Yet one account, buried in an old book about European mythology, caught her attention.

The text described a type of goblin called an "Idan"—a creature driven by obsessive love that manifested as protective violence against perceived threats to its beloved. According to the legend, Idans were small but possessed supernatural strength, and they took perverse joy in their violent acts, believing they were performing noble deeds in service of love.

Sarah closed the book and rubbed her tired eyes. Even if such creatures existed—which her scientific training insisted they did not—what would one be doing in Burke? And more importantly, who or what could it possibly love enough to attack innocent people?

As she prepared for bed, Sarah made a decision that would change the course of her life forever. Tomorrow, she would begin her own investigation into the creature that had attacked Morrison. Whether it was human, animal, or something else entirely, she was determined to find it before it hurt anyone else.

Outside her window, hidden in the shadows of the alley behind the bakery, something small and green watched her silhouette move across the lit apartment. Its glowing eyes tracked her movements with predatory intensity, and when she finally turned off the lights, it released another quiet chuckle of anticipation.
