The morning fog clung to Norwich like a damp blanket, seeping through the narrow streets and settling against the Tudor facades that lined the cobblestone paths. Malcolm Henderson pulled his van into the familiar driveway on Magdalen Street, the engine coughing once before falling silent. He sat for a moment, watching the condensation form on his windscreen, thinking about how the mist reminded him of smoke from his father's old pipe - something comforting yet ephemeral, always disappearing just when you wanted to hold onto it.

At thirty-two, Malcolm had developed the kind of steady routine that some might call boring but he found reassuring. Wake at six, strong tea with two sugars, check the weather forecast though it hardly mattered since electrical work waited regardless of rain or shine, then off to whatever house or business needed his particular brand of problem-solving. Today's job was at Mrs. Pemberton's Victorian terrace, where the upstairs lighting had been flickering like a nervous tic for the better part of a month.

He gathered his tools from the back of the van, the familiar weight of his kit bag settling against his shoulder like an old friend's hand. The smell of leather and metal, wire strippers and electrical tape, had become as much a part of him as his own skin. Malcolm took pride in his work - not the showy kind of pride that needed announcing, but the quiet satisfaction that came from solving puzzles that left other people scratching their heads in bewilderment.

The front door opened before he could knock, revealing Mrs. Pemberton in her usual floral housecoat, gray hair pinned back with bobby pins that had probably been purchased sometime during the Thatcher years.

"Oh Malcolm, thank goodness you're here," she said, stepping aside to let him in. "The lights were acting up something dreadful last night. My grandson Thomas was visiting, and the poor boy thought we had ghosts in the walls."

Malcolm smiled, setting his bag down in the narrow hallway. "Well, let's see what kind of spirits we're dealing with then. Though I suspect they're the kind that can be exercised with a bit of rewiring rather than an exorcist."

Mrs. Pemberton chuckled, leading him toward the staircase. "You always did have a way with words, Malcolm. Your mother must be proud."

The mention of his mother brought the familiar tightness to Malcolm's chest. Margaret Henderson had passed three years ago, leaving behind a small flat filled with china figurines and the lingering scent of lavender talcum powder. She'd been proud, certainly, but she'd also worried constantly about Malcolm's solitary nature, his reluctance to settle down with what she called "a nice girl" and give her the grandchildren she'd dreamed about.

"She would have liked knowing her lights stayed on," Malcolm replied, testing the switch at the bottom of the stairs. The bulb above flickered uncertainly, casting dancing shadows against the wallpaper.

As he worked, carefully tracing wires through walls that had been hiding electrical mysteries for decades, Malcolm's mind wandered to his plans for the evening. Thursday meant dinner at The Bell, the pub where he'd been meeting David Ashworth every week for the past eight years. Their friendship had begun in the most mundane way possible - Malcolm had been called to fix the faulty wiring in David's flat above the bookshop he managed, and they'd discovered a shared appreciation for real ale and terrible jokes.

David. Even thinking his name caused something warm to unfold in Malcolm's chest, a feeling he'd been trying to ignore for months now but which seemed to grow stronger with each passing week. Somewhere between their hundredth pint and their thousandth conversation about everything and nothing, Malcolm had realized that what he felt for his best friend had shifted into territory that made him simultaneously hopeful and terrified.

The problem with rewiring Mrs. Pemberton's upstairs lighting turned out to be delightfully straightforward - a loose connection in the junction box that had been causing intermittent contact. Malcolm tightened the wire nuts, tested each switch methodically, and felt the small satisfaction that came with restoring order to chaos.

"There you are," he called down to Mrs. Pemberton. "Try the lights now."

The hallway flooded with steady, reliable illumination. No more flickering, no more uncertainty. If only, Malcolm thought, human connections could be fixed with such straightforward solutions.

By four o'clock, he'd finished the job and collected his payment, Mrs. Pemberton pressing a tin of homemade biscuits into his hands despite his protests. The afternoon had cleared, leaving Norwich looking freshly washed under pale autumn sunshine. Malcolm decided to walk through the city center before heading home, enjoying the way the light caught the flint walls of the cathedral and turned the River Wensum into a ribbon of molten silver.

He found himself standing outside Ashworth Books, the narrow shop front squeezed between a charity shop and a café that specialized in organic everything. Through the window, he could see David's familiar silhouette moving between the stacks, probably reshelfing returns or helping some customer track down an obscure poetry collection. David had always been the literary one in their friendship, quoting half-remembered lines from novels Malcolm had never read, recommending books that sat hopefully on Malcolm's bedside table for months before being quietly returned, unfinished.

The shop bell chimed as Malcolm pushed inside, breathing in the familiar smell of old paper and the coffee David kept brewing in the back room. The space was cramped but welcoming, with books stacked floor to ceiling and narrow aisles that encouraged browsing rather than hurried shopping.

"Malcolm!" David's voice carried from somewhere among the poetry section. "You're early. Give me two minutes to finish with Mrs. Hartley's special order."

Malcolm wandered toward the biography section, running his fingers along the spines of books about people who'd lived extraordinary lives. He paused at a thick volume about electrical pioneers, amused by the coincidence, and was flipping through pages of early telegraph diagrams when David appeared at his elbow.

"Finding inspiration in the great electricians of history?" David asked, peering over Malcolm's shoulder. He smelled faintly of the sandalwood soap he'd been using since university, a scent that Malcolm had come to associate with comfort and possibility.

"Just wondering if any of them had to deal with Mrs. Pemberton's Victorian wiring," Malcolm replied, closing the book. "Though I suspect they had enough problems with their own inventions without worrying about maintenance calls sixty years later."

David laughed, the sound bright in the quiet bookshop. He was wearing his usual work uniform of dark jeans and a button-down shirt that had seen better days, sleeves rolled up to reveal forearms that Malcolm tried not to notice too obviously. At twenty-nine, David still had the slightly rumpled appearance of the English literature student he'd been when they first met, all unruly brown hair and reading glasses that he was constantly misplacing.

"Ready for dinner then?" David asked, already reaching for his jacket. "I've been thinking about their steak and ale pie all afternoon."

They walked to The Bell through streets that Malcolm had known all his life, past shops he'd helped wire and houses where he'd solved electrical mysteries. Norwich felt manageable at this time of day, neither the sleepy market town of his childhood nor the bustling university city it became during term time, but something comfortably in between.

The pub was filling with the usual Thursday crowd - office workers stopping for a quick pint before heading home, university lecturers engaging in animated discussions about subjects Malcolm couldn't identify, and a scattering of locals who'd been claiming the same stools since before Malcolm was born. He and David had long ago established their preferred table by the window, where they could watch the street life and maintain their ongoing commentary on the peculiarities of their fellow Norwich residents.

"So," David said once they'd settled with their pints, "tell me about your day. Any particularly dramatic electrical emergencies?"

Malcolm found himself describing Mrs. Pemberton's flickering lights with more detail than the story warranted, partly because he enjoyed making David laugh and partly because talking about work was safer than the topics his mind wanted to explore. David listened with the kind of attention that had always made Malcolm feel heard in a way that was rare and precious.

"You know what I love about your job?" David said, taking a sip of his beer. "There's always a solution. Someone calls you with a problem, you show up with your tools and your expertise, and by the end of the day, everything works the way it's supposed to. Very satisfying."

"Not always," Malcolm replied. "Sometimes the problem's more complicated than it appears. Sometimes you think you've fixed something, but the real issue is hidden behind the walls where you can't see it."

David nodded thoughtfully. "I suppose that's true of most things, isn't it? The surface problem and the actual problem aren't always the same thing."

Their conversation was interrupted by the arrival of their food, the steak and ale pie living up to David's anticipation, rich gravy steaming in the thick pastry crust. They ate with the comfortable rhythm of old friends, discussing David's latest book recommendations, Malcolm's weekend plans to help his neighbor repair a garden shed, and the ongoing mystery of whether the new coffee shop on Elm Hill would survive its first year.

As the evening progressed and the pub grew more crowded, Malcolm found himself studying David's face in the warm light from the table lamp, noting the way his eyes crinkled when he smiled and the gesture he made with his left hand when he was searching for exactly the right word. These observations felt both natural and dangerous, like standing too close to an electrical current - the pull was undeniable, but the risk of getting burned was very real.

"Malcolm?" David's voice pulled him back to the present. "You've gone quiet. Everything alright?"

"Just thinking," Malcolm replied, which was true enough, though he couldn't very well explain what he'd been thinking about. "Long day, I suppose."

David studied him for a moment with the perceptive look that Malcolm both valued and feared. David had always been able to read people, to sense undercurrents and unspoken concerns. It was part of what made him so good at recommending books - he could match readers with exactly the story they needed, even when they couldn't articulate what they were looking for.

"You know you can talk to me about anything, right?" David said quietly. "Whatever's going on in that head of yours."

The sincerity in his voice made Malcolm's chest tighten with something that felt dangerously close to hope. For a moment, he considered telling the truth - explaining how his feelings had shifted and deepened, how their weekly dinners had become the highlight of his week, how he'd begun imagining conversations and scenarios that went far beyond friendship.

But fear won out, as it always did. Fear of changing something good, fear of losing the friendship that had become central to his life, fear of discovering that David's warm affection was purely platonic and always would be.

"I'm fine," Malcolm said instead. "Just tired. Mrs. Pemberton's wiring was more of a puzzle than I expected."

David accepted this explanation, though Malcolm suspected he wasn't entirely convinced. They finished their meal and their pints, settling into the easier territory of David's stories about difficult customers and Malcolm's observations about the peculiarities of Norwich's electrical infrastructure.

By closing time, they were both slightly drunk and considerably more relaxed, walking slowly through the empty streets toward their respective homes. David lived in the flat above his bookshop, while Malcolm's terraced house was a fifteen-minute walk in the opposite direction. They always parted ways at the corner of Magdalen Street, usually with plans for the following week and David's invariable reminder that Malcolm should call if he needed anything.

Tonight, though, David hesitated at their usual parting spot, shifting his weight from one foot to the other in a gesture Malcolm recognized as uncertainty.

"Listen," David began, then stopped, running a hand through his hair. "Never mind. It's nothing important."

"What is it?" Malcolm asked, though part of him dreaded the answer. David's hesitation felt loaded with possibility, and Malcolm wasn't sure he was prepared for whatever revelation might be coming.

"I was just thinking," David continued slowly, "about what we were talking about earlier. About problems that aren't what they seem on the surface. Sometimes I wonder if there are things we don't talk about because we're afraid they'll change everything."

Malcolm's heart began beating faster, a rhythm he could feel in his temples and the base of his throat. The street was empty except for the two of them, lit by the occasional streetlamp and the glow from curtained windows. The moment felt suspended, full of potential energy like a circuit waiting to be completed.

"What kind of things?" Malcolm managed to ask, though his voice sounded strange to his own ears.

David looked at him directly then, and Malcolm saw something in his expression that he'd never noticed before, or perhaps had been too afraid to see. Uncertainty, yes, but also something warmer and more complex.

"Things that might be obvious to everyone except us," David said quietly.The silence stretched between them, thick with unspoken possibilities. Malcolm felt as though he were standing at the edge of a cliff, knowing that whatever he said next would either pull him back to safety or send him tumbling into unknown territory. The familiar streets of Norwich seemed to hold their breath around them, windows dark and empty, the distant sound of a car engine fading into the night.

"David," Malcolm began, then found himself unable to continue. The words he wanted to say felt too large, too dangerous to release into the space between them. Instead, he watched as David's expression shifted from hopeful expectation to something that looked dangerously close to disappointment.

"Right," David said after a moment, his voice carefully neutral. "Well, I should probably head home. Early morning tomorrow - book delivery coming in, and I need to make space in the stockroom." He took a step backward, creating distance that felt both physical and emotional. "Same time next week?"

Malcolm nodded, not trusting himself to speak. He watched David walk away, noting the slight slump to his shoulders and the way he kept his hands shoved deep in his jacket pockets. Only when David had disappeared around the corner did Malcolm allow himself to breathe properly, the air rushing into his lungs like a man surfacing from deep water.

The walk to his own house felt longer than usual, his footsteps echoing off the narrow streets with a hollow sound that matched the feeling in his chest. Malcolm's terraced house sat in the middle of a row of identical properties, each with their small front gardens and painted doors that had probably been chosen to provide some illusion of individuality. His own door was blue - not by any particular design preference, but because blue had been the color left behind by the previous owner, and Malcolm had never felt strongly enough about it to warrant the expense of change.

He settled into his armchair, a piece of furniture that had molded itself to his body over years of evening reading and television watching, but tonight even this comfort felt insufficient. Malcolm found himself replaying the conversation outside the pub, analyzing David's words and expressions for meanings he might have missed. The phrase "things that might be obvious to everyone except us" circled through his mind like a song he couldn't shake, its implications both thrilling and terrifying.

Sleep, when it finally came, brought dreams filled with flickering lights and loose connections, endless corridors where he searched for problems he could never quite locate. Malcolm woke before his alarm, groggy and unrested, with the lingering sense that he'd missed something important during the night. Malcolm found the problem quickly enough - inadequate grounding that was causing voltage variations whenever the air conditioning system cycled on and off. It was exactly the kind of straightforward issue that usually provided satisfaction, but today the solution felt hollow, mechanical rather than meaningful.

During his lunch break, Malcolm found himself driving past Ashworth Books without any conscious decision to do so. Through the window, he could see David serving a customer, gesturing animatedly as he described what was probably some literary masterpiece that Malcolm would never have the patience to read. David looked perfectly normal, showing no signs of the tension that had marked their parting the night before, and Malcolm wondered if he'd imagined the entire awkward exchange.

The afternoon brought a service call to repair damaged wiring in a house where a kitchen renovation had gone badly wrong.
