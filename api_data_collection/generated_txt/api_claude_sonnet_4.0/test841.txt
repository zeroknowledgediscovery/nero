Captain Marcus Webb had never imagined that his calloused hands, so accustomed to gripping the yoke of his Cessna 172, would find such delicate purpose in crepe paper and wire stems. The afternoon sun streamed through the kitchen window of his modest ranch house on Schrock Road, illuminating the scattered rainbow of tissue paper that covered his dining table like fallen autumn leaves. Each sheet whispered against his fingers as he carefully separated the layers, a sound so different from the roar of engines that had dominated his life for the past fifteen years.

It had started innocently enough three weeks ago, when his sister Rachel visited from Columbus with her twin daughters. Rachel, ever the resourceful mother, had produced a craft kit from her oversized purse—one of those emergency entertainment packages that experienced parents carry like survival gear.

"Uncle Marcus, will you help us?" little Sarah had asked, her gap-toothed smile impossible to refuse. And so he had found himself sitting cross-legged on his hardwood floor, fumbling with crimson tissue paper while the girls giggled at his clumsy attempts to create something that resembled a rose.

The first flower had been a disaster, naturally. A crumpled mess that looked more like roadkill than anything botanical. But something about the process had captivated him—the precision required, the way each fold and twist contributed to the final result, the transformation of ordinary materials into something beautiful and purposeful. It reminded him of flying, in a way. The careful attention to detail, the patience required, the satisfaction of creation.

Now, three weeks later, Marcus had progressed far beyond those initial fumbling attempts. His latest creation, a delicate white lily with subtle yellow shading at the center, sat propped against a coffee mug on the table. He had watched seventeen YouTube tutorials to perfect that particular technique, staying up until two in the morning to get the petal curves just right.

The sound of a lawn mower starting up next door interrupted his concentration. Marcus had meant to introduce himself properly—they had exchanged waves and brief pleasantries over the fence, but nothing substantial. He appeared to be around Marcus's age, maybe a few years older, with prematurely gray hair and the lean build of someone who worked with his hands.
