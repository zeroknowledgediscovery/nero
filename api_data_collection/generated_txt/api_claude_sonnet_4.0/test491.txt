The morning Eliza Thornwick discovered her husband bleating in the kitchen, she thought perhaps the coffee hadn't quite kicked in yet. Marcus stood there—or rather, what had been Marcus—chewing contemplatively on the morning newspaper, his rectangular pupils fixed on her with an expression that seemed almost apologetic. Where his weathered carpenter's hands should have been, cloven hooves scratched nervously at the linoleum floor.

"Well," Eliza said, setting down her mug with deliberate care, "this is certainly unexpected."

The creature that had been her husband of fifteen years let out a soft bleat and tilted his head, horns catching the early sunlight streaming through their kitchen window. His beard, always scraggly, now seemed perfectly natural framing his elongated face. The transformation appeared complete—from the tip of his twitching ears to his newly acquired tail.

Eliza had always prided herself on adaptability. Twenty years of carpentry work had taught her to roll with unexpected problems, to find solutions when the measurements didn't quite add up or when the client changed their mind halfway through a project. But this particular challenge seemed to fall well outside her usual expertise.

The phone rang, shrill and insistent. Eliza answered without taking her eyes off Marcus, who had moved on from the newspaper to investigate a potted plant with obvious culinary interest.

"Eliza? Thank God you're there." The voice belonged to her neighbor, Mrs. Chen, though it carried an edge of hysteria Eliza had never heard before. "Have you looked outside? Have you seen what's happened to the men?"

"I'm currently observing the situation firsthand," Eliza replied, watching as Marcus—or the goat that had been Marcus—successfully knocked over her favorite succulent. "How widespread are we talking about?"

"Every single one of them, as far as I can tell. My husband, my son, the mailman—they're all..." Mrs. Chen's voice broke slightly. "They're all goats now, Eliza. Every last man in the neighborhood has turned into a goat."

Eliza moved to the window, cordless phone pressed to her ear. The street below confirmed Mrs. Chen's assessment. Where usually she might see Mr. Rodriguez tending his garden or young Timothy Fletcher riding his bicycle to school, now she observed a peculiar pastoral scene. Goats wandered the sidewalks with varying degrees of purpose. Some seemed to be attempting their usual routines—she watched one goat that she suspected had been the postal worker trying to navigate a mail truck with obvious frustration.

"The news is saying it's everywhere," Mrs. Chen continued. "The entire country, maybe the whole world. Every adult male just... changed overnight."

The implications hit Eliza like a poorly swung hammer. If every man had transformed, that meant the government, the military, law enforcement—the entire power structure that had been predominantly male—was now comprised of livestock. The thought should have been almost comical, but something cold settled in her stomach instead.

"Mrs. Chen, I need to make some calls. Check on your family and stay inside for now."

After hanging up, Eliza tried calling her great-aunt Margarette, the only family she had left since her parents died in a car accident five years ago. Margarette had always been an odd woman, brilliant but secretive, with government connections that she never quite explained. She worked for some obscure department that seemed to require extensive travel and long periods of radio silence.

The call went straight to voicemail, which wasn't unusual, but today it felt ominous.

Marcus bleated softly from beside her, and she looked down to find him nudging her hand with his snout. His eyes—still recognizably his eyes, despite everything else—held an intelligence that seemed frustratingly trapped behind his new form. She scratched between his horns absently, surprised by how natural the gesture felt.

"I suppose we need to figure out what comes next," she murmured.

The television provided a surreal soundtrack to her morning. News anchors—all female now, she realized—struggled to maintain professional composure while reporting on the global transformation. The stock markets had crashed. Several governments had declared states of emergency, though it remained unclear who exactly was left to enforce them. In some countries, female military personnel were attempting to maintain order, but the situation was chaotic at best.

Dr. Sarah Martinez, apparently the new head of the Centers for Disease Control following the transformation of her predecessor, appeared on screen looking haggard but determined. "We want to emphasize that the affected individuals appear to be in good health," she said, consulting notes with shaking hands. "They retain their memories and personalities, though obviously communication has become challenging. We're treating this as a medical emergency and are working around the clock to understand the cause and potential reversal methods."

Eliza snorted. Medical emergency. As if this was something that could be cured with antibiotics and bed rest. She'd seen enough of the world to recognize something far more deliberate when she saw it. The precision of the transformation—affecting only adult males, leaving them otherwise healthy and recognizable—suggested intention rather than random catastrophe.

Her phone buzzed with a text message from an unknown number: "The goats are not the problem. Trust no one in power. The real danger is what comes next. —A friend."

Before she could respond or even process the message fully, another text arrived: "Check your basement. Your aunt left something for you."

Eliza stared at the phone, her mind racing. Margarette had visited three weeks ago, an unusual occurrence since her great-aunt typically preferred to maintain contact through cryptic postcards from various international locations. She'd spent an afternoon in the basement, claiming she wanted to examine the foundation work Eliza had done the previous year.

Marcus followed her downstairs, his hooves clicking against the wooden steps with a rhythm that was almost musical. The basement looked exactly as she'd left it—tool bench organized, lumber stacked neatly against one wall, her current project (a custom bookshelf for a client) half-finished in the center of the space.

But when she examined the foundation more carefully, running her hands along the stone walls, she found it. A loose stone, recently mortared back into place with slightly different colored cement. Behind it, wrapped in oilcloth, was a metal box about the size of a hardcover book.

The lock was electronic, requiring a code. Eliza tried Margarette's birthday, then her own, then the date of her parents' accident. Nothing. She was about to give up when she noticed tiny scratches in the metal near the lock—almost like tally marks. Fifteen scratches. She tried her wedding anniversary: 0315.

The lock clicked open.

Inside, she found a letter in her great-aunt's precise handwriting, a small digital tablet wrapped in bubble wrap, and what appeared to be a government ID badge with Margarette's photo but an unfamiliar name: Dr. M. Voss, Department of Biological Contingencies.

"My dear Eliza," it began, "if you're reading this, then the Capra Protocol has been activated and I am likely dead. Eliza's hands trembled as she continued reading. Marcus had settled beside her on the basement floor, his presence oddly comforting despite the circumstances.

"The goats themselves are harmless—this was always intended as a temporary measure while the real plan unfolds. Within six months, if the Protocol is not reversed, a second phase will be activated. The transformed individuals will begin to deteriorate rapidly, their human consciousness fading until only the animal remains. This is not a bug in the system—it is a feature."

The faction responsible believed that reducing the global population by half would solve climate change, resource scarcity, and political instability in one decisive action.

"The tablet contains all the evidence I have gathered, as well as the technical specifications for reversing the Protocol.
