At forty-two, he had built a reputation as one of the city's most meticulous lawyers, known for his ability to navigate the labyrinthine world of Russian business law with surgical precision.

His office, situated on the fifteenth floor of a gleaming tower near Red Square, reflected his success. Dark mahogany furniture, leather-bound legal volumes, and awards from various bar associations lined the walls. Yet despite all his achievements, Viktor felt an inexplicable emptiness that seemed to grow more pronounced with each passing day. The victories in court, the substantial fees, the respect of his peers—none of it filled the void that had settled in his chest like a cold stone.

Viktor nodded absently, though the thought of another evening spent discussing market trends and political gossip filled him with dread. Anya had been increasingly distant lately, their conversations reduced to logistical exchanges about social obligations and household matters. As Elena retreated, Viktor's gaze wandered to the window again. Through the distorted glass, he could see construction cranes dotting the skyline like mechanical birds frozen mid-flight. Moscow was changing rapidly, old buildings giving way to modern structures, tradition yielding to progress. Sometimes he wondered if he was changing too, or if he had become trapped in amber like those ancient insects he had seen in museums as a child.

The intercom buzzed. "Yes?"

"Your three o'clock appointment is here, sir. The Komarovsky industrial case."

Viktor straightened his tie and prepared to immerse himself once again in the intricate dance of legal strategy. The Komarovsky case involved a dispute over mining rights in Siberia, with millions of rubles at stake. It was exactly the kind of high-profile matter that had built his career, yet as he reviewed his notes, he felt detached from it all, as if he were watching someone else's life unfold.

Viktor played his role perfectly, citing precedents and crafting arguments with the skill that had made him wealthy. The rain had stopped by the time he gathered his things to leave. Moscow's early evening light cast long shadows across his office, and for a moment, he paused to really look at the space that had been his second home for nearly two decades. The achievements displayed on the walls suddenly seemed like artifacts from someone else's life, monuments to a success that felt increasingly hollow.
