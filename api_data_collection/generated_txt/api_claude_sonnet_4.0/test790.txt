The morning mist clung to the cobblestone streets of Richmond Hill like a shroud, and Jennings Whitmore pressed his face against the cold windowpane of the townhouse that had never quite felt like home. His breath fogged the glass as he watched the lamplighter make his rounds, each flame casting dancing shadows that seemed to mock his restless spirit. At nineteen, he possessed a voice that could make angels weep and devils repent, yet here he was, trapped in a life that felt more like a carefully constructed prison than the blessing his stepfather insisted it to be.

"Jennings, come away from that window," called Margaret Whitmore from somewhere in the depths of the house. Her voice carried the particular strain that came from managing a household filled with secrets and unspoken tensions. "Your breakfast is getting cold, and you know how your father despises waste."

But Edmund Whitmore was not his father, would never be his father, despite the man's best efforts to claim ownership over every aspect of Jennings' existence. The marriage to his mother had been one of convenience, a transaction that elevated them from their modest circumstances into this world of morning rooms and calling cards, of expectations that sat heavy on his shoulders like an ill-fitting coat.

Jennings turned from the window, his dark hair catching the pale light that filtered through the curtains. The house was stirring to life around him, servants moving through the corridors with practiced silence, the familiar sounds of another day beginning in the Whitmore household. Yet something felt different this morning, charged with an electricity that made his skin prickle with anticipation.

The dining room was awash in golden sunlight when he entered, and there, seated at the mahogany table with a casualness that belied the storm he had brought into Jennings' carefully ordered world, was Thomas Whitmore. His stepbrother had returned from London without warning, without fanfare, as if three months of absence could be dismissed with nothing more than his presence at the breakfast table.

"Tommy," Jennings whispered, the name escaping his lips like a prayer. Thomas looked up from his morning paper, and their eyes met across the room with an intensity that could have ignited the very air between them. Time seemed to fracture, splitting into a thousand crystalline moments that scattered and reformed into something new, something dangerous.

"Hello, Jennings," Thomas replied, his voice deeper than Jennings remembered, roughened perhaps by London smoke and late nights spent in places their father would never approve of. "Did you miss me?"

The question hung in the air like incense, heavy with implications that neither of them dared voice in the bright morning light. "We all missed you," Jennings managed, though the words felt inadequate, a pale shadow of the truth that burned in his chest like swallowed fire. "Mother was worried. Father was... Father was Father."

Thomas laughed, a sound that sent shivers racing down Jennings' spine. "Yes, I can imagine Edmund's reaction to my sudden departure. Though I suspect he was secretly relieved to have one less complication in his perfectly ordered household."

They ate in companionable silence, the morning light shifting and changing around them as if the very world was holding its breath. Jennings found himself stealing glances at Thomas when he thought the other man wasn't looking, cataloguing the changes that three months had wrought. "Here?" Jennings asked, his voice barely above a whisper. "Now?"

"Always," Thomas replied, and the single word contained multitudes, promises and threats and declarations that neither of them was brave enough to speak aloud.

So Jennings sang, his voice rising and falling like ocean waves, like the breath of sleeping gods. When the last note faded into silence, Thomas was on his feet, moving around the table with purpose.
