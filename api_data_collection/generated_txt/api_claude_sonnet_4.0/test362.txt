The morning fog clung to the hills of San Francisco like a shroud, obscuring the city's familiar landmarks in a gray embrace that seemed almost deliberate. Mrs. Eleanor Nuttall adjusted her reading glasses as she stepped off the Greyhound bus at the downtown terminal, her sensible shoes clicking against the wet pavement. The substitute teaching assignment had come suddenly – a frantic phone call from the district office about a teacher who'd vanished without explanation, leaving behind only a half-eaten sandwich and a classroom full of confused teenagers.

Eleanor had driven up from Los Angeles overnight, her ancient Honda struggling with the mountain passes. At fifty-three, she'd seen enough troubled schools and difficult students to know when something felt wrong, and everything about this assignment screamed danger. The principal, Mr. Kowalski, had been evasive on the phone, mentioning only that the missing teacher, James Morrison, had been "dealing with personal issues" before his disappearance.

The taxi driver who picked her up from the terminal was unusually talkative, rambling about the strange happenings in the neighborhood where Lincoln High School sat nestled between run-down apartment buildings and small businesses that seemed to change hands every few months.

"Three young men gone missing in the past two months," the driver said, glancing at Eleanor in the rearview mirror. "All of them around eighteen, nineteen years old. Police say they probably just ran off, but their families ain't buying it. Kids these days don't just disappear without their phones, you know?"

Eleanor nodded politely, though something cold settled in her stomach. She'd spent thirty years teaching in some of Los Angeles's most challenging schools, and she'd developed an instinct for when danger lurked beneath the surface of seemingly ordinary situations. The missing teacher, the vanished young men – it felt connected somehow, though she couldn't yet see how.

Lincoln High School squatted like a concrete fortress against the gray sky, its windows reflecting nothing but emptiness. Eleanor paid the taxi driver and stood for a moment on the sidewalk, studying the building that would be her workplace for the next few weeks. Students moved past her in small groups, their voices subdued, their eyes darting nervously toward the shadows between buildings.

Inside, the hallways smelled of disinfectant and fear. Principal Kowalski met her at the main office, a thin man whose hands shook slightly as he handed her the classroom keys. His smile never reached his eyes, and Eleanor noticed how he kept glancing toward the windows as they talked.

"Mr. Morrison's classroom is on the third floor," Kowalski explained, his voice barely above a whisper. "Room 314. The students have been... unsettled since his departure. They're good kids, but they're scared. We all are."

"Scared of what?" Eleanor asked directly. She'd learned that principals often spoke in euphemisms, but fear was fear, and it deserved honest discussion.

Kowalski hesitated, then led her to his private office and closed the door. "There've been incidents. Strange phone calls to some of our male students. Someone's been following them home from school. And now with James missing..." He trailed off, rubbing his temples.

"Have you contacted the police?"

"Of course. Detective Martinez has been handling the missing persons cases, but there's no evidence of foul play. The boys just... disappeared. Different neighborhoods, different social circles. The only connection is their age and the fact that they all attended schools in this district at some point."

Eleanor accepted the classroom keys and her temporary ID badge, but her mind was already working through the implications. Thirty years of teaching had taught her to recognize patterns, and this felt like a pattern with teeth.

Room 314 still carried traces of James Morrison's presence – a coffee mug on the desk with a ring of mold around the bottom, student papers half-graded and abandoned, a small potted plant dying slowly on the windowsill. Eleanor spent her first hour cleaning and organizing, trying to make the space feel less like a crime scene and more like a place where learning could happen.

Her first class was senior English, and the twelve students who shuffled in looked like they'd been through a war. "I'm Mrs. Nuttall," she began, writing her name on the whiteboard in clear, bold letters. "I'll be your teacher until Mr. Morrison returns or until the district finds a permanent replacement."

She had spent much of the previous evening researching the missing persons cases online, cross-referencing news articles with the notes Mrs. Chen had shared with her. The three young men who had disappeared—David Restrepo, Marcus Williams, and Tony Valdez—had more in common than just their age and connection to the school district. What she found was troubling—over the past two years, there had been a pattern of young men disappearing from working-class neighborhoods throughout the state. The disappearances seemed to follow a geographic progression, moving slowly northward from Los Angeles through the Central Valley and now apparently reaching San Francisco.

Armed with this information and a growing sense of urgency, Eleanor arrived at school thirty minutes earlier than required. She wanted to observe the morning routine, to see how students arrived at the building and whether anyone was watching them. The early morning fog provided excellent cover for surveillance, both for her and potentially for anyone with malicious intent.

She positioned herself in the faculty parking lot with a cup of coffee from the gas station across the street, using the pretense of organizing her teaching materials in her car while actually studying the flow of students arriving for classes. Most came on public transportation or were dropped off by family members, but a significant number walked to school through the surrounding neighborhood streets.

It was during this observation period that Eleanor noticed something that made her blood run cold. The same man she had seen the previous day was back, but this time he had changed his position. Instead of standing across the street from the main entrance, he was sitting in a battered white van parked at the corner of the block, partially obscured by the overhang of a large oak tree that someone had planted decades ago when the neighborhood had been more prosperous.

From his new vantage point, the man had a clear view of three different routes that students used to approach the school building. The most disturbing aspect of his surveillance was its systematic nature. This was not the random behavior of someone with a casual or impulsive interest in young men—this was the methodical data collection of someone who was planning something specific and potentially very dangerous. Eleanor realized that she was witnessing the early stages of whatever process had led to the disappearances of David, Marcus, and Tony.

After the morning bell rang and the last of the students had entered the building, Eleanor expected the man to leave, but he remained in position. She decided to take a calculated risk and approach his vehicle directly, reasoning that her appearance as a middle-aged woman in professional clothing would not immediately alert him to any danger.
