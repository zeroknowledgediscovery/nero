Marcus Hendricks had always believed that the sky was his sanctuary, a place where earth-bound troubles dissolved into wisps of cloud and shadow. From the cockpit of his Cessna Citation, thirty thousand feet above the Ohio countryside, the world below looked manageable—neat squares of farmland, ribbons of highway threading through miniature towns, and somewhere down there, Westerville, his hometown that seemed both impossibly small and suffocatingly familiar from this altitude.

The radio crackled with air traffic control instructions, but Marcus barely heard them. His hands moved across the instrument panel with practiced precision, adjusting altitude and heading with the unconscious competence of someone who had been flying for fifteen years. Commercial aviation had been good to him—predictable routes, steady pay, and the kind of respect that came with wearing captain's stripes. But lately, something had changed, something that made his stomach clench with a dread that had nothing to do with turbulence or weather conditions.

It had started three weeks ago on a routine flight from Columbus to Denver. A passenger in first class had suffered what appeared to be a minor nosebleed, nothing dramatic or particularly alarming. The flight attendant, Jennifer, had handled it with typical professionalism, offering tissues and reassurance. But when Marcus had glanced back during a routine cabin check, he had seen the small dark spots on the passenger's white shirt, and the world had tilted sideways in a way that had nothing to do with the aircraft's movement.

The blood—just a few drops, really—had seemed to pulse with its own malevolent life. His vision had tunneled, his breathing had become shallow and rapid, and for a terrifying moment, he had felt as though he might pass out right there in the aisle. He had managed to make it back to the cockpit, where his co-pilot, Dave Morrison, had taken one look at his pale face and asked if he needed medical attention.

"Just tired," Marcus had lied, though his hands were shaking as he strapped himself back into the captain's seat. "Been pulling too many doubles."

Dave had nodded sympathetically, but Marcus had caught him stealing concerned glances for the rest of the flight. Word traveled fast in the aviation community, and Marcus knew that any sign of physical or mental instability could ground a pilot permanently. The Federal Aviation Administration didn't take chances with passenger safety, and neither did the airlines. One report of erratic behavior, one failed medical examination, and his career could be over.

Since that incident, Marcus had found himself hyper-aware of anything that might trigger another episode. A passenger's scraped knuckle, a child's bloody lip from a fall in the terminal, even the sight of rare steak in the crew lounge—all of it sent waves of nausea washing over him. He had started avoiding medical dramas on television, steering clear of action movies, and making excuses to avoid social gatherings where injuries might occur.

The irony wasn't lost on him. As a pilot, he was trained to handle emergencies, to remain calm under pressure, to make life-or-death decisions with clarity and precision. Yet the sight of something as commonplace as blood—something that every human being carried gallons of—had become his kryptonite. He had tried to rationalize it, to trace its origins to some childhood trauma or repressed memory, but the fear seemed to have materialized out of nowhere, as sudden and inexplicable as a summer thunderstorm.

Now, as he began his descent into Port Columbus International Airport, Marcus found himself dreading the post-flight routine. There would be passengers to greet, baggage to oversee, and the ever-present possibility that someone might have a minor accident or medical issue. He had become expert at delegating such situations to his crew, but he worried that his colleagues were beginning to notice his reluctance to handle certain types of emergencies.

The landing was smooth, textbook perfect, and Marcus felt a momentary surge of professional satisfaction. Whatever was happening to him psychologically, his flying skills remained intact. He guided the aircraft to the gate with practiced ease, running through the shutdown checklist with mechanical precision. It wasn't until he was gathering his flight bag and preparing to leave the cockpit that Dave brought up what Marcus had been dreading.

"Hey, Cap," Dave said, his tone carefully casual, "you might want to check on Mrs. Patterson in 4A. She took a pretty good knock to the head when we hit that patch of turbulence over Indiana. Nothing serious, but she's got a small cut on her forehead."

Marcus felt his mouth go dry. "Is Jennifer handling it?"

"She asked if you could take a look. Passenger specifically requested to speak with the captain." Dave was watching him closely now, and Marcus knew that his reaction was being evaluated. In the close-knit world of commercial aviation, crew members looked out for each other, but they also had an obligation to report anything that might compromise flight safety.

"Of course," Marcus managed, though his voice sounded strained even to his own ears. "Just... give me a minute to finish up here."

Dave nodded and headed toward the cabin, leaving Marcus alone with his rising panic. He closed his eyes and tried the breathing exercises he had learned from a stress management seminar years earlier. In for four counts, hold for seven, out for eight. The technique had always worked for pre-flight jitters or difficult weather conditions, but it seemed pitifully inadequate against this new enemy.

When he finally made his way into the passenger cabin, Mrs. Patterson was easy to spot. She was an elderly woman in a burgundy sweater, holding a blood-stained tissue to her forehead while Jennifer knelt beside her seat offering first aid supplies. The sight of the crimson stain on the white tissue hit Marcus like a physical blow. His knees went weak, and he had to grab the back of a nearby seat to keep from falling.

"Captain Hendricks," Mrs. Patterson said with a grateful smile, "I just wanted to thank you for such a smooth flight. This little bump is nothing—I've had worse injuries gardening."

Marcus tried to respond, but the words wouldn't come. The blood on the tissue seemed to be spreading, growing larger and more vivid with each passing second. His peripheral vision began to darken, and he could hear his heartbeat thundering in his ears. Somewhere in the distance, he heard Jennifer asking if he was all right, her voice tinged with concern and confusion.

The next thing he knew, he was sitting in an airport wheelchair with Dave and Jennifer hovering over him like anxious parents. A paramedic was checking his pulse while asking questions about his medical history. The passenger cabin was empty now, and Marcus realized with mortification that he must have caused quite a scene.

"I'm fine," he insisted, though his voice was still shaky. "Just got a little dizzy. Probably low blood sugar."

The paramedic, a middle-aged man with kind eyes and steady hands, wasn't buying it. "Your vitals are stable now, but your blood pressure was pretty elevated when we first got here. Any history of panic attacks or anxiety disorders?"

Marcus shook his head, though he was beginning to suspect that's exactly what he was dealing with. "Nothing like that. I've been flying for fifteen years without any problems."

"Well, stress can manifest in different ways at different times in our lives," the paramedic said diplomatically. "Might not hurt to follow up with your doctor, just to rule out any underlying issues."

As the paramedic packed up his equipment, Marcus caught sight of Dave and Jennifer exchanging worried glances. He knew what they were thinking—the same thing he would be thinking if he witnessed a fellow pilot collapse during a routine passenger interaction. Questions would be asked, reports would be filed, and his fitness for duty would be scrutinized by people who had never met him but held his career in their hands.

Marcus lived alone in a modest two-bedroom house on the outskirts of town, a place he had bought during his first year as a commercial pilot when the future had seemed bright and uncomplicated. The house sat on a quiet street lined with maple trees, the kind of neighborhood where people still waved to each other from their front porches and children rode bicycles without helmets.

He sat in his leather recliner, the one extravagance he had allowed himself when he got his captain's stripes, and tried to make sense of what was happening to him.

Marcus managed to sound normal during their conversation, deflecting her questions about his love life and career with practiced ease. "Listen, Marcus," Dave said as their conversation wound down, "if you need to talk to someone, I know a guy. Dr. Peterson. He works with a lot of pilots, understands the pressures we face. Very discreet."

Marcus appreciated the gesture, but the suggestion that he might need professional help felt like another step toward the end of his flying career. "Thanks, Dave. I'm sure it was just a one-time thing. Too much caffeine, not enough sleep—you know how it is."

"Yeah, sure," Dave replied, but Marcus could hear the doubt in his voice. "Just... take care of yourself, okay? And maybe consider taking a few personal days. You've got plenty of vacation time built up."

After hanging up, Marcus found himself staring at his reflection in the darkened window. At thirty-eight, he looked older than his years, with premature gray threading through his brown hair and lines around his eyes that spoke of too many early morning flights and late-night landings. He had always prided himself on being dependable, steady, the kind of man who could be counted on in a crisis. Now he felt like a fraud, a pilot who couldn't handle the sight of a passenger's minor injury.

By morning, he felt exhausted and hollow, like a shell of his former self.The morning brought no relief from the previous day's anxieties. The headlines swam before his eyes—a story about a car accident on Route 23, a feature about the local blood drive at Westerville High School, even an advertisement for a vampire movie at the multiplex. The day you stop being a little bit afraid is the day you become dangerous." At the time, Marcus had understood this as wisdom about weather conditions, mechanical failures, and the hundred other things that could go wrong in an aircraft. He had never imagined that his greatest fear would turn out to be something so basic, so human, as the sight of blood.
