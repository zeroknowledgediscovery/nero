Marcus Thornfield had been grinding pills and counting capsules for seventeen years when the revelation struck him like lightning on that humid Tuesday morning in Round Rock. The fluorescent lights of Willow Creek Pharmacy buzzed overhead as he mechanically filled prescription bottles, their amber surfaces catching the artificial gleam. His hands moved with practiced precision, but his mind wandered to places far removed from blood pressure medications and antihistamines.

The words felt hollow, meaningless, like echoes in an empty cathedral. He found himself staring at the small crucifix that hung beside his pharmacy degree, a gift from his late grandmother that he'd never given much thought to before.

"Are you alright, dear?" Mrs. Henderson asked, her weathered face creasing with concern. "You look like you've seen a ghost."

Marcus blinked, refocusing on the elderly woman before him. "I'm fine, Mrs. Henderson. Just tired, I suppose." But he wasn't fine, and he wasn't merely tired. Something fundamental had shifted, like tectonic plates moving beneath his carefully constructed life.

That evening, Marcus sat in his modest apartment above the pharmacy, staring at his reflection in the bathroom mirror. At forty-two, his dark hair showed threads of silver at the temples, and lines had begun to etch themselves around his green eyes. He'd always been tall and lean, but lately, he seemed to carry himself with a perpetual stoop, as if the weight of unfulfilled purpose pressed down upon his shoulders.

His phone buzzed with a text from his best friend, Danny Reeves. "Beer tonight? The usual spot?" Marcus typed back a quick decline, claiming exhaustion. The truth was more complicated. How could he explain to Danny, who managed the local hardware store and seemed perfectly content with his straightforward existence, that he was questioning everything he'd built over the past two decades?

The next morning brought no relief from the strange restlessness that had settled over Marcus like a shroud. He moved through his routine with mechanical precision, greeting customers, filling prescriptions, and offering health advice, but his heart wasn't in it. During his lunch break, he found himself walking past St. Mary's Catholic Church, its red brick facade and white steeple reaching toward the Texas sky with quiet dignity.

On impulse, he pushed open the heavy wooden doors and stepped into the cool, incense-scented interior. The afternoon light filtered through stained glass windows, casting rainbow patterns across the polished pews. Marcus had attended church sporadically throughout his adult life, usually on major holidays or when his conscience pricked him sufficiently. But now, sitting in the back pew, he felt something he couldn't quite name stirring in his chest.

"Marcus, isn't it? From the pharmacy down on Main Street?" Father Rodriguez's voice carried a slight accent, a remnant of his childhood in San Antonio.

"Yes, Father. I hope you don't mind my being here. I know it's not Sunday." Marcus felt oddly self-conscious, like a child caught somewhere he didn't belong.

"God's house is always open, my son. You're welcome here anytime." Father Rodriguez settled into the pew beside him. "Is everything alright? You seem troubled."

"Have you ever considered that God might be calling you to something different?" Father Rodriguez asked finally. "Sometimes He speaks to us through that very restlessness you describe."
