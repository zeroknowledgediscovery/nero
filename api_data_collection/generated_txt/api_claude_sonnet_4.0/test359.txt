The morning sun cast long shadows across the desolate cityscape, illuminating thousands upon thousands of brown and black forms moving through the abandoned streets like a living carpet. Sarah pressed her face against the cracked window of her third-floor hideout, watching the endless procession of apes below. Chimpanzees swung from streetlight to streetlight, gorillas lumbered through intersections that once bustled with human traffic, and countless smaller primates scurried between the ruins of civilization.

It had been three years since the Great Migration, when seemingly overnight every ape, monkey, and primate on Earth had descended upon the human settlements. Nobody understood why they came or how they coordinated such a massive movement. Scientists had theories before they disappeared – climate change, habitat destruction, some kind of evolutionary leap. But Sarah didn't care about theories anymore. She only cared about survival, and lately, survival meant making impossible choices.

"Mama," whispered Emma from behind her, the ten-year-old's voice barely audible over the constant chattering from the streets. "Are we going outside today?"

Sarah's jaw tightened. Her daughter still held onto hope that things would return to normal, that they could walk freely again without fear of being overwhelmed by the sheer numbers pressing in from all sides. But Sarah knew better. She had seen what desperation could drive a person to do, had felt the darkness creeping into her own thoughts with each passing day of confinement.

"Not today, sweetheart," Sarah replied, though her mind was already calculating, always calculating. The food supplies were running dangerously low, and their water collection system had failed during last week's storm. Something had to change, and soon.

Emma padded over on bare feet, her ribs showing through the thin fabric of her oversized t-shirt. "I'm hungry, Mama."

Those three words hit Sarah like physical blows. She had failed in the most fundamental way – she couldn't provide for her child. The weight of that failure pressed down on her shoulders, mixing with something darker that had been growing inside her chest for months now. A cold, calculating voice that whispered solutions she didn't want to hear.

"I know, baby. I know." Sarah knelt down and pulled Emma close, breathing in the scent of her daughter's unwashed hair. "Mama's working on it."

But working on what, exactly? The apes had eaten or destroyed most of the remaining food sources in the city. The few other human survivors she'd encountered had either fled or turned hostile when resources became scarce. And Sarah herself... well, she was beginning to realize that the person she used to be might not be equipped to handle this new world.

The voice in her head, the one that had started as a whisper but now spoke with increasing clarity, suggested that maybe the old Sarah had been too weak anyway. Too bound by conventional morality and social expectations. This new world demanded a different kind of person – someone willing to cross lines that the old Sarah never would have approached.

A commotion outside drew her attention back to the window. A group of orangutans had discovered an overturned delivery truck and were methodically unpacking its contents, examining each item with what looked like curiosity rather than aggression. Sarah watched one of them hold up a can of soup, turn it over several times, then gently place it in a growing pile with other canned goods.

"They're... organizing it," she murmured to herself.

This wasn't the first time she'd noticed behavior like this. The apes weren't just mindlessly consuming everything in their path. They seemed to be cataloging, sorting, almost like they were taking inventory rather than pillaging. It was strange, but Sarah's growing obsession with immediate survival had left little room for wondering about ape psychology.

Emma joined her at the window, standing on her tiptoes to see over the sill. "Look, Mama, that one's being gentle with the pretty bottles."

She was right. A young chimpanzee had found what looked like perfume bottles and was carefully arranging them in a neat row, occasionally lifting one to catch the light. There was something almost... respectful about the way it handled the items.

"Emma," she said slowly, her voice taking on a strange, distant quality. "Come away from the window."

Emma was small, weak from malnutrition. She wouldn't survive much longer anyway, not without food, not without hope of rescue.
