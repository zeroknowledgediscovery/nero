The rain hammered against the windows of Lincoln Elementary with the persistence of tiny fists, each droplet carrying the weight of autumn's dying breath. Inside classroom 3B, Mrs. Henderson adjusted her reading glasses and surveyed the empty desks that would soon fill with the chatter and energy of twenty-three third-graders. She had no way of knowing that across town, Guy Marlowe was sharpening his collection of knives with the methodical precision of someone who had found his calling.

Guy's basement workshop smelled of metal polish and something else—something organic that would make most people's stomachs turn. But Guy breathed it in like perfume. The fluorescent light above his workbench flickered intermittently, casting dancing shadows across newspaper clippings he had taped to the wall. Headlines screamed about teacher shortages, education budget cuts, and the declining state of public schools. To most people, these were societal problems. To Guy, they were opportunities.

His fingers traced the edge of his favorite blade, a German steel butcher's knife he had inherited from his grandfather. The old man had used it to carve Sunday roasts and Thanksgiving turkeys. Guy had found a different purpose for it entirely. The irony wasn't lost on him—his grandfather had always said education was the key to success. Guy had simply redefined what success meant.

The transformation hadn't happened overnight. Three years ago, Guy Marlowe had been just another face in the crowd, working a dead-end job at a packaging plant, watching his dreams evaporate like morning dew. The plant had closed down during the recession, leaving him with nothing but severance pay that barely covered two months of rent. That's when the hunger had started—not just for food, though that had been part of it, but for something more substantial. Something that would fill the hollow ache that had taken residence in his chest.

The first time had been almost accidental. Mrs. Patel from the local high school had been jogging through Riverside Park, the same route she took every Tuesday and Thursday. Guy had been sitting on a bench, feeding stale breadcrumbs to pigeons and contemplating whether his life insurance policy would cover suicide. She had stopped to pet a stray dog, and something about her kindness had triggered a response he couldn't quite explain. Before he knew what he was doing, his hands were around her throat.

What happened next had surprised him more than the murder itself. As he dragged her body deeper into the woods, his mind had begun calculating. Mrs. Patel had been wearing a Rolex—probably a gift from her husband for their anniversary. Her purse contained three hundred and forty-seven dollars in cash and credit cards that might be useful before they were reported stolen. But it was what he discovered when he got her home that truly opened his eyes to the possibilities.

Teachers, Guy realized, were perfect victims. They were trusted members of the community, often working late, frequently alone. They carried themselves with a certain vulnerability that came from spending their days nurturing others. Most importantly, they were undervalued by society—underpaid, overworked, and largely invisible until they were gone. Who would really notice if a few went missing? The school system would simply hire replacements, probably at lower salaries.

Mrs. Henderson finished arranging the day's worksheets and glanced at the clock. Seven-fifteen. The children wouldn't arrive for another hour, but she liked having time to prepare. She had been teaching for eighteen years, and the routine brought her comfort. Coffee first, then a review of the lesson plan, followed by writing the daily schedule on the whiteboard in her careful cursive handwriting.

She had no way of knowing that Guy had been watching her for three weeks, learning her patterns. He knew she arrived early every Tuesday. He knew she parked in the same spot, walked through the same entrance, and always stopped at the main office to chat with Principal Morrison about weekend plans or student concerns. Guy knew about her cat, Mr. Whiskers, who had diabetes and required twice-daily insulin shots. He knew about her elderly mother in the assisted living facility across town, and her weekly visits every Sunday after church.

Most importantly, Guy knew that Mrs. Henderson lived alone in a small cottage on Elm Street, with neighbors who minded their own business and a backyard that backed up to undeveloped woodland. Perfect for his purposes.

The basement workshop had evolved over the past three years into something that would have impressed a forensic pathologist. Guy had invested his victims' assets wisely—stolen jewelry sold to pawn shops in distant cities, credit cards used strategically before cancellation, and cash hoarded with the discipline of a monk. The profits had allowed him to purchase equipment that most amateur butchers could only dream of: professional-grade meat grinders, vacuum sealers, and a chest freezer large enough to store significant quantities of processed protein.

Guy had always been methodical, even as a child. His mother used to joke that he organized his toy cars by color and year, creating elaborate parking lots in the sandbox behind their house. Now that same attention to detail served him well in his chosen profession.
