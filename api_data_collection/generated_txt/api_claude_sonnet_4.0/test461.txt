The morning mist clung to Victoria Harbour like a shroud, obscuring the familiar skyline that had welcomed Mei-Lin Chen home each day for the past fifteen years. Standing at her apartment window on the forty-second floor, she pressed her palm against the cool glass and watched the ferries cut through the grey water below. Once, the sight of those vessels dancing across the harbour had filled her with a sense of belonging, of being part of something larger than herself. Now, her stomach churned with an inexplicable dread.

It had started three months ago during what should have been a routine swimming lesson at the Wan Chai Sports Complex. Mei-Lin had been teaching her Year 8 students the backstroke when suddenly the chlorinated water seemed to close in around her like liquid concrete. Her breathing became shallow, her vision blurred, and panic seized her chest with iron claws. The children's laughter echoed strangely in the cavernous pool area as she hauled herself onto the deck, water streaming from her teaching suit, her hands trembling uncontrollably.

"Miss Chen, are you alright?" Timothy Wong, one of her brightest students, had asked with genuine concern etched across his young face. She had managed to nod, forcing a reassuring smile while her heart hammered against her ribs.

That incident should have been isolated, a momentary lapse brought on by exhaustion or stress. But the fear had grown like a malignant vine, spreading through her consciousness until even the sound of running taps made her palms sweat. Showers became hurried ordeals of barely contained anxiety. The daily ferry ride to Central, which she had enjoyed for over a decade, now required careful timing to secure a seat away from the windows, where the vast expanse of water couldn't taunt her peripheral vision.

Mei-Lin turned away from her apartment window and moved toward the kitchen, where her morning routine awaited. The kettle sat on the counter, innocuous and familiar, yet she found herself staring at it with apprehension. Water was everywhere, unavoidable, essential to life itself. How could she have developed such an irrational terror of something so fundamental?

Her mobile phone buzzed against the marble countertop, displaying a message from her colleague, Janet Lau: "Ready for another thrilling day molding young minds? Coffee before first period?" Janet's perpetual optimism usually lifted Mei-Lin's spirits, but today even her friend's cheerful emoji felt like an accusation. How could she explain that the woman who had once led weekend hiking trips to waterfalls and organized annual swimming competitions now couldn't bear to look at a glass of water without feeling nauseated?

The commute to St. Margaret's International School typically took forty-five minutes, a journey Mei-Lin had previously savored for its meditative quality. Today, she deliberately chose the longer route through the Mid-Levels, avoiding the harbour-front roads that would force her to confront the endless stretch of water. The taxi driver, a weathered man in his sixties, attempted friendly conversation about the unseasonably humid October weather, but Mei-Lin found herself fixated on the windshield wipers and the possibility of sudden rain.

"You look worried, Miss," the driver observed, catching her eye in the rearview mirror. "First time visiting Hong Kong?"

"No," she replied quietly, "I've lived here all my life."

"Ah, then you know our weather. Nothing to fear from a little water, lah."

His words struck her like a physical blow, though he couldn't have known their significance. Nothing to fear from a little water. If only it were that simple. The irony wasn't lost on her that she had chosen to make her home on an island, surrounded entirely by the very element that now terrorized her.

St. Margaret's International School occupied a prestigious location on the Peak, its colonial architecture speaking to Hong Kong's complex history. Mei-Lin had always felt proud to teach there, proud of her students' achievements and her own reputation as one of the most dedicated educators on staff. But as she approached the main entrance, she noticed the new fountain that had been installed over the summer holidays. Water cascaded down a series of granite tiers, creating what the school board had described as a "calming and inspirational centerpiece for our academic community."

The sound hit her before the sight did. That gentle splashing, the musical quality of water meeting stone, sent a wave of panic through her nervous system. Her breathing became shallow and rapid, her vision narrowed to a tunnel, and she felt as though she might collapse right there on the manicured pathway. Students streamed around her, chattering in English, Cantonese, and Mandarin, their voices seeming to come from very far away.

"Mei-Lin!" Janet's voice cut through the fog of anxiety. "There you are. I was starting to worry when you didn't respond to my text."

Mei-Lin turned toward her colleague and oldest friend, attempting to compose her expression into something resembling normalcy. Janet Lau had been her confidante since their university days at Hong Kong University, where they had both studied education with dreams of making a difference in young people's lives. Janet's round face was creased with concern, her usual bright smile replaced by a frown of genuine worry.

"I'm fine," Mei-Lin managed, though her voice sounded unconvincing even to herself. "Just tired. Didn't sleep well last night."

"You've been saying that a lot lately," Janet observed, linking their arms in the casual way she had done since they were twenty-two. "Everything okay at home? Problems with your parents?"

The concern in Janet's voice made Mei-Lin's chest tighten with guilt. Her friend deserved honesty, but how could she explain something she didn't understand herself? How could she admit that a successful, rational woman in her late thirties had suddenly developed a phobia so severe that she couldn't even look at photographs of the ocean without feeling physically ill?

The discussion that followed was everything she could have hoped for pedagogically. Her students engaged with the poetry on multiple levels, drawing connections between the metaphorical use of water and their own experiences of displacement and adaptation.
