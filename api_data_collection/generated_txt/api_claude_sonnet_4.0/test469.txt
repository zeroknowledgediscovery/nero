The morning sun cast long shadows across the dusty streets of Kibera as Jonas Mwangi adjusted his worn leather satchel and checked his watch for the third time. He was never late for his tutoring sessions, a trait that had earned him a reputation among Nairobi's middle-class families as reliable, if somewhat intense. At twenty-four, Jonas carried himself with the quiet confidence of someone who had learned early that knowledge was the only currency that couldn't be stolen from you.

The matatu lurched to a stop near Valley Arcade, and Jonas stepped off, navigating through the familiar chaos of vendors, schoolchildren, and office workers beginning their day. His destination was a modest house in Karen, where he had been tutoring the Kimani family's children for the past eight months. What made this job different from his others wasn't the pay, though it was generous enough to cover his rent and send money home to his mother in Kisumu. What made it different was David.

David Kimani wasn't technically his step-brother yet, but the wedding was only three months away. Jonas's mother, Grace, had met Dr. Samuel Kimani at a church conference in Nakuru, and their whirlwind romance had surprised everyone, including Jonas. Grace, who had raised Jonas alone after his father died when he was seven, had never shown interest in remarrying. But Samuel was different—gentle, educated, and most importantly, he treated Grace like the remarkable woman she was.

The irony wasn't lost on Jonas that he had been tutoring Samuel's son months before their parents even met. When Grace had shyly introduced her fiancé at a family dinner, Jonas had nearly choked on his ugali. The universe, it seemed, had a sense of humor that bordered on cruelty.

"Jonas!" David's voice carried across the compound as Jonas approached the house. At nineteen, David possessed an easy charm that Jonas both envied and found irresistible. Where Jonas was all sharp angles and careful words, David was warm curves and spontaneous laughter. They were study in contrasts—Jonas with his serious demeanor and secondhand shirts, David with his casual confidence and designer sneakers.

"You're early today," David observed, jogging over to meet him. His smile was the same one that had been haunting Jonas's dreams for weeks now, the same smile that made Jonas's carefully constructed world feel simultaneously more complete and more precarious.

"Traffic was lighter than usual," Jonas replied, fighting the urge to stare too long at the way David's school uniform shirt had come untucked on one side. "Are you ready for the calculus exam next week?"

David groaned dramatically, throwing his head back. "Do we have to start with the difficult questions? I haven't even had breakfast yet. Come on, Mary made mandazi this morning."

The domestic ease with which David invited him in never stopped feeling surreal to Jonas. Soon, this would be his family too, in the complicated way that blended families worked. David would be his step-brother, and these feelings—these impossible, consuming feelings—would become not just unrequited but forbidden.

In the kitchen, Mary, the house help, greeted Jonas with the same warmth she showed David. "Hujambo, Jonas. The mandazi are still warm."

"Asante, Mary." Jonas accepted the sweet bread gratefully. He had learned early in his tutoring career that refusing hospitality could offend, even when his stomach was already knotted with anxiety about spending the morning alone with David.

They settled into their usual spot in the sitting room, books spread across the coffee table, the scent of Mary's cooking drifting in from the kitchen. For a few minutes, Jonas could almost pretend this was normal—that he was just a tutor helping a student prepare for university entrance exams, that his pulse didn't quicken every time David leaned close to look at a problem, that he didn't memorize every casual touch of David's hand against his own.

"I still don't understand why we need to learn about limits," David said, tapping his pen against the textbook. "When am I ever going to use this in real life?"

"Mathematics isn't just about practical application," Jonas replied, settling into the familiar rhythm of teaching. "It's about learning to think logically, to break down complex problems into manageable parts. These skills transfer to everything—business, medicine, even relationships."

"Not everything," Jonas said carefully. "Some things can't be solved with equations."

"Like what?"

The question hung in the air between them. Jonas could feel the weight of all the things he couldn't say, all the feelings he couldn't acknowledge. In three months, they would be family. In three months, whatever this was between them would have to end before it properly began.

David smiled, but there was something sad in it. "Maybe that's what makes them interesting."

"Jonas," David said suddenly, setting down his pen. "Can I ask you something?"

Jonas's heart began to race, but he kept his expression neutral. "Of course."

"I think they're happy together. Your father is good to my mother."

The word hit Jonas like a physical blow. Brothers. It was the reality he had been trying not to think about, the future that would make these feelings not just impossible but wrong.
