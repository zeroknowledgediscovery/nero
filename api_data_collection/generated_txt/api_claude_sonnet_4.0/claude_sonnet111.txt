The morning sun cast long shadows across the training track at the Bandung Sports Complex as Sari Andini finished her final sprint. Sweat dripped from her forehead onto the red clay surface, each drop marking another step toward her Olympic dream. At twenty-two, she had already broken three national records in the 400-meter dash, and whispers of international recognition followed her everywhere she went.

"Excellent form today, Sari," called out Coach Hartono from the sidelines, his weathered face creased with approval. "Your time was two seconds faster than last month."

Sari nodded, still catching her breath. The cool mountain air of Bandung filled her lungs as she walked slowly around the track, allowing her heart rate to return to normal. She loved these early morning sessions when the complex was nearly empty, save for a few dedicated athletes and their coaches. The tranquility helped her focus, pushing away the constant pressure that came with being Indonesia's rising track star.

As she bent down to retie her spikes, something caught her eye near the starting blocks. A small, dark shape scurried across the white painted line. Sari's blood ran cold as she recognized the distinctive movement—a spider, its legs moving with that particular jerky motion that made her stomach lurch. She stumbled backward, nearly losing her balance, her heart suddenly pounding harder than it had during her sprint.

"What's wrong?" Coach Hartono approached with concern evident in his voice. "You look pale."

Sari couldn't take her eyes off the spider as it disappeared into a crack in the track surface. Her hands trembled slightly as she stood up, brushing imaginary dirt from her running shorts. "Nothing, Coach. Just... tired, I think."

But it wasn't nothing, and they both knew it. This had been happening more frequently over the past few weeks. What had once been a mild discomfort around spiders had transformed into something more debilitating. During her last competition in Jakarta, she had nearly missed her heat because she'd spotted a web in the warm-up area and couldn't bring herself to approach the starting blocks.

The drive home through Bandung's bustling streets usually calmed Sari, but today her mind raced with worry. She gripped the steering wheel of her modest Honda Civic tighter than necessary, navigating through the familiar chaos of motorcycles, angkots, and street vendors. The city she'd grown up in felt simultaneously comforting and overwhelming—a place where everyone knew her story, her struggles, and increasingly, her fears.

Her phone buzzed with a text message from her manager, Pak Rahman: "Nike wants to meet next week. Big sponsorship opportunity. Don't mess this up." The message should have filled her with excitement, but instead, it only added to the weight pressing down on her shoulders. What if her spider phobia interfered with the meeting? What if they wanted to film a commercial outdoors where she might encounter webs?

Sari pulled into the driveway of the modest two-story house she shared with her mother and step-brother. The house, painted a fading yellow with terracotta roof tiles, had been home for the past eight years since her mother remarried. It wasn't much, but it was theirs, and the small garden in front always made her smile—except today, when she noticed the intricate web stretched between two rose bushes near the front door.

She parked as far from the garden as possible and hurried inside, keeping her eyes fixed straight ahead. The familiar scent of her mother's cooking—rendang simmering slowly on the stove—usually welcomed her home, but today even comfort felt elusive.

"Sari, is that you?" her mother, Ibu Lestari, called from the kitchen. "How was training?"

"Good, Ma," Sari replied, forcing cheerfulness into her voice as she climbed the stairs to her room. She couldn't bring herself to mention the spider incident again. Her mother had already suggested she see a therapist, but the idea of admitting weakness—especially now, when everything was finally falling into place professionally—felt impossible.

Her room, decorated with medals, ribbons, and photographs from various competitions, usually inspired her. The walls told the story of her journey from a small-town girl with big dreams to Indonesia's fastest female sprinter. But today, she found herself checking every corner for webs before sitting down on her bed.

The sound of coughing from the next room reminded her of another ongoing struggle in their household. Her step-brother Adi had been battling lymphoma for the past six months, and the chemotherapy treatments had left him weak and confined to bed most days. At nineteen, he should have been starting university, not fighting for his life. The irony wasn't lost on Sari—she was afraid of tiny spiders while her brother faced something truly terrifying with remarkable courage.

A soft knock on her door interrupted her thoughts. "Kak Sari? Are you okay?" Adi's voice was hoarse but concerned. Despite his own struggles, he always seemed to sense when she was troubled.

"Come in, Adi," she said, grateful for the distraction. Her step-brother appeared in the doorway, thin from the treatments but with eyes still bright with determination. His bald head, once covered with thick black hair, was wrapped in a colorful bandana their mother had bought to cheer him up.

"You look worried," he observed, settling carefully into the chair at her desk. "Bad training session?"

Sari hesitated. Adi was the only person who didn't treat her like a fragile celebrity or a commodity to be managed. To him, she was simply his older sister, flaws and all. "Not exactly. I... I had another episode with the spider thing."

Adi nodded understandingly. He'd been there during her first panic attack three months ago when a large spider had appeared in their shared bathroom. While everyone else had dismissed it as a temporary quirk, he'd recognized something deeper troubling her.

"Did you tell Coach Hartono?" he asked gently.

"How can I? The Olympics are less than two years away. Nike wants to sponsor me. Everyone's counting on me to succeed." Sari ran her hands through her hair, still damp with sweat. "I can't be the athlete who's afraid of spiders. It's ridiculous."

"It's not ridiculous if it's affecting your life," Adi replied with the wisdom that seemed to come from facing mortality so young. "Remember when I was first diagnosed? I was embarrassed to tell my friends. I thought it made me weak."

Sari looked at her step-brother, this young man who'd shown more courage in six months than most people displayed in a lifetime. "That's completely different, Adi. You're fighting cancer. I'm afraid of tiny creatures."

"Fear is fear, Kak. It doesn't matter if other people think it's justified." He paused, struggling slightly with a cough before continuing. "Besides, you're not just any person. You're carrying the hopes of a lot of people. That's a different kind of pressure."

Their conversation was interrupted by their mother calling them for lunch. As they made their way downstairs, Sari felt a familiar mixture of gratitude and guilt. Gratitude for Adi's understanding, and guilt that she was struggling with something so seemingly trivial while he faced life-threatening challenges daily.

The dining room table was set with their mother's best dishes—a sign that she was trying to maintain normalcy despite the circumstances. Ibu Lestari, a woman in her mid-forties with graying hair always pulled back in a neat bun, had worked as a seamstress for most of her adult life. The long hours at her sewing machine had paid for Sari's early coaching and equipment, sacrifices that weighed heavily on the young athlete's mind.

"How's my champion today?" her step-father, Pak Budi, asked as he entered the dining room. A quiet man who worked as a mechanic at a local garage, he'd always treated Sari as his own daughter, never making her feel like an outsider in the family he'd formed with her mother.

"Training went well," Sari replied diplomatically, accepting a plate of rice and rendang. "Coach thinks I'm improving."

"The newspaper mentioned you again yesterday," Ibu Lestari said proudly, settling into her chair. "They called you 'Indonesia's golden hope.'"

Sari forced a smile, but the phrase felt like another weight added to her shoulders. Golden hope. What would they call her if they knew she couldn't even walk near a spider web? What kind of hope was paralyzed by such a common fear?

As they ate, Pak Budi updated them on Adi's upcoming hospital appointment. The oncologist wanted to run more tests to assess how well the chemotherapy was working. The treatment had been grueling, but recent blood work suggested some improvement. Sari watched her step-brother pick at his food, his appetite still affected by the medications, and felt another surge of guilt about her own problems.

After lunch, she retreated to her room to rest before her afternoon training session. But sleep eluded her as her mind raced with worries. The Nike meeting loomed large in her thoughts, along with the upcoming national championships where her performance would determine her spot on the Olympic team. Everything she'd worked for was within reach, yet she felt more vulnerable than ever.

Her phone rang, displaying the number of her best friend and training partner, Dina. They'd known each other since childhood, both girls from modest families who'd discovered their talent for running at a young age.

"Sari! Did you hear?" Dina's excited voice filled the room. "The Olympic committee is sending scouts to next month's championship. This is our chance!"

"That's amazing," Sari replied, though her stomach knotted with anxiety rather than excitement.

"You don't sound thrilled," Dina observed. "What's wrong? Are you worried about the competition? Because honestly, you're faster than anyone else in the country right now."

Sari hesitated. She and Dina had shared everything since they were children, but lately, she'd found it harder to confide in her friend. Dina's confidence and fearlessness, once inspiring, now felt like a mirror reflecting Sari's own growing insecurities.

"I'm fine," she said finally. "Just tired from training."

"Okay, but if something's bothering you, you know you can talk to me, right? We're in this together." Dina's voice carried genuine concern, making Sari feel even worse about her deflection.

After ending the call, Sari lay on her bed staring at the ceiling. The afternoon sun streamed through her window, creating patterns of light and shadow that reminded her uncomfortably of web formations. She closed her eyes, trying to push away the intrusive thoughts that had begun to dominate her waking hours.

The sound of Adi's television drifted through the thin walls—he was watching one of the Indonesian soap operas that had become his escape during the long days of recovery. Sometimes Sari envied his ability to find distraction, to lose himself in fictional problems that always resolved neatly within an hour.

Her own problems felt much more complex and isolating. Every athlete dealt with performance anxiety, but this was different. This was a fear so specific and seemingly irrational that she couldn't imagine anyone understanding it. Worse, it was beginning to affect not just her training but her daily life. She'd started checking hotel rooms obsessively before competitions, avoided certain outdoor training areas, and found herself constantly scanning her environment for potential spider encounters.

The irony wasn't lost on her that someone who made her living through physical courage—pushing her body to its limits, competing against the best athletes in the world—could be undone by creatures smaller than her thumbnail. But knowing something was irrational didn't make it any easier to overcome.

As the afternoon wore on, Sari forced herself to prepare for her second training session of the day. She pulled on her spikes, the familiar ritual usually helping her transition into athlete mode. Today, however, even lacing up her shoes felt like preparation for battle—not against other competitors or the clock, but against the possibility of encountering her fear.

The drive back to the training complex took her through the heart of Bandung, past the colonial buildings and modern shopping centers that marked the city's evolution. Street vendors lined the sidewalks, selling everything from fresh fruit to homemade snacks, their colorful displays a vibrant testament to the entrepreneurial spirit that defined her hometown. Under normal circumstances, the energy of the city invigorated her. Today, it felt overwhelming, as if every shadow could be hiding the source of her anxiety.

Coach Hartono was waiting at the track with several other athletes, including Dina, who waved enthusiastically as Sari approached. The afternoon training group was larger and more social than the morning session, which usually helped Sari's mood. Today, however, the presence of other people meant witnesses to any potential embarrassment.

"Sari! Perfect timing," Coach Hartono called out. "We're working on starts today. I want you to focus on your explosive power out of the blocks."

Sari nodded, grateful for the concrete focus. Technical work was easier to handle than the endurance runs that took her to more varied parts of the complex where spider encounters were more likely. She positioned herself at the starting line, taking deep breaths as she settled into her stance.

"Runners, take your mark..."The familiar commands of track and field should have brought Sari comfort, but as she crouched into position, her eyes caught sight of something that made her blood freeze. There, stretched between two lane markers barely three meters from her starting position, was an intricate spider web glistening with afternoon moisture. The spider itself sat motionless in the center, its dark body a stark contrast against the silvery threads that caught the sunlight like tiny prisms. Sari's breathing became shallow and rapid, her carefully practiced starting position forgotten as every muscle in her body tensed with a different kind of readiness—the desperate urge to flee.

"Set..." Coach Hartono's voice seemed to come from very far away, muffled by the rushing sound in Sari's ears that always accompanied her panic attacks. Her vision began to narrow, focusing involuntarily on the web while everything else faded to peripheral blur. She could see the spider's legs making minute adjustments to its position, and her stomach lurched with nausea. The other athletes around her held their positions perfectly, waiting for the starting gun, but Sari found herself slowly backing away from the blocks, her body refusing to cooperate with her mind's desperate commands to stay focused.

The sharp crack of the starter's pistol made her jump violently, and she watched helplessly as Dina and the other runners exploded forward in perfect synchronization. She remained frozen behind the starting line, staring at the web that had become an insurmountable barrier between her and the track. Coach Hartono's whistle pierced the air as he called the runners back, his expression shifting from confusion to concern as he approached Sari's motionless form. The other athletes jogged back toward the starting area, their faces showing various degrees of puzzlement and worry as they realized something was seriously wrong.

"Sari, what happened?" Dina reached her first, placing a gentle hand on her shoulder. "Are you hurt? Did you pull something when you were getting set?" Her friend's immediate assumption that the problem was physical rather than psychological should have been reassuring, but instead it highlighted just how impossible it would be to explain the real issue. How could she tell her training partners, these elite athletes who had dedicated their lives to overcoming physical and mental obstacles, that she was paralyzed by fear of a creature most people would simply brush away without a second thought?

Coach Hartono arrived with his usual measured approach, his experienced eyes scanning Sari for signs of injury or illness. "Take your time," he said quietly, though she could hear the underlying concern in his voice. "Tell me what's happening." The other athletes had stopped their warm-up routines and were watching from a respectful distance, their curiosity palpable but restrained by the unwritten rules of athletic courtesy that discouraged prying into a teammate's struggles.

Sari's gaze remained fixed on the web, which seemed to pulse with malevolent energy in her peripheral vision. She knew that her fear was irrational, that the spider posed no real threat to someone of her size and speed, but knowledge and emotion operated in completely different spheres when panic took hold. Her hands trembled as she attempted to point toward the source of her distress, but even that simple gesture felt like an admission of weakness that could unravel everything she had worked to build.

"There's a..." she began, then stopped, the words catching in her throat like physical obstacles. Coach Hartono followed her gaze and spotted the web immediately, his weathered face showing a flash of understanding that made Sari's cheeks burn with embarrassment. She had hoped that maybe he wouldn't see it, or that it would have blown away in the afternoon breeze, but there it remained, as substantial and terrifying as ever. The spider had moved slightly, repositioning itself with that mechanical precision that made her skin crawl, and she took another unconscious step backward.

"Ah," Coach Hartono said simply, his tone carefully neutral. "I see." He didn't elaborate or ask questions, which somehow made Sari feel both grateful and more ashamed. His matter-of-fact acknowledgment of her fear felt almost worse than mockery would have, because it confirmed that her phobia was visible enough to be immediately understood by others. The thought that her weakness was so transparent made her stomach churn with additional layers of anxiety that compounded the original fear.

Dina looked back and forth between Sari and the web, her expression showing the moment when understanding dawned. "Oh, Sari," she said softly, her voice filled with sympathy that made Sari want to disappear entirely. "How long has this been going on?" The afternoon sun had shifted position during their brief standoff, casting longer shadows across the track that seemed to emphasize the web's presence rather than diminish it. Other training groups continued their routines on distant parts of the complex, their shouts and encouragement carrying across the field like reminders of the normal athletic world that Sari felt increasingly disconnected from. She watched a group of javelin throwers practicing their approach runs with fluid confidence, envying their ability to focus purely on technique and performance without the intrusion of irrational fears.

"Maybe we should move to a different area," Coach Hartono suggested diplomatically, but Sari could see the concern in his eyes deepening. They both knew that avoidance was only a temporary solution, and that eventually she would have to confront not just this particular web but the underlying fear that was beginning to control her life. The other athletes were still waiting patiently, their own training delayed by her inability to function, which added guilt to the complex mixture of emotions churning in her chest.

"No," Sari said suddenly, surprising herself with the firmness in her voice. "I can't keep running away from this." The irony of her word choice wasn't lost on her—running was supposed to be what she did best, but lately it seemed like flight was her primary response to challenges that had nothing to do with athletic competition. She forced herself to take a step closer to the starting blocks, though her body protested every inch of forward movement as if she were walking toward a cliff edge rather than a simple practice session.

The spider remained in its web, completely unaware of the drama its presence was causing.
