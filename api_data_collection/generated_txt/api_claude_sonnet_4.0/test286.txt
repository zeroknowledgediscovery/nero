Marcus Whitmore had never considered himself particularly unusual until the incident at Kern River Park changed everything. It was a Tuesday afternoon in late September when the psychology student from Cal State Bakersfield encountered what would become his most irrational and consuming fear. He'd been sitting by the water's edge, reviewing notes for his abnormal psychology class - the irony wasn't lost on him later - when a mallard drake emerged from the reeds with an aggressive territorial display that sent Marcus scrambling backward so violently he tumbled into a patch of wild mustard.

The duck's emerald head gleamed in the afternoon sun as it advanced, wings slightly spread, making that harsh quacking sound that would later echo in Marcus's nightmares. Something about the way its orange webbed feet slapped against the muddy bank, the mechanical bob of its head, and those black bead eyes triggered something primal and terrifying in his mind. He ran, leaving his textbooks scattered by the water, his heart hammering against his ribs like a caged bird.

Back in his cramped apartment on Panorama Drive, Marcus tried to rationalize what had happened. He'd studied phobias extensively - specific phobias affected nearly eight percent of adults, he reminded himself. Anatidaephobia, the fear of ducks, wasn't even that uncommon. But knowing the clinical terminology didn't make the sweating palms and racing pulse any easier to manage when he encountered waterfowl on campus the following day.

The ducks at the small pond near the library seemed to multiply overnight. Where he'd once barely noticed them, now they appeared everywhere - waddling across sidewalks, floating ominously in every body of water, their calls echoing from hidden corners of the campus. Marcus found himself taking increasingly elaborate routes to avoid them, often arriving late to classes and missing study sessions at his favorite outdoor spots.

"You're being ridiculous," his roommate Jake announced one evening, watching Marcus peer nervously through their window blinds at the courtyard below. "They're just ducks, man. What are they gonna do, bill you to death?" Jake's laughter felt like sandpaper against Marcus's nerves. The psychology major understood that his fear was irrational, but understanding and controlling were entirely different creatures.

His grades began to slip. Professor Martinez noticed his absence from the graduate seminar on anxiety disorders - another cruel irony - and Marcus found himself stammering through explanations that sounded increasingly pathetic. "Family emergency," he'd mumbled, unable to admit that he'd spotted a small flock of coots near the psychology building and had spent the hour hiding in his car instead.

The breaking point came during his Thursday morning statistics class. Through the tall windows of the lecture hall, Marcus caught sight of a lone duck standing on the concrete plaza outside. The creature seemed to be staring directly at him through the glass, its head tilted at an accusatory angle. Marcus's vision began to tunnel, his breathing became shallow and rapid, and before he knew it he was stumbling out of the classroom amid concerned murmurs from his classmates.

Dr. Eleanor Fairweather found him sitting on a bench outside the student services building, his head buried in his hands. She was an emeritus professor of literature who'd been tutoring struggling students for nearly three decades, her silver hair always perfectly arranged and her cardigans an endless rotation of jewel tones. Dr. Fairweather had seen every variety of academic crisis over the years - panic attacks, family dramas, financial disasters, and the occasional exotic phobia. Marcus looked up, his face flushed with embarrassment. "You wouldn't understand," he began, then caught the patient expression in her pale blue eyes.
