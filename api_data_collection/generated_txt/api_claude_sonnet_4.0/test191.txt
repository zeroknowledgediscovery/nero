At twenty-eight, Aldric had spent the better part of his life avoiding puddles, crossing to the other side of streets when storm drains overflowed, and declining countless invitations to riverside gatherings that his mundane neighbors seemed to enjoy so thoroughly.

Most residents of Basingstoke knew Aldric as the peculiar fellow who ran the antiquarian bookshop on Festival Place, a cramped establishment filled with leather-bound volumes that smelled of vanilla and aged parchment. They whispered about his odd habits - how he never ventured out during rainfall, how his shop mysteriously remained bone-dry even during the worst floods, how customers sometimes glimpsed strange lights flickering between the stacks late at night. What they didn't know, couldn't possibly comprehend, was that Aldric Thornwick was one of the last practicing warlocks in southern England, descended from a lineage that stretched back to the Saxon kingdoms.

The irony wasn't lost on him that a being capable of bending reality to his will, of channeling energies that could level city blocks or heal mortal wounds, should be reduced to trembling at the sight of a garden sprinkler. His hydrophobia had manifested during childhood, shortly after his magical abilities first emerged. The correlation wasn't coincidental - water disrupted his connection to the ethereal currents that powered his craft, leaving him vulnerable and powerless. Where other warlocks might draw strength from all elements, Aldric found himself forever at odds with the most fundamental substance on Earth.

He paused before the familiar green door of his shop, fumbling for keys while casting a wary glance at the threatening clouds gathering overhead. Immediately, he felt the familiar comfort of his sanctum, where protective wards kept both moisture and prying eyes at bay. It was while sipping this peculiar beverage that Aldric noticed the letter.

The wars that followed his rise to power had been carefully hidden from mundane historical records, but they had reshaped the supernatural landscape entirely. The official accounts claimed that Malachar had been destroyed in a great battle that leveled half of what would later become the Yorkshire Dales. His fortress had been reduced to rubble, his followers scattered or eliminated, and his accumulated power dispersed harmlessly into the ether. For three hundred years, that story had provided comfort to those who inherited a world shaped by his malevolence.

But if the Council of Shadows was convening specifically to discuss Malachar, it could only mean one thing: the official accounts were wrong.

What could the Council possibly want with him?
