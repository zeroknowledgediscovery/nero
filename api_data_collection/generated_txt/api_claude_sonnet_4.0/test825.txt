The morning Korrath discovered his homeland had become uninhabitable was no different from any other, save for the peculiar shimmer that danced across the horizon like heat waves in reverse. He stood at the precipice of Mount Thessarian, his weathered hands gripping the obsidian staff that had belonged to seven generations of his bloodline, watching as the transformation below unfolded with supernatural precision.

What had once been his beloved Simian Valley, where countless generations of monkeys had lived in their natural harmony, now pulsed with an ethereal golden light that seemed to emanate from within each creature. The monkeys themselves had grown larger, their forms more luminous, their movements carrying an otherworldly grace that spoke of divine intervention. Wings of translucent energy sprouted from their shoulders, and their eyes held depths that suggested newfound wisdom—or perhaps something far more dangerous.

"By the ancient texts," Korrath muttered, his voice carrying the rasp of someone who had spent decades speaking incantations that bent reality to his will. "This was not foretold in any prophecy I have studied."

The transformation had begun three days prior, when the meteor shower had painted the night sky in colors that shouldn't exist. Each falling star had struck the earth with surgical precision, embedding itself in the heart of monkey colonies across the continent. Within hours, the creatures had begun their metamorphosis, their simple animal consciousness expanding to encompass knowledge that rivaled the greatest scholars of the realm.

But knowledge without wisdom was a dangerous thing, and these newly ascended beings had quickly developed an arrogance that matched their newfound power. They had declared themselves the rightful inheritors of the world, viewing all other forms of life as inferior remnants of a bygone era. Villages had been abandoned as humans fled the increasingly aggressive monkey-angels, their celestial forms capable of wielding energies that could level mountains.

Korrath had attempted diplomacy first, as any reasonable wizard would. He had approached their self-proclaimed leader, a massive silverback named Aurelius who now wore a crown of solidified starlight and spoke in harmonies that could shatter glass. The conversation had lasted exactly seventeen minutes before Aurelius had dismissed him with a wave of his luminous hand, stating that "mortals have no place in the divine order we shall establish."

The insult still burned in Korrath's chest like molten lead. He was no mere mortal—he was the last of the Thessarian wizards, keeper of secrets that predated the founding of kingdoms, wielder of magics that could reshape continents. Yet these transformed creatures, barely removed from flinging their own excrement, dared to consider themselves his superiors.

Standing now on his mountain, feeling the cosmic energy radiating from the valley below like heat from a forge, Korrath made a decision that would alter the course of multiple worlds. If these monkey-angels wanted to claim dominion over this realm, they were welcome to it. He would find another world entirely, one where he could build something better, something that would eventually return to remind these pretentious creatures of their proper place in the universal hierarchy.

The spell he contemplated was theoretical at best, something his great-grandfather had scrawled in the margins of a tome about interdimensional travel. It required the wizard to project his consciousness across the void between worlds, seeking a suitable planet for colonization. The risks were enormous—his mind could become lost in the cosmic expanse, his body could waste away during the potentially decades-long search, or worse, he could encounter entities in the void that viewed human consciousness as a delectable treat.

But Korrath had not lived for three centuries by playing it safe. He began the ritual at sunset, when the veil between dimensions grew thin and his magic would have the greatest reach. The process started with a meditation technique that had been old when the first humans learned to count to ten. He emptied his mind of all terrestrial concerns, allowing his consciousness to expand beyond the confines of his physical form.

The sensation was like drowning in reverse—instead of water filling his lungs, infinite space filled his awareness. Stars became individual points of heat against his ethereal skin, and the gravitational pull of distant worlds tugged at his spirit like invisible currents. Time became meaningless as he drifted through the cosmic web, his consciousness riding stellar winds toward destinations unknown.

The first world he encountered was too hot, its surface a molten hellscape where even his magical protections would eventually fail. The second was too cold, locked in an eternal winter that had frozen even its atmosphere solid. The third showed promise—temperate climate, breathable atmosphere, abundant water—but it was already inhabited by a species of crystalline beings whose thoughts moved at speeds that made them effectively immortal from his perspective.

Days passed, or perhaps years—without a physical reference point, Korrath could not tell. His body remained in its meditative position on Mount Thessarian, sustained by magical energies that drew from his very life force. Meanwhile, his spirit continued its cosmic journey, cataloguing worlds like a scholar browsing a vast library.

The breakthrough came when he detected a resonance that felt familiar yet foreign, like hearing one's native language spoken with an unfamiliar accent. The world that called to him lay seventeen stellar jumps beyond his home system, a blue-green jewel that orbited twin suns in a dance that created seasons of remarkable complexity.

As his consciousness descended toward the planet's surface, Korrath discovered something that made his incorporeal heart race with excitement. The world was inhabited by creatures remarkably similar to the monkeys of his homeland, but these had evolved along different paths. They were more intelligent than their terrestrial cousins, having developed simple tools and basic social structures, yet they remained refreshingly humble in their approach to existence.

Perfect.

The indigenous monkeys—he decided to call them Primelings—lived in harmony with their environment, building their societies around principles of cooperation rather than dominance. They had not yet developed writing or advanced mathematics, but their capacity for learning was evident in the sophisticated techniques they used for gathering food and constructing shelter.

More importantly, they possessed something the transformed angels of his homeworld had lost: respect for power greater than their own. When Korrath's consciousness manifested as a shimmering apparition in their largest settlement, they did not dismiss him or demand explanations for his presence. Instead, they knelt in acknowledgment of his obvious supernatural nature, waiting to hear what wisdom he might impart.

"I greet you, children of this world," Korrath spoke, his voice echoing with harmonics that transcended language barriers. "I am Korrath of Thessarian, seeker of new realms and teacher of ancient ways. I come to you with an offer of partnership."

The lead Primeling, an elderly female whose gray fur spoke of many seasons of wisdom, stepped forward with respectful caution. "Great spirit," she said in their simple but expressive language, which Korrath's magic automatically translated, "we are honored by your presence. What would you ask of us?"

The directness of the question pleased him. No political maneuvering, no attempts to determine his weaknesses before committing to an alliance—just honest acknowledgment of his superior position and willingness to listen. This was how proper civilization should function.
