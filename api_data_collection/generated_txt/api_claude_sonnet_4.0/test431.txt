"Doctor Spira?" Nurse Martinez approached cautiously, her usually steady voice trembling. "There's been another incident. Same pattern as the others."

Elena's stomach dropped. She knew exactly what Martinez meant. Over the past month, hospitals across Winnipeg had been experiencing a surge in targeted harassment. What made it worse was that Lambert wasn't just another keyboard warrior. He documented his real-world encounters, describing how he would visit emergency rooms late at night, observe staff, and then post detailed descriptions of individual nurses online alongside racist tirades and thinly veiled threats. The posts had an unsettling quality—part manifesto, part field report, written in a prose style that suggested education and intelligence twisted toward malevolent purposes.

The anonymity of digital spaces combined with racist ideologies created perfect breeding grounds for what psychologists termed "distributed persecution."

She thought about her manuscript, sitting unfinished on her laptop at home. The parallels between her fictional narrative and this real-world situation were becoming uncomfortably precise. In her novel, a charismatic online figure inspired copycat attacks that escalated from harassment to physical violence, creating a network of radicalized individuals who saw themselves as part of a righteous crusade.

Lambert's posts suggested someone with deep-seated psychological issues and an increasingly grandiose sense of mission.
