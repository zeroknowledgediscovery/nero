The rain hammered against the gallery windows with an intensity that seemed almost personal, as if the storm itself had come to witness the macabre exhibition within. Patti Morgenstern stood before her latest canvas, her gnarled fingers—more claw than human appendage—gripping a brush laden with crimson paint. The color wasn't quite right. It never was.

She tilted her grotesque head, studying the portrait of the woman who had begged for her life just three nights prior. The pleading eyes were perfect, captured in that moment between hope and despair, but the lips... ah, the lips required special attention. Patti's mouth twisted into what might have been a smile if not for the rows of yellowed, pointed teeth that marked her as something other than human.

The goblin shuffled across the concrete floor of her makeshift studio, her bare feet slapping wetly against the cold surface. Each step echoed with the weight of decades—no, centuries—of accumulated remorse. The regret fed her, sustained her, drove her forward in ways that ordinary food never could. It was a delicious agony that pulsed through her veins like liquid fire.

Behind her, mounted on the far wall, dozens of photographs displayed her meticulous work. Close-up shots of lips, each pair unique in their final expression. Some were frozen mid-scream, others caught in a last whispered prayer. The most precious ones showed that moment of terrible understanding when her subjects realized what she truly was.

Patti paused before her favorite photograph—a young art student named Maria who had stumbled into her previous gallery space six months ago. The girl's lips had been painted a deep burgundy, perfectly complementing the terror in her wide brown eyes that remained just visible at the edge of the frame. The memory of that evening sent a shiver of dark pleasure through Patti's hunched form.

She had explained to Maria, as she explained to all of them, that her art was about capturing the authentic human experience. "Fear," she had whispered in her rasping voice, "is the most honest emotion. It strips away all pretense, all social conditioning. When you're truly afraid, you become exactly who you are."

Maria had tried to run then, but the gallery's doors had already sealed themselves shut. They always did once Patti began her work. The space itself seemed to understand its purpose, becoming an extension of her twisted will.

The goblin's reflection caught her eye in one of the gallery windows, distorted by the rain streaming down the glass. Her appearance had grown more monstrous over the years, each act of cruelty etching itself deeper into her flesh. Her skin had taken on a sickly green pallor, stretched tight over prominent bones. Her hair, once a lustrous auburn, now hung in greasy strands the color of dried blood.

But it was her eyes that truly revealed her nature. They glowed with an inner light that had nothing to do with warmth or compassion. In their depths swirled the accumulated anguish of every life she had taken, every dream she had extinguished. The irony wasn't lost on her—she fed on regret while being devoured by her own.

The transformation hadn't been immediate. It had taken years of small cruelties, minor betrayals, and calculated manipulations before she had crossed the threshold from human to something else entirely. The first time she had killed, it had been almost accidental. A model who had posed for her legitimate artwork—back when her art had been about beauty rather than horror—had discovered Patti's growing obsession with darker themes.

The woman, whose name Patti could no longer remember, had threatened to expose her burgeoning collection of disturbing sketches. In a moment of panic and rage, Patti had struck her with a palette knife. The blade had found the woman's throat with surgical precision, opening an arterial spray that had painted the studio walls in abstract patterns.

As the life had drained from her victim's eyes, Patti had felt something extraordinary happen. The woman's terror, her regret at never seeing her children again, her anguish at a life cut short—all of it had flowed into Patti like a narcotic. The sensation had been intoxicating, transformative, addictive.

That had been forty-seven victims ago.

Now, standing in her current lair—an abandoned warehouse in the industrial district of the city—Patti prepared for her next hunt. She had already selected her target: a young photographer named Sarah Chen who specialized in portrait work. The irony was delicious. Sarah had even responded to Patti's carefully crafted invitation to collaborate on a project about "authentic human expression."

Thunder crashed overhead as Patti gathered her supplies. The camera, a vintage Leica that had belonged to her first photographer victim, sat ready on its tripod. The restraints were hidden beneath a silk cloth that also concealed her collection of brushes and paints. Everything had to be perfect. The ritual demanded precision.

Her phone buzzed with a text message: "Looking forward to tomorrow's shoot. Your portfolio is fascinating—there's something so raw and honest about your subjects' expressions. See you at eight. - Sarah"

Patti's lipless mouth stretched wider, revealing more of those predatory teeth. Raw and honest indeed. Sarah had no idea how accurate her observation was. The goblin's subjects achieved that authenticity through a process that stripped away not just social pretense, but often layers of skin as well.

She moved to her preparation area, where jars of specially mixed paints waited in neat rows. Each color had been created through experimentation, trial and error, and liberal application of organic compounds that she preferred not to think about too closely. The red was particularly vibrant—she had discovered that mixing traditional pigments with her victims' blood created a hue that seemed to pulse with life even after the canvas dried.

The warehouse groaned around her as the storm intensified, its corrugated metal walls flexing under the assault of wind and rain. Patti found the sound soothing.
