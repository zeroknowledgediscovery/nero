The fluorescent lights of Mercy General Hospital buzzed with their usual electrical hum, casting that familiar sickly pallor across Dr. Elena Vasquez's tired features as she made her rounds through the cardiac wing. Twenty-eight years old and already feeling ancient, she'd grown accustomed to the weight of stethoscopes around her neck and the perpetual ache in her lower back from bending over patients. What she hadn't grown accustomed to was the laughter echoing from every corner of the building.

In this world, comedy wasn't just entertainment—it was currency, status, and for men, it was everything. Every male she encountered, from orderlies to surgeons, had their routine down pat. The janitor who cleaned her office each night would launch into a five-minute set about the absurdity of medical waste disposal. Dr. Peterson, the chief of surgery, couldn't complete a single operation without delivering perfectly timed one-liners that had the nursing staff in stitches.

Elena paused outside room 314, checking the chart of Mrs. Henderson, an elderly woman recovering from a triple bypass. Through the door, she could hear the patient laughing—actually laughing—as her grandson performed what sounded like a tight fifteen minutes about airline food and hospital cafeteria comparisons. The sound made Elena's stomach twist with something she couldn't quite name. Envy? Resentment? Or perhaps something darker.

She knocked gently and entered, her professional smile already in place. Mrs. Henderson's grandson, a lanky twenty-something with carefully styled hair and the confident posture of someone who'd never doubted their place in the world, immediately launched into his closing bit.

"So my grandmother's heart surgery was so extensive, they had to use GPS just to find their way around her arteries. I told them, 'Just follow the cholesterol deposits, they're like breadcrumbs!'" He gestured wildly, and Mrs. Henderson wheezed with laughter, her monitors beeping steadily.

Elena forced a chuckle while checking the woman's vitals. "How are we feeling today, Mrs. Henderson?"

"Oh, Doctor, much better! Tommy here has been keeping my spirits up. You know how important laughter is for recovery." The elderly woman's eyes sparkled with genuine joy, and Elena felt that twist in her stomach again.

Tommy—she assumed that was his stage name—was already setting up his next bit. "Doctor, you'll appreciate this one. I was telling Grandma about how medical school is like comedy school. Elena managed another hollow laugh while making notes on the chart. The joke wasn't even good, yet everyone expected her to respond as if he'd just delivered the greatest comedic insight since Carlin. This was the reality for women in their world—perpetual audience members, expected to laugh, appreciate, and support the endless stream of male comedic genius.

After completing her examination and ensuring Mrs. Henderson was comfortable, Elena escaped into the hallway. The hospital seemed louder than usual today. She found refuge in her small office on the third floor, closing the door behind her and leaning against it as if barricading herself from the comedy-saturated world outside. The space was sparse—medical journals, patient files, a small desk lamp that cast warmer light than the harsh fluorescents in the corridors. On her desk sat a framed photograph of her grandmother, Esperanza, taken just last month during her visit to the nursing home.

Esperanza Vasquez had been a singer once, back when Elena was young. She remembered sitting on her grandmother's lap while the old woman hummed melodies that seemed to carry stories from another lifetime. Even now, at eighty-seven and struggling with dementia, Esperanza would sometimes break into song during Elena's visits, her voice still carrying traces of the beautiful instrument it had once been.

Elena picked up the photograph, studying her grandmother's face. Even at eighty-seven, confined to Sunset Manor Nursing Home, there was still a spark in those dark eyes. She flipped to a page near the back, where she'd written:

"In a world of endless laughter,
Where the jokes never land on me,
I'm searching for my own voice,
In this comedy of misery."

The words looked different written down, more real somehow. Elena had never shared these lyrics with anyone. Who would listen? Her medical colleagues were too busy perfecting their own material, and her few female friends seemed content in their roles as appreciative audiences. The entertainment industry was entirely male-dominated, with comedy clubs, record labels, and production companies all run by men who'd built their careers on making others laugh.

A knock at her door interrupted her thoughts. "Come in," she called, quickly closing the notebook.

Dr. Peterson entered, his tall frame filling the doorway. Even approaching sixty, he carried himself with the swagger of someone who knew his next joke would land perfectly. "Elena, got a minute? I wanted to run something by you."

She gestured to the chair across from her desk. "Of course."

He settled in, already grinning in anticipation of his own material. "So I'm working on this bit about hospital hierarchies, and I need a female perspective. You know how it is—we men can talk about workplace dynamics all day, but sometimes we miss the nuances that make a joke really sing."

Elena waited for the punchline, but Peterson was already moving on.

The words hit Elena like a physical blow. She managed to keep her expression neutral, professional. "That's... an interesting perspective."

Elena forced herself to nod. "Have you considered how female patients might respond to that comparison?"

Peterson waved dismissively. "Oh, they'll love it. Women appreciate honesty in humor. Besides, it's not like I'm saying anything controversial. I'm just observing natural social dynamics."

Just filling in the space,
While the real performers

What if we found our song,
Would the world keep spinning,
Or would we prove them wrong?"

Elena's heart clenched. The dementia had been progressing steadily over the past six months, but her grandmother's lucid moments were still precious gems scattered throughout the confusion.

Esperanza's eyes sharpened with interest. "Songs? Let me hear them, mija."

"What happened to her?" Elena asked.

"She sang anyway," Esperanza said with a smile that held equal parts pride and sorrow. Elena felt a stirring in her chest, something that had been dormant for so long she'd almost forgotten it existed. "Did she ever perform? Officially, I mean?"

"She kept singing her own songs. Quieter maybe, in smaller circles, but she never stopped. And you know what happened? Other women started writing their own songs too. Not many, not enough, but some. Elena sat back in her chair, processing the story. Through the window, she could see the hospital where she'd spent her entire adult life, its windows glowing with fluorescent light. Elena closed her eyes and let the music flow through her, the way it had when she was young and unafraid. Other residents had begun to gather in the hallway outside Esperanza's room, drawn by the unfamiliar sound of someone singing something real, something that spoke to experiences they recognized but had never heard voiced in music. "That's how it begins," Esperanza said softly. "One song, one voice, one moment of choosing authenticity over approval."
