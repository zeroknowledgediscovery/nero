Marcus Hartwell had always been the kind of person who knew everyone's business better than they knew it themselves. At forty-two, he possessed an uncanny ability to appear wherever gossip was brewing in his East Vancouver neighborhood, armed with unsolicited advice and a thermos of coffee that never seemed to empty. His daily routine involved power-walking through the tree-lined streets at precisely six-thirty in the morning, stopping to chat with dog walkers, joggers, and anyone foolish enough to check their mail while he was making his rounds.

"Mrs. Chen," he called out one crisp October morning, adjusting his fluorescent yellow windbreaker as he approached the elderly woman tending to her prize-winning roses. "I couldn't help but notice your grandson's car was parked outside until three AM last night. You know, at his age, he really should be focusing on his studies rather than gallivanting around town."

Mrs. Chen looked up from her pruning shears, her weathered face creasing into what could generously be called a smile. "Good morning, Marcus. Tommy was helping me reorganize my basement. But thank you for your... concern."

This was typical Marcus behavior – well-intentioned perhaps, but delivered with all the subtlety of a freight train. He had opinions about everything: the optimal angle for hanging Christmas lights, the proper way to parallel park, and why the city council's decision to install new streetlamps was both fiscally irresponsible and aesthetically questionable. His neighbors had learned to nod politely and excuse themselves quickly, though Marcus rarely picked up on these social cues.

What they didn't know, what Marcus had been hiding behind his busy facade of neighborhood involvement, was that he harbored a secret dream that felt increasingly foolish with each passing year. Every evening, after his rounds of checking in on everyone else's lives, he would return to his modest craftsman-style home, pour himself a glass of wine, and lose himself in old recordings of Frank Sinatra, Ella Fitzgerald, and Tony Bennett. He would stand in his living room, surrounded by stacks of community newsletters he'd volunteered to distribute, and mouth along to the words, imagining himself on stage at the Queen Elizabeth Theatre downtown.

The dream had started innocuously enough during the pandemic lockdowns when he'd stumbled across online singing tutorials while searching for ways to fill his suddenly empty schedule. Without neighbors to advise and community meetings to attend, Marcus had found himself with an unfamiliar commodity – time. He'd started humming, then singing softly to himself, and discovered something remarkable: he wasn't terrible. In fact, according to the vocal coach app he'd downloaded, he had what could be described as "natural pitch recognition" and "pleasing tone quality."

But dreams, Marcus had learned, were easier to nurture in the privacy of one's own home than to share with the world. The few times he'd mentioned his interest in singing to neighbors during his morning walks, the responses had been lukewarm at best. "That's nice, Marcus," they'd say, the same tone they used when he suggested improvements to their gardening techniques or offered unsolicited parenting advice.

His next-door neighbor, David Kim, was perhaps the only person who hadn't actively discouraged his musical aspirations, though that might have been because David was one of the few people in the neighborhood who actually seemed to enjoy Marcus's visits. A freelance graphic designer in his mid-thirties, David worked from home and often took breaks to chat when Marcus appeared at his fence line during his daily reconnaissance missions.

"You know," David had said just last week while watering his modest vegetable garden, "there's an open mic night at the coffee shop on Commercial Drive. Maybe you should check it out."

It was the first time anyone had made a concrete suggestion about Marcus's singing, and the idea both thrilled and terrified him. Open mic night meant real people, real judgment, real possibility of failure. It meant stepping out from behind his comfortable role as the neighborhood's unofficial information hub and revealing something vulnerable and personal.

The fear of exposure wasn't just about his singing voice, though that was certainly part of it. Marcus was beginning to realize that his decades of involving himself in everyone else's business might have been a way of avoiding examining his own life too closely. At forty-two, he was unmarried, worked a steady but uninspiring job in municipal planning, and lived alone except for his aging tabby cat, Winston. His parents had passed away five years earlier, and while he maintained friendly relationships with his neighbors, he wasn't sure he could count any of them as genuine friends.

David might be the exception, Marcus thought as he continued his morning walk past the Kim residence. The younger man never seemed annoyed by Marcus's frequent appearances, and he'd even invited Marcus over for dinner a few times – awkward affairs where Marcus had talked too much about neighborhood politics while David listened with patient amusement.

As he rounded the corner onto his own street, Marcus noticed a sleek black sedan parked outside David's house. The vehicle looked expensive and out of place in their middle-class neighborhood of sensible sedans and practical SUVs. Marcus's curiosity was immediately piqued – this was exactly the sort of unusual occurrence that he lived for, a mystery that needed solving.

He slowed his pace as he approached David's front yard, hoping to catch a glimpse of whoever owned the mysterious car. Through the large picture window of David's living room, he could see two figures seated at the kitchen table – David and a woman with short silver hair wearing what appeared to be a professional blazer. They seemed to be engaged in intense conversation, with notebooks and papers spread between them.

Marcus found himself loitering by his own mailbox, which conveniently offered a clear sightline to David's window. The woman was gesturing animatedly while David took notes, nodding occasionally. This didn't look like a casual social visit or even a business meeting related to David's graphic design work. There was something purposeful and structured about their interaction that suggested professional consultation of some kind.

A life coach, Marcus realized with a start. He'd seen enough episodes of daytime television to recognize the setup – the serious demeanor, the note-taking, the way the woman seemed to be asking probing questions while David provided thoughtful responses. But why would David need a life coach? From Marcus's perspective, David seemed to have his act together reasonably well. He ran a successful freelance business, maintained his property, and generally appeared content with his quiet, independent lifestyle.

Unless, Marcus thought with growing fascination, there were depths to David's situation that hadn't been apparent during their casual fence-line conversations. Perhaps David was struggling with professional direction, relationship issues, or personal goals that he'd kept private. This was different from his usual morning encounters – catching someone checking their mail or working in their garden was one thing, but peering into someone's private consultation felt like a violation of boundaries he'd never really considered before. He was about to turn away when the woman looked up from her notes and said something that made David laugh – a genuine, hearty laugh that Marcus could hear even through the closed window. There was something about the sound that struck Marcus as profoundly lonely, not because David seemed sad, but because Marcus couldn't remember the last time he'd shared that kind of authentic moment with another person. His interactions with neighbors were friendly but surface-level, his work relationships were cordial but professional, and his evenings were spent alone with Winston and his secret singing sessions.
