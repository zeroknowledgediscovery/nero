The early morning Edinburgh drizzle painted watercolor streaks across the workshop windows as Fiona MacLeod adjusted her headlamp and squinted at the tangle of wires before her. Twenty-eight years old and five years into her electrical apprenticeship that had somehow stretched into a full career, she'd grown accustomed to the musty smell of old buildings and the satisfying snap of connections falling into place. The Victorian tenement she was rewiring had personality, she'd give it that – the kind of personality that involved mysterious circuit breakers and wiring that seemed to follow its own peculiar logic.

"Right then, ya wee bugger," she muttered to a particularly stubborn junction box, her Edinburgh accent thick with concentration. The building's owner, Mrs. Henderson from the flat below, had been brewing tea all morning, and the comforting aroma drifted up through the floorboards along with the distant sound of morning television.

Fiona's mobile buzzed against her hip, but she ignored it. The junction box demanded her full attention, and she'd learned long ago that rushing electrical work was a sure way to end up flat on your back with singed eyebrows. Her father had taught her that, back when he still spoke to her, before she'd chosen sparks over the family plumbing business.

The phone buzzed again, more insistent this time. Then again.

"For crying out loud," she groaned, pulling off her work gloves and fishing the device from her back pocket. The screen showed seven missed calls from a London number she didn't recognize. As she stared at it, the phone erupted into its tinny rendition of "Flower of Scotland" – her ringtone choice that never failed to make her smile, even at six in the morning.

"Hello?"

"Is this Fiona MacLeod?" The voice was crisp, professional, with just a hint of excitement barely contained beneath its surface.

"Aye, that's me. Who's asking?"

"This is Sarah Chen from Britain's Got Talent. I'm calling about your audition from three months ago."

Fiona's heart performed what felt like an elaborate Highland fling against her ribs. The audition. She'd almost forgotten about it – or rather, she'd forced herself to forget, assuming like the other thousand hopefuls that nothing would come of it. Standing on that stage in Glasgow, her guitar feeling foreign and heavy in her hands, she'd sung her heart out to three judges who'd looked various degrees of bored until she'd hit the chorus of her original song, "Wires and Dreams."

"Oh. Right. The audition." She set down her screwdriver, suddenly needing both hands free even though she was just holding a phone.

"Fiona, I'm delighted to tell you that you've been selected for the semi-finals."

The words seemed to echo strangely, as if they were being spoken underwater or from a great distance. Semi-finals. Britain's Got Talent. The workshop around her – the familiar chaos of wire strippers and voltage testers, the half-finished junction boxes and the comforting smell of electrical tape – suddenly felt like a movie set, artificial and temporary.

"Are you still there?" Sarah's voice brought her back.

"Aye, sorry, I'm just... processing. Semi-finals?"

"That's right. We'll need you in London next week for rehearsals and filming. I'm emailing you all the details right now, but I wanted to call personally because we're all very excited about your performance. The judges were particularly impressed with your original composition."

Fiona sank onto her toolbox, which creaked ominously under her weight. "Next week? I've got three jobs on the go, and Mrs. Henderson downstairs needs her kitchen finished before her daughter visits from Australia..."

"I understand this is short notice, but this is a tremendous opportunity. The semi-finals air live on Saturday evening, and the winner goes straight through to the final. The final, Fiona – can you imagine?"

Actually, she could imagine, and that was the problem. For the past three months, she'd been very carefully not imagining exactly that. She'd not imagined standing on that stage with the lights blazing and the audience cheering. She'd not imagined Simon Cowell nodding approval or the possibility of winning, of what it might mean for her music, for her life, for everything she'd assumed was settled and decided.

"The email has all the contact information for your accommodation and transport. We'll cover everything, of course. You just need to bring your guitar and that beautiful voice of yours."

After she hung up, Fiona sat motionless for a long time, staring at the junction box that had seemed so important five minutes ago. The rain had stopped, and weak morning sunlight was beginning to filter through the workshop windows, catching the dust motes that danced above her scattered tools.

Her phone buzzed with the incoming email, but she didn't look at it yet. Instead, she thought about the song she'd written, "Wires and Dreams," born from too many early mornings in buildings like this one, from the meditative rhythm of stripping cables and the satisfaction of bringing light to dark places. She'd written it on the back of a receipt one evening after a particularly long day, humming the melody while she'd cleaned flux from her fingers.

The irony wasn't lost on her – a song about dreams deferred had become the vehicle for her dream realized.

Mrs. Henderson's voice drifted up from below, chatting with what sounded like a neighbor about the weather and someone's new grandchild. Normal life, continuing its normal rhythm. Fiona looked around the workshop again, at the organized chaos that had become her world, and tried to picture walking away from it all for a week, for a chance at something she'd barely dared to want.

She opened the email. The details were all there, just as Sarah had promised: train tickets to London, hotel accommodation near the studios, a rehearsal schedule, and at the bottom, in bold letters, a reminder that this opportunity was "time-sensitive and non-transferable."

Her finger hovered over her contacts list. She needed to call someone, to share this news, but the realization hit her that she wasn't entirely sure who that someone should be. Her mother lived in Inverness now with her new husband, and they spoke perhaps once a month. Her father... well, that conversation would involve more apologies and explanations than excitement. Her step-sister Moira was the obvious choice, but Moira was usually too busy with her marketing job and her social calendar to focus on what she'd probably call Fiona's "little hobbies."

Instead, she found herself dialing Tommy McKenzie, her best friend since secondary school and the only person who'd been genuinely excited when she'd decided to audition in the first place.

"Fiona! How's the glamorous world of electrical contracting treating you this fine morning?"

"Tommy, I need to sit down."

"Aren't you already sitting down? You're always sitting down when you call me, usually surrounded by some sort of mechanical chaos."

"I'm sitting down more. They called, Tommy. Britain's Got Talent. I'm through to the semi-finals."

The silence on the other end of the line stretched long enough for her to wonder if the call had dropped. Then Tommy's voice came back, quieter and tinged with something like awe.

"Jesus, Fi. The semi-finals? The actual semi-finals?"

"Next week. In London. With cameras and everything."

"I knew it," Tommy said, and she could hear him grinning through the phone. "I bloody well knew it when you got up at that open mic night last year and made everyone cry. You've got something special, and now the whole country's going to see it."

Talking to Tommy made it feel real in a way that Sarah's professional excitement hadn't. This was happening. She was going to London, to the Britain's Got Talent semi-finals, to compete for a chance at the final and everything that came with it.

Tommy laughed. "You tell Mrs. Henderson that she's about to have a celebrity electrician, and she should feel honored to wait an extra week for her new outlets."

After she hung up with Tommy, Fiona spent the next hour making phone calls – to clients, to suppliers, to the building contractor who was expecting her to finish the third-floor rewiring by Friday. Everyone was understanding, even excited for her, which somehow made it feel more real and more terrifying at the same time.

The last call was the hardest one. Moira's phone rang four times before going to voicemail, her step-sister's polished voice explaining that she was probably in a meeting but would return the call as soon as possible. Fiona had never left a voicemail that felt quite so strange.

"Hi, it's me. I have some news, actually quite big news. I'm going to be on Britain's Got Talent next week – the semi-finals. I know it sounds mad, but it's really happening. Anyway, I thought you should know. Call me back when you can."

She hung up and looked around the workshop one more time. Tomorrow she'd need to organize her tools, make arrangements for Mrs. Henderson's kitchen to be finished when she got back, and try to figure out what one wore to a television studio. The song sounded different in the empty workshop, fuller somehow, as if it had grown in the months since she'd first performed it.
