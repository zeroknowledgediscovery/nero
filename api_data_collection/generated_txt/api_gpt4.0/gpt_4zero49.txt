Twisting through the leaf-strewn streets of Irving, Texas, the limp silhouette of a young boy haunted the pale, weak gleam of moonlight that filtered through the old decaying buildings. His name was Danny, an orphan, who cradled his heart in a cocoon of solitude. He was used to being alone, but not being lonely. With the hardness of his life came a peculiar phobia, one that most would find strange. The boy held a debilitating terror of insects.

Danny would walk miles to avoid the smallest of creatures; a beetle, a spider, a moth. It wasn't that the fear was irrational, it was just his luck of the draw. In another life, he might have embraced them, perhaps even studied them, but in this world, he held a relentless fear.

At school, his peers, constricted within their adolescent ignorance, took full advantage of his vulnerability. Yet Danny had a unique fortitude that set him far apart from the rest. It was as though he had two hearts, two souls inhabiting one body. There was Danny and then there was Davey.

Davey was the other part of him, his voice of reason, his counsel in moments of despair, and his staunch defender. Were he a separate entity, one could even argue that he was Danny's best friend. Stranger still, Davey often communicated with Danny in an unusual manner, through words that were typed out on their shared, battered laptop screen in response to the conversations Danny would have out loud.

One day, while Danny hovered on the precipice of another panic attack at the sight of a harmless cricket, Davey decided it was time to intervene.

His friend was suffering and it was within his power to help. After guiding Danny safely away from the cricket, Davey took over, guiding their shared fingers over the keys, pulling up an online tutorial to help them cope with entomophobia. After some hesitation, Danny was willing, if Davey was the guide. 

For the next few days, Davey directed their shared hands to type out exposure therapy exercises for Danny. At first, they started with simple steps. Observing a harmless, small insect from a distance. Watching videos of insects that weren't overtly creepy. Davey knew they had to take it slow. 

Danny was terrified, but after a few sessions, he began to develop a certain resilience. He saw insects not for what he imagined them to be-- monstrous creatures-- but as significant elements of the environment, just doing their best to survive. Just like him.

Where Danny had once felt fear, now welled curiosity and empathy. Even his schoolmates, initially amused, gradually turned confused, and then impressed. Danny never tried to explain the change. He thanked Davey silently for being there. For being him.

Each day brought improvement. With Davey's persistent guidance and a newfound desire to overcome, Danny would inch towards being free of his fear. With each advance came incredible euphoria, the freedom to navigate his world without the constant paranoia of a creeping insect sending him spiraling. 

But there was a hidden gift within his struggles, a deeper understanding of himself. Danny was not alone. Danny was Davey. Danny was a resilient fourteen-year-old, an orphan with a fear of insects. But most importantly, Danny was capable of self-redemption. All it took was a fear, a friend, and a little help from the information technology. What he was left with was a stronger, better, unified version of himself. A boy unbounded by irrational fear, who could shape his future with the might of not just one, but two souls.

His was a shared existence. An existence that needed not the validation of the world but the understanding and acceptance from within. He was an orphan, he had a fear of insects, but now a chance to seize life with alacrity presented itself.
