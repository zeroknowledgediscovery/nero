Despite his physical strength and calm exterior, Stan was grappling with something frail and unexpected - a debilitating fear of insects.

This phobia loomed over his life, edging into his mind in the middle of the night, in the early hours of morning, tortuously relentless. It started when he was elbow-deep in a gritty kitchen sink, the rusted pipes worn from years of water and neglect. One could call it entomophobia, platyetymology, or what else have you. 

Stan found himself a prisoner to his fear, unable to step back into the trenches of his work. He spent sleepless nights staring at the old deep-set ceiling of his room, hearing the imaginary rustle of tiny legs and wings, while his real world began to crumble around him. He became disengaged, a soul lost in the tides of fear. 

There were the folks who refused to understand, people like Bert Krakowski with his grating laugh, chiding Stan for his fears. "Damn, Stan, haven't seen a man so twisted up about a damn bug," he would drawl, a cruel twist to his voice. This isolation and ridicule only exacerbated Stan's situation, driving him further into a pit of despair.

It was more a family-by-law formality than anything. Though Frida was renown for her seemingly unconventional lifestyle, she was more understanding than most. While others ridiculed Stan, Frida saw his journey as an opportunity. The pain, the fear, the struggle. To her, these were just notes on the grand staff of life, an invisible symphony waiting to be composed, the melody waiting to be played.

With Frida's success blooming, the money began trickling into Stan's life too, allowing him to breathe a little easier. But money never really does bring happiness, does it? Sudden change brought on a discomfort. It felt too unfamiliar, too hard to adapt to.
