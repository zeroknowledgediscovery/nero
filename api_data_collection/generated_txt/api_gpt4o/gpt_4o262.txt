In the modest, cozy diner of Waukesha, the scent of fresh coffee blended with the sweet aroma of pancakes on a chilly Tuesday morning. The clinking of cutlery and soft chatter of patrons filled the air, stitching the threads of daily life with mundane miracles. Ainslie, a lithe and observant waiter, moved between tables with the grace of a violinist, carrying breakfast platters to waiting customers.

Ainslie, with his boyish charm and perpetual half-smile, was a favorite among the regulars. With a napkin tucked neatly into his apron, he glided, attentive to the slightest sign of need or wish expressed with a subtle gesture. His hands were quick but gentle, and his eyes sparkled with a warmth that colored even the grayest of Wisconsin winters.

Outside, the first whispers of spring made promises of warmth, giving life to budding flowers and love alike. Unknown to many, inklings of such sentiments nestled within Ainslie's heart for his best friend, Ross. They were kindred spirits, bound by shared laughter and the adventures that brewed like fine ale in evenings at The Horned Hare pub. Ross, with his thick glasses and perpetual curiosity, painted a picture that Ainslie could never tire of staring at from afar.

Their days were filled with camaraderie and the comforting rhythm of shared silences. It was as though the universe had woven their lives with threads that never frayed, until the edge of dusk one fateful day when the crackling of fate reverberated louder than the coming spring.

It happened without warning, a shrill cluck disrupting the harmony as Ross found himself standing amidst a storm of feathers and panic. The car, a trusted companion of rust and resilience, had met with an unyielding chicken in a tragic embrace. Ross, his face a map of unfolding despair, came to a halt in the face of the beady-eyed chaos, realization dawning with heavy hands upon his shoulders.

True to the nature of their friendship, Ainslie stood beside Ross, offering solace in whispered comforts. He alone understood the shape of Ross's heart, the underlying guilt casting shadows where light once danced. The event, though trivial to some, cleaved a chasm between them. Ross, consumed by self-reproach, withdrew into a world where each cluck of a chicken was a haunting reminder of innocence lost beneath tire treads.

Days bled into weeks, and Ainslie found himself wandering alone through city streets, the absence of Ross a tangible void more palpable than the afternoon sun. Life retained its color but blurred at the edges, like a painting left unfinished. Conversations echoed hollow in spaces they once filled with warmth and laughter.

In his solitude, Ainslie turned to the amber solace of drink, an attempt to drown the pangs within a heart shackled by yearning. At The Horned Hare, pints were poured, and words mumbled only to be lost between the syllables of slow-spoken songs. Conversations blurred, spinning into the night as a dizzying kaleidoscope, while Ainslie attempted to weave tales of heroism behind eyes blurred by tears.

The misstep was neither deliberate nor anticipated, a misplaced moment that hung in the air like a tragic note on an unfinished symphony. It was a night shrouded in too much amber light and too little judgment, the drive back a fog of clouded intentions. Eyes sharp now only in retrospect, Ainslie didn't see the figure stepping into the heartbeat of destiny's path, Ross caught in the gravity of the moment—paralyzed like a deer in headlights.

The screech of brakes was too late, reality colliding harshly against the fabric of fate. Ross's body crumpled with a finality that stole the breath from Ainslie's lungs, leaving an emptiness that echoed more hauntingly than any lonely night. That single, irreversible instant cracked open the world Ainslie knew, casting him adrift in its tumultuous wake.

In the aftermath, time slowed to mimic an eternity. Days enveloped Ainslie in solitude, a landscape barren of redemption but heavy with grief. He haunted familiar places now phantoms of their former joy, everything marked by an absence that ran deeper than the cuts on his soul.

Judgement came as a slow and haunting march of time. Yet amid all the heartache, Ross lingered, his voice a whisper in the wind, urging forgiveness from beyond the tangible grasp of memory. And therein lay the question: could love, impaired by unintended wrongs, find salvation?
