In the quiet town of Spruce Grove, nestled amidst the whispering pines and the gentle slopes of the hills, the people led lives interspersed with simplicity and a touch of quirkiness. Amidst this rustic canvas existed Arthur Page, a humble cleaner by trade, known for his diligence in tidying up the local school. Though his days were consumed by vacuuming carpets and polishing floors, Arthur nurtured a secret dream beneath his utilitarian exterior—a dream of competitive pie eating.

Arthur's affection for pies was not merely an idle fancy. It was a legacy, one his grandfather imparted to him in the form of buttery crust and steaming fruit. 'A slice of life,' his grandfather used to say. Arthur, however, took it quite literally, striving to make every bite count. Year after year, he religiously participated in the local fair's pie-eating contest, his enthusiasm unfaltering, yet victory perpetually eluded him.

It was a balmy spring morning when the seed of possibility sprouted unexpectedly for Arthur. As he shuffled down Main Street, his thoughts wandering between cleaning supplies and apple turnovers, a vibrant poster caught his eye. “The Great Canadian Pie Championship Final – One Slot Open,” it declared in bold, inviting letters. Arthur's heart leapt like a lively trout encountering a stream. This was his chance—his ticket to gastronomic glory and beyond!

The contest was in Calgary, a considerable drive from the quietude of Spruce Grove, but the idea of participating in such a gathering kindled a fire in Arthur’s heart that had long lay dormant. Could he compete against the nation's most formidable pie aficionados? Could this be his path to pie immortality? Casting off routine like a well-worn coat, he enrolled his name and was accepted, thus setting the cogs of destiny into motion.

As the days drew closer to the contest, Arthur’s presence at the school took on a new tenor. "Hey there, Pie Guy!" chorused the cheerful chants of schoolchildren who had caught wind of his impending adventure. Though bashful at the spotlight, Arthur would return their greetings with a wave and a shy grin. His colleagues, too, encouraged his endeavor. Mabel, the kindhearted librarian with a penchant for mystery novels, believed in him fiercely, often expounding theories of how the brain, when focused on a delicious motivation, could propel a man to great feats.

Amidst his escalating excitement, Arthur failed to notice the subtle, unsettling enmity of a peer. A rival in both occupation and ambition, Ethan Kaine, known for his sharp features and sharper tongue, had entered the same contest. Within Ethan burned a competitive spirit, one stoked by a desire not merely to win, but to ensure others did not. To him, victory was not so much a celebration as it was a conquest.

The eve before Arthur’s departure to Calgary, the community gathered at the local hall for a farewell potluck in his honor. Lulled by the laughter and camaraderie, Arthur’s guard slipped. Arthur's lunch, a modest sandwich nestled among the offerings, became Ethan's target.
